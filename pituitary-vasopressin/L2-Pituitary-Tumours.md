## The single idea that unlocks L2

**Each functioning tumour is one of L1's five cell types going rogue and over-producing its hormone.** The presentation is the mirror image of the deficiency:

- Lactotroph underactive (L1) → can't breastfeed. **Lactotroph overactive → galactorrhoea + menstrual disturbance + low libido.**
- Somatotroph underactive (L1, adult) → ↓QoL. **Somatotroph overactive → big hands/feet/jaw + cardiovascular risk.**
- Corticotroph underactive (L1) → fatigue + crisis risk. **Corticotroph overactive → moon face, striae, diabetes, hypertension.**

Excess and deficiency are anatomical siblings. Same cell, opposite volume knob.

### Q: Which cell types produce which functioning tumours, and what's the clinical weight of each?

| Cell type | Tumour | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Somatotroph | **Acromegaly** / gigantism | Major |
| Lactotroph | **Prolactinoma** | Major — commonest |
| Corticotroph | **Cushing's disease** | Major |
| Thyrotroph | TSHoma | Minor — very rare |
| Gonadotroph | Gonadotrophinoma | Minor — very rare |

Plus **non-functioning pituitary adenoma (NFPA)** — presents by mass effect, major.

### Q: Three mechanically different ways a pituitary tumour causes problems?

- **Hormone excess** from the proliferated cell type (functioning adenoma)
- **Mass effect** — chiasm (bitemporal hemianopia), cavernous sinus (CN III/IV/VI), normal pituitary (hypopituitarism, L1), stalk (stalk effect → ↑ PRL)
- **Apoplexy** — haemorrhage or infarction (L1 card)

## Classification

📎 Figure L2.3 — Pituitary tumour classification with MRI example

### Q: Three axes of pituitary tumour classification?

- **Size**: microadenoma <1 cm, macroadenoma >1 cm; sellar vs suprasellar; compressing chiasm or not; invading cavernous sinus or not
- **Function**: excess secretion of a specific hormone vs non-functioning
- **Behaviour**: carcinoma <0.5%; **Ki67 <3% = benign**

> [!warning]
> ⚠️ **Trap — benign histology ≠ benign behaviour**
> Pituitary adenomas with Ki67 <3% can still invade the cavernous sinus, encase the carotid, and recur after surgery. Low Ki67 does NOT predict clinical course. Don't reassure on histology alone.

## Prolactinoma

### Q: How does excess prolactin cause the presentation symptoms?

📎 Figure L2.4 — Hyperprolactinaemia inhibits kisspeptin neurons

- PRL binds receptors on **kisspeptin neurons** in hypothalamus
- Inhibits kisspeptin → ↓GnRH → ↓LH/FSH → ↓sex steroids
- Net effect: **hypogonadism** — menstrual disturbance, ED, low libido, infertility, osteoporosis
- Plus direct effect on breast: **galactorrhoea**

### Q: What prolactin level is typical of a prolactinoma, and how does it relate to tumour size?

- **Usually >5000 mU/L** (upper limit of reference ~550 in women)
- **[PRL] roughly proportional to tumour size**
- Microprolactinoma ~10,000; macroprolactinoma ~60,000+

### Q: Physiological and iatrogenic causes of raised prolactin to exclude before diagnosing prolactinoma?

| Category | Causes |
|---|---|
| Physiological | Pregnancy, breastfeeding, stress / venepuncture, nipple or chest-wall stimulation |
| Pathological (non-tumour) | Primary hypothyroidism, PCOS, chronic renal failure |
| Iatrogenic | Antipsychotics, SSRIs, antiemetics, high-dose oestrogen, opiates |

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Pattern — iatrogenic hyperprolactinaemia**
> Almost all drug causes work by **interfering with dopamine** (the tonic suppressor of lactotrophs). Antipsychotics and metoclopramide are D2 antagonists. Same axis you'll exploit for treatment.

### Q: First-line treatment for prolactinoma, and why it's an exception?

- **Medical, not surgical** — the only functioning pituitary tumour managed this way
- **Cabergoline** (D2 agonist) first-line in UK; bromocriptine less effective
- Cabergoline is **safe in pregnancy** — critical for subfertile women trying to conceive
- Shrinks tumour AND normalises prolactin in one step

### Q: How does cabergoline shrink prolactinomas?

- Lactotrophs express **D2 receptors**
- Hypothalamic dopamine normally travels down the stalk → binds D2 → suppresses PRL
- Cabergoline mimics this dopamine → reinstates suppression and drives tumour regression

## Acromegaly

📎 Figure L2.7 — Gigantism (children) vs acromegaly (adults)
📎 Figure L2.8 — Acromegaly facial coarsening + spade hand

### Q: Why do children get gigantism and adults get acromegaly from the same tumour?

- GH excess drives bone and soft-tissue growth
- **Children**: growth plates open → grow tall (gigantism)
- **Adults**: growth plates fused → cannot grow taller; soft tissue and appositional bone growth only

### Q: How does GH cause its physiological effects?

📎 Figure L2.9 — GH and IGF-1 mechanism of action

- GH acts **directly** on body tissues (metabolic — diabetogenic)
- GH stimulates **liver to produce IGF-1**
- IGF-1 drives tissue growth
- **Why this matters clinically**: IGF-1 is the stable read-out, not pulsatile GH

### Q: Classic presenting features of acromegaly?

- **Sweatiness** and **headache** — active disease markers
- Coarsening of facial features — **macroglossia**, prominent nose, supraorbital ridges
- **Prognathism** (large jaw)
- Increased hand and foot size — rings stop fitting, shoe size increases
- **Snoring / obstructive sleep apnoea**
- Hypertension; impaired glucose tolerance / T2DM

### Q: Why is acromegaly dangerous if untreated?

- **Increased cardiovascular mortality**
- Hypertension + T2DM + cardiomyopathy
- Treatment rationale is survival, not cosmetic
- Mean time to diagnosis from symptom onset ~10 years — insidious

### Q: Diagnostic biochemistry for acromegaly?

📎 Figure L2.10 — OGTT paradoxical rise of GH

- Screen: **elevated serum IGF-1**
- Confirm: **failed suppression (paradoxical rise) of GH on OGTT** — 75 g glucose → GH rises instead of falling
- Then: pituitary MRI to locate and size

> [!warning]
> ⚠️ **Trap — random GH is never the right answer**
> GH is pulsatile. A single measurement could be a peak or a trough. If an SBA option is "random serum GH", it's wrong — go for IGF-1 (screen) or OGTT (confirm).

### Q: First-line treatment of acromegaly, and the medical adjuncts?

📎 Figure L2.11 — Trans-sphenoidal surgical approach

- First-line: **trans-sphenoidal pituitary surgery** — up the nose, through sphenoid sinus, through fossa floor, out through nostril
- Aim: normalise GH and IGF-1
- Medical adjuncts: **somatostatin analogues (octreotide)** — "endocrine cyanide", side effects from gut hormone suppression; **dopamine agonists (cabergoline)**; radiotherapy (slow)

## Cushing's disease

📎 Figure L2.12 — Cushing's syndrome clinical features

### Q: Cushing's SYNDROME vs DISEASE — define both.

- **Syndrome** = any cause of excess cortisol (exogenous steroids most common overall)
- **Disease** = specifically a **pituitary corticotroph adenoma secreting ACTH**
- All disease is syndrome; most syndrome is not disease

### Q: Classify causes of Cushing's syndrome by ACTH status.

| ACTH | Cause |
|---|---|
| Dependent (ACTH ↑ or inappropriate) | **Cushing's disease** (pituitary); ectopic ACTH (small-cell lung Ca) |
| Independent (ACTH ↓) | **Exogenous steroids** (commonest overall); adrenal adenoma / carcinoma |

### Q: Clinical features of Cushing's syndrome?

- **Moon face**, red cheeks
- **Buffalo hump** (interscapular fat pad)
- Thin skin, easy bruising, **purple striae**, poor wound healing
- Central obesity, **proximal myopathy** (thin limbs)
- **Hypertension, impaired glucose tolerance / diabetes**, osteoporosis
- Mood changes, depression

### Q: Three first-line biochemical tests for Cushing's — what does each exploit?

📎 Figure L2.13 — Loss of cortisol diurnal rhythm in Cushing's

- **24-hour urine free cortisol** — bypasses diurnal variation
- **Late-night cortisol** (salivary or blood) — loss of the diurnal nadir
- **Dexamethasone suppression test** — failure to suppress ACTH/cortisol via feedback

> [!warning]
> ⚠️ **Trap — "elevated early-morning cortisol"**
> Morning cortisol is high in everybody. It is NOT a Cushing's screening test. The discriminator is loss of the nadir (late-night) or failure to suppress (dex).

### Q: How do you localise confirmed hypercortisolism?

- **Measure ACTH**
- High ACTH → ACTH-dependent → **pituitary MRI** (Cushing's disease) first; if negative, chest imaging for ectopic
- Suppressed ACTH → ACTH-independent → adrenal imaging

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Pattern — "measure the upstream signal to localise the excess"**
> Mirrors L1's "both low = secondary" logic, applied to excess: end-organ high + upstream high = pituitary or ectopic; end-organ high + upstream low = adrenal or exogenous.

### Q: First-line treatment of Cushing's disease?

- **Trans-sphenoidal pituitary surgery**
- Same route as acromegaly / NFPA
- Aim: resect adenoma, restore normal cortisol regulation

## Non-functioning pituitary adenoma (NFPA)

### Q: Three presentation patterns of NFPA?

📎 Figure L2.14 — Bitemporal hemianopia lecture-theatre visual

- **Visual disturbance** — classically **bitemporal hemianopia** (chiasm compressed from below)
- **Hypopituitarism** (L1 material) — mass effect on normal pituitary
- **Moderately raised prolactin** from the stalk effect

### Q: What is the stalk effect, and how do you tell it from a prolactinoma?

📎 Figure L2.15 — Stalk effect in NFPA

- Tumour squashes stalk → dopamine can't reach lactotrophs → PRL rises
- Elevation is **modest** — hundreds to low thousands
- Prolactinoma is usually **>5000** and proportional to tumour size

> [!warning]
> ⚠️ **Trap — large tumour + PRL ~1000**
> A 2-cm pituitary mass with PRL 980 is **NFPA with stalk effect**, not a macroprolactinoma (which would give PRL in the tens of thousands). Proportionality is the tell.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: First-line treatment by tumour — what's the rule?

| Tumour | First-line |
|---|---|
| Prolactinoma | **Medical** — cabergoline |
| Acromegaly | **Surgical** — trans-sphenoidal |
| Cushing's disease | **Surgical** — trans-sphenoidal |
| NFPA | **Surgical** (if mass effect) |

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Prolactinoma is the only medical-first exception**
> DA agonists shrink tumour AND treat hormone excess in one step. Every other functioning tumour lacks an equivalently effective medical option — surgery first.

### Q: What must be screened for after trans-sphenoidal surgery?

- **Immediate (first 48h)**: urine output, serum sodium, urine osmolality — detects **AVP-D** (L3)
- **Immediate**: morning cortisol — detects ACTH deficiency (L1)
- **4–6 weeks**: full anterior pituitary screen (fT4, LH/FSH + sex steroids, IGF-1, PRL)

### Q: Receptor-selectivity as a drug-design principle across the block — examples?

| Drug | Target | Why selective |
|---|---|---|
| Cabergoline (L2) | D2 agonist | Avoid D1 off-target |
| Octreotide (L2) | Somatostatin analogue | Non-selective — causes "endocrine cyanide" gut side effects |
| Desmopressin (L3) | V2 agonist | Avoid V1 vasoconstriction |
| Tolvaptan (L3) | V2 antagonist | Avoid V1 vasoactive effects |

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA L2.1 — 28-year-old woman with 6 months of irregular periods and bilateral milky nipple discharge. Prolactin 8200 mU/L. Not pregnant, no drugs. First-line management?

- A. Trans-sphenoidal surgery
- B. **Cabergoline** ✅
- C. Repeat prolactin in 4 weeks
- D. Radiotherapy
- E. Reassurance

**Why B**: PRL >5000 with oligomenorrhoea + galactorrhoea in absence of pregnancy/drugs → prolactinoma. Prolactinoma is the medical-first exception. C wastes time at this level. A/D wrong first-line.

### Q: SBA L2.2 — 55-year-old man: 10 years of worsening headaches, sweating, shoe-size increase. BP 158/98, random glucose 11.3 mmol/L. Single best confirmatory test?

- A. Random GH
- B. Random IGF-1
- C. **Rise in GH following oral glucose load** ✅
- D. 24h urine free cortisol
- E. Late-night salivary cortisol

**Why C**: acromegaly. IGF-1 is screening; **OGTT paradoxical rise** is confirmatory. A wrong (GH pulsatile). D/E are for Cushing's. Suspect excess → suppression test → failure to suppress confirms.

### Q: SBA L2.3 — 43-year-old woman: 15 kg weight gain over 2 years, plethora, striae, diabetes. 24h urine cortisol elevated; dex fails to suppress; ACTH 62 ng/L (ref <50). Next investigation?

- A. Adrenal CT
- B. **Pituitary MRI** ✅
- C. Chest CT
- D. Bilateral adrenal venous sampling
- E. Repeat dex test

**Why B**: hypercortisolism confirmed, ACTH not suppressed → ACTH-dependent Cushing's → commonest cause is pituitary (Cushing's disease) → pituitary MRI first. If negative, THEN chest CT for ectopic. A is for ACTH-independent.

### Q: SBA L2.4 — 62-year-old man: progressive peripheral vision loss. MRI: 2.4 cm suprasellar pituitary mass compressing chiasm. PRL 980 mU/L. Testosterone low, LH/FSH low-normal, cortisol 140 (ref >300). Most likely?

- A. Microprolactinoma
- B. Macroprolactinoma
- C. **NFPA with stalk effect** ✅
- D. Craniopharyngioma
- E. Cushing's disease

**Why C**: PRL 980 is **disproportionately modest** for a 2.4 cm tumour — stalk effect, not prolactinoma. Low cortisol and secondary hypogonadism are mass-effect hypopituitarism. B is the distractor — a macroprolactinoma that size would give PRL in the tens of thousands.

### Q: SBA L2.5 — 50-year-old woman post-trans-sphenoidal adenomectomy for Cushing's disease. Most important immediate post-op monitoring?

- A. Serum prolactin
- B. **Fluid balance, serum sodium, urine output** ✅
- C. Visual field testing
- D. Serum IGF-1
- E. Bone mineral density

**Why B**: trans-sphenoidal surgery risks posterior-pituitary / stalk damage → acute AVP-D. Polyuria + rising Na⁺ + falling urine osmolality in the first 48h is the presentation. Bridge into L3.
