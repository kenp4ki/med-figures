## The single idea that unlocks L3

**AVP's only job is water reabsorption.** It tells the kidney collecting duct: "pull water back out of the urine, into the blood." Every disease in this lecture is about whether that signal is absent, ignored, or overshooting.

Three scenarios, three diseases, plus one behavioural mimic:

- **Absent** (can't make AVP) → **AVP deficiency** (formerly cranial DI)
- **Ignored** (kidney can't respond) → **AVP resistance** (formerly nephrogenic DI)
- **Overshooting** (too much AVP) → **SIADH**
- **Behavioural mimic** (drinks too much) → **psychogenic polydipsia**

### Q: Where is AVP made, and where is it released?

- Synthesised in **magnocellular neurons** of the hypothalamus
- Specifically **supraoptic** and **paraventricular** nuclei
- Axons run down the stalk → terminate in **posterior pituitary**
- PP stores and releases — it's a terminal, not a producer

### Q: Why is there no portal circulation for AVP (unlike anterior pituitary hormones)?

- AVP-producing neurons are the same cells at hypothalamus AND posterior pituitary
- No intermediate epithelial gland to signal
- Damage anywhere along the path (nuclei, stalk, PP) causes AVP deficiency

### Q: Primary and secondary actions of AVP?

- Primary: **stimulates water reabsorption in renal collecting duct** via V2 receptor
- Secondary: vasoconstriction via V1 receptor ("vaso-pressin" was named for this)
- Secondary: stimulates ACTH release from anterior pituitary

### Q: Step through the molecular mechanism by which AVP concentrates urine.

📎 Figure L3.3 — Molecular mechanism of AVP water reabsorption

- AVP binds **V2 receptor** on basolateral (blood-side) membrane
- V2 is a GPCR → adenylate cyclase → **cAMP** → **PKA**
- PKA triggers **aquaporin-2** insertion into apical (urine-side) membrane
- Water enters via AQP2, exits via AQP3 on basolateral side
- Net: ↓ urine volume, ↑ urine osmolality, ↓ plasma osmolality

### Q: How does an osmoreceptor detect rising plasma osmolality?

- ↑ extracellular Na⁺ → water moves OUT of osmoreceptor cell by osmosis
- Cell **shrinks** → increased firing rate
- Triggers AVP release from hypothalamic neurons in PP
- "Cell shrinks → cell fires → AVP released"

📎 Figure L3.5 — Osmoreceptor shrinkage-firing

### Q: What does the posterior pituitary look like on MRI, and what does its absence suggest?

- Characteristic hyperintense **"bright spot"** on T1-weighted sagittal MRI
- Caused by AVP-containing neurosecretory granules
- **Absent bright spot → AVP deficiency**

📎 Figure L3.4 — Posterior pituitary bright spot MRI

## When the cascade breaks

### Q: Define AVP-D vs AVP-R. Why are their symptoms identical?

| | Upstream | Kidney |
|---|---|---|
| AVP-D | Can't **make** AVP | Normal |
| AVP-R | Makes AVP fine | Can't **respond** |

Both produce the same downstream — no water reabsorption, dilute urine, dehydration, thirst. Only the break point differs.

### Q: Why did "diabetes insipidus" get renamed?

- Patient-led international campaign ("No need to DI")
- Staff confused diabetes **insipidus** with diabetes **mellitus**
- Patients denied water; some died of dehydration
- **Kane Gorny case** at St George's (2009) — placed on psychiatric watch, denied water despite thirst, died
- Thirst was the **physiological response** keeping him alive, not hysteria

### Q: Presentation of AVP-D / AVP-R?

- **Polyuria** — large daytime urine volumes
- **Nocturia** — repeated night-time waking to pass urine
- **Thirst** — often extreme, drinking all night
- **Polydipsia** — driven by osmoreceptor stimulation

📎 Figure L3.7 — AVP-D/R cascade leading to death (no-water-access pathway)

> [!warning]
> ⚠️ **Trap — the commonest cause of these four symptoms is diabetes mellitus**
> In DM, glucose >~10 mmol/L overwhelms renal reabsorption → osmotic diuresis. Vastly more common than AVP-D/R. **Always check blood glucose first.**

### Q: Typical biochemistry of AVP-D / AVP-R (once DM excluded)?

- Urine: very dilute (hypo-osmolar), large volumes
- Plasma: hyperosmolar, **hypernatraemia**
- Glucose: **normal** (rules out DM)

### Q: Causes of AVP deficiency, ranked?

📎 Figure L3.8 — Pituitary stalk thickening

- **Acquired (more common)** — traumatic brain injury, **pituitary surgery**, pituitary tumours, inflammation of stalk (TB → thickened stalk on MRI; sarcoid), autoimmune
- **Congenital** — rare

### Q: Causes of AVP resistance?

- **Much less common** than AVP-D
- Congenital (rare): **V2 receptor** or **aquaporin-2** mutations
- Acquired: **lithium** (the one to remember)

> [!tip]
> ⭐ **Lithium + polyuria + normal glucose → AVP resistance**
> Classic SBA stem. Lithium is the single acquired drug cause to know.

## The mimic — psychogenic polydipsia

### Q: Why does psychogenic polydipsia look identical to AVP-D/R on symptoms, but come from the opposite mechanism?

- Symptoms are identical: polyuria, nocturia, polydipsia, thirst
- Mechanism is **opposite**: patient drinks too much water (behavioural)
- Body appropriately suppresses AVP in response to dilution
- Kidney correctly makes dilute urine to shed excess water
- Plasma sodium is **low-normal**, not high — discriminator on bloods

## Discrimination — the water deprivation test

📎 Figure L3.9 — Water deprivation test graph

### Q: Setup and safety rule for water deprivation test?

- Patient in a room with **no access to water** (no taps, jug, or flowers)
- Measure urine volume, urine osmolality, plasma osmolality over hours
- **Weigh regularly — stop if >3% body weight lost**
- 3% weight loss marks clinically significant dehydration, dangerous in real AVP problem

### Q: Part 1 — how do the three candidate diagnoses behave on water deprivation?

- **Normal**: urine concentrates robustly to ~800–1000 mOsm/kg
- **Psychogenic polydipsia**: concentrates but **blunted** (~400–600) — chronic overhydration has washed out the medullary gradient
- **AVP-D or AVP-R**: stays **<200 mOsm/kg** regardless of dehydration

### Q: Part 2 — how does desmopressin distinguish AVP-D from AVP-R?

📎 Figure L3.10 — Desmopressin test response

- Give synthetic AVP (desmopressin, V2-selective) to patients who failed to concentrate
- **AVP-D**: kidney is fine, just missing signal → urine **concentrates sharply**
- **AVP-R**: kidney can't respond → urine osmolality **stays flat**

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Pattern — "when upstream signal is broken, deliver it and watch the end-organ"**
> Same intellectual move as L1's titration rule (use fT4, not TSH, when pituitary is broken). L3 version: don't measure AVP directly — deliver synthetic AVP and watch the kidney. **See also: L1 — "TSH deficiency treatment trap".**

## Treatment

### Q: Treatment of AVP deficiency?

- **Desmopressin** — synthetic, **V2-selective**
- Preparations: tablets, intranasal spray
- Monitor plasma sodium, urine output, symptoms

### Q: Why must desmopressin be V2-selective and not also V1-active?

- V1 activation would cause vasoconstriction — unwanted side effect
- V2-selectivity limits action to kidney collecting duct
- Same receptor-selectivity principle as cabergoline (L2) for D2 vs D1

### Q: Treatment of AVP resistance?

- **Very rare, very difficult to treat** (explicitly de-emphasised in the lecture)
- Remove cause if possible (stop lithium)
- Adjuncts: salt/protein restriction, thiazide diuretics (paradoxically reduce urine volume)

## The opposite problem — SIADH

### Q: How does SIADH produce its biochemistry?

- "Too much AVP" — inappropriately secreted or ectopic
- ↑ water reabsorption → water retention
- Dilutes blood → **hyponatraemia, low plasma osmolality**
- Urine stays **concentrated** (AVP is working on the kidney)
- Urine volume falls

### Q: AVP-D vs SIADH biochemistry — the mirror-image table.

| | AVP-D / AVP-R | SIADH |
|---|---|---|
| Plasma Na⁺ | ↑ (hyper) | ↓ (hypo) |
| Plasma osm | ↑ | ↓ |
| Urine osm | ↓ (dilute) | ↑ (concentrated) |
| Urine volume | ↑↑ | ↓ |

> [!tip]
> ⭐ **Mirror-image table is the core discrimination skill**
> Any two values usually fix the other two. Hypernatraemia rules out SIADH and psychogenic polydipsia immediately — useful shortcut.

### Q: Causes of SIADH?

- **CNS**: head injury, stroke, tumour, meningitis
- **Lung infections**: pneumonia
- **Malignancy**: small-cell lung cancer (ectopic AVP)
- **Drug-related**: antiepileptics, antidepressants
- Idiopathic — very common in elderly inpatients

### Q: Work-up for new SIADH?

- **Chest X-ray** — lung cancer, pneumonia
- **CT brain** — CNS cause
- Both routine because both categories are prevalent and treatable

### Q: Management of SIADH?

- First-line: **fluid restriction** (usually <500 mL/day)
- Second-line: **vaptans (eg. tolvaptan)** — V2 **antagonists** that block the kidney's response to excess AVP
- Vaptans are £££££ — reserved for malignancy-related chronic SIADH
- Common cause of prolonged hospital stay in elderly

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Pattern — V2 agonist vs V2 antagonist**
> Same receptor, opposite drug direction, opposite disease. Desmopressin (V2 agonist) treats AVP-D. Vaptan (V2 antagonist) treats SIADH. Direction of the arrow depends on the problem.

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Post-trans-sphenoidal surgery polyuria — what is it?**
> Stalk / posterior pituitary damage → acute AVP-D. Polyuria + rising Na⁺ in the first 48h is the classic presentation. **See also: L2 — "Post-operative monitoring priorities".**

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Desmopressin is V2-selective — same principle as cabergoline?**
> Yes — receptor-subtype selectivity to avoid off-target activation. Cabergoline: D2, not D1. Desmopressin: V2, not V1. **See also: L2 — "How cabergoline works".**

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Why isn't Sheehan's on the list of AVP-D causes?**
> Because Sheehan's spares the posterior pituitary (unique vascular pattern). Apoplexy, surgery, TBI, tumour, radiation can all cause AVP-D. Sheehan's is the single exception. **See also: L1 — "Sheehan's syndrome".**

## SBAs

### Q: SBA L3.1 — 67-year-old man on lithium: 3 months of thirst, large urine volumes day and night. Random glucose 5.8. Na⁺ 148, plasma osm 305, urine osm 120. Most likely?

- A. Diabetes mellitus
- B. AVP deficiency
- C. **AVP resistance** ✅
- D. Psychogenic polydipsia
- E. SIADH

**Why C**: glucose normal (rules out A). Hyperosmolar plasma + hypernatraemia + dilute urine = AVP-D/R picture (rules out E). Lithium is the classic drug cause of AVP resistance. D would have **low** Na⁺, not hypernatraemia.

### Q: SBA L3.2 — 72-year-old woman admitted with pneumonia: Na⁺ 124 (was 138), plasma osm 258, urine osm 510. Euvolaemic. Initial management?

- A. 0.9% saline infusion
- B. Desmopressin
- C. **Fluid restriction to 500 mL/day** ✅
- D. Hypertonic saline bolus
- E. Oral salt tablets

**Why C**: low Na⁺ + low plasma osm + **concentrated** urine + euvolaemic + pneumonia trigger = **SIADH**. First-line is fluid restriction. A worsens it. B is wrong direction (V2 agonist — worsens retention). D only for symptomatic severe hyponatraemia with seizures/coma.

### Q: SBA L3.3 — 22-year-old admitted with suspected psychogenic polydipsia. Water deprivation test: urine osm rises 95 → 520 over 6h. Plasma osm 291. Weight loss 1.4%. Next step?

- A. Give desmopressin; measure at 9 hours
- B. Diagnose AVP deficiency, start desmopressin
- C. Stop test — positive for AVP resistance
- D. **Diagnose psychogenic polydipsia and stop test** ✅
- E. Continue deprivation for another 6 hours

**Why D**: concentrated urine (95 → 520) rules out AVP-D/R (would stay <200). Response is blunted (normal would reach 800–1000) but clearly positive → psychogenic polydipsia (washed-out medullary gradient). Plasma osm normal, weight loss within safety limits. Desmopressin phase is only for patients who DIDN'T concentrate.

### Q: SBA L3.4 — Day 1 post-trans-sphenoidal resection of NFPA: urine output 400 mL/hour, Na⁺ rising 140 → 149. Diagnosis?

- A. SIADH
- B. Acute kidney injury
- C. **Post-operative AVP deficiency** ✅
- D. Psychogenic polydipsia
- E. Diabetes mellitus

**Why C**: trans-sphenoidal surgery can damage stalk / PP → acute AVP-D. Polyuria + rising Na⁺ in the first 48h is classic. A gives opposite Na⁺. B causes oliguria. D/E don't match the acute post-op tempo. Convergence of L2 and L3.

### Q: SBA L3.5 — 45-year-old with NFPA started on desmopressin 3 weeks ago. Now Na⁺ 122, confused. What happened?

- A. AVP deficiency resolved
- B. Developed AVP resistance
- C. **Over-treated with desmopressin → iatrogenic SIADH-like picture** ✅
- D. Acute adrenal insufficiency
- E. NFPA converted to functioning adenoma

**Why C**: too much desmopressin in an AVP-D patient → excess water retention → dilutional hyponatraemia. Hormone doesn't self-limit — give too much, keep reabsorbing water. D is always a differential post-pituitary-surgery but typically presents with hypotension + hypoglycaemia, not isolated hyponatraemia.
