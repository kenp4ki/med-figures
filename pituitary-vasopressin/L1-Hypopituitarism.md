## The single idea that unlocks L1

Every anterior pituitary axis is a chain of command: **hypothalamus → pituitary → target gland → peripheral hormone**. Feedback runs backwards. If you know where the break is, you can derive every blood-test pattern instead of memorising it.

- Gland fails (primary) → peripheral hormone LOW → upstream signal HIGH
- Pituitary fails (secondary) → peripheral hormone LOW + pituitary signal LOW

**Corollary for treatment**: when the upstream signal is broken, titrate by **end-organ effect** (fT4, IGF-1), not by the broken signal.

## Anatomy and axes

### Q: Name the five anterior pituitary cell types and their hormones.

- Somatotroph → **GH**
- Lactotroph → **prolactin**
- Thyrotroph → **TSH**
- Gonadotroph → **LH, FSH**
- Corticotroph → **ACTH**

### Q: Why must hypothalamic releasing hormones travel via the portal system and not the systemic circulation?

- Made in tiny amounts — wouldn't survive systemic dilution
- Hypophyseal portal veins in the stalk bathe the anterior pituitary directly
- A severed/compressed stalk disconnects control without killing either gland
- Lactotrophs lose tonic dopamine → **prolactin rises** (stalk effect, L2)

### Q: Why is aldosterone preserved in secondary adrenal failure but lost in primary?

- Cortisol is controlled by **ACTH**
- Aldosterone is controlled by **renin–angiotensin**, not ACTH
- Pituitary failure kills cortisol arm only
- Adrenal gland destruction (Addison's) kills both

> [!warning]
> ⚠️ **Trap — "low TSH means hyperthyroidism"**
> Not always. Low TSH + **low** fT4 = secondary hypothyroidism. Low TSH + **high** fT4 = primary hyperthyroidism. Always look at both numbers.

> [!tip]
> ⭐ **The "both low" pattern**
> In secondary dysfunction, both the pituitary signal AND the peripheral hormone are low. One low hormone tells you nothing on its own.

## Causes of hypopituitarism

📎 Figure L1.4 — Anterior pituitary failure schematic

### Q: How common is congenital hypopituitarism, and what's the minimum clinical feature set?

- Rare
- Transcription factor mutations for AP development
- **Deficient in GH + ≥1 other AP hormone**
- Short stature; hypoplastic AP on MRI

### Q: List the major acquired causes of hypopituitarism, ranked by clinical weight.

- **Pituitary tumours** (adenomas, mets, cysts) — commonest
- **Pituitary surgery** — iatrogenic, also risks AVP-D
- **Traumatic brain injury**
- **Pituitary apoplexy** — acute emergency
- **Peri-partum infarction (Sheehan's)** — classic SBA
- **Radiation** — delayed, up to 10y risk
- Minor: infection (meningitis), hypophysitis

### Q: What's the key timing rule for radiotherapy-induced hypopituitarism?

- Risk persists **up to 10 years** post-RT
- **GH and gonadotrophins most sensitive** (drop first)
- ACTH and TSH more resistant
- Needs **annual endocrine assessment** for a decade

### Q: Why does Sheehan's syndrome happen, and what's its distinguishing feature?

- PPH → hypotension → infarction of hypertrophied AP
- Pregnancy causes lactotroph hyperplasia → enlarged AP vulnerable in a fixed bony cup
- Presents with lactation failure, no return of menses, fatigue/weight loss
- **Posterior pituitary usually NOT affected** — key discriminator

> [!tip]
> ⭐ **Sheehan's is unique among pituitary insults**
> Apoplexy, surgery, TBI, tumour, radiation can all hit both halves of the pituitary. Sheehan's spares the posterior lobe. This is a high-frequency SBA discriminator.

### Q: What is pituitary apoplexy and what precipitates it?

- Sudden haemorrhage or infarction of the pituitary
- Usually into a **pre-existing adenoma** (may be first presentation)
- **Anticoagulants** are a classic precipitant
- Medical emergency — adrenal crisis from acute ACTH loss is the immediate life-threat

### Q: Why does pituitary apoplexy cause bitemporal hemianopia, diplopia, and ptosis?

📎 Figure L1.8 — Apoplexy MRI + cavernous sinus anatomy

- **Above** pituitary: optic chiasm → compression → **bitemporal hemianopia**
- **Flanking** pituitary: cavernous sinus carrying **CN III, IV, V₁, V₂, VI** + ICA
- CN IV and VI palsy → **diplopia**
- CN III palsy → **ptosis** (down-and-out pupil)

## Presentations per axis

### Q: How does LH/FSH deficiency present?

- Reduced libido (both sexes)
- Secondary amenorrhoea
- Erectile dysfunction
- Reduced pubic / axillary hair

### Q: How do ACTH, TSH, GH, and PRL deficiency present in adults?

- **ACTH** → fatigue; adrenal crisis risk in intercurrent illness
- **TSH** → fatigue (milder than primary)
- **GH (adults)** → reduced QoL (non-specific)
- **PRL** → inability to breastfeed

> [!warning]
> ⚠️ **Trap — adult GH deficiency and short stature**
> Short stature is only a feature in **childhood** GH deficiency. Adults have fused growth plates — they present with QoL changes, not height.

## Investigations

### Q: Why are basal pituitary hormone measurements often unreliable?

- **Cortisol** — strong diurnal rhythm (peaks AM, trough midnight)
- **T4** — half-life 6 days; acute failure delayed on fT4
- **FSH/LH** — cyclical in women
- **GH and ACTH** — pulsatile, undetectable between pulses in health

### Q: What does the insulin tolerance test (ITT) measure, and what stimulus does it use?

- Give IV insulin → hypoglycaemia <2.2 mmol/L = "stress"
- Stress stimulates **ACTH and GH** (both stress hormones)
- Measure cortisol rise (as ACTH proxy) + GH
- Tests pituitary deficiency, not excess

> [!tip]
> 🧠 **Pattern — stimulation vs suppression tests**
> Suspect **deficiency** → stimulate and see if the gland responds (ITT). Suspect **excess** → try to suppress and see if it refuses (OGTT for acromegaly, dex suppression for Cushing's, L2).

### Q: What is "empty sella", and what's the imaging modality of choice?

- **Pituitary MRI** — CT is inadequate for pituitary tissue
- Empty sella = thin rim of pituitary tissue in the fossa (rest has atrophied)
- Same scan is used in L2 (tumours) and L3 (posterior pituitary bright spot)

## Treatment

### Q: What's the unifying treatment principle for hypopituitarism?

- Replace the **peripheral (end-organ) hormone**, not the pituitary signal
- Prolactin is never replaced (inability to breastfeed is the only consequence)
- Exception for fertility: replace LH/FSH themselves (testosterone alone can't drive sperm/ova)

### Q: How is TSH deficiency treated, and what's the titration trap?

- **Levothyroxine** once daily PO
- **Aim fT4 above middle of reference range**
- TSH stays low (it's broken) — **cannot** titrate by TSH, unlike primary hypothyroidism
- Same logic will return in L3 (water deprivation test)

### Q: What are the two UK regimens for ACTH deficiency?

- **Hydrocortisone** TDS (eg. 10/5/5 mg) — mimics diurnal rhythm better
- **Prednisolone** once daily AM (eg. 3 mg) — simpler

📎 Figure L1.10 — Diurnal cortisol replacement graph

### Q: How is male hypogonadism treated — no fertility vs fertility required?

- **No fertility**: testosterone (topical or IM); monitor plasma testosterone
- **Fertility**: gonadotropin injections (LH/FSH) — testosterone alone won't produce sperm
- Sperm production takes 6–12 months
- Best response if hypogonadism developed **after** puberty

### Q: How is female hypogonadism treated, and what's the endometrial trap?

- **No fertility**: oral or topical oestrogen
- **Must add progestogen if uterus is intact** — unopposed oestrogen → endometrial hyperplasia → cancer risk
- Fertility: timed gonadotropin injections (IVF)

### Q: How is GH deficiency treated and monitored?

- **Daily subcutaneous injection** (no oral form — it's a peptide)
- Confirm deficiency on dynamic test first (NICE)
- Monitor by **plasma IGF-1** + QoL questionnaire

### Q: What are the sick day rules for patients on steroid replacement?

- **Steroid alert pendant/bracelet** at all times
- **Double the glucocorticoid dose** if febrile or unwell
- If unable to take tablets (vomiting) → **IM hydrocortisone or straight to A&E**
- Applies to Addison's AND secondary adrenal failure

> [!tip]
> ⭐ **Why sick day rules are tested every year**
> Adrenal crisis (dizziness, hypotension, vomiting, weakness → collapse and death) during intercurrent illness is a predictable, preventable death. This is a classic SBA management answer.

## SBAs

### Q: SBA — 72-year-old woman on hydrocortisone 10/5/5 mg for secondary adrenal insufficiency develops UTI with fever. Next dose is due. Best action?

- A. Take her usual dose and attend GP
- B. Skip the dose
- C. **Double hydrocortisone dose and continue** ✅
- D. Stop hydrocortisone entirely
- E. Switch to prednisolone

**Why C**: sick day rules — double the dose during intercurrent illness to cover physiological stress demand. Skipping or stopping precipitates adrenal crisis. Usual dose is inadequate stress coverage.

### Q: SBA — 29-year-old woman 6 weeks post-delivery (PPH requiring transfusion) reports inability to breastfeed, exhaustion, periods haven't returned. Most likely hormone profile?

- A. **Low PRL, low FSH/LH, low cortisol, normal ADH** ✅
- B. High PRL, high FSH/LH, high cortisol, normal ADH
- C. Low PRL, low FSH/LH, low cortisol, low ADH
- D. High PRL, low FSH/LH, high cortisol, normal ADH
- E. Normal PRL, high FSH/LH, low cortisol, normal ADH

**Why A**: classic Sheehan's — PPH → infarction of hyperplastic AP → all anterior hormones drop. **Posterior usually spared → ADH normal** (rules out C). This distinction is why Sheehan's is tested so often.

### Q: SBA — 52-year-old woman 6 months post-radiotherapy for parasellar meningioma. New fatigue and low libido. Cortisol 320 (ref >300), fT4 11 (ref 12–22), TSH 1.6 (ref 0.4–4.0), LH 0.8 (low), oestradiol low. Most likely explanation?

- A. Perimenopause
- B. Primary ovarian failure
- C. **Secondary hypogonadism and secondary hypothyroidism from RT-induced hypopituitarism** ✅
- D. Sheehan's syndrome
- E. NFPA

**Why C**: **GH and gonadotrophins are the most radiosensitive axes** (drop first). Low LH with low oestradiol = secondary hypogonadism (menopause would give HIGH LH/FSH). Inappropriately normal TSH with low fT4 = secondary hypothyroidism. Tests the "both low = secondary" pattern applied post-RT.
