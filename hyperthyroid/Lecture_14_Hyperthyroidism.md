## 1 — Mechanism spine

### Q: Draw the HPT axis in one line with the feedback targets.

```
Hypothalamus ──TRH──▶ Pituitary thyrotrophs ──TSH──▶ Thyroid ──T4, T3──▶ Body (BMR)
      ▲                       ▲                                │
      └───────────────────────┴──────── negative feedback ─────┘
```

T3/T4 feed back on **both** pituitary (main) and hypothalamus.

🟨 Lecturer: "if asked to draw this, this is it" — drawable exam answer.

### Q: In primary hypothyroidism, what does TSH do and why?

- **TSH rises** (high).
- Gland can't make T4 → T4 falls → pituitary senses low T4 and secretes more TSH trying to flog the dead gland.
- In primary gland disease, TSH always moves **opposite** to T4.

### Q: When replacing thyroxine, what do you monitor and how do you titrate?

- Monitor **TSH**.
- Increase thyroxine dose slowly until TSH falls into normal range.
- T4 levels fluctuate with dose timing — TSH is the integrator.

### Q: What are the four stages of thyroid hormone synthesis?

1. **Iodide uptake** — active transport via iodide trap
2. **Iodination** — TPO + H₂O₂ attach I to tyrosine on thyroglobulin → MIT, DIT
3. **Coupling + storage** — TPO couples MIT+DIT→T3, DIT+DIT→T4; stored in colloid (~1 month supply)
4. **Endocytosis + secretion** — TSH triggers release into blood

📎 Figure L14.1 — Netter thyroid follicular cell

### Q: Why is thyroid peroxidase the key enzyme to know?

- TPO works **twice** in synthesis — both iodination and coupling.
- Blocking TPO disables the entire middle of the pathway.
- Both thionamides (carbimazole, PTU) inhibit TPO.

### Q: Why does the ~1 month colloid warehouse matter clinically?

- Thionamides take **weeks** to work clinically (block synthesis but warehouse keeps releasing).
- Viral thyroiditis causes ~1 month of hyperthyroidism **without new synthesis** (warehouse dumping).
- 1 month after viral damage → hypothyroid (warehouse empty, factory dead).

### Q: How does T3 cause hyperthyroid symptoms given that adrenaline is normal?

- T3 **sensitises β-adrenoceptors** to ambient adrenaline/noradrenaline.
- Normal sympathetic tone feels like an adrenaline surge.
- β₁ sensitisation → tachycardia, palpitations.
- β₂ sensitisation → fine tremor, warm moist palms.
- Müller's muscle sensitisation → lid lag.

🟦 **Reusable pattern — receptor sensitisation, not hormone flood.** Hyperthyroid symptoms come from amplified response to *normal* catecholamines. Same pattern appears in thyroid storm and explains why β-blockade works so fast.

### Q: Lid lag vs exophthalmos — mechanism?

| Sign | Mechanism | Seen in | Reverses with Tx? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lid lag | Functional — T3-sensitised sympathetic tone on Müller's muscle | Any hyperthyroidism | Yes |
| Exophthalmos | Anatomical — Graves' antibodies cause retro-orbital muscle hypertrophy | Graves' only | Often no |

🟥 "Eye signs" in a stem does not automatically mean Graves'. Lid lag occurs in toxic nodular too. Only **exophthalmos** (and pretibial myxoedema) lock it to Graves'.

## 2 — Atlas: Graves' disease

### Q: What is the core pathophysiology of Graves' disease?

- **Autoimmune.**
- IgG antibodies (TRAb) bind and **stimulate** the TSH receptor.
- Whole gland driven hyperactive → diffuse smooth goitre + hyperthyroidism.

### Q: What are the three antibody targets in Graves' disease and the sign each produces?

| Antibody target | Sign |
|---|---|
| TSH receptor on thyroid | Diffuse smooth goitre + hyperthyroidism |
| Retro-orbital muscle growth factor receptors | Exophthalmos |
| Pretibial fibroblast growth factor receptors | Pretibial myxoedema |

📎 Figure L14.3 — Exophthalmos · 📎 Figure L14.4 — Pretibial myxoedema

### Q: Who typically gets Graves' disease?

- **Women**, 12–50 years, typically 30–40.
- Family history common.
- Associated with other autoimmune disease.

### Q: What is the first-line investigation to confirm Graves'?

- **TSH-receptor antibody (TRAb)** in bloodstream.
- Positive = Graves'.
- Bloods before scan.

🟨 Transcript: "you just order it on the computer and you get the result the next day. Positive. You got your diagnosis." 📎 Figure L14.5

### Q: What does the radioiodine scan show in Graves'?

- **Diffuse, symmetrical, uniformly intense uptake** — butterfly/inverted-V shape.
- Pyramidal lobe may be visible in midline.
- Gland so hungry for iodine it absorbs nearly all the tracer.

📎 Figure L14.6 — Graves' scintigram

🟥 **Myxoedema vs pretibial myxoedema.** "Myxoedema" (old term) = severe **hypo**thyroidism. "Pretibial myxoedema" = **hyper**thyroid Graves' skin sign. Lecturer: "don't mix that up in an exam."

## 3 — Atlas: Toxic nodular thyroid disease

### Q: What is the core pathophysiology of toxic nodular disease?

- **Not autoimmune.**
- Benign adenoma(s) — single toxic nodule or multinodular goitre.
- Autonomously produce T4 without needing TSH.
- No antibodies → **no exophthalmos, no pretibial myxoedema.**

### Q: Who typically gets toxic nodular disease?

- Older patients (**50s–60s**).
- Either sex (not female-skewed).
- Onset slow — can be present for years.

### Q: Why does the non-nodular part of the gland appear cold on scintigraphy?

- Autonomous nodule raises T4 → suppresses TSH.
- Normal TSH-dependent follicles lose their stimulus → stop taking up iodine → atrophy.
- Scan: **hot nodule, cold surrounding gland.**

📎 Figure L14.7 — Toxic nodule composite · 📎 Figure L14.8 — Hot nodule scintigram

### Q: Why is a hot nodule reassuring against malignancy?

- Malignant thyroid nodules are almost always **cold** (non-functioning).
- A hot nodule on scan → benign autonomous adenoma → FNA not needed.
- Cold nodules with suspicious features → FNA.

## 4 — Atlas: de Quervain's viral thyroiditis

### Q: What is the core pathophysiology of de Quervain's thyroiditis?

- Virus destroys thyroid follicles.
- Stored pre-formed T3/T4 leaks into blood → transient hyperthyroidism.
- **No new synthesis** — factory dead.
- Self-limiting; recovery over 2–4 months.

### Q: What are the key clinical features of de Quervain's?

- **Painful dysphagia**, thyroid tender to palpate
- Pain often **radiates to the ear**
- Fever, malaise (builds then subsides over ~7 days)
- Tender pretracheal lymph nodes
- Raised ESR/CRP

📎 Figure L14.10 — viral thyroiditis clinical figure

🟥 **Pain radiating to ear** is a high-yield transcript-flagged pointer — easy to miss, diagnostic when paired with hyperthyroid bloods.

### Q: What is the diagnostic fingerprint of viral thyroiditis?

- Low TSH + high free T4 (toxic bloods) **plus**
- **Zero radioiodine uptake** on scan — blank scan
- No other hyperthyroid cause produces toxic bloods + zero uptake.

### Q: What is the natural history of viral thyroiditis?

```
Month 1 ── Month 2 ── Month 2–4
HYPER       HYPO       Recovery → EUTHYROID

Warehouse   Warehouse   Gland slowly
dumping     empty,      resumes function
stored T4   factory off
```

- 1 month hyperthyroid → 1 month hypothyroid → recovery by 2–4 months.

### Q: Why are thionamides useless in viral thyroiditis?

- Thionamides block new T4/T3 synthesis.
- In viral thyroiditis the problem is **released pre-formed hormone** — no new synthesis to block.
- Treatment is supportive: NSAIDs (pain/fever), propranolol (sympathetic symptoms).

🟦 **Reusable pattern — drug choice depends on what's wrong, not on whether the patient is hyperthyroid.** Synthesis block (thionamide) only works against synthesis.

## 5 — Atlas: Postpartum thyroiditis

### Q: What is postpartum thyroiditis?

- **Same pathophysiology and natural history as de Quervain's.**
- But **painless** and only after pregnancy.
- 1 month hyper → 1 month hypo → slow recovery.

### Q: Why does pregnancy trigger this?

- Immune system is modulated (suppressed) during pregnancy to tolerate fetal antigens.
- After delivery, that modulation releases — rebound autoimmunity attacks thyroid.
- Same mechanism explains why Graves' peaks in young women.

### Q: Classic missed stem — why is postpartum thyroiditis easy to miss?

- Symptoms (palpitations, weight loss, anxiety) often attributed to "just had a baby".
- Always check TFTs in a recently-pregnant woman with these features.
- Suppressed TSH + raised T4 + **no pain** + recent delivery = postpartum thyroiditis.

## 6 — The 4-way discrimination

### Q: Which of the four causes has eye or shin signs?

- Only **Graves' disease**.
- Exophthalmos (anatomical) and pretibial myxoedema (non-pitting shin swelling).
- Both are **pathognomonic** — stop thinking, diagnose Graves'.

### Q: Which of the four causes presents with neck pain?

- Only **de Quervain's viral thyroiditis**.
- Plus fever, raised CRP, pain radiating to ear.
- Postpartum is **painless**; Graves' and toxic nodular never cause pain.

### Q: What do the three scintigraphy patterns mean?

| Scan pattern | Mechanism | Cause |
|---|---|---|
| Diffuse, uniform, intense | Whole gland stimulated | Graves' |
| Focal hot spot, cold periphery | Autonomous lump | Toxic nodule |
| Zero / blank | Gland destroyed, leaking stored T4 | Viral or postpartum |

🟦 **The scan tells you mechanism, not biochemistry.** All four causes can give low TSH + high T4. The scan is the branch-point test.

### Q: Four-step discrimination mnemonic for a hyperthyroid stem?

1. **Eye or shin signs?** → Graves'.
2. **Painful neck ± fever?** → de Quervain's.
3. **Recently pregnant?** → Postpartum.
4. **Older patient, palpable nodule?** → Toxic nodular.

🟦 **Reusable pattern — symptom/sign branch points.** When labs are identical across diseases, use history + examination to drive mechanism search, then confirm with the mechanism-specific test.

### Q: How is antibody status used to distinguish causes?

- **TRAb positive** → Graves'.
- TRAb negative + focal hot nodule → toxic nodular.
- TRAb negative + zero uptake → thyroiditis (viral or postpartum by clinical context).

## 7 — Thyroid storm (emergency)

### Q: What defines thyroid storm?

- Decompensated, life-threatening hyperthyroidism.
- **50% mortality untreated.**
- Occurs in undiagnosed / untreated hyperthyroidism + trigger (infection, surgery, trauma).

### Q: What are the 5 diagnostic features of thyroid storm?

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Hyperpyrexia | **>41°C** |
| Tachycardia / arrhythmia | **>140 bpm** (transcript) |
| Cardiac failure | High-output failure |
| CNS | Delirium, frank psychosis |
| Liver | Hepatocellular dysfunction, jaundice |

🟥 **HR >140 bpm** is a transcript-only threshold — not on slide. Classic stem trigger.

### Q: What is the drug order in thyroid storm treatment?

```
1. Propranolol      → β-block the storm
2. PTU / carbimazole → block new synthesis FIRST
3. Potassium iodide → block release (Wolff–Chaikoff)
                      — ONLY after thionamide
4. Supportive      → cooling, fluids, treat precipitant
```

🟥 **Thionamide before iodide.** Iodide given to an unblocked gland becomes substrate for more T4 — makes it worse.

## 8 — Treatment ladder

### Q: What is the role of β-blockers in hyperthyroidism?

- **Symptom bridge** — effect within **hours**.
- Drug of choice: **propranolol** (non-selective β₁ + β₂).
- Non-selectivity matters: β₁ (HR) and β₂ (tremor) both need blocking.
- Always adjunctive — does not treat underlying disease.

### Q: Why is propranolol non-selective specifically needed?

- β₁ blockade → controls tachycardia, palpitations.
- β₂ blockade → controls tremor, warm moist palms.
- A cardio-selective agent (bisoprolol) misses the tremor.

### Q: How do thionamides work and what is the timing mismatch?

- Inhibit **thyroid peroxidase** → block iodination + coupling → no new T4/T3 synthesis.
- **Biochemical effect: hours.**
- **Clinical effect: weeks** — the ~1 month stored warehouse must drain first.
- Propranolol bridges the gap.

### Q: Which two thionamides are used and when?

- **Carbimazole (CBZ)** — first choice in UK.
- **Propylthiouracil (PTU)** — preferred in pregnancy (first trimester) and thyroid storm.
- Used for Graves' and toxic nodular disease. **Not for thyroiditis.**

### Q: What is the classic exam stem for thionamide agranulocytosis?

- Patient on carbimazole develops **sore throat + fever** (any time in treatment).
- **Stop drug, urgent FBC.**
- Rare (~1 in 10,000) but reversible on withdrawal.
- If confirmed → can **never** have any thionamide again (class effect) → move to RAI / surgery.

🟥 Sore throat + fever on carbimazole = agranulocytosis until proven otherwise. Do not switch to PTU; both cause it.

### Q: How long is a typical thionamide course and what comes next?

- Usually **18 months**, then stop and reassess.
- Monitor TFTs for remission vs relapse.
- ~50% relapse → definitive treatment (RAI or surgery).

### Q: What are the 3 indications for potassium iodide, and the one "NOT"?

- ✅ Preparation of hyperthyroid patients for **surgery** (10-day course)
- ✅ Severe **thyrotoxic crisis** (thyroid storm)
- 🟥 **NOT** for patients on medical management of Graves' — gland escapes after ~10 days and makes more T4.

### Q: What is the Wolff–Chaikoff effect and its timing?

- Large iodide load → autoregulatory suppression of thyroid synthesis + secretion.
- Mechanism: inhibits iodination of thyroglobulin and inhibits H₂O₂/TPO.
- **Symptom reduction: 1–2 days.**
- **Vascularity + gland size reduction: 10–14 days.**

📎 Figure L14.9 — gland comparison (hyperplastic / +thiouracil / +thiouracil+iodide)

### Q: Why do surgeons like a pre-op KI course?

- Gland becomes pale, firm, less vascular.
- A toxic Graves' gland bleeds heavily during surgery.
- 10 days of KI → atrophy + reduced bleeding → safer operation.

### Q: What are the key specifics of radioiodine therapy?

- **I-131** (β-emitter — destroys follicles)
- Dose **~370 MBq (10 mCi)**, oral capsule
- 🟥 **Contraindicated in pregnancy** — crosses placenta, ablates fetal thyroid
- Avoid close contact with children and pregnant women for several days
- Outcome: permanent hypothyroidism → lifelong thyroxine

### Q: I-131 vs Tc-99m — how do they differ?

- **I-131** = therapy. β-radiation destroys follicles.
- **Tc-99m pertechnetate** = scanning only. γ-camera imaging. Trapped but not organified. Low radiation dose.
- Both concentrated by the iodide trap.

### Q: What are the four risks of thyroidectomy?

- **Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury** → voice change (career-ending for singers/teachers)
- **Parathyroid gland loss** → hypocalcaemia, lifelong Ca/vit D replacement
- Scar (cosmetic)
- Anaesthetic risk (heightened if gland still toxic — use KI pre-op)

### Q: How do you choose between radioiodine and surgery for definitive treatment?

- **Radioiodine** preferred: most Graves'/toxic nodular; convenient oral dose.
- **Surgery** preferred: large compressive goitre, severe eye disease (RAI can worsen), drug intolerance, pregnancy (RAI contraindicated), patient preference (e.g. no radiation exposure).
- **Voice-reliant occupation** (singer, teacher) → favour RAI over surgery.

🟦 **Reusable pattern — definitive-treatment choice weighs anatomy (compression, eye disease) vs occupation-specific risk (RLN) vs physiology (pregnancy).** Not a one-size answer.

## 9 — Interleaved SBAs (all 4 causes + treatment)

### Q: SBA 1 — 58-year-old man, 6 months palpitations, weight loss, 3 cm firm left-lobe nodule, no eye signs, TSH suppressed, T4 raised. Best next investigation?

- A. TRAb
- B. Anti-TPO
- C. **Radioisotope scintigram** ✅
- D. Repeat TFTs in 6 weeks
- E. FNA of nodule

**C.** Palpable nodule + older + no Graves' signs → toxic nodule. Scan shows hot nodule + cold rest. FNA is for cold (not hot) nodules. TRAb would be wrong first step with absent eye/shin signs.

### Q: SBA 2 — 34-year-old woman, 3 months heat intolerance / weight loss / anxiety, proptosis with sclera above iris, diffuse smooth goitre, non-pitting shin swelling. First-line confirmatory test?

- A. USS
- B. **TRAb** ✅
- C. Scintigram
- D. CT orbits
- E. FNA

**B.** Textbook Graves'. TRAb is first-line (slide 18 callout + transcript). Scan would also be diagnostic but bloods come first.

### Q: SBA 3 — Hyperthyroid patient with zero uptake on radioiodine scan. Which diagnosis fits?

- A. Graves' (diffuse uptake)
- B. Toxic solitary nodule (focal hot)
- C. Toxic MNG (focal hot)
- D. **de Quervain's viral thyroiditis** ✅
- E. TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma (would raise uptake, not suppress)

**D.** Zero uptake + hyperthyroid bloods = destruction + leaking warehouse = thyroiditis.

### Q: SBA 4 — 28-year-old woman, 10 weeks postpartum, palpitations + weight loss, thyroid not tender, no eye signs, CRP normal, TSH low, T4 high. Diagnosis?

- A. Graves' postpartum
- B. Toxic MNG
- C. de Quervain's (would be painful + raised CRP)
- D. **Postpartum thyroiditis** ✅
- E. Exogenous thyroxine

**D.** Recent delivery + painless + hyperthyroid bloods + no Graves' signs.

### Q: SBA 5 — 55-year-old, 3 cm right-lobe nodule, scan shows intense uptake in nodule with suppressed rest of gland. Diagnosis?

- A. Graves' (would be diffuse)
- B. **Toxic solitary adenoma** ✅
- C. de Quervain's (zero uptake)
- D. Postpartum (zero uptake)
- E. Thyroid carcinoma (almost always cold)

**B.** Focal hot + cold periphery = autonomous adenoma.

### Q: SBA 6 — Patient on carbimazole 3 weeks, severe sore throat and fever started yesterday. Most appropriate next action?

- A. Reassure — likely viral
- B. Increase propranolol
- C. **Stop carbimazole, urgent FBC** ✅
- D. Switch to PTU
- E. Urgent thyroidectomy

**C.** Classic agranulocytosis stem. Never switch to PTU (both drugs can cause it). Surgery is premature without confirming.

### Q: SBA 7 — 62-year-old with toxic MNG, thyroidectomy in 10 days, moderately hyperthyroid. Pre-op adjunct alongside propranolol?

- A. 18-month carbimazole
- B. Radioiodine
- C. **Potassium iodide for 10 days** ✅
- D. Hydrocortisone
- E. Aspirin

**C.** KI for 10 days: Wolff–Chaikoff effect reduces vascularity, pre-empts escape, safer surgery.

### Q: SBA 8 — 35-year-old pregnant woman with Graves'. Which treatment is absolutely contraindicated?

- A. Propranolol
- B. PTU (preferred in first trimester)
- C. Carbimazole (used cautiously)
- D. **Radioiodine** ✅
- E. Thyroidectomy (safe in 2nd trimester)

**D.** I-131 crosses placenta, ablates fetal thyroid.

### Q: SBA 9 — 48-year-old in A&E, fever 41.8°C, HR 165, jaundice, agitated, known Graves' off treatment. Correct drug order?

- A. KI then PTU
- B. **PTU then KI** ✅
- C. RAI then propranolol
- D. CBZ + KI simultaneously
- E. β-blocker alone

**B.** Thyroid storm. Block synthesis first (thionamide), THEN block release (KI). Giving iodide to an unblocked gland makes it worse.

### Q: SBA 10 — Opera singer with relapsing Graves' choosing definitive treatment. Strongest factor favouring radioiodine?

- A. Faster onset (no — surgery is immediate)
- B. **Avoids recurrent laryngeal nerve injury** ✅
- C. Avoids lifelong thyroxine (both cause hypothyroidism)
- D. No TFT follow-up (both need it)
- E. Safer in pregnancy (RAI is contraindicated)

**B.** Protecting the voice is the decisive factor for a professional singer.

### Q: SBA 11 — Which sign, if present, nails Graves' diagnosis without further investigation?

- A. Diffuse smooth goitre (characteristic, not specific)
- B. Lid lag (any hyperthyroidism)
- C. Palpitations + tremor (any hyperthyroidism)
- D. **Exophthalmos** ✅ (pathognomonic)
- E. AF (any hyperthyroidism)

**D.** Exophthalmos and pretibial myxoedema are the Graves'-only signs.

### Q: SBA 12 — Patient with Graves' on carbimazole + propranolol feels no better after 3 days. Best explanation?

- A. Carbimazole not working (actually biochem effect is in hours)
- B. **Clinical effect of carbimazole takes weeks — stored T4 still releasing** ✅
- C. Need higher propranolol dose
- D. Needs radioiodine urgently
- E. Likely wrong diagnosis

**B.** The ~1 month colloid warehouse must drain before blood T4 falls clinically. Propranolol covers symptoms in the meantime.

## 10 — Reusable reasoning pattern

### Q: 🟦 What is the universal workflow for endocrine hyperfunction?

```
1. Biochemistry proves the STATE
   (low TSH + high T4 → hyperthyroid — tells you WHAT)
2. History + examination direct the MECHANISM search
   (pain? pregnancy? eye/shin signs? nodule?)
3. Mechanism-specific test picks the winner
   (TRAb for autoimmunity; scan for structural vs destructive)
4. Treatment matches the MECHANISM, not the state
   (synthesis block / supportive / ablation)
```

🟦 Transferable to: hypothyroidism causes, Cushing's subtypes, hyperprolactinaemia, any hormone-excess case. Same four steps.

### Q: 🟦 What single principle explains both timing mismatches in this lecture?

- The thyroid has a **~1 month colloid warehouse** of stored hormone.
- Explains why thionamides take weeks clinically despite hours biochemically.
- Explains why viral thyroiditis gives 1 month hyper → 1 month hypo.
- Explains why Graves' patient needs propranolol bridge.
