## Section 1 — Definition, drivers, cardinal features

> 🧠 **The one idea:** COPD = lungs too big. Mucus + inflammation narrow the airway lumen, and elastin destruction by neutrophil elastase robs the bronchioles of their tethers, so they collapse on expiration → air trapped behind. Everything else (hyperinflation, scooped flow loop, low DLCO, cor pulmonale) flows from this.

### Q: What is the umbrella COPD definition and its three pathological components?
- COPD = group of conditions causing **poorly reversible airflow obstruction + air trapping**.
- Three components: **chronic bronchitis** (mucus + airway inflammation), **chronic bronchiolitis** (small-airway inflammation, plugs), **emphysema** (alveolar destruction).
- Acute exacerbations usually triggered by infection.

### Q: GETomics — three causation buckets for COPD?
- **G**enes — rare risk factors e.g. **SERPINA1** (alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency).
- **E**nvironment — tobacco smoke (UK #1), indoor/outdoor pollution, biomass fuels.
- Life**t**ime — abnormal lung development, accelerated lung ageing.
- All three interact across the life course.

### Q: Why does alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency cause early emphysema?
- AAT is the main antiprotease in the lung.
- Without it, normal levels of neutrophil elastase destroy elastin unchecked.
- Result: panlobular emphysema, often basal, presenting young (<45) and in non-smokers.

### Q: Four pathological drivers in COPD?
- **Amplified inflammation** (neutrophil-dominated).
- **Oxidative stress** (ROS from neutrophils + smoke).
- **Protease–antiprotease imbalance** (elastase vs AAT).
- **Endothelial dysfunction** (senescence, hypoxia-driven adhesion).

### Q: Walk through the neutrophil cycle that drives COPD.
- Inhaled toxin → epithelial injury → neutrophils recruited.
- Neutrophils become **trapped** in COPD lung (impaired chemotaxis — they move fast but in wrong direction).
- Degranulate → **neutrophil elastase + ROS + proteases**.
- Elastase chews elastin → loss of recoil + alveolar attachments.
- Endothelial damage → senescence → systemic inflammation.

### Q: What does loss of elastin physically do to expiration?
- Healthy: elastin holds bronchioles open against external pressure during forced expiration.
- COPD: elastase-destroyed elastin → bronchiole collapses on expiration → **gas trapping in distal alveoli** → hyperinflation.
- Inspiration is preserved; expiration is the problem.

> ⚠️ **Trap:** ICS *increases* neutrophil survival. So in pure neutrophil-driven COPD (low eos, no asthma overlap, no exacerbations), ICS does not help and increases pneumonia risk.

## Section 2 — Diagnosis and lung physiology

> 🧠 **The one idea:** spirometry tells you the **camp** (obstructive vs restrictive); lung volumes confirm hyperinflation; DLCO indexes alveolar destruction; HRCT shows the emphysema. The diagnostic question is binary first, then severity.

### Q: Diagnostic spirometry threshold for COPD (GOLD 2025)?
- Post-bronchodilator **FEV1/FVC ≤ 0.7** is mandatory.
- Persistence after bronchodilator distinguishes from asthma (which reverses).
- Symptoms + risk factors must also fit clinically.

### Q: What does the COPD flow-volume loop look like?
- **Scooped/concave** expiratory limb after the peak.
- Sometimes called a "horseshoe" shape.
- Inspiratory limb usually preserved.

### Q: How do lung volumes change in COPD vs ILD?

| Volume | COPD | ILD | Why COPD ↑ |
|---|---|---|---|
| TLC | ↑ | ↓ | Loss of recoil, gas trapping |
| FRC | ↑ | ↓ | Air trapped at end-expiration |
| RV | ↑↑ | ↓ | Marker of gas trapping |

### Q: What is GOLD 1–4 grading and what is it for?
- Severity grading by post-bronchodilator FEV1 % predicted (when ratio ≤0.7).
- GOLD 1 ≥80% (mild); GOLD 2 50–79% (moderate); GOLD 3 30–49% (severe); GOLD 4 <30% (very severe).
- Used for **prognosis and transplant timing**, NOT for choosing inhaler.

### Q: GOLD ABE assessment — three groups and their criteria?
- **Group A:** mMRC 0–1 / CAT <10 + 0–1 moderate exacerbations (no hospital).
- **Group B:** mMRC ≥2 / CAT ≥10 + 0–1 moderate exacerbations.
- **Group E:** ≥2 moderate exacerbations OR ≥1 hospitalisation in last year.
- This grid drives inhaler choice (next section).

### Q: What CXR findings suggest COPD?
- **Hyperinflation** (>6 anterior ribs visible above diaphragm).
- **Flat hemidiaphragms.**
- Hyperlucent lung fields, bullae sometimes visible.
- Narrow vertical heart shadow.

### Q: What is "supra-normal" lung function and why does it matter?
- Some people start with above-predicted lung function for their demographic.
- They can lose substantial function but still test "normal" for the population.
- They may be missed if you rely only on % predicted; trajectory over time matters.

### Q: What is PRISm and why does it matter?
- **P**reserved **R**atio **I**mpaired **S**pirometry.
- Both FEV1 and FVC reduced, but ratio still ≥0.7 — so doesn't meet COPD criteria.
- Increased risk of progressing to airflow obstruction; window for prevention.

> ⭐ **High-yield:** A patient with classic symptoms but no obstruction is "Pre-COPD" or PRISm — treat the smoking, vaccinate, follow up. They are not yet diagnosable as COPD.

## Section 3 — Atlas: pharmacological treatment ladder

> 🧠 **The one idea:** match the inhaler to the **treatable trait**. How symptomatic? How often exacerbating? Eosinophil count? Triple therapy is reserved for frequent exacerbators with eosinophil signal — it improves mortality but raises pneumonia risk.

### Q: LAMA mechanism and example?
- Long-acting **m**uscarinic **a**ntagonist.
- Blocks acetylcholine binding to **M3 receptor** on bronchial smooth muscle.
- Blocks parasympathetic-driven contraction → bronchodilation.
- Examples: tiotropium, glycopyrronium, umeclidinium.

### Q: LABA mechanism and example?
- Long-acting **β2 agonist**.
- Binds β2-adrenergic receptor → Gs → ↑cAMP → smooth muscle relaxation.
- Synergistic with LAMA (different pathway).
- Examples: salmeterol, formoterol, indacaterol, vilanterol.

### Q: ICS mechanism in two lines?
- Binds glucocorticoid receptor → translocates to nucleus → binds GREs.
- **Switches on** anti-inflammatory genes; **suppresses** NF-κB-driven inflammatory genes (via HDAC2, CREB-binding protein).
- Net: ↓ eosinophils, ↓ cytokines, restores epithelial integrity, reduces airway hyperresponsiveness.

### Q: GOLD ABE inhaled-therapy algorithm — what do you start each group on?
- **Group A** → A bronchodilator (LABA or LAMA, single).
- **Group B** → LABA + LAMA.
- **Group E** → LABA + LAMA; **add ICS** (triple) if blood eosinophils ≥300/µL.
- Single combined inhalers improve adherence over multiple devices.

### Q: When do you ADD inhaled corticosteroid in COPD?
- Frequent / severe exacerbations.
- Blood eosinophils ≥**300**/µL.
- Asthma–COPD overlap (atopic, partly reversible).
- Triple therapy reduces symptoms, lung function decline, exacerbations AND mortality in this group.

### Q: When do you AVOID ICS in COPD?
- Repeated pneumonia (ICS raises pneumonia risk).
- Blood eosinophils <0.1 ×10⁹/L (<100/µL) — unlikely to benefit.
- Pure neutrophilic phenotype with no exacerbations.

> ⚠️ **Trap:** AECOPD is **not** pneumonia. AECOPD = symptom worsening ± infection without consolidation. Pneumonia has consolidation on CXR. Both can coexist; ICS decision pivots on which dominates.

### Q: What are the specialist anti-inflammatories for COPD?
- **Azithromycin** (macrolide) — anti-inflammatory + antimicrobial; chronic frequent exacerbators despite triple.
- **Roflumilast** (PDE4 inhibitor) — chronic bronchitis phenotype, frequent exacerbations.
- Specialist-only — minor on most exam stems but recognise.

### Q: New COPD drugs and the principle behind them?
- **Dupilumab** (anti-IL-4Rα) — type-2 inflammation phenotype; eosinophil-guided.
- **Ensifentrine** (nebulised PDE3+PDE4 inhibitor) — broader licence.
- **Mepolizumab/benralizumab** (anti-IL-5) — eosinophilic COPD.
- Principle: precision medicine — match drug to dominant inflammatory cell, with **eosinophil count** as the main biomarker.

## Section 4 — Atlas: ventilation, oxygen, lung volume reduction

> 🧠 **The one idea:** oxygen is a *drug* with a survival indication; NIV treats the CO2 not the O2; lung volume reduction is for hyperinflation only. Each tool has a specific physiological niche — match the tool to the gas problem.

### Q: When does long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT) extend survival in COPD?
- Stable resting **PaO2 <7.3 kPa** (≈55 mmHg) on ABG.
- OR **PaO2 <8 kPa** plus right heart failure / cor pulmonale / polycythaemia.
- Patient must be non-smoker (combustion + O2 = fire risk).
- Used **≥15–16 hours/day**.

### Q: What does SBOT/AmBOT do (vs LTOT)?
- Short-burst / ambulatory oxygen — symptomatic relief for exertional desaturation.
- **No survival benefit.**
- Useful when LTOT criteria not met but desaturation limits activity.

### Q: How does BiPAP relieve hypercapnia?
- IPAP (inspiratory pressure) drives a deeper breath in.
- EPAP (expiratory pressure) splints airways open, recruits alveoli.
- IPAP − EPAP = **pressure support** → ↑ tidal volume → CO2 washout.
- O2 fraction handles hypoxia separately.

### Q: Indications for NIV in COPD — acute and chronic?
- **Acute:** AECOPD with persistent hypercapnic respiratory failure (pH <7.35 + PaCO2 >6.5) despite controlled O2.
- **Chronic (home BiPAP):** severe chronic hypercapnia AFTER hospitalisation with acute respiratory failure.
- Stable hypercapnia without prior admission = no clear benefit.

### Q: Why do COPD patients become hypercapnic?
- **Will not breathe** — reduced central drive, respiratory muscle weakness, obesity, hyperinflation hampers diaphragm.
- **Cannot breathe effectively** — V/Q mismatch from heterogeneous capillary destruction, bronchospasm, mucus, dynamic hyperinflation.
- AECOPD adds increased dead space + rapid shallow breathing → acute decompensation.

### Q: Lung volume reduction — when and how?
- **Heterogeneous emphysema with hyperinflation** (selected patients).
- Options: surgical lung volume reduction, **bullectomy**, **bronchoscopic endobronchial valves** (one-way valve → distal lobe collapses → other lobes expand).
- Lung transplant for end-stage in suitable candidates.

## Section 5 — Acute exacerbation management

> 🧠 **The one idea:** acute treatment maps directly to what's gone wrong — bronchodilators for obstruction, steroids for inflammation, antibiotics for infection, oxygen for hypoxia, NIV for hypercapnia. And don't miss the heart.

### Q: AECOPD definition and common triggers?
- Acute worsening of respiratory symptoms beyond normal day-to-day variation.
- Trigger most often **infective** — viral (rhinovirus, influenza) or bacterial (H. influenzae, Strep pneumoniae, Moraxella).
- Non-infective: pollution, missed inhalers, pulmonary embolism.

### Q: Five pillars of AECOPD management?
- **Bronchodilators** — nebulised salbutamol + ipratropium.
- **Corticosteroids** — oral prednisolone 30–40 mg, 5 days.
- **Antibiotics** — if increased sputum purulence/volume or suggestive CRP/imaging.
- **Controlled oxygen** — target SpO2 88–92% in those at risk of CO2 retention.
- **NIV (BiPAP)** — if persistent hypercapnic acidaemia despite above.

### Q: What's the cardiovascular pitfall around AECOPD?
- MI risk is sharply elevated in the **30 days following AECOPD**.
- A patient admitted with "AECOPD" can die of MI.
- Always check ECG, troponin, BNP — exacerbation symptoms (dyspnoea, chest tightness) overlap with cardiac.

### Q: Difference between Type 1 and Type 2 respiratory failure?
- Type 1 RF: low PaO2, normal/low PaCO2 (V/Q mismatch dominant).
- Type 2 RF: low PaO2 + high PaCO2 (hypoventilation dominant).
- T2RF with respiratory acidosis (pH <7.35) is the BiPAP indication.

> ⭐ **High-yield:** controlled O2 in CO2 retainers. Use venturi 24–28% to target SpO2 88–92%. Driving SpO2 to 100% can worsen V/Q matching, abolish hypoxic drive, and cause hyperoxic hypercapnia.

## Section 6 — Extra-pulmonary effects and cardiovascular comorbidity

> 🧠 **The one idea:** COPD is a systemic disease. The same triggers (smoking, hypoxia, systemic inflammation) damage muscle, bone, vessel, and heart. The lung is the loudest organ but rarely the killer in isolation.

### Q: List the extra-pulmonary effects of COPD.
- Anxiety, depression, social isolation.
- Muscle wasting + adipose wasting + musculoskeletal dysfunction.
- Respiratory muscle dysfunction.
- Osteoporosis (compounded by recurrent steroid courses).
- Altered gut integrity, metabolic syndrome.

### Q: Why is cardiovascular risk so high in COPD?
- **Shared risk factors** — smoking, age.
- **Shared pathways** — accelerated cellular ageing.
- **Shared pathology** — systemic inflammation, hypoxia, endothelial dysfunction.
- Net: ↑ RR of AAA, angina, AF, HF, MI, PAD, PH, stroke, TIA, sudden cardiac arrest.

### Q: How does pulmonary hypertension develop in COPD?
- Chronic alveolar hypoxia → hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction.
- Capillary destruction (emphysema) → loss of vascular bed.
- Vascular remodelling: intimal thickening → smooth muscle hypertrophy → obliteration.
- "Out of proportion" PH (severe PH with mild lung disease) carries especially poor prognosis.

### Q: Clinical clue that PH is contributing to a COPD patient's dyspnoea?
- Breathlessness disproportionate to FEV1 or HRCT findings.
- Loud P2, raised JVP, peripheral oedema.
- Echocardiogram (TR jet → estimated PASP) is the screen; right heart catheter is gold standard.

## Cross-references

> 🧠 **Q: A 70-year-old with classic ILD on HRCT. How does the spirometry differ from a COPD patient?**
>
> ILD shows **FEV1/FVC ≥0.7** with both volumes reduced; COPD shows **FEV1/FVC ≤0.7** with hyperinflation (TLC↑, RV↑↑). DLCO drops in both but is often the **first** abnormality in ILD vs late in emphysema. *See also: L2 — "Lung physiology in ILD".*

> 🧠 **Q: ICS is helpful in eosinophilic COPD but harmful in IPF — why?**
>
> COPD with eosinophilia behaves partly like asthma: steroid-responsive cells dominate. IPF is driven by aberrant repair of injured alveolar epithelium, not active inflammation — immunosuppression worsens outcomes (PANTHER-IPF). Match drug to dominant cell. *See also: L2 — "PANTHER-IPF — immunosuppression is harmful".*

> 🧠 **Q: A COPD patient and an IPF patient both end up on home oxygen and listed for transplant. Where do the management pathways converge?**
>
> Both: pulmonary rehabilitation, vaccinations, palliative care option, transplant assessment in suitable candidates. Diverge: LTOT survival evidence is robust in COPD (PaO2 <7.3) but less so in ILD; antifibrotics are unique to ILD. *See also: L2 — "ILD general management".*

> 🧠 **Q: AECOPD vs AE-IPF — what's the same, what's different?**
>
> Both: acute deterioration on a chronic disease, often need oxygen and supportive care. AECOPD is usually infective and responds to bronchodilators + steroids + antibiotics. AE-IPF is often *non-infective*, has ~50% in-hospital mortality, and steroids/immunosuppression have weaker evidence. *See also: L2 — "Acute exacerbations of IPF (AE-IPF)".*

## Interleaved SBA block

### Q: SBA 1 — A 67-year-old man with diagnosed COPD: FEV1 60%, CAT 18, mMRC 3. Two GP exacerbations + one hospitalisation last year. Eosinophils 0.4 ×10⁹/L. No history of pneumonia. What inhaled regimen?
- **Answer: LABA + LAMA + ICS (triple therapy).**
- GOLD Group E (≥1 hospitalisation), high symptoms, eosinophils ≥300 → triple is indicated and improves mortality.
- LABA+LAMA alone (Group E baseline) misses the eosinophilic benefit.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 70-year-old man, severe COPD, has stable ABG: PaO2 6.9 kPa, PaCO2 5.8, pH 7.40. No right heart failure. He stopped smoking 6 months ago. Most appropriate ventilatory intervention?
- **Answer: long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT).**
- PaO2 <7.3 kPa + stable + non-smoker → LTOT (survival benefit).
- SBOT/AmBOT only relieves symptoms, no survival benefit.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 65-year-old woman with AECOPD. ABG on 28% O2: PaO2 7.5, PaCO2 9.5, pH 7.28, HCO3 28. CXR: hyperinflation, no consolidation. Next ventilatory intervention?
- **Answer: NIV (BiPAP).**
- T2RF with respiratory acidosis (pH <7.35) in AECOPD = NIV indication.
- Increasing FiO2 alone risks worsening hypercapnia.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 60-year-old man, stable COPD, FEV1 45%. Three pneumonias requiring hospitalisation in 2 years. Eosinophils 0.05 ×10⁹/L. Currently on LABA+LAMA+ICS. What inhaled change?
- **Answer: stop the ICS, continue LABA + LAMA.**
- Repeated pneumonia + low eosinophils = ICS harms outweigh benefits.
- ICS raises pneumonia risk; eosinophils <100 predict no benefit.

### Q: SBA 5 — A 55-year-old man, never smoked, with new-onset breathlessness. Spirometry: FEV1/FVC 0.55, partial bronchodilator reversibility. Lower-zone emphysema on CT. Most likely additional test?
- **Answer: serum alpha-1 antitrypsin level.**
- Non-smoker + early-onset emphysema + lower-zone predominance = AAT deficiency.
- Genetic confirmation alters monitoring and family screening.

### Q: SBA 6 — A 72-year-old man hospitalised for AECOPD 10 days ago, now presents with central chest pain. Most important diagnostic step?
- **Answer: 12-lead ECG and serial troponin.**
- MI risk is dramatically elevated in the 30 days following AECOPD.
- The exam pattern: never assume chest pain in a recovering AECOPD patient is "just COPD".
