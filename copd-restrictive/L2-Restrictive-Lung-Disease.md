## Section 1 — What is restrictive lung disease?

> 🧠 **The one idea:** restrictive disease = lungs too small. Either the parenchyma is filled with inflammation or scar (intrinsic, ILD), or something outside stops the lung expanding (extrinsic). Both shrink TLC, but only intrinsic disease drops DLCO.

### Q: Define restrictive lung disease and its two big categories.
- **Small lung volumes** (TLC, FRC, RV all reduced) limiting expansion.
- **Intrinsic** = altered parenchyma → interstitial lung disease (ILD).
- **Extrinsic** = compression / restricted expansion: pleural disease, chest wall (kyphoscoliosis), neuromuscular (motor neurone disease, GBS).

### Q: Key cellular components of the lung parenchyma.
- **Alveolar type 1 (AT1)** — gas exchange surface (~70 m² total).
- **Alveolar type 2 (AT2)** — surfactant; **stem cell** for AT1 repair.
- **Fibroblasts** — produce ECM (collagen type 1).
- **Alveolar macrophages** — phagocytose debris; mop up surfactant.

### Q: What is the interstitial space and why is its thinness essential?
- The few-micrometre space between alveolar epithelium and capillary endothelium.
- Contains lymphatics, occasional fibroblasts, ECM scaffold.
- Its thinness is what allows oxygen to diffuse across in microseconds.
- Filling it (inflammation or fibrosis) lengthens diffusion distance → ↓ DLCO.

> ⭐ **High-yield (extrinsic restrictive — minor topic):** restrictive spirometry (low FVC, low TLC) with **normal DLCO** = lung parenchyma intact; cause is mechanical (chest wall) or neuromuscular. In intrinsic ILD, DLCO is low.

## Section 2 — ILD overview, classification, presentation

### ILD territory map

```
ILD (>200 types)
├── Idiopathic ─────── IPF, NSIP, DIP, RBILD
├── Autoimmune ─────── CTD-ILD: SSc-ILD, RA-ILD, myositis-ILD
├── Exposure-related ── Hypersensitivity pneumonitis, drug-induced
├── Cysts/airspace ──── LAM, PLCH
├── Granulomatous ──── Sarcoidosis
└── Other ───────────── Eosinophilic pneumonia, others
```

> 🧠 **The one idea:** ILD is "interstitium full of stuff". The "stuff" can be inflammation (often reversible) or fibrosis (irreversible). HRCT pattern + history (exposure or autoimmune) + auto-Abs narrow >200 entities to a workable shortlist.

### Q: ILD clinical presentation — history red flags?
- Progressive **breathlessness** + **non-productive cough** + reduced exercise tolerance.
- **Exposure history** — birds, mould, hot tubs, hay, occupational dusts.
- **Drug history** — nitrofurantoin, methotrexate, amiodarone (see pneumotox.com).
- **Family history** — up to **20%** of "idiopathic" ILDs are familial.
- CTD symptoms — Raynaud's, joint pain, skin changes.

### Q: ILD examination signs?
- Low SpO2 at rest or on exertion.
- **Fine bilateral inspiratory ("Velcro") crackles**, basal predominance.
- **Digital clubbing** (especially IPF).
- ± CTD features: skin thickening, telangiectasias, Raynaud's, joint changes.

### Q: ILD investigations — blood, function, imaging, invasive?
- **Bloods:** ANA, RhF, anti-CCP, anti-Scl-70, anti-centromere, IgG precipitins (HP).
- **PFTs:** restrictive pattern + reduced DLCO; **6MWT — SpO2 ≤88% = increased mortality**.
- **HRCT** (no contrast — causes artefact) is essential.
- **Invasive (selected):** BAL (lymphocytes >30% supports HP); surgical lung biopsy (**2–4% mortality**).

### Q: How is ILD physiology different from COPD on PFTs?

| Parameter | ILD | COPD |
|---|---|---|
| FEV1/FVC ratio | ≥0.7 (normal/↑) | ≤0.7 |
| Lung volumes (TLC, FRC, RV) | All ↓ | All ↑ |
| DLCO | ↓ — often **first** abnormality | ↓ in emphysema (later) |
| Compliance | ↓ (stiff) | ↑ (floppy) |

### Q: Why does pattern of fibrosis matter for survival?
- **UIP** (IPF) — worst prognosis.
- **Fibrotic NSIP** — intermediate.
- **DIP / RBILD / cellular NSIP** — best (largely inflammatory, treatment-responsive).
- The histological/HRCT pattern drives both treatment choice and prognosis.

### Q: General principles of ILD management.
- **Early disease:** immunosuppression (if inflammatory) or antifibrotic (if fibrotic); clinical trials; vaccination; smoking cessation; treat GORD/OSA/PH; pulmonary rehab.
- **Late disease:** supplemental oxygen; lung transplantation; palliative care.
- MDT diagnosis is the standard — clinical + radiological ± pathological.

## Section 3 — Atlas card 1: Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)

> 🧠 **The one idea:** IPF is recurrent injury to alveolar epithelium that fails to heal properly — fibroblasts run amok and deposit collagen. There is no inflammation engine to suppress, so immunosuppression *harms*. Antifibrotics slow, do not cure.

### Q: IPF — what it is, who gets it, how fast?
- Progressive, scarring lung disease of **unknown cause**.
- ~6,000 new UK cases/year; 1% of UK deaths.
- Incidence rises with age — most >60; **more common in men**.
- Average **FVC decline 150–200 mL/year**.
- Median untreated survival **3–5 years**.

### Q: Proposed mechanism of IPF?
- **Predisposing factors:** genetic (MUC5B, DSP), environmental triggers (smoke, viruses, dust), cellular ageing (telomere attrition).
- Recurrent injury to alveolar epithelium, especially **AT2 cells**.
- Failed re-epithelialisation → aberrant fibroblast activation.
- Excess ECM accumulation → microscopic honeycomb cysts → confluent honeycombing.

### Q: UIP histology — three defining features?
- **Spatial AND temporal heterogeneity** (normal lung, established fibrosis, active disease in same biopsy).
- **Fibroblastic foci** — proliferating fibroblasts/myofibroblasts = active fibrogenesis.
- **Microscopic honeycomb cysts.**

### Q: Characteristic HRCT features of IPF?
- **Subpleural** distribution.
- **Basal** predominance.
- **Honeycombing** (clustered cystic airspaces in subpleural regions).
- **Traction bronchiectasis** (airways pulled open by surrounding fibrosis).
- Often diagnostic without biopsy if classic + autoimmune/exposure work-up negative.

### Q: Variable IPF trajectory — what does it look like?
- Slow decline (most common).
- Rapid decline.
- Acute exacerbation events.
- Cannot reliably predict at diagnosis.

### Q: AE-IPF (acute exacerbation of IPF) — key numbers?
- Occurs in **5–15%** of IPF patients per year.
- Often **non-infective** trigger.
- Median survival **3–4 months** post-event.
- **In-hospital mortality ~50%.**

### Q: What did PANTHER-IPF show, and what changed because of it?
- RCT: prednisone + azathioprine + N-acetylcysteine vs placebo in IPF.
- **Combination arm: increased death + hospitalisation.**
- Stopped a treatment that had been standard for years.
- Established that **immunosuppression is harmful in IPF**.

> ⚠️ **Trap:** never give a triple of prednisone + azathioprine + NAC to an IPF patient. The classic exam stem offers it as a tempting wrong answer.

### Q: Antifibrotic drugs for IPF — names, mechanisms, side effects?
- **Nintedanib** (INPULSIS) — tyrosine kinase inhibitor (PDGFR, FGFR, VEGFR). Side effect: **diarrhoea**.
- **Pirfenidone** (ASCEND) — pyridine compound, TGF-β pathway. Side effect: **photosensitive skin rash**.
- Both slow FVC decline ~50% — **slow, do not cure**.
- A third agent (PDE4B inhibitor) entering clinical use.

## Section 4 — Atlas card 2: Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (HP)

> 🧠 **The one idea:** HP is "wrong cell in wrong place" — inhaled environmental antigen triggers a T-cell + IgG response in a susceptible host, with bronchiolitis + interstitial inflammation. Find the antigen, remove it, and most of the disease can reverse.

### Q: HP definition and key epidemiological points?
- ILD from immune-mediated response to inhaled environmental antigen in **susceptible AND sensitised** individuals.
- Involves **small airways AND parenchyma**.
- Rare (UK ~1 per 100,000); mean onset 50–60; M=F.
- **Less frequent in smokers** (counter-intuitive).
- 3-fold ↑ risk of death vs general population.

### Q: Three HP forms and their courses?
- **Acute** — high-level intermittent exposure → flu-like syndrome **4–12 hours** after exposure.
- **Chronic non-fibrotic** — ongoing low-level exposure, inflammatory only, **reversible**.
- **Chronic fibrotic** — progressive scarring, behaves like IPF, higher mortality.

### Q: Common HP antigens by category?
- **Bacteria:** thermophilic actinomycetes → farmer's lung (mouldy hay), bagassosis (sugar cane).
- **Mycobacteria:** M. avium → hot-tub lung.
- **Fungi:** Penicillium roqueforti → cheese-worker's lung; Trichosporon → summer-type HP.
- **Animal proteins:** bird feathers/excrement → bird fancier's/breeder's lung.
- **Chemicals:** isocyanates → paint-sprayer's lung; metals (zinc, zirconium).

### Q: HP examination clue distinct from IPF?
- **Inspiratory squeaks** on auscultation (in addition to crackles).
- Caused by associated **bronchiolitis** (small-airway involvement) — IPF lacks this.

### Q: HP HRCT pattern?
- **Centrilobular** ground-glass nodules (NOT subpleural like IPF).
- **Mosaic attenuation** — patchwork of normal and air-trapped lobules.
- **Three-density sign** — normal + ground glass + air trapping in same scan.
- Mid-to-upper zone predominance.

### Q: HP work-up — three test pillars?
- **Detailed exposure history** — antigen identified in only ~50% of cases.
- **Specific IgG antibodies** (serum precipitins) to suspected antigens.
- **BAL lymphocyte count >30%** supports HP.

### Q: HP treatment ladder?
- **Antigen avoidance / removal — single most important intervention.**
- Corticosteroids — for inflammatory or symptomatic disease.
- Immunosuppressants (MMF, azathioprine) — used; weak evidence base.
- **Nintedanib** if progressive fibrotic phenotype.

## Section 5 — Atlas card 3: SSc-ILD

> 🧠 **The one idea:** SSc-ILD is autoimmune fibrosis. Endothelial injury + autoimmunity + fibroblast activation. Treat the immune system (MMF or cyclophosphamide), avoid high-dose steroids (renal crisis), add an antifibrotic if it progresses.

### Q: SSc-ILD key epidemiology?
- Rare (10–50/million/year SSc); affects **young/middle-aged women**.
- ILD develops in **30–40%** of SSc patients.
- ILD is the **most common cause of death** in SSc; 10-year mortality ~40%.

### Q: Two SSc subtypes by skin extent — which complication does each favour?
- **Limited cutaneous SSc** (skin distal to elbows/knees + face) → **PH more common**.
- **Diffuse cutaneous SSc** (proximal skin) → **ILD more common**.
- Memory anchor: limited → vessels; diffuse → lungs.

### Q: SSc-ILD autoantibodies and their clinical link?
- **Anti-Scl-70** (anti-topoisomerase I) → ILD risk ↑.
- **Anti-centromere** → PH risk ↑.
- ANA usually positive; anti-RNA polymerase III associated with renal crisis.

### Q: Clinical features pointing to SSc on examination?
- **Sclerodactyly** (skin thickening of fingers).
- **Raynaud's** (triphasic colour change of fingers).
- **Telangiectasias** (especially face).
- Abnormal nailfold capillaroscopy; digital ulcers.

### Q: Worse-prognosis features in SSc-ILD?
- Male sex; older age; smoker.
- **HRCT extent >20%.**
- **FVC <70%** of predicted.

### Q: Most common HRCT pattern in SSc-ILD?
- **NSIP** — non-specific interstitial pneumonia.
- Symmetric ground glass + reticulation, basal predominance.
- More uniform than UIP; less honeycomb.

### Q: SSc-ILD treatment principles?
- Monitor lung function every **3–6 months**.
- Immunosuppression first-line: **mycophenolate mofetil (MMF)** or cyclophosphamide (SLS II).
- Add **nintedanib** for progressive fibrotic phenotype.
- Lung transplant for end-stage selected.

> ⚠️ **Trap:** prednisolone **>10 mg/day** in SSc carries risk of **scleroderma renal crisis**. Steroids are used cautiously in SSc — unlike most autoimmune conditions.

## Section 6 — Cross-cutting patterns

> 🧠 **The reusable skill:** match the dominant cell type to the drug class. Neutrophils → bronchodilators (COPD). Epithelium/fibroblasts → antifibrotics (IPF). Immune cells (T, B, plasma) → immunosuppression (HP, SSc-ILD). This three-way split decides every ILD treatment question.

### Q: Match dominant cell to ILD/disease and to drug class.

| Disease | Dominant cell | Treatment class |
|---|---|---|
| IPF | Epithelial injury → fibroblast | Antifibrotic; **NO immunosuppression** |
| HP | T-helper + IgG plasma | Antigen avoidance + steroid ± immunosuppressant |
| SSc-ILD | Endothelial + B-cell + fibroblast | MMF/cyclophosphamide ± antifibrotic |

### Q: Three HRCT patterns and their disease anchors.
- **UIP** = subpleural + basal honeycombing + traction bronchiectasis → **IPF**.
- **NSIP** = uniform ground glass + reticulation, basal → **SSc-ILD** (and other CTD-ILD).
- **Centrilobular GGOs + mosaic + 3-density** = **HP**.

### Q: When are corticosteroids helpful, harmful, or risky in ILD?
- **Helpful:** HP (especially inflammatory phase), organising pneumonia.
- **Harmful:** IPF — PANTHER-IPF showed increased death.
- **Risky:** SSc — high doses (>10 mg/day) can trigger scleroderma renal crisis.

### Q: When is nintedanib used across ILD?
- IPF (first-line antifibrotic).
- Progressive fibrotic HP.
- Progressive fibrotic SSc-ILD.
- Common thread: **progressive fibrotic phenotype**, regardless of underlying ILD.

## Cross-references

> 🧠 **Q: A patient has a restrictive PFT pattern with normal DLCO. Why doesn't this fit ILD?**
>
> ILD thickens the interstitium and *always* drops DLCO. Normal DLCO with restriction implies the lung itself is intact — the cause is extrinsic (chest wall, neuromuscular, pleural). *See also: L1 — "Lung physiology in ILD" comparison row.*

> 🧠 **Q: A COPD patient's spirometry shows FEV1/FVC 0.85 with low FVC. Are they restrictive instead?**
>
> Possibly — confirm with lung volumes. In COPD you'd expect TLC ↑; in restrictive disease TLC ↓. A "pseudo-restrictive" picture can occur in obese or air-trapped COPD patients. Volumes resolve. *See also: L1 — "GOLD diagnosis spirometry".*

> 🧠 **Q: ICS for COPD when eosinophils ≥300, but immunosuppression for IPF is harmful — same drug class principle?**
>
> No — different disease engines. Eosinophil-rich COPD is partly steroid-responsive (asthma overlap). IPF is *not* inflammation-driven; it is aberrant repair. Match the cell to the disease, not the drug class to the symptom. *See also: L1 — "ICS in COPD" + this lecture's "PANTHER-IPF".*

> 🧠 **Q: AECOPD vs AE-IPF — same name, what's the same?**
>
> Both: acute deterioration on chronic disease, often need oxygen and ITU input. Different: AECOPD usually infective, treated with bronchodilators + steroids + antibiotics + NIV; AE-IPF often non-infective, ~50% in-hospital mortality, no bronchodilator role. *See also: L1 — "Acute exacerbation management".*

> 🧠 **Q: Pulmonary rehabilitation — how universal is it?**
>
> Universal across chronic respiratory disease — COPD, IPF, HP, SSc-ILD. Six-week supervised exercise + education programme. Improves dyspnoea, exercise tolerance, quality of life regardless of underlying diagnosis. *See also: L1 — "Non-pharmacological management".*

## Interleaved SBA block (mixed across the three ILDs + cross-cutting)

### Q: SBA 1 — A 56-year-old non-smoking woman, 18 months progressive cough/dyspnoea. Owns four parrots. HRCT: centrilobular ground-glass nodules upper zones, mosaic attenuation. BAL lymphocytes 38%. **First-line** management?
- **Answer: remove the birds (antigen avoidance).**
- HP confirmed by exposure + classic HRCT + BAL lymphocytosis.
- Antigen removal is the single most important intervention; without it, no drug works lasting.
- Steroids may follow, but A precedes B.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 68-year-old man with confirmed IPF (UIP HRCT, autoimmune negative, no exposures). FVC declined 200 mL over 6 months. Most appropriate next step?
- **Answer: nintedanib.**
- Antifibrotic is the standard.
- Prednisone + azathioprine + NAC is the PANTHER-IPF combination — increases mortality.
- MMF, rituximab, high-dose steroids — all immunosuppressive, harmful in IPF.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 42-year-old woman with diffuse cutaneous SSc, anti-Scl-70 positive, progressive dyspnoea. HRCT: bibasal symmetric ground glass + reticulation. FVC 65%. Rheumatologist proposes prednisolone 30 mg/day. Most important concern?
- **Answer: scleroderma renal crisis.**
- Prednisolone >10 mg/day in SSc raises renal crisis risk.
- This is the SSc-specific steroid trap.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 65-year-old non-smoking man, 6 months dyspnoea. Bibasal Velcro crackles + clubbing. FEV1 65%, FVC 60%, FEV1/FVC 0.86, TLC 65%, DLCO 40%. HRCT: peripheral basal honeycombing + traction bronchiectasis. Most likely diagnosis?
- **Answer: idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis.**
- Restrictive PFT (ratio ≥0.7) + low DLCO + classic UIP HRCT.
- COPD wrong (ratio is preserved). Asthma wrong (no obstruction, wrong age). Bronchiectasis wrong (no chronic sputum, HRCT pattern wrong).

### Q: SBA 5 — A 30-year-old man with motor neurone disease, declining FVC. PFTs: FEV1 50%, FVC 50%, FEV1/FVC 0.85, TLC 55%, DLCO **90%**. Cause of restrictive pattern?
- **Answer: respiratory muscle weakness.**
- Restriction with **normal DLCO** = lung parenchyma intact.
- Cause is mechanical/neuromuscular — not ILD.
- Watch for hypercapnia developing.

### Q: SBA 6 — A 60-year-old woman with longstanding RA on methotrexate. New 6-month progressive dyspnoea. HRCT: peripheral basal reticulation + minor honeycombing. FEV1/FVC 0.82, FVC 60%, DLCO 45%. **Least appropriate** management?
- **Answer: LABA + LAMA inhaler.**
- This is RA-ILD (CTD-ILD) — restrictive, not obstructive.
- Bronchodilators don't help restrictive disease; no airflow obstruction to relieve.
- Other options (vaccinations, pulmonary rehab, GORD treatment, rheumatology liaison, considering methotrexate as drug-induced ILD) are all reasonable.

### Q: SBA 7 — A 70-year-old woman with limited cutaneous SSc, anti-centromere positive, stable for 4 years. Now 3 months progressive exertional dyspnoea **out of proportion to HRCT** (minimal NSIP, FVC 85%). Most likely cause?
- **Answer: pulmonary arterial hypertension.**
- Limited SSc + anti-centromere = PH-prone phenotype.
- Dyspnoea out of proportion to lung findings → pulmonary vascular disease.
- Echocardiogram is the screen.

### Q: SBA 8 — Which ILD pattern is **reversible** with steroids?
- **Answer: organising pneumonia (OP).**
- Patchy peripheral consolidation; predominantly inflammatory.
- Responds to oral corticosteroids.
- Contrast: established UIP fibrosis is irreversible — antifibrotics slow but do not reverse.
