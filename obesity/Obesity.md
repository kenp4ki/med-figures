## Section 1 — Definition and BMI

### Q: What is the formal definition of obesity?

A condition of **abnormal or excessive fat accumulation in adipose tissue, to the extent that health is impaired**.

- **Key:** "to the extent that health is impaired" is the pivotal clause — obesity is a *health* condition, not aesthetic.
- Adipose tissue per se is essential; congenital lipodystrophy (no fat) → severe insulin resistance.
- BMI is a **proxy** for this definition, not the definition itself.

### Q: How is BMI calculated and classified?

BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)²

- Healthy weight: 18.5–24.9
- Overweight: 25–29.9
- Obesity I: 30–34.9
- Obesity II: 35–39.9
- Obesity III: ≥40

### Q: Why was BMI created and where does it fail at the individual level?

- Designed by **insurance companies** for population-level mortality risk estimation.
- Fails for muscular individuals — Vin Diesel BMI 29.9 ≈ Jonah Hill 29.8 with very different body composition.
- Cut-offs derived in **Western European white males** — needs ethnicity adjustment.

### Q: How do BMI thresholds differ by ethnicity?

- **South Asian:** ↑ diabetes risk at lower BMI.
- **Black populations:** worse lipid profile at lower BMI.
- UK GP risk calculators (e.g. QRISK) bake in ethnicity adjustments alongside BMI.

### Q: Why does waist circumference add information BMI cannot?

- Distinguishes **visceral/abdominal fat** (high CV risk) from **subcutaneous fat** (lower risk).
- Men → middle-pattern fat → ↑ MI/stroke earlier in life.
- Women → subcutaneous (oestrogen-driven) → CV risk catches up post-menopause.

⭐ **Exam framing:** obesity is defined by *health impairment*; BMI is the population-level estimate; waist circumference + ethnicity refine the individual risk.

## Section 2 — What drives obesity

### Q: What proportion of BMI variance is genetic vs environmental?

- Twin studies: BMI heritability **~60–80%**.
- Lecturer reconciliation: "genes ~60–80%, environment ~95%" — these aren't additive.
- **Pattern:** genes set susceptibility; environment determines whether susceptibility expresses.

### Q: How is obesity inherited genetically?

- **Polygenic** — hundreds of loci of small effect (GWAS).
- Different gene sets control: total body fat, visceral vs subcutaneous distribution, birthweight, extreme obesity.
- **Key:** "where you put fat" genes are different from "how much fat" genes.
- Monogenic forms (leptin, MC4R) are rare but pedagogically important.

📎 Figure L1.1 — GWAS loci by obesity-related trait (Goodarzi 2018)

### Q: Why must obesity be largely environmental despite high heritability?

- US obesity prevalence doubled in <20 years.
- Human generation time is too long for genetic shifts of that magnitude.
- Conclusion: gene pool didn't change → environment must explain the rise.

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern.** When prevalence shifts within one generation, the cause is environmental, not genetic. Apply to T2DM, allergic disease, myopia, mental health trends — the same logic rules out genetic explanations.

### Q: What environmental factors correlate with obesity?

- **Food environment:** cheaper, denser, tastier (sugar/salt/fat) since the 1970s.
- **Physical environment:** car-dependent neighbourhoods, less walking.
- **Screen time:** linear positive relationship with overweight prevalence.
- **Socioeconomic gradient:** lower education and income → higher obesity.
- UK childhood deprivation gap: 8.5% (2006) → **15.0%** (2016/17).

⚠️ **Trap.** "Lack of exercise" alone is a poor explanation. Most daily energy expenditure is resting metabolic rate, and exercise calories are easily replaced by small dietary additions. The dominant environmental driver is **calorie consumption**, not exercise.

### Q: What does the gene × environment interaction graph show?

```
BMI ↑
     │              ●  Genetically prone
     │            ╱
     │           ╱
     │          ╱
     │   ●─────────────●  Genetically resistant
     └────────────────────→
       Healthy env   Toxic env
```

- Genetically prone: small BMI rise in healthy environment, large BMI rise in toxic environment.
- Genetically resistant: flat across environments.
- **Key:** genetic susceptibility *amplifies* environmental exposure — it does not act independently.

## Section 3 — Appetite regulation (mechanism bridge)

📎 Figure L1.2 — Hypothalamic appetite regulation (arcuate nucleus circuits)

### Q: Which hypothalamic circuits regulate appetite?

- **NPY/AgRP neurons** — orexigenic (drive eating); AgRP blocks MC4R.
- **POMC/CART neurons** — anorexigenic (suppress eating); activate MC4R via melanocortin.
- Net effect = balance between these two arcuate nucleus populations.

### Q: What peripheral signals reach these circuits and what do they signal?

- **Leptin** (adipose) — long-term: "fat reserves are sufficient".
- **Insulin** (pancreas) — long-term adiposity signal.
- **Ghrelin** (stomach) — short-term: "I'm empty, eat now" (only orexigenic gut hormone).
- **GLP-1, PYY3-36, CCK** (gut) — short-term satiety signals.
- **Vagal afferents** — gut distension and nutrient sensing.

### Q: Why does conscious dieting usually fail long-term?

- Hypothalamus interprets weight loss as **starvation** (fat reserves dropped).
- Defends prior weight by ↑ hunger and ↓ energy expenditure → rebound weight gain (often overshooting baseline).
- **Pattern:** body weight is mostly defended subconsciously by a hypothalamic set-point. Diets that work require permanent restructuring, not temporary restriction.

## Section 4 — Comorbidities and mortality

### Q: List the major comorbidities of obesity.

- **Cardiovascular:** hypertension, MI, stroke, peripheral vascular disease.
- **Metabolic:** type 2 diabetes.
- **Mechanical:** osteoarthritis, obstructive sleep apnoea.
- **Cancers:** bowel cancer; obesity is the **biggest preventable cancer risk factor after smoking**.
- **Other:** gallbladder disease, gout, infertility, depression.

### Q: What is the shape of the BMI–mortality relationship?

- **U-shaped.**
- Nadir at BMI 20–25.
- At BMI ~35–40, mortality ratio ≈ **2× healthy-weight reference**.
- Excess mortality at very low BMI reflects undernutrition, eating disorders, undiagnosed disease — not a healthy zone.

📎 Figure L1.3 — BMI vs all-cause mortality (U-shaped curve)

### Q: What does the Cuba natural experiment show?

- Soviet collapse 1989 → Cuban economic crisis → forced calorie reduction, more walking, less smoking.
- Population BMI shifted left; **diabetes incidence and CV mortality fell** within a decade.
- As economy recovered → all metrics rose again.
- **Key:** obesity-related morbidity is reversible at population level — public-health interventions work fast.

📎 Figure L1.4 — Cuba 1980–2010 (Franco BMJ 2013)

## Section 5 — Treatment pathway

### Q: What is the assessment pathway in primary care?

1. Determine degree of overweight/obesity (BMI + waist circumference).
2. Assess lifestyle, comorbidities, willingness to change.
3. Initiate management: lifestyle ± drug treatment.
4. Refer to specialist if higher-tier intervention indicated.

### Q: How is initial intervention level chosen?

Decision matrix: BMI class × waist circumference (low/high/very high) × comorbidities present.

- **Overweight + low waist:** general lifestyle advice.
- **Overweight + high waist OR Obesity I:** diet + physical activity.
- **Obesity I + comorbidities OR Obesity II:** add drug treatment.
- **Obesity III OR Obesity II + comorbidities:** consider surgery.

⭐ Drugs enter consideration from **Obesity I + comorbidity or high waist**; surgery from **Obesity II/III** or **Obesity I + new T2DM**.

## Section 6 — Drug atlas

### Q: Orlistat — what is it and how does it work?

- **Mechanism:** gastric and pancreatic **lipase inhibitor**.
- Derivative of bacterial lipstatin (*Streptomyces toxytricini*).
- Reduces dietary fat absorption by **~30%**.
- Old generation; first oral weight-loss drug widely used.

### Q: Orlistat — efficacy and side effects?

- **Weight loss:** ~2.9% (1-year meta-analysis, n=6021).
- **Attrition ~33%.**
- Side effects: fatty/oily stool, faecal urgency, oily spotting, **faecal incontinence in ~7%**.
- Risk of **fat-soluble vitamin (A, D, E, K) deficiency**.
- No long-term morbidity/mortality data.

⚠️ **Trap.** Patients commonly self-stop orlistat before high-fat meals to avoid steatorrhoea — this defeats adherence. The side-effect profile, not efficacy, limits real-world use.

### Q: GLP-1 — what does it do physiologically?

- Released from gut L-cells after meals.
- **Pancreas:** ↑ insulin, ↓ glucagon (incretin effect).
- **Brain:** ↓ appetite, ↑ satiety.
- **CV/renal:** ↑ endothelial function, ↓ BP, ↑ natriuresis.
- Net: ↓ body weight, ↓ glucose, ↓ lipids.

📎 Figure L1.10 — GLP-1 multi-organ effects

### Q: Liraglutide (Saxenda) — what is it?

- **Long-acting GLP-1 receptor agonist** (Novo Nordisk).
- **Daily subcutaneous injection.**
- Dose: **double the T2DM dose**.
- Weight loss: **4–5%**.
- First-generation effective GLP-1 RA for obesity.

### Q: Semaglutide (Wegovy) — what is it?

- **Long-acting GLP-1 RA**, **once-weekly injection**.
- **Weight loss ~15%** at 68 weeks (STEP 1, NEJM 2021).
- Major side effect: **nausea**.
- **~50% discontinue within 1 year.**

### Q: Tirzepatide (Mounjaro) — what is it?

- **Dual GLP-1 + GIP receptor co-agonist** (once-weekly).
- **Weight loss up to ~21%** at 15 mg (SURMOUNT-1, NEJM 2022).
- GIP = glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (the second incretin).
- Side-effect profile similar to semaglutide (nausea-dominant).

### Q: Cagrilintide and retatrutide — what are the newest agents?

- **Cagrilintide:** long-acting **amylin analogue**; combined with semaglutide (CagriSema) → additive weight loss.
- **Retatrutide:** **triple agonist** of GIP + GLP-1 + **glucagon** receptors (Eli Lilly); phase 2 weight loss up to **~24%**.
- Glucagon receptor activation drives **energy expenditure** and chronically improves glycaemia.
- **Concern:** retatrutide may cause lean muscle loss.

🧠 **Drug-class evolution pattern.** Orlistat (gut lipase) → liraglutide (single GLP-1) → semaglutide (long-acting GLP-1) → tirzepatide (GLP-1 + GIP) → retatrutide (GLP-1 + GIP + glucagon). Each step adds receptors → more weight loss. Trade-offs: nausea, muscle loss, cost, limited supply.

## Section 7 — Leptin (mechanism deep-dive)

### Q: What does leptin do, and where is it secreted from?

- Produced by **adipose tissue** (Ob gene product).
- Acts on **hypothalamic leptin receptors** — activates POMC/CART, suppresses NPY/AgRP.
- Reports long-term fat reserves to the brain.

📎 Figure L1.5 — Leptin systemic actions

### Q: What is the phenotype of congenital leptin deficiency?

- Massive **hyperphagia** (overrides cognition — children eat anything calorific).
- **Infertility** (no pubertal onset, no LH pulsatility).
- **Stunted linear growth.**
- **↓ body temperature, ↓ energy expenditure, ↓ immune function.**
- Reversed by recombinant leptin therapy.

📎 Figure L1.6 — Recombinant leptin in deficient children (Farooqi 2002)

### Q: Why does the leptin-deficient phenotype mimic starvation?

- Without leptin, the brain perceives **zero fat reserves** = starvation signal.
- Adaptive response: ↑ hunger, shut down reproduction, ↓ thermogenesis, ↓ immunity (immune system is energetically expensive).
- **Key:** leptin reports the *presence* of fat. Its absence = "we are starving — switch on emergency mode".

⭐ **Lecturer's headline.** Leptin is an **anti-starvation hormone, not an anti-obesity hormone**. The body cares much more about being too thin (life-threatening) than too fat (slow-burn risk). This is the master-key for understanding leptin resistance.

### Q: How does leptin link adiposity to reproduction?

- Low fat → low leptin → loss of GnRH/LH pulsatility → amenorrhoea / hypogonadism.
- Recombinant leptin restores LH pulsatility in leptin-deficient children (induces puberty).
- Recombinant leptin restores LH pulsatility in women with hypothalamic amenorrhoea (low BMI).

📎 Figure L1.7 — Leptin restores LH/FSH in deficient child
📎 Figure L1.8 — Leptin restores LH in hypothalamic amenorrhoea

### Q: What is leptin resistance, and why does it kill leptin as a drug?

- Obese people have **high circulating leptin** but blunted brain response.
- Recombinant leptin in common obesity → no weight loss (Shetty 2011).
- Adaptive logic: evolution selected hard for starvation defence (high pressure), weakly for over-nutrition defence (low pressure).
- Leptin works as a drug **only in low-leptin states** (lean amenorrhoea, congenital deficiency).

## Section 8 — Bariatric surgery

### Q: What are the referral thresholds for bariatric surgery?

- **BMI ≥40**, OR
- **BMI 35–40 + comorbidities**, OR
- **BMI 30–34.9 + newly-diagnosed T2DM**.

PLUS: non-surgical measures failed ≥6 months, fit for anaesthesia, committed to long-term follow-up.

⭐ **First-line option:** BMI **>50 kg/m²** — surgery is offered without trying non-surgical measures first.

### Q: What are the three main bariatric procedures?

- **Gastric bypass** — small upper-stomach pouch joined to small intestine. **Restriction + malabsorption.** Most effective long-term.
- **Sleeve gastrectomy** — most of stomach removed, leaving banana-shaped sleeve. **Restriction + ↓ ghrelin.**
- **Gastric band** — adjustable band around stomach. **Restriction only.** Least effective.

📎 Figure L1.11 — Three bariatric procedure anatomies

### Q: What does the Swedish Obese Subjects (SOS) study show?

- 20-year follow-up.
- Weight loss: gastric bypass ~−25%, VBG ~−18%, banding ~−14%, control ~0%.
- Cumulative mortality: surgery vs control adjusted **HR 0.71 (95% CI 0.54–0.92, p=0.01)**.
- **Key:** bariatric surgery is the **only modality with proven long-term mortality reduction**.

📎 Figure L1.12 — SOS study weight + mortality outcomes

⚠️ **Practical limit.** Surgery cannot scale to a population where ~25% are obese — insufficient surgical capacity, patient acceptance, and cost. Pharmacotherapy (GLP-1 RAs and successors) is the population-level lever.

## Section 9 — Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: What is the efficacy ladder across weight-loss interventions?

- Lifestyle alone: 0–3% (rebounds).
- Orlistat: ~3%.
- Liraglutide (daily GLP-1): 4–5%.
- Semaglutide (weekly GLP-1): ~15%.
- Tirzepatide (GLP-1 + GIP): ~21%.
- Retatrutide (triple agonist): ~24%.
- Gastric bypass: ~25% sustained 20+ years.

### Q: How do BMI cut-offs map to exam answer choices?

- BMI ≥30 + comorbidity → **drugs** likely correct.
- BMI ≥35 + comorbidity → **surgery** in range.
- BMI ≥40 → surgery first-line consideration.
- BMI ≥50 → surgery first-line, no non-surgical trial needed.
- BMI 30–34.9 + new T2DM → **surgery exception** (metabolic indication).

🧠 **Stem-decoding pattern.** In any obesity case-stem, locate three numbers first: BMI, waist circumference, and HbA1c (or comorbidity count). Those three numbers decide whether the answer is lifestyle, drugs, or surgery — before you even look at the answer options.

## Section 10 — Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 38-year-old man, BMI 38, has T2DM (HbA1c 67), hypertension and OSA. He has tried two structured weight-loss programmes over 18 months without sustained loss. What is the most appropriate next step?

A) Continue lifestyle programme  B) Start orlistat  C) Start semaglutide  D) Refer for bariatric surgery  E) Start liraglutide

**Answer: D — Refer for bariatric surgery.**

- BMI 35–40 + comorbidities meets surgical threshold.
- Non-surgical measures already failed >6 months.
- A: lifestyle alone has already failed.
- B: orlistat ~3% loss won't shift the comorbid burden.
- C/E: GLP-1 RAs reasonable but surgery is the more definitive option given multiple comorbidities and failed non-surgical trial.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 6-year-old presents with severe early-onset obesity, hyperphagia, recurrent infections and short stature. Which single test best identifies the underlying mechanism?

A) Serum cortisol  B) Serum leptin  C) Thyroid function tests  D) Karyotype  E) Serum IGF-1

**Answer: B — Serum leptin.**

- Phenotype = leptin deficiency: hyperphagia + ↓immunity + stunted growth + likely hypogonadism.
- Recombinant leptin reverses the syndrome.
- A/C/E: would explain weight gain but not the immune/growth/reproductive picture together.
- D: Prader-Willi possible, but FISH/methylation is the right test, not karyotype.

### Q: SBA 3 — A patient on semaglutide for 8 months reports persistent nausea and is considering stopping. Roughly what proportion of patients discontinue GLP-1 RAs within a year, and why does this matter for counselling?

- **~50%** discontinue within 1 year.
- Set realistic expectations early; nausea typically improves with slower dose titration.
- Consider switching to weekly tirzepatide or trial dose reduction before abandoning the class.

### Q: SBA 4 — Why does giving recombinant leptin to an obese adult fail to reduce weight?

- **Leptin resistance:** circulating leptin is already high; brain has blunted response.
- Leptin is an **anti-starvation hormone** — strong adaptive pressure to defend against low fat, weak pressure to defend against high fat.
- Recombinant leptin works only in leptin-low states (deficiency, lean amenorrhoea).

### Q: SBA 5 — A patient on long-term orlistat develops night vision difficulty. What is the likely cause and how do you investigate?

- **Vitamin A deficiency** — orlistat blocks fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K).
- Check serum retinol; review multivitamin adherence.
- Reinforces why fat-soluble vitamin supplementation is recommended with long-term orlistat use.

### Q: SBA 6 — Which is the only weight-loss intervention with proven all-cause mortality reduction in long-term randomised follow-up?

- **Bariatric surgery** (SOS study, adjusted HR 0.71 over 20 years).
- Lifestyle, orlistat and GLP-1 RAs lack equivalent long-term mortality data.
- GLP-1 RAs do show CV outcome benefits in T2DM trials (a separate question).

### Q: SBA 7 — A 14-year-old girl, BMI 17, presents with secondary amenorrhoea after weight loss. What is the underlying endocrine mechanism?

- **Hypothalamic amenorrhoea** — low fat → low leptin → loss of GnRH/LH pulsatility.
- Treatment: weight restoration; recombinant leptin can restore LH pulsatility experimentally but is not standard care.
- Reinforces leptin's role as the link between adiposity and reproduction.

### Q: SBA 8 — A patient asks why their friend with the same diet is much heavier. Best explanation?

- **Gene × environment interaction:** genetic susceptibility *amplifies* environmental exposure.
- Same environment → variable BMI depending on inherited polygenic risk.
- Heritability of BMI is **60–80%** in twin studies.

### Q: SBA 9 — Which patient is most likely to benefit from bariatric surgery as a first-line option, without trialling drugs first?

A) BMI 32, T2DM  B) BMI 38, hypertension  C) BMI 42, no comorbidity  D) BMI 52, no comorbidity  E) BMI 36, OSA

**Answer: D — BMI ≥50 is first-line surgical territory.** All others meet *referral* criteria but normally trial non-surgical measures first.

### Q: SBA 10 — Tirzepatide acts on which receptor pair?

- **GLP-1 + GIP** (glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide).
- GIP is the second incretin alongside GLP-1.
- Adding GIP gives more weight loss than GLP-1 alone (~21% vs ~15%).

### Q: SBA 11 — A man's BMI rises from 32 to 38 over 5 years on the same job and similar diet, while his identical twin in the same household stays at BMI 31. Which best explains the divergence?

- **Stochastic environmental variation** (small differences in food choices, sleep, activity, illness) acting on shared genetic susceptibility.
- Genes set the *range* of possible BMI; environment determines *where in that range* he lands.
- Identical genes ≠ identical phenotype, especially for a polygenic, environment-sensitive trait.

### Q: SBA 12 — A patient on retatrutide loses 22% body weight over 12 months but DEXA shows disproportionate loss of lean mass. What is the most likely contributor and management?

- Triple agonism including **glucagon receptor** drives energy expenditure but may accelerate **lean muscle loss**.
- Management: ensure adequate protein intake, structured resistance exercise, monitor lean mass.
- Reinforces the trade-off pattern of newer agents — bigger weight loss, new side-effect profile.
