**Flagged as exam-critical**
- "Always check PTH first" in any hypercalcaemia workup
- PTH's indirect action on the gut (via calcitriol) — called a classic MCQ point

## Section A — The machinery

### Q: Which three hormones regulate serum calcium?

- Calcitriol (active vitamin D) — raises Ca²⁺
- PTH — raises Ca²⁺
- Calcitonin — lowers Ca²⁺ acutely; clinically trivial

Calcitriol and PTH are the main regulators of calcium and phosphate homeostasis.

### Q: Where does each Ca-regulating hormone come from?

| Hormone | Source | Cell type |
|---|---|---|
| Calcitriol | Skin (with UVB) or diet → liver → kidney | N/A — synthesised |
| PTH | 4 parathyroid glands behind thyroid | Chief cells |
| Calcitonin | Thyroid gland | Parafollicular (C) cells |

Parathyroids sit behind the thyroid but have no role in thyroid hormone.

### Q: What are the three territories where Ca-regulating hormones act?

Bone, gut, kidney. Every PTH, calcitriol and FGF23 action maps onto one of these three.

### Q: What does calcitriol do in each territory?

- **Gut** — ↑ Ca²⁺ absorption; ↑ PO₄³⁻ absorption
- **Kidney** — ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption; ↑ PO₄³⁻ reabsorption
- **Bone** — supplies Ca + PO₄ as building blocks for mineralisation

Calcitriol raises both Ca and PO₄ — bone mineral is calcium phosphate.

### Q: What does PTH do in each territory?

| Territory | Action | Direct / indirect |
|---|---|---|
| Bone | ↑ Ca²⁺ resorption via osteoclasts | Direct (via osteoblast → RANKL) |
| Kidney | ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption; ↑ PO₄³⁻ excretion; ↑ 1-α-hydroxylase | Direct |
| Gut | ↑ Ca²⁺ and PO₄³⁻ absorption | Indirect (via calcitriol) |

🟨 Gut is the only indirect PTH action — via 1-α-hydroxylase → calcitriol. Classic MCQ point.

🟥 PTH raises calcium but **lowers** phosphate. Easily confused with calcitriol, which raises both. Mechanism: PTH blocks the proximal tubule Na⁺/PO₄ cotransporter.

### Q: Why does PTH dump phosphate in urine while mobilising calcium?

If PTH raised both, serum [Ca × PO₄] would spike → calcium phosphate precipitates in soft tissue. Dumping phosphate is the escape valve.

### Q: How does PTH act on bone — which cell, what signal?

PTH binds **osteoblasts** (B = Build), which release **RANKL**. RANKL activates osteoclasts (C = Consume) → bone resorption.

Chain: PTH → osteoblast → RANKL → osteoclast → bone resorption.

📎 Figure L5.5.3 — companion PDF.

Osteoclasts do not express PTH receptors — PTH cannot act on them directly.

### Q: How does PTH lower phosphate at the kidney?

PTH inhibits the Na⁺/PO₄³⁻ cotransporter on the apical membrane of the proximal tubule. Phosphate can't be reabsorbed from filtrate → excreted in urine.

📎 Figure L5.5.1 — companion PDF.

### Q: What is FGF23 and what does it do?

FGF23 (Fibroblast Growth Factor 23) is a hormone from bone. Its job: lower serum phosphate.

### Q: By what two mechanisms does FGF23 lower phosphate?

1. Blocks the Na⁺/PO₄³⁻ cotransporter at the proximal tubule → phosphate excreted
2. Inhibits calcitriol production → less gut phosphate absorption

PTH and FGF23 both block the same cotransporter, but have opposite effects on calcitriol (PTH ↑, FGF23 ↓).

### Q: How do the parathyroid glands sense calcium?

Via calcium-sensing receptors on their surface. They monitor serum Ca²⁺ continuously.

### Q: What is the inverse feedback loop between Ca²⁺ and PTH?

- ↓ Ca²⁺ → ↑ PTH → Ca normalises → PTH falls
- ↑ Ca²⁺ → ↓ PTH → Ca normalises → PTH rises

This is the physiologically appropriate response. Deviation means the parathyroid itself is pathological.

### Q: Reusable pattern — how to interpret any Ca / PTH combination?

🟦 Ask whether PTH is moving **opposite** to Ca (appropriate) or the **same direction** (pathological).

- ↑ Ca + ↓ PTH — appropriate → parathyroid innocent → malignancy
- ↑ Ca + ↑ PTH — inappropriate → 1° or 3° HPT (split by renal function)
- ↓ Ca + ↑ PTH — appropriate → 2° HPT
- ↓ Ca + ↓ PTH — inappropriate → hypoparathyroidism

## Section A — Quick recall

### Q: Which enzyme activates vitamin D, and where?

1-α-hydroxylase, in the kidney.

### Q: What is the biochemical triad of primary hyperparathyroidism?

↑ Calcium + ↑ PTH (inappropriate) + ↓ Phosphate.

### Q: How does PTH raise calcium — name the three mechanisms.

1. ↑ Bone resorption (osteoblast → RANKL → osteoclast)
2. ↑ Renal Ca reabsorption
3. ↑ Calcitriol production → ↑ gut Ca absorption

## Section B — The vitamin D cascade

### Pathway skeleton

```
                    SKIN                        DIET
                      │                           │
             7-dehydrocholesterol          Vitamin D₂
                      │                    (Ergocalciferol)
                  [UVB light]                    │
                      ↓                          │
               Pre-vitamin D₃                    │
                      ↓                          │
                 Vitamin D₃                      │
              (Cholecalciferol)                  │
                      │                          │
                      └────────────┬─────────────┘
                                   ↓
                               LIVER
                          25-hydroxylase
                                   ↓
                      25-OH cholecalciferol (calcidiol)
                          ← INACTIVE — this is what we MEASURE →
                                   ↓
                              KIDNEY
                         1-α-hydroxylase   ← stimulated by PTH
                                   ↓        ← inhibited by calcitriol (autoregulation)
                    1,25-(OH)₂ cholecalciferol
                            = CALCITRIOL
                           ← ACTIVE form →
```

### Q: Where is calcitriol produced?

By 1-α-hydroxylase in the kidney — the final activation step (25-OH-D → calcitriol).

### Q: Why does chronic kidney disease break calcium homeostasis?

Loss of 1-α-hydroxylase → no calcitriol → low gut Ca absorption → hypocalcaemia → chronic PTH drive. This is why CKD patients progress from 2° to 3° HPT.

### Q: What's the difference between 25-OH-D and calcitriol?

| | 25-OH-D (calcidiol) | Calcitriol |
|---|---|---|
| Activity | Inactive | Active |
| Where made | Liver | Kidney |
| Half-life | Long | Very short |
| Assay | Easy, stable | Light-sensitive, foil-wrapped tube |
| Clinical use | Standard vitamin D measurement | Rarely measured |

### Q: Difference between cholecalciferol and ergocalciferol?

| | Cholecalciferol | Ergocalciferol |
|---|---|---|
| Vitamin form | D₃ | D₂ |
| Source | Skin / animal | Plants / fungi / diet |
| Clinical role | Standard D₃ supplement | Alternative D₂ supplement |

Both go through identical liver + kidney steps; functionally equivalent once activated.

### Q: What is Alfacalcidol?

1-α-hydroxy vitamin D — a synthetic preparation already hydroxylated at the 1-α position. Only needs liver activation; bypasses the broken renal step.

### Q: Who gets Alfacalcidol vs cholecalciferol?

- **Normal kidneys** → cholecalciferol (25-OH-D₃, OTC, cheap) — kidneys activate it
- **Renal failure** → Alfacalcidol (prescription only) — kidneys can't activate 25-OH-D

🟥 Don't give Alfacalcidol to a patient with working kidneys or hypercalcaemia — it's active vitamin D and will push Ca dangerously high.

## Section C — Clinical presentations

### Q: Why does low calcium make nerves and muscles hyperexcitable?

Extracellular Ca²⁺ stabilises voltage-gated Na⁺ channels. Low Ca²⁺ removes stabilisation → channels open more readily → spontaneous firing → tingling, twitching, spasm, seizures.

### Q: Signs and symptoms of hypocalcaemia — CATs go numb

- **C — Convulsions** (hyperexcitable CNS)
- **A — Arrhythmias** (heart muscle vulnerable)
- **T — Tetany** (sustained muscle contraction)
- **numb** — Paraesthesia: perioral, hands, feet, lips

### Q: What are Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs?

Both are provocation tests for hyperexcitable nerves in hypocalcaemia.

- **Chvostek's** — tap facial nerve below zygomatic arch → ipsilateral facial twitching
- **Trousseau's** — inflate BP cuff above systolic for 1–2 min → carpopedal spasm (wrist flexed, fingers drawn together)

📎 Figure L5.5.2 — companion PDF.

🟥 Chvostek = **Cheek**. Trousseau = **Tourniquet** (BP cuff). Standard mix-up.

### Q: Causes of hypocalcaemia?

- **Low PTH (hypoparathyroidism)** — surgical (neck surgery, e.g. thyroidectomy); autoimmune; congenital agenesis (rare)
- **Low calcitriol** — poor diet / malabsorption; lack of UV light (housebound, nursing home); renal failure (no 1-α-hydroxylation)

### Q: Why does high calcium cause sluggish nerves and atonic muscle?

High extracellular Ca²⁺ further stabilises Na⁺ channels → harder to depolarise → reduced excitability across CNS, GI, and skeletal muscle.

### Q: Signs and symptoms of hypercalcaemia — stones, moans, groans

| Category | Features | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Stones (renal) | Nephrocalcinosis, kidney stones, renal colic | Filtered Ca precipitates; muscular ureter painful on passage |
| Moans (GI) | Anorexia, nausea, dyspepsia, constipation, pancreatitis | Sluggish peristalsis (GI is a muscle tube) |
| Groans (CNS) | Fatigue, depression, impaired concentration, altered mentation, coma | Sluggish CNS neurons |

🟨 Coma threshold: usually Ca²⁺ > 3 mmol/L. Only concrete number in the lecture.

### Q: Three causes of hypercalcaemia by frequency?

| Cause | Mechanism | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| 1° hyperparathyroidism | Adenoma making PTH autonomously | Most common |
| Malignancy | PTHrP secretion (SCC, breast, myeloma) | Common in inpatients |
| Vitamin D excess | Over-supplementation | Very rare |

## Section D — Atlas: the four hypercalcaemia / PTH entities

### Card 1 — Primary hyperparathyroidism (1° HPT)

#### Q: Pathophysiology of 1° HPT?

Benign adenoma in one of four parathyroid glands secretes PTH autonomously. Ignores the calcium-sensing receptor; negative feedback is broken.

#### Q: Biochemistry triad of 1° HPT?

- Calcium ↑ — PTH mobilising from bone / kidney / gut
- Phosphate ↓ — PTH blocking Na⁺/PO₄ cotransporter
- PTH ↑ — secreted autonomously despite high Ca

Renal function normal.

#### Q: Treatment of 1° HPT and why surgical?

**Parathyroidectomy** — remove the adenoma, curative.

Untreated risks:
- Osteoporosis (chronic osteoclast drive)
- Renal calculi (chronic filtered Ca load)
- Mood / concentration impact (psychic groans)

🟦 Once a gland secretes autonomously, medical treatment can't suppress it. Operate.

### Card 2 — Secondary hyperparathyroidism (2° HPT)

#### Q: Pathophysiology of 2° HPT?

Not a parathyroid disease — a normal physiological response to low calcium. Commonest trigger: vitamin D deficiency.

```
Low vitamin D → Low calcitriol → Low gut Ca absorption → Low serum Ca
  → Parathyroids sense correctly → Appropriate ↑ PTH
  → PTH pulls Ca from bone + kidney to normalise
```

#### Q: Biochemistry of 2° HPT?

- Calcium ↓ or low-normal (partially compensated by PTH rise)
- PTH ↑ (appropriately)
- 25-OH-D usually low

🟦 High PTH + high Ca = inappropriate (1° or 3°). High PTH + low Ca = appropriate (2°). Always read the calcium before judging PTH.

#### Q: Causes of 2° HPT?

- Vitamin D deficiency (commonest) — poor diet, reduced sunlight
- Vitamin D activation failure — renal failure (no 1-α-hydroxylase)
- Malabsorption — coeliac, bariatric surgery, chronic GI disease

#### Q: Treatment of 2° HPT?

- **Normal kidneys** → cholecalciferol (D₃) or ergocalciferol (D₂); own kidneys activate via 1-α-hydroxylase
- **Renal failure** → Alfacalcidol, which bypasses the broken kidney step

### Card 3 — Tertiary hyperparathyroidism (3° HPT)

#### Q: Pathophysiology of 3° HPT?

Sequel to long-standing 2° HPT that was never fixed — classically CKD.

```
CKD (or chronic severe vit D deficiency)
  → Can't make calcitriol → Chronic low Ca → Sustained PTH drive
    → All four parathyroid glands become hyperplastic
      → Glands go autonomous, ignore feedback
        → Calcium rises ABOVE normal → hypercalcaemia
```

Rare overall; in the UK, almost always driven by CKD.

#### Q: Biochemistry of 3° HPT vs 1° HPT?

| | 1° HPT | 3° HPT |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium | ↑ | ↑ |
| PTH | ↑ | ↑↑↑ |
| Renal function | Normal | CKD |
| Gland anatomy | Single adenoma | All 4 glands hyperplastic |

🟨 Sole reliable discriminator: **renal function**. Both have ↑Ca + ↑PTH. Check eGFR / creatinine in the stem.

#### Q: Treatment of 3° HPT?

Parathyroidectomy — usually sub-total (remove 3½ glands).

### Card 4 — Hypercalcaemia of malignancy

#### Q: Pathophysiology of hypercalcaemia of malignancy?

Tumour secretes PTH-related peptide (**PTHrP**), which binds PTH receptors and mimics PTH. Real parathyroid glands are innocent — they correctly suppress their own PTH in response to the high Ca.

Classic tumours: squamous cell carcinoma (lung, head and neck), breast, multiple myeloma.

#### Q: Biochemistry of hypercalcaemia of malignancy?

- Calcium ↑ — PTHrP mobilising Ca
- PTH ↓ (suppressed) — real parathyroids correctly shutting off

🟦 ↑ Ca + ↓ PTH → think malignancy. If no known cancer, investigate.

#### Q: Initial management of hypercalcaemia of malignancy?

1. IV 0.9% NaCl (rehydrate first) — hypercalcaemia → polyuria → dehydration → worsening Ca. Fluids alone drop Ca substantially.
2. IV bisphosphonate — inhibits osteoclasts. 2–4 days for full effect. Also reduces bony metastatic pain.
3. Treat the underlying cancer.

📎 Figure L5.5.4 — companion PDF.

Bisphosphonates act on osteoclasts, not PTH; they don't treat the tumour.

## Section E — Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: Diagnostic algorithm for any abnormal calcium?

```
Abnormal calcium on bloods.
         ↓
Is Ca HIGH or LOW?
         ↓
Measure PTH.
         ↓
Is PTH moving OPPOSITE to Ca (appropriate)?
   │
   ├── YES → parathyroid innocent.
   │     • ↑Ca + ↓PTH → malignancy
   │     • ↓Ca + ↑PTH → 2° HPT → measure 25-OH-D
   │
   └── NO → parathyroid pathological.
         • ↑Ca + ↑PTH → 1° or 3° HPT → check renal function
              Normal → 1° HPT
              CKD    → 3° HPT
         • ↓Ca + ↓PTH → hypoparathyroidism
              (post-surgical, autoimmune, congenital)
```

### Q: Master discriminator — the four entities

| | 1° HPT | 2° HPT | 3° HPT | Malignancy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ca²⁺ | ↑ | ↓ / low-normal | ↑ | ↑ |
| PTH | ↑ (inappropriate) | ↑ (appropriate) | ↑↑↑ | ↓ (suppressed) |
| Renal function | Normal | Normal or abnormal | CKD | Usually preserved |
| Driver | Adenoma (1 gland) | Low vit D | 4-gland hyperplasia | Tumour PTHrP |
| Treatment | Parathyroidectomy | Vit D replacement | Parathyroidectomy | Fluids + bisphosphonate |

### Q: When to measure 25-OH-D vs calcitriol?

Always measure 25-OH-D — stable, easy, reflects stores. Calcitriol is light-sensitive and rarely measured; infer from 25-OH-D + renal function + clinical picture.

## Section F — Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1. 59M, Ca 2.85 (high), phosphate 0.60 (low), PTH 14 pmol/L (high), eGFR 92. Diagnosis?

Primary hyperparathyroidism.

- Classic triad: ↑Ca + ↑PTH + ↓phosphate + normal kidneys
- Malignancy: PTH would be suppressed
- 2° HPT: Ca would be low
- 3° HPT: would need CKD
- Vit D toxicity: PTH would be suppressed

### Q: SBA 2. 35M on haemodialysis, Ca 2.10 (low-normal), PTH 180 (very high), 25-OH-D low. Diagnosis?

Secondary hyperparathyroidism.

- CKD → no calcitriol → low Ca → appropriate ↑ PTH
- Ca is low-normal, not high — hasn't crossed into 3°

🟥 CKD + high PTH does NOT automatically mean tertiary. Calcium must rise above normal for tertiary.

### Q: SBA 3. 74F with metastatic SCLC, confused, Ca 3.6, PTH 3 (suppressed). First intervention?

IV 0.9% NaCl (rehydration).

- Severe hypercalcaemia → polyuria → dehydration → worsening Ca
- Fluids drop Ca substantially; correct dehydration before bisphosphonates
- Bisphosphonates come second (2–4 day onset; safer when rehydrated)

### Q: SBA 4. 52F, 24h post-total-thyroidectomy, perioral tingling. Cheek tap → facial twitching. Sign + problem?

Chvostek's sign; hypocalcaemia.

Parathyroids sit behind thyroid → damaged in neck surgery → hypoparathyroidism → low Ca → hyperexcitable nerves.

### Q: SBA 5. 63M, CKD stage 5 for 8 years, Ca 2.90, PTH 650. Definitive management?

Parathyroidectomy.

- Tertiary HPT — autonomous 4-gland hyperplasia
- Medical treatment can't suppress autonomous glands
- Definitive = surgical removal

### Q: SBA 6. Which patient gets Alfacalcidol rather than cholecalciferol?

70-year-old with CKD 4, low-normal Ca, raised PTH.

- Alfacalcidol is pre-activated at 1-α; use when kidneys can't do that step
- Working kidneys → cheap cholecalciferol
- Hypercalcaemia → don't give any active vitamin D

### Q: SBA 7. Why does 1° HPT cause low phosphate?

PTH inhibits the Na⁺/PO₄³⁻ cotransporter in the proximal renal tubule → phosphate excreted in urine → serum phosphate falls.

### Q: SBA 8. Single best next test to distinguish 1° HPT from malignancy?

Serum PTH.

- ↑ PTH → parathyroid pathology (1° or 3°)
- ↓ PTH → malignancy
- PTH is the first split in any hypercalcaemia workup

### Q: SBA 9. 82F housebound, Ca 1.98, phosphate 0.70, PTH 110, 25-OH-D 15, eGFR 78. Diagnosis + management?

Secondary hyperparathyroidism from vitamin D deficiency. Oral cholecalciferol.

- Low Ca + high PTH + low 25-OH-D + normal kidneys
- Housebound / no UV exposure = classic risk profile
- Not Alfacalcidol — kidneys work fine

### Q: SBA 10. Which cell does PTH bind to in bone, and what's the downstream signal?

Osteoblast; RANKL.

- PTH receptors are on osteoblasts (Build)
- Osteoblasts release RANKL → activates osteoclasts (Consume)
- Osteoclasts don't have PTH receptors
- OPG is a decoy receptor that blocks RANKL (brake, not accelerator)

### Q: SBA 11. 50F with breast cancer, Ca 3.1. Expected PTH and mechanism?

PTH low / suppressed; tumour secretes PTHrP.

- PTHrP mimics PTH at PTH receptors → drives Ca up
- Real parathyroids correctly suppress their own PTH
- High Ca + low PTH IS the diagnostic signature

🟥 Don't pick "PTH high because the tumour drives Ca up." PTHrP drives Ca; real PTH is suppressed.

### Q: SBA 12. Single best feature to distinguish 1° HPT from 3° HPT?

Renal function.

- Both have ↑Ca + ↑PTH
- Normal renal function → 1°
- CKD → 3°
