## §1 — Cardinal features & the pathophysiology pair

⭐ **Asthma is eosinophilic, Type-2-driven, REVERSIBLE airflow obstruction in a genetically susceptible airway.** Every test confirms one of those four words; every drug attacks one.

📎 Figure L1.1 — Healthy vs asthmatic airway wall: epithelium, matrix, smooth muscle, eosinophils

### Q: What are the four cardinal pathophysiological features of asthma?

- **Atopy / allergen sensitisation**
- **Reversible airflow obstruction**
- **Airway inflammation that is eosinophilic**
- **Type-2 / Th2 / CD4⁺-driven immunity**

### Q: What clinical syndrome do those features produce?

**Wheeze ± dry cough ± dyspnoea**, in **episodes** ± persistent symptoms, **precipitated by exertion, viral colds, allergen exposure**.

### Q: Why is calling asthma "eosinophilic inflammation" examinable, not just descriptive?

The cell type tells you the drug class. **ICS induce eosinophil apoptosis**; **anti-IL-5 biologics remove the cytokine that keeps eosinophils alive**. Forget the cell, lose the management logic.

### Q: What single feature distinguishes asthma from COPD on spirometry, and why?

**Reversibility** (≥12% rise in FEV1 post-bronchodilator). In asthma, smooth muscle constriction and mucosal oedema are reversible. In COPD, the parenchymal destruction (emphysema) and structural fibrosis are not.

### Q: What does "the asthmatic airway is abnormal even at baseline" mean clinically?

The wall is permanently thickened (eosinophils, oedema, matrix, more smooth muscle) and the lumen is permanently narrowed. **No reserve.** A cold or pollen tips the patient into an attack because they were already on the edge.

### Q: Two pathophysiological hallmarks of asthma — what's the trick about them?

They are held together by **AND**, not OR.

- **Reversible airflow obstruction** — physiology (spirometry).
- **Airway eosinophilia** — pathology (biopsy, sputum, blood proxy, FeNO proxy).

If a stem gives only one, look for a mimic (vocal-cord dysfunction, eosinophilic pneumonia).

### Q: Three numerical thresholds defining reversible airflow obstruction.

- **FEV1/FVC <0.7 (adults), <0.8 (children)** — defines obstruction.
- **Bronchodilator reversibility ≥12%** (NICE adults add ≥200 mL absolute).
- **PEFR variability >20%** — supportive over days.

### Q: What is "airway remodelling" and why does it matter therapeutically?

⚠️ **Trap:** ICS and biologics treat **inflammation** but cannot reverse **remodelling** (matrix deposition, smooth-muscle hyperplasia, basement membrane thickening). This is why early control matters — once remodelling is laid down, it stays.

## §2 — Wheeze and its mimics

🧠 **Wheeze is a finding, not a diagnosis.** Anything that turbulently narrows airflow makes a noise. Three discriminators tell you whether it's asthma: **timing** (inspiratory vs expiratory), **reversibility** (responds to bronchodilator?), and **flow-loop shape** (scooped vs flat-topped).

📎 Figure L1.4 — Asthmatic flow-volume loop: scooped expiratory curve

📎 Figure L1.7 — Flat-topped flow-volume loop = fixed upper-airway obstruction (NOT asthma)

### Q: Mechanical origin of the wheeze sound — why expiratory?

Air through a **wide tube** flows **laminar** and silent; through a **narrowed tube** it flows **turbulent** and audible. Expiration is longer and more passive, and intrathoracic airways narrow further on expiration → turbulent flow loudest there.

### Q: Wheeze vs stridor: three differences.

| Feature | Wheeze | Stridor |
|---|---|---|
| Timing | Expiratory (mainly) | Inspiratory (mainly) |
| Pitch | High-pitched, musical | Harsh, monophonic |
| Origin | Lower airway (bronchi/bronchioles) | Upper airway (larynx/trachea) |

### Q: What flow-volume loop pattern signals fixed upper-airway obstruction?

**Flat-topping of BOTH expiratory and inspiratory limbs.** A fixed narrowing throttles flow at a near-constant low rate regardless of effort → plateau instead of a peak. Asthma scoops the **expiratory limb only**.

### Q: Three named differentials for "wheeze that isn't asthma".

- **Endotracheal tumour** (mass on bronchoscopy).
- **Inhaled foreign body** (radio-opaque object on CXR; usually paediatric).
- **Vocal-cord dysfunction / subglottic stenosis** (episodic stridor mistaken for asthma).

📎 Figure L1.8 — Differentials for wheeze: endotracheal tumour and inhaled foreign body

### Q: Patient labelled "asthma" not responding to inhalers — how do you triage?

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern:** If treatment fails AND tests are inconsistent (no scooped loop, no FeNO rise, no eosinophilia), **rethink the diagnosis** — don't escalate. Order spirometry, look at the loop shape, and consider bronchoscopy for fixed obstruction. Lecturer cited two real cases mistreated as asthma for 6–12 months.

## §3 — Genes × environment: who actually gets asthma

⭐ **Sensitisation is necessary but not sufficient.** Hay fever and eczema can occur from sensitisation alone; **asthma additionally needs a genetically susceptible airway**.

📎 Figure L1.9 — GWAS Manhattan plot for asthma susceptibility (GSDMB, IL33, CDHR3)

### Q: What does susceptibility actually consist of, mechanistically?

- **Abnormal epithelial barrier** — leaky tight junctions; allergen, virus, and pollutants all penetrate easily.
- **Alarmin-prone epithelium** — releases TSLP, IL-25, IL-33 when breached.
- **Hyperresponsive smooth muscle** — twitchy, primed to constrict.

### Q: Top GWAS hits and which were flagged for childhood-onset asthma?

- **GSDMB** (chr 17) — strongest single signal.
- **IL33** — epithelial alarmin gene; **flagged for childhood-onset**.
- **CDHR3** — receptor for **rhinovirus C**; **flagged for childhood-onset** viral exacerbations.
- IL1R1, RAD50 — weaker associations.

### Q: Why is gene therapy not feasible for asthma?

Asthma is **multi-genic and multifactorial** — no single locus contributes enough effect size. Contrast with cystic fibrosis (single CFTR gene), where targeted gene/protein therapy works.

### Q: Why does "one broken barrier" predispose to multiple sensitivities?

The same defect that lets allergen through also lets **virus** through (worse colds) and **pollution** through (worse pollution reactivity). One mechanism explains the asthma–rhinitis–eczema overlap and the bad reaction to NO₂, mould, smoke.

## §4 — Type-2 immunology (CASCADE)

⭐ **Memorise the chain in this order — every cytokine, cell, biomarker, and drug clips onto it.**

```
   ABNORMAL EPITHELIAL BARRIER
        │                       │
    INNATE (fast)           ADAPTIVE (slower)
   alarmins released:      DC takes up allergen
   TSLP, IL-25, IL-33      → mediastinal LN
        │                  → MHC class II to CD4⁺
        │                  → Th0 differentiates → Th2
        └──────────┬───────────┘
                   ▼
   Th2 secretes 3 cardinal Type-2 cytokines:
   ┌─────────┬─────────┬───────────────────┐
   │  IL-5   │  IL-4   │      IL-13        │
   │   ↓     │   ↓     │       ↓           │
   │ EOS     │ B-cell  │ goblet cell mucus │
   │ recruit │ → IgE   │ + smooth-muscle   │
   │ + grow  │ class-  │ + FeNO (iNOS)     │
   │ + live  │ switch  │                   │
   └─────────┴─────────┴───────────────────┘
                   ▼
        IgE binds FcεRI on MAST CELL
                   ▼
        Re-exposure crosslinks IgE
                   ▼
   Mast cell degranulation: histamine,
   leukotrienes, cytokines, chemokines
                   ▼
              ALLERGIC ASTHMA
```

📎 Figure L1.10 — Allergic asthma immunopathology: epithelial alarmins, APC/Th2 axis, eosinophil, IgE/mast cell

### Q: Three cardinal Type-2 cytokines and one effector role each.

| Cytokine | Effector role | Drug that targets it |
|---|---|---|
| **IL-5** | Eosinophils (recruit, grow, activate, sustain) | Mepolizumab; benralizumab (anti-IL-5Rα) |
| **IL-4** | B-cell class-switch to **IgE** | Dupilumab (via shared IL-4Rα) |
| **IL-13** | Goblet-cell **mucus** + smooth-muscle effects + drives FeNO | Dupilumab (via shared IL-4Rα) |

### Q: What are the three epithelial alarmins, and why do they matter?

**TSLP, IL-25, IL-33.** They drive Type-2 inflammation **without needing an APC** (innate arm). Targeting them (e.g. **tezepelumab, anti-TSLP**) blocks the cascade upstream of all three classical cytokines — the strategic direction of new biologics.

### Q: Antigen-presenting cell of the lung; which MHC class?

**Dendritic cell**, presenting allergen on **MHC class II** (because it activates CD4⁺ Th cells).

### Q: Why does dupilumab block IL-4 AND IL-13 with a single antibody?

It binds **IL-4Rα**, which is part of **both** receptor complexes (IL-4 type II receptor and IL-13 receptor). Block IL-4Rα → both signalling pathways shut.

### Q: Mepolizumab vs benralizumab — same target family, different mechanism?

- **Mepolizumab** = anti-**IL-5 cytokine**. Neutralises free IL-5 → eosinophils starved.
- **Benralizumab** = anti-**IL-5 receptor α** on eosinophils. Triggers ADCC → eosinophils **directly depleted** (counts near zero).

### Q: Where in the cascade does IgE crosslinking happen, and what's the consequence?

On the **surface of mast cells** (and basophils), where IgE is pre-loaded onto **FcεRI**. Re-exposure to the allergen crosslinks two IgE molecules → mast-cell degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes, eicosanoids → **early-phase bronchoconstriction (10 min)**.

📎 Figure L1.2 — Local allergen challenge at 10 minutes (early-phase bronchoconstriction)

📎 Figure L1.3 — Local allergen challenge at 24 hours (late-phase persistent narrowing)

### Q: Early-phase vs late-phase response — what causes each?

- **Early (10 min):** mast-cell degranulation → histamine/leukotriene-driven bronchoconstriction.
- **Late (24 h):** IL-5-recruited **eosinophils** release granule proteins (MBP, ECP) → epithelial damage, sustained inflammation, smooth-muscle hyperresponsiveness.

## §5 — Diagnostic tests as a decision tree

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern:** every test exists to confirm ONE of the four cardinal-feature words. Match clue → word → test, and SBA-style "next test" questions become mechanical.

📎 Figure L1.13 — NICE 2024 asthma diagnosis algorithm: children 5–16 years

📎 Figure L1.14 — NICE 2024 asthma diagnosis algorithm: adults

### Q: Match each cardinal-feature word to its confirmatory test.

| Cardinal word | Test | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Reversible obstruction | Spirometry ± BD | FEV1/FVC <0.7 / <0.8; BDR ≥12% |
| Eosinophilic inflammation | FeNO; blood eos; sputum eos | FeNO ≥50 / ≥35 ppb; eos ≥300/µL; sputum ≥3% |
| Atopy / sensitisation | Skin prick test; **specific** IgE | Positive wheal vs control |
| Type-2 / hyperresponsive | Bronchial challenge (methacholine) | Drop in FEV1 at provocation dose |

### Q: How does FeNO actually work, mechanistically?

**IL-13** activates **inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS)** in airway epithelium. Activated epithelium releases more NO into the breath. **High FeNO = active Type-2-high inflammation.**

### Q: FeNO cut-offs — adults vs children.

| Group | Normal | Elevated | High (Type-2 likely) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adults | <20–25 | 20/25–50 | **>50 ppb** |
| Children | <15–20 | 15/20–35 | **>35 ppb** |

### Q: Why ICS confounds FeNO interpretation.

⚠️ **Trap:** ICS dampens FeNO. A low FeNO in a treated patient does **not** rule out asthma — it might just mean adherence is good. Test in a steroid-naïve patient where possible. Slide 27 quotes "≥40 ppb adult" but the algorithm slide and the lecturer's voice both say **≥50 ppb** — use 50.

### Q: Total IgE vs specific IgE — what's the difference for diagnosis?

- **Total IgE** = sum across everything; raised by parasites, eczema, lab variation. Does NOT define atopy.
- **Specific IgE** to a defined allergen (HDM, grass, cat) = sensitisation to that allergen.
- Skin prick test does the same job as specific IgE — wheal vs negative control.

### Q: NICE 2024 — order of tests in CHILDREN (5–16 yrs).

```
FeNO → BDR with spirometry → PEFR variability → SPT / total IgE / blood eos
```

Confirm asthma if **any** of: FeNO ≥35 ppb · BDR ≥12% · PEFR variability >20% · HDM sensitisation OR raised total IgE OR blood eos ≥0.5 ×10⁹/L.

### Q: NICE 2024 — order of tests in ADULTS.

```
Blood eos OR FeNO → BDR with spirometry → PEFR variability → bronchial challenge
```

Confirm asthma if **any** of: eos above lab range OR FeNO ≥50 ppb · BDR ≥12% AND ≥200 mL · PEFR amplitude % mean ≥20% · bronchial hyperresponsiveness.

### Q: Children vs adults algorithm — where do they differ?

- Adults use **blood eosinophils as a first-line equal partner with FeNO**.
- Adults end on **bronchial challenge** (children don't routinely).
- Children end on **allergen testing** (skin prick / specific IgE / eos ≥0.5).
- BDR threshold same (≥12%) but adults additionally need **≥200 mL absolute**.

## §6 — Management: three pillars from the mechanism

⭐ **Three things are wrong → three pillars of treatment.** Eosinophilic inflammation → ICS. Bronchoconstriction → β2-agonist. Severe Type-2 disease → biologic.

📎 Figure L1.15 — Mechanism of corticosteroids on inflammatory and structural airway cells

### Q: The three management pillars and their primary drug class.

| Problem | Drug class | Mechanism summary |
|---|---|---|
| Eosinophilic inflammation | **ICS** ± LTRA | Eosinophil apoptosis; multi-cell anti-inflammatory |
| Acute bronchoconstriction | **β2-agonist** (SABA / LABA) | Smooth-muscle relaxation |
| Severe Type-2 disease | **Biologic** (anti-IgE / IL-5 / IL-4Rα / TSLP) | Steroid-sparing, biomarker-stratified |

### Q: What is the single most important effect of ICS?

**Induction of eosinophil apoptosis.** That's the disease-modifying step. Other effects (↓ T-cell cytokines, ↓ mast-cell numbers, ↑ β2-receptor expression on smooth muscle, ↓ mucus, ↓ vascular leak) are supportive.

### Q: Why do patients underuse ICS — the adherence trap.

⚠️ **Trap:** ICS gives **no immediate symptom benefit** per puff. SABA gives immediate relief lasting ~2 hours but **does nothing to inflammation**. Patients feel SABA work and stop ICS → inflammation accumulates → severe attack with no reserve. This is the central mechanism of UK asthma deaths.

### Q: What single property of formoterol made MART possible?

**Rapid onset (1–3 min, like a SABA) AND long duration (≥12 h, like a LABA).** No other LABA combines both. Salmeterol is slow-onset and **cannot** be used in MART.

### Q: AIR vs MART/SMART — what's the difference?

- **AIR (Anti-Inflammatory Reliever)** = ICS-formoterol used **as-needed only**, for very mild/infrequent symptoms. Step 1.
- **MART / SMART** = same combination inhaler used as **scheduled maintenance + as-needed reliever**. Steps 2–3.

📎 Figure L1.16 — Anti-inflammatory reliever (AIR) therapy stepwise algorithm

### Q: AIR/MART steps 1, 2, 3.

- **Step 1:** budesonide-formoterol PRN only.
- **Step 2:** 1 actuation BD or 2 OD maintenance + PRN.
- **Step 3:** 2 actuations BD maintenance + PRN.
- **Before stepping up:** review inhaler **technique, use, and treatable traits** — don't blindly escalate.

### Q: Why does SMART reduce total ICS dose despite being used for symptoms?

- Patients **self-titrate** — more ICS during symptomatic periods, less during well periods.
- Every reliever puff carries some ICS — even "reliever-only" use gives anti-inflammatory benefit.
- Net effect: better control, **less total ICS** delivered.

### Q: Why is SABA monotherapy no longer first-line?

⭐ Lecturer's verbatim: *"any of you with asthma should not have a blue and a brown inhaler."* SABA-only is associated with **worse outcomes including death**. GINA, NICE, BTS/SIGN 2024 all moved first-line to **AIR/MART** (single ICS-formoterol inhaler).

## §7 — Acute asthma attack pathophysiology

⭐ **Attacks happen because multiple insults converge on an already-vulnerable airway with poor antiviral defence.** Not "one trigger, one attack."

📎 Figure L1.19 — Pathogenesis of acute asthma attack: triggers, reduced IFN, eosinophilia, obstruction

📎 Figure L1.17 — Asthma mortality 10–24 years (Nuffield Trust 2019): UK 3rd-highest

📎 Figure L1.18 — Asthma mortality 10–14 years: UK 2nd-highest

### Q: Four trigger classes for acute attacks — how do they combine?

- **Allergens** (pollen, HDM, dander) — drive Type-2 inflammation.
- **Pathogens** (rhinovirus > RSV, flu, bacteria).
- **Pollution** (NO₂, particulates).
- **Tobacco smoke**.

They combine **multiplicatively**, not additively. Classic stem: cold + pollen + thunderstorm → admission.

### Q: Three downstream consequences of a hit, and their treatments.

- **↓ Interferons (α/β/λ)** → prolonged viral illness — no acute treatment, prevent at baseline.
- **↑ Airway eosinophilia** → **responsive to systemic corticosteroids**.
- **↑ Smooth-muscle constriction** → ↓ PEFR, wheeze — **responsive to bronchodilators**.

### Q: Why is a viral cold worse and longer in an asthmatic?

The asthmatic epithelium produces **less IFN-α/β/λ** → reduced antiviral response → increased viral replication → prolonged illness. This is intrinsic, not treatment-related.

### Q: Acute attack — minimum drug recipe.

- **Oxygen** to keep SpO₂ ≥94%.
- **Salbutamol** nebs (β2-agonist) — reverses bronchoconstriction.
- **Oral or IV corticosteroid** (prednisolone / hydrocortisone) — addresses eosinophilic inflammation.
- **Ipratropium** add-on if severe; **IV magnesium** if life-threatening.

### Q: Why give systemic steroids in an attack — bronchodilator alone won't do?

Bronchodilator buys time (smooth-muscle relaxation, hours). **Corticosteroid buys recovery** by dampening the eosinophilic inflammation that won't reverse with β2-agonists. Skip the steroid → patient relapses.

⚠️ **Trap:** antibiotics are NOT routine. Most exacerbations are viral. Add antibiotics only if bacterial superinfection is documented.

### Q: Why is the UK paediatric asthma death rate among the worst in the developed world?

- Under-use of ICS / over-use of SABA (the adherence trap).
- Multiple environmental triggers (pollution, mould housing, tobacco).
- Late presentation; suboptimal escalation in primary care.
- A child dies from asthma in the UK roughly every 4 weeks.

## §8 — Severe asthma biologics (ATLAS)

⭐ **All biologics share four features:** monoclonal antibody, parenteral (s/c), used after standard therapy fails, biomarker-stratified. **What separates them is WHERE in the Type-2 cascade they act.**

```
ASTHMA BIOLOGICS — TERRITORY MAP

Upstream  TSLP            tezepelumab     (concept; broadest)
     │
Mid    IL-4 / IL-13       dupilumab        (anti-IL-4Rα — blocks both)
     │  IL-5              mepolizumab      (neutralises cytokine)
     │  IL-5Rα            benralizumab     (depletes eosinophils via ADCC)
     │
Downstream  IgE           omalizumab       (captures circulating IgE)
```

### Q: Card — Omalizumab (anti-IgE).

- **Target:** circulating IgE → blocks IgE binding to FcεRI on mast cells/basophils.
- **Indication (UK):** severe persistent **allergic** asthma, ≥4 OCS courses/yr, ≥6 yrs.
- **Eligibility:** total IgE **30–1500 IU/mL**; documented aeroallergen sensitisation.
- **Efficacy:** ~37% reduction in exacerbations (RR 0.63). Only 60% eligible; only 50–60% of those respond.

📎 Figure L1.20 — Omalizumab vs placebo: pooled exacerbation risk ratio 0.63 (Lai 2015)

### Q: Card — Mepolizumab (anti-IL-5).

- **Target:** free IL-5 → eosinophils starved of recruitment/survival signal.
- **Indication (UK):** severe **eosinophilic** asthma; ≥6 yrs.
- **Eligibility (NICE):** blood eos **≥300 cells/µL** in last 12 mo AND **≥4 OCS courses** in 12 mo.
- **Efficacy:** ~50% adult exacerbation reduction; only **~27% in children**.

📎 Figure L1.21 — Mepolizumab vs placebo: cumulative exacerbations in adults (Pavord 2012)

📎 Figure L1.22 — Mepolizumab vs placebo: paediatric exacerbation rate (Jackson 2022)

### Q: Card — Benralizumab (anti-IL-5Rα).

- **Target:** IL-5 receptor α on eosinophils → **ADCC depletes eosinophils** directly.
- **Indication:** severe eosinophilic asthma.
- **Licensed:** ≥12 yrs (USA per slide 52).
- **Distinction:** mepolizumab blocks the **cytokine**; benralizumab kills the **cell**. Same downstream goal.

### Q: Card — Dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα).

- **Target:** **shared IL-4 receptor α** → blocks BOTH IL-4 and IL-13 signalling.
- **Indication:** severe **Type-2-high** asthma; ≥12 yrs (EU/USA).
- **Eligibility:** eos ≥150 (or ≥300) cells/µL OR FeNO ≥20 ppb.
- **Efficacy:** ~46% exacerbation reduction AND **uniquely improves FEV1** (4.7–6.7%).

📎 Figure L1.23 — Dupilumab vs placebo: exacerbations and pp-FEV1 improvement (Bacharier 2021)

### Q: Card — Tezepelumab (anti-TSLP, concept).

- **Target:** TSLP, the epithelial alarmin — most upstream available.
- **Mechanism:** blocks alarmin → all three classical Type-2 cytokines fall.
- **Why mentioned:** strategic direction is to go **upstream, closer to the epithelium** — works regardless of biomarker phenotype.

📎 Figure L1.24 — Biologic targets in paediatric severe asthma (Scotney 2021)

## §9 — Cross-cutting biologic patterns

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern:** the SBA gives you a biomarker; you map it to the biologic. Biomarker is the question; biologic is the answer.

### Q: Biomarker → biologic mapping (the SBA shortcut).

| Stem clue | Best biologic | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Allergic + IgE 30–1500 IU/mL | **Omalizumab** | Only it captures IgE |
| Eos ≥300 + ≥4 OCS courses | **Mepolizumab** | NICE-defined severe eosinophilic |
| Eos high + want depletion | **Benralizumab** | ADCC kills eosinophils |
| Mixed Type-2 OR want lung function | **Dupilumab** | Blocks IL-4 + IL-13; only one to ↑ FEV1 |

### Q: What four features do all asthma biologics share?

- **Monoclonal antibody, parenteral (subcutaneous).**
- **Only after optimised standard therapy** has failed (high-dose ICS-LABA, treatable traits addressed).
- **Biomarker-stratified** eligibility.
- **12-month review:** if exacerbations haven't fallen by ~50%, discontinue.

### Q: Why is the eosinophilic phenotype not always allergic?

⚠️ **Trap:** **adult-onset eosinophilic asthma** is often **non-allergic** — high blood eos, normal IgE, no sensitisations. These patients fail omalizumab eligibility (no allergic phenotype) but are perfect mepolizumab candidates. Don't assume "severe asthma = allergic".

### Q: Of the listed biologics, which uniquely improves FEV1?

**Dupilumab.** Per Bacharier 2021, ppFEV1 improves by 4.7–6.7% across subgroups. Every other listed biologic only reduces exacerbations.

## §10 — Interleaved SBA bank

### Q: SBA 1 — A 17-yr-old with hay fever has FEV1/FVC 0.65, FEV1 rising 15% post-salbutamol. Which feature most strongly supports asthma?

- A. Hay fever history
- B. Reduced FEV1/FVC
- **C. 15% post-bronchodilator FEV1 rise ✓**
- D. Intermittent cough

**C** demonstrates **reversibility** — the cardinal physiological hallmark (≥12%). A and B are supportive but non-specific. D is symptom description only.

### Q: SBA 2 — 50-yr-old smoker, FEV1/FVC 0.55, only 5% post-BD rise. Feature against asthma?

- A. Age 50
- B. Smoking
- C. Cough/dyspnoea
- **D. 5% post-BD rise ✓**

**D** fails the ≥12% reversibility threshold — the asthma-defining counterpoint. A is weak (asthma at 50 is common). B raises COPD suspicion but doesn't exclude asthma overlap. C is non-specific.

### Q: SBA 3 — 14-yr-old "asthma" not responding for 6 months; monophonic inspiratory wheeze; flat-topped expiratory loop. Next step?

- A. ↑ ICS
- B. Add LABA
- **C. Bronchoscopy ✓**
- D. Methacholine challenge

**C.** Monophonic + inspiratory + flat-topped loop = **fixed central-airway obstruction**. Visualise it. A/B treat the wrong disease. D is for suspected asthma where loop is normal.

### Q: SBA 4 — 6-yr-old, severe eczema, recurrent rhinovirus-triggered wheeze, multiple specific IgEs. Most likely contributing genetic locus?

- A. CFTR
- **B. CDHR3 ✓**
- C. ADRB2
- D. HLA-DQB1*02

**B.** **CDHR3 = rhinovirus C receptor**, strongly tied to childhood viral asthma exacerbations. A is CF. C affects β2-receptor pharmacology, not susceptibility. D is autoimmune (coeliac).

### Q: SBA 5 — Severe asthma, FeNO 65 ppb, eos 180/µL, IgE 80 IU/mL, no sensitisations. Best biologic?

- A. Omalizumab
- B. Mepolizumab
- **C. Dupilumab ✓**
- D. Benralizumab

**C.** FeNO is high (IL-13 driven) — fits dupilumab's FeNO ≥20 criterion. Eos below 300 → fails mepolizumab/benralizumab. IgE low + non-allergic → fails omalizumab.

### Q: SBA 6 — 50-yr-old, adult-onset, non-allergic, eos 600/µL, 5 admissions last year. Best biologic?

- A. Omalizumab
- **B. Mepolizumab ✓**
- C. Dupilumab
- D. Tezepelumab

**B.** Adult-onset, non-allergic, eosinophilic = textbook mepolizumab phenotype (NICE: eos ≥300, ≥4 OCS courses). A wrong (non-allergic). C/D are alternatives but mepolizumab is the cleanest NICE-eligible choice.

### Q: SBA 7 — Newly diagnosed mild asthma, SABA 2–3×/week. Per GINA/NICE 2024, first-line?

- A. SABA alone PRN
- B. Low-dose ICS BD + SABA PRN
- **C. As-needed ICS-formoterol (AIR) ✓**
- D. Oral montelukast

**C.** Modern first-line is **AIR**. SABA monotherapy (A) is no longer recommended — worse outcomes including death. B is older two-inhaler regimen. D is add-on, not first-line.

### Q: SBA 8 — Patient on MART using 6 reliever puffs/day. Best action?

- A. Reassure — self-titrating
- B. Switch to oral steroids
- **C. Review technique/use/treatable traits → step up maintenance ✓**
- D. Stop maintenance

**C.** 6 puffs/day = poor control; AIR/MART algorithm explicitly says review **technique, use, treatable traits** before stepping up maintenance. A misreads the principle. B/D are wrong escalation/de-escalation.

### Q: SBA 9 — Most likely Type-2 cytokine driving elevated FeNO?

- A. IL-4
- B. IL-5
- **C. IL-13 ✓**
- D. TSLP

**C.** **IL-13 activates iNOS** in airway epithelium → ↑ NO output. IL-5 → eosinophils (blood eos), IL-4 → IgE, TSLP is upstream alarmin.

### Q: SBA 10 — 9-yr-old: FeNO 28 ppb, FEV1/FVC 0.81, BDR 8%, eos 0.4 ×10⁹/L, no HDM, IgE 60 kU/L. Per NICE 2024 paeds?

- A. Confirm — eos exceed 0.5
- B. Confirm — IgE positive
- **C. None met → alternative diagnosis ✓**
- D. Confirm — FeNO close to 35

**C.** All four paeds tests fail (FeNO 28 < 35; ratio 0.81 ≥ 0.8; BDR 8 < 12; eos 0.4 < 0.5; no specific sensitisation). A misreads cut. B — total IgE alone insufficient. D — close ≠ enough.

### Q: SBA 11 — Severe attack, multiple SABA pre-admission, SpO₂ 92%. Most important add-on?

- A. IV magnesium
- **B. Oral prednisolone ✓**
- C. IV antibiotics
- D. Inhaled budesonide

**B.** Acute attacks need **systemic corticosteroid** to dampen eosinophilic inflammation that bronchodilator can't touch. A is for life-threatening/refractory. C only if bacterial superinfection documented. D is too slow and not the acute answer.

### Q: SBA 12 — Patient on mepolizumab: 1 OCS course this year vs 5 last year. Review action?

- A. Discontinue — insufficient response
- B. Switch to dupilumab
- **C. Continue mepolizumab ✓**
- D. Add benralizumab

**C.** UK rule: **≥50% reduction at 12 mo → continue.** 5 → 1 = 80% reduction. A misreads criterion. B/D unnecessary when monotherapy is working.
