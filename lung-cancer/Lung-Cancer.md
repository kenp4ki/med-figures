## Mechanism spine — how four diseases come from one organ

🧠 **The single intuition: location of cell of origin predicts everything.** Where the cell sits in the airway determines location on imaging, presenting symptoms, biopsy choice, and which drugs work.

### Q: Why does lung cancer present as four different diseases?

- Carcinogens (smoke, asbestos, radon) bind covalently to DNA → **DNA adducts**.
- Persisting/mis-repaired adducts → mutation → genomic alteration in proto-oncogenes.
- Exposure is diffuse, so **any airway cell can transform** — cell of origin determines subtype.
- Central airways: ciliated, mucous, basal, **neuroendocrine** cells → squamous + small cell.
- Peripheral airways: club cells, type I/II pneumocytes, glandular cells → adenocarcinoma.

### Q: What is a DNA adduct, and why does it matter?

- Cancer-causing chemical bound covalently to DNA.
- Persistent adducts get mis-replicated → mutation.
- Single tumour can carry **up to ~20,000 mutations** — disease is highly heterogeneous.

📎 Figure L1.3 — Airway histology: central bronchial vs peripheral alveolar epithelium

### Q: What are the major non-smoking risk factors for lung cancer, ranked?

- **Asbestos** — plumbers, ship-builders, carpenters; risk **up to ×2**.
- **Radon** — historic silver/uranium miners.
- Indoor cooking fumes (wood smoke, frying fats).
- Chronic lung disease (**COPD, fibrosis**).
- Air pollution; familial loci (no single autosomal-dominant gene).

⭐ **10–15% of lung cancer patients have never smoked** — never let the absence of smoking history stop you considering it.

## Atlas card 1 — Histological subtypes

🧠 **First fork in any lung cancer question: NSCLC (80–85%) vs SCLC (15%).** This determines whether the treatment ladder is surgery-based or chemo-based — almost every SBA pivots on this binary first.

### Q: Compare the four histological subtypes by location and smoking link.

| Subtype | % | Location | Smoking |
|---|---|---|---|
| Squamous | ~30% | **Central** | Very strong |
| Adenocarcinoma | ~40% | **Peripheral** | Strong; commonest in never-smokers |
| Large cell | ~15% | Anywhere | Strong |
| Small cell | ~15% | **Central** | Very strong |

### Q: Why is adenocarcinoma now the commonest lung cancer overall?

- Overtook squamous from the 1980s.
- Shift attributed to **low-tar cigarettes** → deeper inhalation, longer retention.
- Carcinogens deposit peripherally rather than centrally.
- Also the commonest subtype in never-smokers.

### Q: Why is small cell lung cancer almost never managed with primary surgery?

- Originates from pulmonary **neuroendocrine cells** in central airways.
- Doubles fast, disseminates early → almost always extensive-stage at diagnosis.
- Responds quickly to chemotherapy initially but recurs; prognosis poor.
- Treatment is chemo ± immunotherapy, not resection.

### Q: What is the cell of origin for each subtype?

- Squamous → bronchial epithelial cells (central).
- Adenocarcinoma → mucus-producing glandular cells (peripheral).
- Large cell → undifferentiated (heterogeneous group).
- Small cell → pulmonary **neuroendocrine** cells (central).

### Q: How do central vs peripheral location predict presentation?

- **Central** (squamous, small cell) → cough, haemoptysis, post-obstructive pneumonia early; SVC obstruction, Horner's, recurrent laryngeal palsy if invading mediastinum.
- **Peripheral** (adeno) → asymptomatic for ages; pleural effusion or chest pain when large; often found incidentally.
- Apical adenocarcinomas → **Pancoast tumours** with arm symptoms.

## Atlas card 2 — Paraneoplastic syndromes by subtype

🧠 **Biochem fingerprints map to subtype: hyperCa²⁺ = squamous; hypoNa⁺ or Cushing's = small cell.** This is one of the most reliably tested patterns in respiratory oncology.

### Q: Which paraneoplastic syndrome maps to which lung cancer subtype?

| Syndrome | Subtype | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Hypercalcaemia | **Squamous** | PTHrP secretion |
| SIADH → hyponatraemia | **Small cell** | Ectopic ADH |
| Ectopic Cushing's | **Small cell** | Ectopic ACTH |
| Clubbing / HPOA | Any NSCLC (often adeno) | Paraneoplastic |

### Q: What is Horner's syndrome and where is the tumour?

- Triad: **ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis** (drooping lid, small pupil, dry/no-sweat half-face).
- Caused by an **apical Pancoast tumour** compressing the cervical sympathetic chain.
- Often accompanied by ipsilateral arm pain or wasting (brachial plexus).

### Q: What is Pemberton's sign and why does it matter?

- Patient lifts arms above head → face becomes **plethoric/cyanotic** + neck veins distend.
- Sign of **superior vena cava obstruction** by central tumour.
- **Oncological emergency** — risk of airway compromise + haemodynamic collapse.

⚠️ **Trap:** SVCO can be the *presenting* feature of an undiagnosed lung cancer — facial swelling worse on bending forward + distended neck veins in a smoker is not "just oedema". Stem will reward urgent admission, steroids, SVC stenting.

### Q: What general signs should you look for on examination?

- **Clubbing** — loss of nail-bed angle (also COPD, ILD, IE — not specific).
- **Cachexia** — wasting from catabolic disease + inflammatory cytokines.
- Lymphadenopathy (especially supraclavicular = N3 disease).
- Signs of metastasis: hepatomegaly, focal neuro deficit, bone tenderness.

📎 Figure L1.5 — Clinical signs: clubbing, cachexia, Horner's syndrome, Pemberton's sign

### Q: What are the key symptoms a GP should screen for?

- Cough (persistent, unexplained).
- **Haemoptysis** (red flag in any smoker).
- Weight loss, fatigue, breathlessness.
- Chest pain (often dull, persistent).
- Or **completely asymptomatic** — incidental imaging finding.

### Q: What features point toward advanced/metastatic disease?

- Focal neurological signs, seizures, **spinal cord compression** (brain/cord mets).
- Bone pain (skeletal mets).
- Paraneoplastic biochemistry.
- Common met sites: **liver, brain, bones, adrenals, contralateral lung, lymph nodes**.

## Atlas card 3 — Driver oncogenes (NSCLC)

🧠 **Each oncogene is a growth-signal switch stuck ON. A TKI ending in "-ib" jams it back OFF.** No targetable mutation, no TKI — so molecular testing is mandatory in NSCLC.

### Q: Compare the four actionable oncogenes by demographic and TKIs.

| Gene | % NSCLC | Demographic | Example TKIs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EGFR** | 15–30% adeno | Women, Asian, never-smoker | Osimertinib |
| **ALK** | 2–7% | Younger, never-smoker | Alectinib, crizotinib |
| **ROS1** | 1–2% | Younger, never-smoker | Crizotinib, entrectinib |
| **BRAF** | 1–3% | **Smokers** (odd one out) | Dabrafenib + trametinib |

### Q: How does a tyrosine kinase inhibitor work mechanistically?

- Receptor tyrosine kinases (EGFR, ALK, ROS1) sit on the cell membrane with kinase domain inside.
- Mutation locks kinase ON → constant downstream PI3K/AKT, Ras/MEK/MAPK signalling → proliferation.
- TKI binds the kinase domain → blocks phosphorylation → halts the signal.

📎 Figure L1.4 — EGFR receptor with TKI and anti-EGFR mAb targets

### Q: What's the demographic fingerprint for EGFR-mutant NSCLC?

- **Women.**
- **Asian ethnicity.**
- **Never-smokers.**
- Adenocarcinoma histology.
- Combination of all four → reach for EGFR testing reflexively.

### Q: Which two specific EGFR mutations trigger osimertinib eligibility?

- **Exon 19 deletion.**
- **Exon 21 L858R substitution.**
- These two cover the vast majority of EGFR-mutant adenocarcinoma.
- Both used in metastatic setting AND adjuvant after resection (stage IB–IIIA, up to 3 years).

### Q: What's special about osimertinib compared to older EGFR TKIs?

- 3rd-generation TKI; NICE-preferred first-line.
- **Penetrates CNS well** → effective against brain mets (lung cancer's favourite secondary site).
- Used in two settings: metastatic first-line AND adjuvant post-resection.

### Q: How do you spot a TKI-related side effect on an SBA stem?

- **Rash** (acneiform; counter-intuitively, more rash = better response).
- **Diarrhoea.**
- Uncommonly: **pneumonitis** (new breathlessness + ground-glass on CT → stop drug, steroids).
- Generally well-tolerated as a tablet vs IV chemo.

⚠️ **Trap:** Don't confuse TKI pneumonitis with immunotherapy pneumonitis. Both real. **-ib drug + new dyspnoea** = TKI pneumonitis; **-mab drug + new dyspnoea** = immune-related pneumonitis. Both → stop drug, image, consider steroids.

## Atlas card 4 — Diagnostic pipeline

🧠 **Reusable pattern: each test answers one specific question.** When an SBA asks "next investigation", find where you are on this tree and walk one step right — never skip.

```
Symptoms or incidental finding
       │
       ▼
  CHEST X-RAY        → "Is there anything visible?"
       │
       ▼
  CT chest+abdo      → "Where is it & where's it spread in chest/upper abdo?"
       │              WORKHORSE — assigns T and most of N
       ▼
  PET-CT             → "Are there occult mets I haven't seen?"
       │              Used before committing to radical Rx
       ▼
  BIOPSY             → "What is it histologically + which mutations?"
       │              Method picked by tumour LOCATION
       ▼
  Molecular testing + PD-L1 IHC → drives metastatic Rx choice
```

### Q: Why is CT (not CXR) used for screening high-risk patients?

- CXR misses small and central lesions.
- Low-dose CT in the **NELSON trial** showed a **26% reduction in lung cancer deaths**.
- UK programme: current/ex-smokers aged **55–74**, invited via GP for low-dose CT.
- Pilot data: 2.5% had cancer; **58.6% stage 1** vs ~20% in symptomatic presentations.

### Q: When is PET-CT used and when is it pointless?

- **Used:** suspected cancer on CT, fit for radical treatment, want to exclude occult mets and confirm nodal activity.
- **Pointless:** patient already known to have widespread mets — won't change management.
- Mechanism: ¹⁸F-FDG (radiolabelled glucose) is taken up by metabolically active tumour cells.

⚠️ **Trap:** PET-CT is unreliable for **brain** mets — cortex takes up FDG physiologically. If brain mets are suspected clinically, order **MRI brain** separately.

### Q: What does an "unilateral pleural effusion with a meniscus" imply on a CXR in an adult?

- **Lung cancer until proven otherwise** — lecturer's explicit phrasing.
- Other causes (TB, parapneumonic, PE) exist but cancer must be excluded.
- Next step: pleural aspiration ± CT chest.

## Atlas card 5 — Biopsy modalities

🧠 **Match the tool to the terrain.** Central → bronchoscope. Mediastinal nodes → EBUS. Peripheral → CT-guided. Beyond bronchoscope reach → navigational.

### Q: Compare the four biopsy modalities by location and key role.

| Modality | Best for | Key feature |
|---|---|---|
| Bronchoscopy | Central / segmental airways | Visualise + biopsy under conscious sedation |
| **EBUS-TBNA** | Mediastinal lymph nodes | Stages mediastinum AND gets tissue in one go |
| CT-guided | Peripheral lesions near pleura | ~20% pneumothorax risk |
| Navigational | Peripheral, beyond bronchoscope reach | Used when CT-guided is risky |

### Q: Why is EBUS-TBNA the high-yield biopsy in mediastinal disease?

- Endobronchial ultrasound + transbronchial needle aspiration.
- Samples mediastinal/hilar lymph nodes through the airway wall.
- **Stages the mediastinum AND provides tissue diagnosis** in one procedure.
- Avoids GA (conscious sedation) and avoids surgical mediastinoscopy.

### Q: What's the key complication of CT-guided lung biopsy?

- **Pneumothorax in ~1 in 5** patients.
- Clinical picture: sudden breathlessness post-procedure, hyperresonance, reduced breath sounds.
- Management: CXR → small drain or aspiration depending on size.

### Q: Why is wedge resection considered diagnostic, not curative?

- Removes a small chunk of lung **without** respecting anatomical segmental planes.
- High microscopic recurrence risk.
- Used when frozen section is needed intra-op — surgeon then proceeds to lobectomy.

## Atlas card 6 — Staging (IASLC 9th edition)

🧠 **T, N, M are independent axes — score separately, then combine.** Don't memorise stage groupings cold; own the axis logic and the four decision points where treatment changes.

### Q: How is the N stage determined? (Centripetal logic)

- **N0** — no nodes.
- **N1** — ipsilateral hilar / intrapulmonary (same side, close to tumour).
- **N2** — ipsilateral mediastinal / subcarinal (same side, central).
- **N3** — contralateral mediastinal/hilar OR any supraclavicular.

⚠️ **Trap:** A hilar node on the same side as the tumour is **N1**, NOT N2. N2 requires mediastinal/subcarinal. This is exactly what Q2 in the lecture tested.

### Q: What's the difference between M1a, M1b, M1c1 and M1c2?

- **M1a** — intrathoracic spread (malignant pleural/pericardial effusion, contralateral lobe nodule).
- **M1b** — single extrathoracic met in single organ.
- **M1c1** — multiple mets, **single organ system**.
- **M1c2** — multiple mets, **multiple organ systems**.

### Q: What changed from the 8th to the 9th edition?

- T component: **no changes**.
- N2 split: **N2a** (single station) vs **N2b** (multilevel).
- M1c split: **M1c1** (multi-mets, one organ) vs **M1c2** (multi-organ).
- Lecturer struck through 8th edition and said *"don't need to know in detail"* — own the 9th-edition splits.

### Q: Which transitions actually change treatment decisions?

- T size past 3 cm → no longer eligible for sublobar resection (need lobectomy).
- N1 → N2a → still potentially surgical, often after neoadjuvant.
- N2a → N2b → generally **not surgical** → radical chemoRT + consolidation IO.
- Any N3 or M1 → **palliative intent** (no curative surgery).

### Q: How do you stage Q2 from the lecture (RUL adeno 34 mm + active right hilar node + active left adrenal)?

- T: 34 mm = >3 cm but ≤4 cm → **T2a**.
- N: hilar = same side, NOT mediastinal → **N1**.
- M: single adrenal lesion = single met, single organ → **M1b**.
- Final: **cT2aN1M1b** (Stage IVA).

📎 Figure L1.13 — IASLC 9th edition TNM classification for lung cancer

### Q: Map the four stage groups to treatment intent.

- **Stage I–II** → curative; surgery first, SABR if unfit.
- **Stage III** → mixed; multimodality (surgery if N2a, chemoRT if N2b).
- **Stage IV** → palliative intent; systemic therapy + palliative care.
- **Any PS ≥3** → systemic options usually off the table regardless of stage.

## Atlas card 7 — Performance status

🧠 **Radical treatment is restricted to PS 0–2.** PS ≥3 patients do badly with chemo or radical therapy — toxicity outweighs benefit.

### Q: Define each WHO performance status grade.

- **0** — Asymptomatic, fully active.
- **1** — Symptomatic but ambulatory; light work OK.
- **2** — Up >50% waking; self-care yes, no work.
- **3** — Bed/chair >**50%** waking; limited self-care.
- **4** — Totally bed/chair-bound, no self-care.

### Q: What stem features distinguish PS 2 from PS 3?

- **The 50% threshold is the discriminator** — not whether they self-care.
- "Up and about >50% waking" → PS 2.
- "Spends most of his time sitting/in bed" → PS 3 (even if still self-caring).
- Stem from Q3: "housebound + sits down most of the time" → **Grade 3**.

⚠️ **Trap:** "Independently self-caring" tempts you toward PS 2. But if the stem says >50% sitting/bed, the answer is **PS 3** — the time threshold beats the self-care detail.

### Q: Why does performance status dominate prognosis?

- PS 0–1 → median survival ~2 years in advanced disease.
- PS 2 → median <12 months.
- PS 3–4 → median <6 months.
- Better PS = better tolerance of chemo, fewer infections, faster recovery from procedures.

## Atlas card 8 — Treatment by stage

🧠 **Two-gate decision: (1) Curative possible? → surgery vs SABR vs multimodality. (2) If metastatic → mutation? → PD-L1?** Drag any treatment SBA through these two gates.

### Q: What's the standard surgical approach for early-stage NSCLC?

- **Lobectomy + lymphadenectomy** is gold standard.
- **Sublobar (segmental) resection** if stage 1 and tumour ≤3 cm.
- Approach: VATS (~85%) preferred over open thoracotomy.
- Why LN sampling: stages pathologically + reduces recurrence risk.

### Q: When is SABR used instead of surgery?

- Patient unfit for resection (poor lung function, comorbidity, frailty).
- Patient preference for non-invasive treatment.
- Same intent as surgery: **curative**.
- Slightly worse outcomes — probably because nodes aren't sampled.

### Q: What is SABR and how does it differ from conventional radiotherapy?

- **Stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy.**
- Multiple convergent beams from different angles → high dose at tumour, sparing surrounding lung.
- Daily for ~2 weeks.
- Higher precision and higher per-fraction dose than conventional RT.

### Q: What's the metastatic NSCLC treatment decision tree?

```
Metastatic NSCLC
      │
      ▼
Targetable mutation? (EGFR/ALK/ROS1)
   │              │
  YES            NO
   │              │
   ▼              ▼
  TKI         PD-L1 ≥ 50%? ──YES─→ Immunotherapy alone
              │                    (pembro/nivo/atezo)
              NO
              │
              ▼
              Chemo + immunotherapy
```

### Q: Which TKI matches each driver mutation?

- **EGFR** → osimertinib (first-line); also erlotinib, gefitinib, afatinib, dacomitinib.
- **ALK** → alectinib, crizotinib, ceritinib, brigatinib, lorlatinib.
- **ROS1** → crizotinib, entrectinib.
- All end in **-ib** → oral tablets, generally well-tolerated.

### Q: How does immunotherapy work in lung cancer in one sentence?

- Tumours express **PD-L1**, which binds **PD-1** on T cells and switches them off.
- Anti-PD-1 (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) or anti-PD-L1 (atezolizumab) blocks this "off switch".
- Patient's own T cells then kill the tumour.

📎 Figure L1.17 — PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint and immunotherapy mechanism

### Q: What did KEYNOTE-024 show for first-line pembrolizumab in PD-L1 ≥50% NSCLC?

- **PFS 10 vs 6 months** vs chemo.
- **OS >30 vs 14 months** — and a plateau.
- **32% alive at 5 years** in a historically ~5% disease.
- Established immunotherapy alone as first-line in PD-L1-high tumours.

📎 Figure L1.18 — KEYNOTE-024 progression-free survival (pembrolizumab vs chemotherapy)

### Q: How do you spot an immune-related adverse event on a stem?

- Patient on **-mab** drug (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab).
- New autoimmune-like organ dysfunction: **thyroid, colitis, pneumonitis, hepatitis, dermatitis**.
- Mechanism: T cells unleashed against tumour also attack self.
- Management: **stop drug, give corticosteroids**, escalate (e.g. infliximab) if refractory.

### Q: What's the principle behind adjuvant osimertinib?

- Stage **IB–IIIA** NSCLC after **complete resection**.
- Tumour must have **EGFR exon 19 deletion** OR **exon 21 L858R**.
- Oral osimertinib for **up to 3 years**.
- Reduces recurrence, improves disease-free survival vs placebo.

### Q: What's the principle behind neoadjuvant chemotherapy + nivolumab?

- Resectable NSCLC with tumour ≥4 cm OR node-positive.
- **No EGFR mutation.**
- Nivolumab + platinum-based chemo **before** surgery.
- Improves event-free survival; cost-effective per NICE.

### Q: Why is early palliative care evidence-based, not just "nice to have"?

- Temel et al. NEJM 2010: 151 metastatic NSCLC patients, USA.
- Standard oncology ± early palliative care.
- At 12 weeks: improved QoL, lower depression, lower anxiety.
- **Median survival 11.6 vs 8.9 months** — palliative care added survival.

📎 Figure L1.19 — Temel et al. 2010: early palliative care reduces mood symptoms and improves survival

⭐ **Carry forward: early palliative care = longer + better life.** Likely mechanism: better symptom control, fewer infections, better nutrition, clearer care decisions.

## Cross-cutting reasoning patterns

🧠 **The unifying meta-pattern across all lung cancer SBAs: walk every stem through three questions in order — *what subtype/mutation does the demographic suggest? · where am I on the diagnostic tree? · what does the treatment cascade say?*** Drag those three through any case and you'll handle most distractors without recall.

**Pattern 1 — Demographic fingerprint → histology/mutation.**

- Never-smoker, woman, Asian, peripheral → **Adenocarcinoma + EGFR**.
- Young, never-smoker, peripheral → **Adenocarcinoma + ALK or ROS1**.
- Heavy smoker, central, cavitating, ↑Ca²⁺ → **Squamous**.
- Heavy smoker, central, ↓Na⁺ or Cushing's → **Small cell**.

**Pattern 2 — Drug name suffix decodes the class.**

- **-ib** → oral tyrosine kinase inhibitor (mutation-directed): osimertinib, alectinib, crizotinib.
- **-mab** → IV monoclonal antibody (immunotherapy): pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab.
- **Platinum + adjunct** (carboplatin/cisplatin + pemetrexed/paclitaxel) → cytotoxic chemo.

**Pattern 3 — "Next investigation" walks one step right on the diagnostic tree.**

- Symptoms only → CXR.
- Suspicious CXR → CT chest+abdo.
- CT shows mass, fit patient → PET-CT.
- PET shows mediastinal disease → EBUS-TBNA.
- PET shows peripheral lesion only → CT-guided biopsy.
- Brain mets clinically suspected → **MRI brain** (PET unreliable here).

⚠️ **Trap:** Don't skip steps. If the stem already gave you "CT shows mass", PET-CT is next, not biopsy.

## Interleaved SBA block

### Q: SBA — A 62-year-old woman, never-smoker of Chinese heritage, has a 3 cm peripheral RLL mass. Biopsy: adenocarcinoma. Two small liver lesions on CT. Which next investigation most directly determines her first-line treatment?

**Answer: Molecular testing + PD-L1 IHC of the biopsy.**
- She's M1 already; treatment choice now depends on mutation status, then PD-L1.
- PET-CT wrong: doesn't change M1 status.
- Bronchoscopy wrong: tissue already obtained.
- PD-L1 alone wrong: mutation testing has hierarchy over PD-L1.

### Q: SBA — A 70-year-old smoker has facial swelling worse on bending forward, distended neck veins, and a 5 cm right paratracheal mass. Most likely subtype + immediate priority?

**Answer: Squamous (or small cell); urgent admission for SVCO management.**
- Central + heavy smoker → squamous most likely.
- SVCO is an oncological emergency — steroids, SVC stenting, oncology input.
- Outpatient PET-CT or elective referral wrong: misses the emergency.
- Mesothelioma wrong: pleural-based, not paratracheal.

### Q: SBA — A 60-year-old smoker has a cavitating central mass, haemoptysis, and corrected calcium 3.4 mmol/L. Most likely subtype?

**Answer: Squamous cell carcinoma.**
- Central + cavitating + hyperCa²⁺ (PTHrP) = textbook squamous.
- Adeno wrong: peripheral, doesn't classically secrete PTHrP.
- Small cell wrong: paraneoplastic signature is hypoNa⁺ or Cushing's, not hyperCa²⁺.
- Mesothelioma wrong: pleural with effusion, not central cavitating mass.

### Q: SBA — A 68-year-old smoker has a 4 cm hilar mass + enlarged mediastinal nodes on CT. Single most appropriate next investigation?

**Answer: EBUS-TBNA.**
- Samples mediastinal nodes AND stages mediastinum in one go.
- CT-guided wrong: central mass, too vascular, doesn't sample nodes.
- Bronchoscopy alone wrong: doesn't biopsy inside lymph nodes.
- PET-CT wrong: complementary, but doesn't give tissue.

### Q: SBA — A 64-year-old man on osimertinib develops 3 weeks of dry cough and bilateral ground-glass on CT. SpO₂ 92% on air. Next step?

**Answer: Stop osimertinib and start corticosteroids.**
- New dyspnoea + ground-glass on TKI = **drug-induced pneumonitis** until proven otherwise.
- Antibiotics wrong: pattern fits pneumonitis, not bacterial pneumonia.
- Switch to chemo wrong: dangerous before resolving the acute issue.
- Pembrolizumab wrong: also causes pneumonitis, would worsen things.

### Q: SBA — A 72-year-old man on pembrolizumab has 5 days of profuse watery diarrhoea, cultures negative. Most appropriate management?

**Answer: Stop pembrolizumab and start oral corticosteroids.**
- Picture fits **immune-related colitis**.
- Loperamide wrong: masks an inflammatory process.
- Antibiotics wrong: cultures negative, no infection picture.
- Continue + ondansetron wrong: continuing the drug worsens immune toxicity.

### Q: SBA — A 75-year-old woman with metastatic NSCLC, no mutation, PD-L1 75%, PS 1. First-line systemic treatment?

**Answer: Pembrolizumab monotherapy.**
- No mutation + PD-L1 ≥50% + fit → IO alone (KEYNOTE-024).
- Chemo alone wrong: misses big IO benefit.
- Osimertinib wrong: no EGFR mutation.
- Chemo + pembro wrong: that regimen is for PD-L1 <50%.

### Q: SBA — A 58-year-old man with stage IIIA NSCLC has lobectomy with clear margins. Tumour shows EGFR exon 19 deletion. Best adjuvant therapy?

**Answer: Adjuvant osimertinib for up to 3 years.**
- EGFR exon 19 del or L858R + complete resection of stage IB–IIIA → osimertinib.
- Surveillance only wrong: misses the targeted-therapy benefit.
- Adjuvant chemo only wrong: inferior to TKI in EGFR-mutant disease.
- Adjuvant pembrolizumab wrong: not the EGFR-mutant pathway.

### Q: SBA — A 78-year-old with COPD (FEV1 35%) has a 2.5 cm peripheral N0M0 NSCLC and is PS 2. Best curative treatment?

**Answer: SABR.**
- Early-stage + unfit for surgery → SABR is the radical alternative.
- Lobectomy/pneumonectomy wrong: lung function won't tolerate.
- Wedge wrong: diagnostic, not curative.
- Palliative chemo wrong: this is curable disease.

### Q: SBA — A 64-year-old on osimertinib develops headaches and left arm weakness over 3 weeks. Most appropriate investigation?

**Answer: MRI brain.**
- Focal neurological signs + metastatic cancer → brain mets until proven otherwise.
- MRI more sensitive than CT for small lesions.
- PET-CT wrong: cortex takes up FDG physiologically.
- LP wrong: dangerous without imaging first.
- EEG wrong: characterises seizures, doesn't localise mass lesions.

### Q: SBA — A 58-year-old has a 7 cm RLL mass invading chest wall + two ipsilateral mediastinal stations + no distant disease. Stage and approach?

**Answer: cT4N2bM0, Stage IIIB — radical chemoradiotherapy with consolidation immunotherapy.**
- 7 cm + chest wall invasion = T4.
- Two mediastinal stations = N2b → not surgical.
- Radical chemoRT + durvalumab consolidation is standard.
- N2a (single station) wrong: this has two stations.
- N3 wrong: ipsilateral, not contralateral or supraclavicular.

### Q: SBA — A 71-year-old has a 2.8 cm LUL mass, ipsilateral hilar node, three right adrenal lesions, two liver lesions, no other disease. M stage?

**Answer: M1c2.**
- Multiple mets across two organ systems (adrenal + liver) → M1c2.
- M1a wrong: that's intrathoracic spread.
- M1b wrong: single extrathoracic met.
- M1c1 wrong: multiple mets in **one** organ only.

### Q: SBA — A 69-year-old man is independently self-caring but housebound and spends most of his time sitting because of breathlessness. WHO PS?

**Answer: Grade 3.**
- ">50% time sitting/bed" → PS 3.
- PS 2 wrong: that's >50% up and about; he's the opposite.
- PS 4 wrong: he's still self-caring.
- The 50% threshold beats the self-care detail.

### Q: SBA — Which feature most strongly raises suspicion of small cell carcinoma in a newly diagnosed lung cancer?

**Answer: Central mass + sodium 119 + Cushingoid features.**
- Hyponatraemia (SIADH) + ectopic Cushing's = small cell paraneoplastic signature.
- Cavitation + hyperCa²⁺ wrong: that's squamous.
- Peripheral + EGFR wrong: that's adeno.
- Apical + Horner's wrong: Pancoast tumour, usually NSCLC.
- Pleural + asbestos wrong: mesothelioma.

### Q: SBA — Compared to standard oncology care alone, what did Temel et al. (2010) show with early palliative care?

**Answer: Better QoL, lower depression scores, AND longer median survival (11.6 vs 8.9 months).**
- The headline finding: palliative care extends survival, not just comfort.
- Reduced cost wrong: not the main finding.
- Earlier hospice wrong: not the trial outcome.
- Increased chemo uptake wrong: opposite, if anything.
