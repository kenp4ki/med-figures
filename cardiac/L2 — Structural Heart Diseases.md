## Territory map

### Q: What are the 6 structural diseases in this lecture, grouped?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Valvular (4):** AS, AR, MS, MR
**Muscular / cardiomyopathies (2):** DCM, HCM

Congenital defects (ASD, VSD, coarctation, PFO, PDA, TOF) are mentioned but not taught in depth — background only.

</details>

### Q: The core intuition linking all 4 valve lesions.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟦 🧠 **Which door, going which way, stuck how?**

- **Stenosis** = stuck shut → forward flow obstructed → chamber BEHIND hypertrophies (pressure overload)
- **Regurgitation** = stuck open → backward leak → chamber IN FRONT dilates (volume overload)

| Lesion | Door | Direction | Predicts |
|---|---|---|---|
| AS | Aortic | Forward blocked | LV concentric hypertrophy; systolic murmur |
| AR | Aortic | Back leak | LV eccentric + dilation; diastolic murmur |
| MS | Mitral | Forward blocked | LA dilation → RV; diastolic murmur |
| MR | Mitral | Back leak | LA + LV dilation; systolic murmur |

</details>

📎 Figure L2.2 — Stenosis vs regurgitation schematic.

## Card 1 — Aortic Stenosis (AS)

### Q: Core definition of AS + severity cut-offs by valve area.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- Failure of aortic valve to open fully → LV outflow obstruction
- **Normal** 3.0–5.0 cm²; **Mild** >1.5; **Moderate** 1.1–1.5; **Severe ≤1.0 cm²**
- **Most common valvular disease requiring treatment** in US/Europe
- 2nd most frequent cause of cardiac surgery
- Preceded by **aortic sclerosis** (thickening without flow limitation)

</details>

### Q: AS aetiology and risk factors.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Causes:** rheumatic heart disease, congenital heart disease, calcium build-up.

**Risk factors:** HTN, ↑LDL, smoking, ↑CRP, **congenital bicuspid AV**, CKD, radiotherapy, older age (>70).

🟥 ⚠️ "Bicuspid" = bicuspid **aortic** valve, a congenital cause of early AS. Not the tricuspid valve.

</details>

### Q: AS pathophysiology cascade.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

```
Degeneration / bicuspid        Untreated Group A Strep URT
       │                                  │
  wear & tear                  Anti-strep Abs attack valve
       │                                  │
       └──────────┬───────────────────────┘
                  ▼
      Fibrosis + calcification of aortic valve
                  │
                  ▼
         LV contracts harder to eject
                  │
                  ▼
      CONCENTRIC LV hypertrophy (pressure overload)
                  │
                  ▼
     Stiff LV → diastolic dysfunction → ↓ CO
                  │
                  ▼
    Backflow of pressure to LA → pulmonary congestion
```

</details>

📎 Figure L2.6 — AS pathophysiology + valve morphology panel.

### Q: AS clinical findings — the classic triad and the signature murmur.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟨 ⭐ Classic triad: **syncope, angina, dyspnoea — on exertion**.

Signature murmur: **ejection systolic, crescendo-decrescendo**, between S1 and S2, radiates to **carotids**.

Other signs: slow-rising pulse, soft/absent S2, **S4** (stiff LV), diffuse crackles if pulmonary congestion.

</details>

### Q: Diagnosis of AS — first-line test and why.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟨 ⭐ **Doppler echocardiography — gold standard.**

Why Doppler specifically: calculates **pressure gradient** across the valve (how much pressure LV is generating above aorta). High gradient = severe stenosis. Also visualises leaflet thickening.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.8 — AS parasternal long-axis echo with thickened cusps.

### Q: AS definitive management + valve choice reasoning.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **AVR for severe AS** — TAVR (transcatheter, balloon-deployed via groin) or surgical prosthesis
- **Mechanical valve** → lasts longer → **younger patients** (needs lifelong anticoagulation)
- **Bioprosthetic valve** → shorter lifespan → **older patients** (no anticoag; matches remaining lifespan)
- Symptomatic adjuncts: antihypertensives, antiarrhythmics, diuretics

</details>

## Card 2 — Aortic Regurgitation (AR)

### Q: Core definition of AR + acute vs chronic.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Diastolic leakage** of blood from aorta back into LV due to incompetent leaflets
- Causes: intrinsic valve disease OR aortic root dilation
- **Acute AR = medical emergency** (pulmonary oedema + hypotension / cardiogenic shock)
- **Chronic AR** → culminates in congestive heart failure

</details>

### Q: AR aetiology — the two-column split.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Valve causes (congenital/acquired):** rheumatic heart disease, **infective endocarditis**, AS, congenital heart defects, **congenital bicuspid valves**.

**Aortic root dilation causes:** **Marfan's syndrome**, connective tissue disease, **ankylosing spondylitis**, idiopathic, syphilis, traumatic.

🟨 ⭐ Marfan's + AnkSpond = root dilation → AR classic exam associations.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.11 — AR aetiology morphologies (bicuspid, IE, rheumatic, Marfanoid, senile calcific).

### Q: AR pathophysiology — concentric or eccentric LVH, and why?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Eccentric** LVH + LV **dilation** (volume overload).

- Leaflets close poorly → aortic→LV backflow during diastole
- **Acute:** LV can't dilate in time → Frank-Starling ↑SV briefly → rapid failure
- **Chronic:** LV dilates + eccentrically hypertrophies → eventually excessive stretch weakens myocardium → **systolic heart failure**

🟦 🧠 Contrast with AS: AS is pressure overload → concentric. AR is volume overload → eccentric + dilation.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.12 — AR pathophysiology cascade + jet lesion on septal wall.

### Q: AR clinical findings — the 3 named signs.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Early-diastolic decrescendo** murmur at left sternal edge
- **Wide pulse pressure** (e.g. 170/50)
- **Corrigan / water-hammer pulse** — strong systolic upstroke, rapid diastolic collapse
- Plus **S3 gallop** (rapid LV filling)

🟨 ⭐ Corrigan pulse + wide PP = chronic AR signature pair.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.13 — AR diastolic murmur + Corrigan pulse.

### Q: AR — acute presentation vs chronic presentation.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Acute AR** — cardiogenic shock, tachycardia, cyanosis, pulmonary oedema, diastolic murmur.

**Chronic AR** — wide pulse pressure, Corrigan pulse, S3, angina on exertion, fatigue, eventual CHF.

</details>

### Q: AR diagnosis and management.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Diagnosis:** echocardiography = best non-invasive test to diagnose and grade severity.

**Management:**
- **Acute AR = emergency** → treat underlying cause (rheumatic fever, IE) + urgent AVR
- **Chronic asymptomatic severe** → **vasodilator therapy** improves haemodynamics and delays AVR
- **Chronic symptomatic** → AVR
- **Prevention:** treat rheumatic fever + IE promptly

</details>

## Card 3 — Mitral Stenosis (MS)

### Q: Core definition of MS + dominant cause worldwide.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- Obstruction to LV inflow at the mitral valve due to structural abnormality
- **Rheumatic fever = main cause, especially in developing countries**
- As disease progresses → **pulmonary hypertension + right-sided heart failure**

</details>

### Q: MS pathophysiology cascade.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

```
Rheumatic fever (most common) / IE / calcific / congenital
                       │
                       ▼
            Recurrent inflammation
                       │
                       ▼
  Fibrous deposition + calcification of leaflets AND chordae
                       │
                       ▼
     Thickening/shortening → FUSION of leaflets
                       │
                       ▼
       ↓ orifice area → obstructed LA→LV flow
            │                          │
            ▼                          ▼
   Impaired LA emptying         Back pressure →
   → ↓ LV filling               pulmonary HTN
   → ↓ SV, ↓ CO                 → RV hypertrophy
   → CHF                        → RIGHT-sided HF
```

</details>

📎 Figure L2.16 — MS pathophysiology + pathological MV specimen.

### Q: MS clinical findings — the 3 signature features.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Loud S1 + opening snap** (stiff MV snapping open) + **mid-diastolic rumble**
- **Atrial fibrillation** — stretched LA; mandatory anticoagulation
- **Right-sided heart failure signs** late

Other: dyspnoea, **haemoptysis** (pulmonary vein rupture), **dysphagia** (dilated LA compresses oesophagus).

🟥 ⚠️ **Dysphagia in MS** is easy-to-miss but testable.

</details>

### Q: MS — why does β-blocker help? And why is this a contrast with AS?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟦 🧠 MS is a **diastolic** flow problem. Slower HR → longer diastole → more time to push blood across narrow MV → more filling of LV.

In **AS**, obstruction is in systole, so lengthening diastole doesn't help; β-blockers risk dropping CO without benefit.

See also: L1 — cardiac cycle phase durations.

</details>

### Q: MS diagnosis and staged management.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Diagnosis:** ECG + CXR + **TTE** (TTE for valve + flow; ECG for AF).

**Management — staged:**
- Progressive asymptomatic → no therapy
- Severe asymptomatic → no therapy ± adjuvant **balloon valvotomy**
- Severe symptomatic → **diuretics, balloon valvotomy, valve replacement/repair, β-blockers** + anticoag for AF

🟨 ⭐ Balloon valvotomy is MS-specific — works because MS is a *fusion* problem, not destruction.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.19 — Inoue balloon valvotomy.

## Card 4 — Mitral Regurgitation (MR)

### Q: Core definition of MR + epidemiology.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- Abnormal LV→LA backflow due to disruption in any part of the MV apparatus
- **Most frequent valvular heart disease overall**

🟥 ⚠️ Trap: MR = most frequent overall; **AS = most common requiring treatment**. Both are true; stem wording selects.

</details>

### Q: MR aetiology — acute vs chronic causes.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Acute:** mitral valve prolapse, **papillary muscle rupture (post-MI)**, IE, rheumatic, post-valvular surgery, prosthetic valve dysfunction.

**Chronic:** rheumatic, **SLE**, scleroderma, **hypertrophic cardiomyopathy** (SAM), drug-related.

🟨 ⭐ HCM causes functional MR via SAM — see L3 Activity 2.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.21 — MR aetiology 4-panel (MVP, ruptured chord, annulus dilation).

### Q: MR pathophysiology.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

```
Multiple triggers (CAD, MI, MVP, IE, CTD, rheumatic, HCM)
                        │
                        ▼
    LV→LA backflow in systole (impaired valve closure)
                        │
                        ▼
             ↑ LA volume + pressure
                        │
                        ▼
    Extra volume pushed into LV next diastole
                        │
                        ▼
     LV dilates (remodelling) → ↓ systolic function
            │                       │
            ▼                       ▼
      ↓ SV, ↓ CO               Back pressure to LA
      → CHF                    → pulmonary congestion
```

</details>

📎 Figure L2.27? No — correct reference: MR pathophys is inline on slide p27 (no isolated figure). See 📎 Figure L2.20 for schematic + L2.21 for aetiology panel.

### Q: MR clinical findings — the signature murmur and signs.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Holosystolic (pansystolic) murmur radiating to axilla**
- **S3** heart sound
- Signs of CHF: ↓O₂ sat, tachypnoea, wheeze, crackles, frothy sputum
- History: dyspnoea, CHF symptoms

🟥 ⚠️ In **acute severe MR**, the murmur may be **soft or absent** — LA pressure rises so fast the gradient collapses. Don't miss post-MI acute MR because the murmur is quiet.

</details>

### Q: MR diagnosis — which imaging adds special value and why?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**First-line:** TTE + ECG + CXR.

🟨 ⭐ **Cardiac MRI (CMR) is superior for MR** — quantifies regurgitant volume, leaflet morphology, papillary muscle, and LA/LV remodelling impact. Predicts prognosis.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.23 — MR colour Doppler regurgitant jet.

### Q: MR management by acuity.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Acute severe MR** → **urgent surgery** (repair or replace; prosthetic ring to reshape annulus)
- **Chronic severe asymptomatic** → watchful waiting or surgery
- **Chronic symptomatic** → **surgery first** + medical treatment

🟦 Repair > replace where anatomy allows.

</details>

## Card 5 — Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)

### Q: Core definition of DCM + prevalence.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- Dilated chambers + thin walls + reduced contractility
- **Systolic dysfunction** (↓ EF)
- Prevalence **1:250**
- Typically presents 3rd–4th decade
- One of commonest causes of heart failure; usually progressive and irreversible

</details>

### Q: DCM causes — the examinable list.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Idiopathic** (real category)
- Genetic (~1/3)
- **Alcohol** (often biventricular)
- Cardiotoxic **chemotherapy** (anthracyclines)
- **Pregnancy — peripartum cardiomyopathy**
- Viral (myocarditis)
- Tachycardia-related (may be reversible)

(Also: thyroid disease, muscular dystrophies, autoimmune)

</details>

### Q: DCM pathophysiology — the final common pathway.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Any trigger → myocardial injury (inflammation, oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, haemodynamic overload) → **remodelling + fibrosis** → LV can't pump: ↓ EF, ↓ SV, ↓ CO.

Frank-Starling compensates early (rescue) but chronic stretch worsens HF (enemy). Progressive enlargement → secondary MR/TR from annular dilation → arrhythmias, HF, SCD.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.27 — DCM pathophysiology + management schematic.

### Q: DCM diagnostic criteria on echo.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **LVEDV or diameter >2 SD above normal** (age/BSA-corrected)
- **EF <50%**
- Global hypokinesis
- Often spherical shape

See also: L3 — DCM case.

</details>

### Q: DCM primary treatment goal + first-line drug (2023).

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟨 ⭐ ESC 2023: **SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) Class I first-line** — before ACEi unless contraindicated.

Full HF ladder: SGLT2i → ACEi/ARNI/ARB → β-blocker → MRA → diuretic. Devices: CRT-P, CRT-ICD, LVAD as bridge, transplant.

See also: L3 — DCM management (ESC 2023 detail).

</details>

## Card 6 — Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)

### Q: Core definition of HCM + prevalence + inheritance.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Genetic disease** — increased LV wall thickness **not solely explained by abnormal loading conditions**
- Prevalence **1:500**
- **Autosomal dominant** (most cases)
- **Leading cause of SCD in preadolescents/adolescents**

🟥 ⚠️ The phrase "not solely explained by abnormal loading conditions" distinguishes HCM from LVH caused by AS or HTN.

</details>

### Q: HCM diagnostic echo thresholds.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Adults:** LV wall thickness **≥15 mm** in ≥1 myocardial segment in diastole.

🟥 ⚠️ Lowered to **≥13 mm** if:
- Family history
- LVOTO
- ECG abnormalities

**Children:** >2 SD above BSA-predicted mean.

See also: L3 — HCM diagnostics.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.26 — Echo comparison normal / DCM / HCM.

### Q: HCM pathophysiology — the 3-channel model.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Genetic mutations → 3 converging consequences:
1. **Sarcomere hypercontractility** → ventricular hypertrophy
2. **↑ Calcium sensitivity** → diastolic dysfunction
3. **Microvascular dysfunction** → ischaemia, necrosis, inflammation, fibrosis

All converge on: arrhythmias + HF + risk of SCD. Plus MV impairment → MR.

</details>

📎 Figure L2.28 — HCM pathophysiology + management.

### Q: Obstructive vs non-obstructive HCM.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Obstructive (LVOTO)** — septal hypertrophy narrows LV outflow tract; **SAM** of anterior MV leaflet worsens obstruction + causes functional MR
- Obstructive triad: **dyspnoea, angina, syncope**
- **Non-obstructive** — hypertrophy without outflow obstruction; dyspnoea + angina; still diastolic dysfunction and arrhythmic risk

See also: L3 — LVOTO + SAM detail.

</details>

### Q: HCM drug rules — what to give, what NOT to give.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Give:**
- **Non-vasodilating β-blockers** (first-line)
- **Non-DHP CCBs** (verapamil, diltiazem)
- Disopyramide; myosin inhibitors (mavacamten)

🟥 ⚠️ **Avoid pure vasodilators** (including standard ACEi in obstructive phase, DHP CCBs, nitrates) — they reduce afterload → smaller LV cavity → **worse LVOTO**.

ACEi/ARB/MRA reserved for late "hypokinetic" HCM with reduced EF.

</details>

### Q: Structural interventions for obstructive HCM refractory to medical therapy.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Septal reduction therapy:**
- **Surgical myectomy** — remove thickened septum
- **Alcohol septal ablation (PTSMA)** — controlled necrosis via septal LAD branch

Plus: ICD for SCD risk, heart transplant in severe cases.

</details>

### Q: AF in HCM — the anticoagulation rule.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟥 ⚠️ **Anticoagulate regardless of CHA₂DS₂-VASc score.**

Differs from standard AF. Any AF in HCM = anticoagulate.

See also: L3 — HCM management.

</details>

## Cross-cutting patterns (across the 4 valves)

### Q: "Most common" valve disease — resolve the trap.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟥 ⚠️ Two correct but different answers:
- **MR = most frequent valvular disease overall**
- **AS = most common valvular disease requiring treatment in US/Europe**

Stem wording selects.

</details>

### Q: LV hypertrophy patterns — 4-way split.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

| Pattern | Disease | Driver |
|---|---|---|
| **Concentric** | AS, chronic HTN | Pressure overload |
| **Eccentric + dilation** | Chronic AR, chronic MR | Volume overload |
| **Asymmetric (septal)** | HCM | Sarcomere mutation |
| **Dilated + thinned, spherical** | DCM | Myocardial injury |

Stem → remodelling match → diagnosis. One of the fastest MCQ heuristics.

</details>

### Q: Murmur radiation — how does it discriminate two systolic murmurs?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **AS** radiates to **carotids** (blood heading upward to aorta)
- **MR** radiates to **axilla** (regurgitant jet heads back to posterior LA, near axilla)

Plus: dynamic behaviour (Valsalva) discriminates AS (softer) vs HOCM (louder).

</details>

### Q: Rheumatic fever — which valve lesions does it cause?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**All four** — AS, AR, MS, MR.

🟨 ⭐ **MS is the signature** — rheumatic is the dominant worldwide cause.

Prevention = treat Group A Strep URTI promptly + treat established rheumatic fever aggressively.

</details>

## Interleaved SBA block (discrimination)

### Q: SBA — A 68-year-old woman, long rheumatic heart history, now dyspnoea + palpitations. ECG shows AF. What additional finding is most likely?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Loud S1 + opening snap** — classic MS.

Not carotid-radiating systolic murmur (AS), not wide PP / collapsing pulse (AR), not axilla-radiating holosystolic (MR), not S4 (AS/HCM).

</details>

### Q: SBA — 45-year-old with Marfan's, BP 160/50, bounding pulse, early-diastolic decrescendo murmur. Mechanism?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Aortic root dilation causing leaflet non-coaptation** — Marfan phenotype + wide PP + diastolic murmur = AR.

</details>

### Q: SBA — 75-year-old, frail, severe AS, life expectancy 5–7 years. Best valve approach?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**TAVR with bioprosthetic valve.**

- TAVR → less invasive, suits frail/elderly
- Bioprosthetic → no lifelong anticoag; lifespan matches remaining life
- Mechanical is for younger patients

</details>

### Q: SBA — 58-year-old, 4 days post-MI, sudden dyspnoea, pink frothy sputum, BP 82/54, soft apical systolic murmur. Mechanism?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Papillary muscle rupture → acute severe MR.**

🟥 ⚠️ Murmur is **soft** because LA pressure rises rapidly, collapsing the gradient. Don't miss acute MR because the murmur is quiet.

</details>

### Q: SBA — 30-year-old tall man, systolic murmur that becomes LOUDER on Valsalva. Most likely?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**HOCM.**

🟦 🧠 Valsalva ↓ preload → smaller LV → worse LVOTO → louder murmur. AS murmur gets softer with Valsalva (less preload to eject). Dynamic behaviour is the bedside discriminator.

</details>

### Q: SBA — 36-year-old man, newly diagnosed non-obstructive HCM (IVS 20 mm), one episode of non-sustained VT on Holter. Long-term plan?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Non-vasodilating β-blocker + ICD for primary prevention of SCD + cascade genetic screening of first-degree relatives.**

Not ACEi (that's DCM). Not alcohol septal ablation (non-obstructive). Not MV replacement (no MR here).

</details>

## Reusable reasoning pattern

### Q: Stem → diagnosis → management framework for any valve lesion.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟦 🧠 Sequence:
1. Which valve, which direction, stuck how?
2. Murmur timing + character + radiation → shortlist
3. Dynamic signs + pulse character + S3/S4 → narrow
4. Echo ± CMR confirms
5. Intervention: stenosis → replace; regurg → repair if possible; acute regurg → emergency surgery

See also: L1 — murmur timing; bundle overview — cross-lecture synthesis.

</details>
