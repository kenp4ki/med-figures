## The core intuition

### Q: The one sentence that makes the whole lecture derivable.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The heart is a **timing machine with four one-way doors** — two upstairs (AV: mitral, tricuspid), two downstairs (SL: aortic, pulmonary). In any phase, exactly one pair is open and the other is shut. Every sound, murmur, and sign is a statement about **which door is misbehaving and when**.

</details>

## Cardiac cycle — phases

### Q: In systole, which valves are open vs shut, and what is the ventricle doing?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Downstairs (SL) valves OPEN** — blood ejected to aorta/pulmonary artery
- **Upstairs (AV) valves SHUT**
- Ventricle contracts → ejects

</details>

### Q: In diastole, which valves are open vs shut, and what is the ventricle doing?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Upstairs (AV) valves OPEN** — ventricle fills from atrium
- **Downstairs (SL) valves SHUT**
- Ventricle relaxes → fills

</details>

### Q: Name the 3 subphases of systole in order.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

1. **Isovolumetric contraction** — all 4 valves shut, pressure rises, no volume change
2. **Rapid ejection** — SL valves open; blood shoots out
3. **Reduced ejection** — flow slows; SL valves slam shut → S2

</details>

### Q: Name the 3 subphases of diastole in order.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

1. **Isovolumetric relaxation** — all 4 valves shut, pressure falls, no volume change
2. **Rapid filling** — AV valves snap open; passive fill
3. **Reduced filling** — flow slows; ends with atrial contraction ("atrial kick")

</details>

### Q: Why does tachycardia worsen aortic stenosis and mitral stenosis specifically?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- At rest, ~35% of cycle is systole, ~65% diastole
- **Diastole shortens disproportionately** when HR rises
- AS and MS both depend on time to push blood through narrowed orifices → tachycardia reduces that time → decompensation

🟦 Reusable: β-blockers help MS because they lengthen diastole.

</details>

### Q: Define LVEDV, LVESV, stroke volume, ejection fraction.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **LVEDV** — LV volume at end of diastole (preload)
- **LVESV** — LV volume at end of systole (residual)
- **SV = LVEDV − LVESV**
- **EF = SV / LVEDV** — normal ≥55%; <50% = systolic dysfunction

</details>

## Heart sounds

### Q: What produces S1 and S2, and when do they occur?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **S1** — mitral + tricuspid (AV) valves **close** — start of systole
- **S2** — aortic + pulmonary (SL) valves **close** — start of diastole
- Sounds come from valves **closing**, not opening

</details>

### Q: S3 — when does it occur and what does it mean?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- Low-frequency, **early diastole**
- Produced by **rapid passive filling** of a floppy/volume-overloaded ventricle
- Most common pathological cause = **congestive cardiac failure**
- Can be physiological in children/athletes

</details>

### Q: S4 — when does it occur and what is the mandatory rule?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- Low-frequency, **late diastole** (just before S1)
- Forceful atrial contraction into a **stiff** ventricle

🟨 ⭐ **S4 is ALWAYS pathological** — slide red-font emphasis.

Causes: **aortic stenosis**, systemic hypertension, **hypertrophic cardiomyopathy**, ischaemia.

</details>

### Q: Floppy vs stiff — which sound goes with which?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟦 🧠 **Floppy ventricle → S3** (too much volume filling fast): CHF, AR, MR.

**Stiff ventricle → S4** (atrium slams blood in hard): AS, HCM, HTN, ischaemia.

Own this split — it predicts which sound goes with which disease.

</details>

## Murmur timing — the bridge to L2

### Q: A murmur between S1 and S2 (systolic) — which valves are candidates?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Two mechanisms:
- **SL valve won't OPEN** (stenosis, forward blocked) → **AS** (or PS)
- **AV valve won't CLOSE** (regurg, backward leak in systole) → **MR** (or TR)

Systolic murmur shortlist = **AS, MR**.

</details>

### Q: A murmur between S2 and next S1 (diastolic) — which valves are candidates?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Two mechanisms:
- **SL valve won't CLOSE** (regurg, backward leak in diastole) → **AR** (or PR)
- **AV valve won't OPEN** properly (stenosis, inflow blocked) → **MS** (or TS)

Diastolic murmur shortlist = **AR, MS**.

</details>

### Q: Map the 4 main valve lesions to murmur timing and character.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

| Lesion | Timing | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **AS** | Systolic | Ejection, crescendo-decrescendo |
| **MR** | Systolic | Holosystolic (pansystolic) |
| **AR** | Diastolic | Early, decrescendo |
| **MS** | Diastolic | Mid-diastolic, opening snap + loud S1 |

</details>

## Anatomy (recognition only)

### Q: Name the 4 valves and which chambers they sit between.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Tricuspid** — RA → RV
- **Pulmonary (SL)** — RV → pulmonary artery
- **Mitral / bicuspid** — LA → LV
- **Aortic (SL)** — LV → aorta

</details>

### Q: What is the "tricuspid aortic valve" and why is the terminology a trap?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟥 ⚠️ The **aortic valve has 3 cusps** — so it is anatomically a "tricuspid" valve. This is DIFFERENT from **the tricuspid valve itself** (RA→RV, also 3 cusps). A **bicuspid aortic valve** is a congenital defect that accelerates AS.

Exam trap: stem says "bicuspid valve" → means bicuspid **aortic** valve unless specified.

</details>

### Q: What are the chordae tendineae and papillary muscles, and why do they matter clinically?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

- **Chordae tendineae** — tendon-like cords tethering mitral/tricuspid leaflets
- **Papillary muscles** — ventricular projections anchoring the chordae
- **Rupture of papillary muscle post-MI** → flail leaflet → acute severe MR (emergency)

See also: L2 — MR aetiology.

</details>

## Conduction system

### Q: Trace the electrical pathway from pacemaker to ventricular myocardium.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**SA node → AV node → Bundle of His → L + R bundle branches → Purkinje fibres → ventricular myocardium.**

Structural damage anywhere in this path → arrhythmia.

See also: L2 — HCM (myocyte disarray disrupts conduction → VT → SCD).

</details>

## Recall SBAs

### Q: SBA — A 68-year-old woman has a low-frequency cardiac sound just before S1, best appreciated at the apex. BP 168/92. Echo shows concentric LV hypertrophy. This sound most likely is:

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**S4** — late-diastolic, low-frequency, just before S1. Always pathological. Here reflects a stiff hypertrophied LV from long-standing HTN (pressure overload).

Not S3 (early diastolic, floppy ventricle). Not opening snap (after S2, MS).

</details>

### Q: SBA — Heart sounds are produced when:

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Valves close**, causing leaflet vibration + sudden pressure change.

- S1 = AV closure
- S2 = SL closure
- Turbulent flow through a narrowed valve = **murmur**, not a heart sound.

</details>

### Q: SBA — Which manoeuvre most rapidly distinguishes severe AS from HOCM at the bedside?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

**Valsalva**. HOCM murmur gets **louder** (↓ preload → smaller LV → worse LVOTO). AS murmur gets **softer** (↓ preload → less to eject).

🟦 🧠 Dynamic murmur behaviour = the bedside discriminator across this entire bundle.

See also: L2 — AS vs HCM discrimination.

</details>

## Reusable reasoning pattern

### Q: The 1-line derivation to use for every valve lesion in L2.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

🟦 🧠 **Which door, going which way, and is it stuck-open or stuck-shut?**

From that you derive:
- Phase of murmur (systolic vs diastolic)
- Chamber that suffers first
- Remodelling pattern (concentric vs eccentric vs dilation)
- Classic signs

See also: L2 — Valvular quartet core intuition.

</details>
