# Bundle — ECG & ABG: bedside interpretation

> 📘 **How to use this page.** This is the bundle convergence page. L1 and L2 share a meta-skill (systematic bedside interpretation) and a physiological axis (ventilation ↔ cardiovascular function). Use this page to practise **discrimination** and **integration** — i.e. when a stem gives you both an ECG and an ABG, or asks you to link one to the other. Toggle Qs drive recall. Full content of each investigation sits in the per-lecture pages (L1, L2).

## Why bundle these two lectures

### Q: What shape does this bundle take?
- **Hybrid: parallel contrast + shared outcomes.**
- Parallel contrast: two bedside investigations compared on *meta-dimensions* (what they measure, how fast, how interpreted).
- Shared outcomes: both feed into the cardio-respiratory integration skill, and both depend on the ventilation↔CVS axis.
- Not content-parallel — you can't compare "pathophysiology of ECG" with "pathophysiology of ABG." Compare the skills and the physiology.

### Q: What is the one transferable skill this bundle trains?
- **Systematic bedside interpretation**: pre-check → numbered steps → integrate clinical story → double-check abnormal.
- Same habit works on ECG (7 steps), ABG (4 steps), and any future bedside test (CXR, PFT, echo, bloods).

## Comparator — the two investigations side-by-side

### Q: Compare ECG and ABG on 4 meta-dimensions.

| Dimension | ECG (L1) | ABG (L2) |
|---|---|---|
| What is measured | Electrical activity (indirect) | Chemical analytes in blood (direct) |
| Time-course captured | Instantaneous | Snapshot ± compensation over time |
| Output format | Waveform on grid | Numerical values |
| Interpretation algorithm | **7 steps** | **4 steps** |

### Q: What is the "trap pair" — what's the most-tested error each lecture flags?

| Lecture | Trap | Correct reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| L1 | Defibrillate asystole | Non-shockable — CPR + adrenaline |
| L2 | Report pH of 3.5 | Impossible — repeat sample |

Both are "**scrutinise, don't pattern-match**" traps.

### Q: Where do the two lectures meet physiologically?
- **Vagus-ventilation coupling** appears in both:
  - L1: **sinus arrhythmia** (HR varies with breathing; inspiration ↓vagal tone).
  - L2: **rapid respiratory compensation** (ventilation changes CO₂ → pH in minutes).
- Same autonomic-respiratory axis, different monitored output.

### Q: When would a patient have paired ECG + ABG abnormalities?
- **Acute PE** → ECG: sinus tachy / RAD / S1Q3T3. ABG: respiratory alkalosis + hypoxaemia.
- **COPD exacerbation** → ECG: RAD / P pulmonale. ABG: fully compensated respiratory acidosis ± hypoxaemia.
- **Cardiogenic shock post-MI** → ECG: ST elevation in a territory. ABG: metabolic acidosis (lactate from hypoperfusion).
- **Severe asthma** → ECG: sinus tachy. ABG: respiratory alkalosis early → respiratory acidosis late (tiring).

🟦 **Reusable.** Each condition leaves a fingerprint on **both** tests. A stem combining both findings points to the same diagnosis — don't analyse them in isolation.

## Cross-dimensional comparison: pathology by test signature

### Q: Match clinical scenarios to their expected paired ECG + ABG pattern.

| Scenario | ECG finding | ABG finding |
|---|---|---|
| PE (acute) | Sinus tachy, ± RAD | Resp alkalosis + hypoxaemia |
| Chronic COPD | RAD, P pulmonale | Fully compensated resp acidosis |
| DKA | Sinus tachy | Metabolic acidosis, Kussmaul-driven ↓CO₂ |
| Cardiac arrest from VT | VT (broad, regular) | Metabolic acidosis (lactate) |

🟥 **Trap.** Late-stage severe asthma flips from respiratory alkalosis to respiratory acidosis as the patient tires. A "normal" PaCO₂ in an asthmatic with obvious distress is **ominous**, not reassuring.

## Cross-lecture SBAs (interleaved)

### Q: SBA 1 — 45-year-old collapses. ECG: regular broad-complex tachycardia 180 bpm. ABG: pH 7.18, PaCO₂ 3.2, BE −12, PaO₂ 11.5. Which best explains the ABG?
- **A.** VT has caused respiratory failure → respiratory acidosis
- **B. VT → poor output → tissue hypoperfusion → lactic acidosis, partially compensated by hyperventilation** ✓
- **C.** Vomiting → metabolic alkalosis
- **D.** Panic → respiratory alkalosis
- **E.** Spurious result

**Answer: B.** VT at 180 bpm = inadequate filling → low output → anaerobic metabolism → lactate. pH ↓ (acidaemic), BE ↓ (metabolic aetiology), PaCO₂ ↓ (respiratory compensation). Same pathology leaves fingerprints on both tests.

### Q: SBA 2 — 72-year-old, known COPD. ECG: sinus tachy 110, axis +115°. ABG: pH 7.29, PaCO₂ 8.5, BE +4, PaO₂ 7.1. Best integration?
- **A.** ECG normal axis; fully compensated resp acidosis
- **B. RAD consistent with chronic lung disease; partially compensated resp acidosis with moderate hypoxaemia — acute-on-chronic COPD** ✓
- **C.** LAD; metabolic alkalosis
- **D.** Unrelated findings
- **E.** PE

**Answer: B.** Axis +115° = RAD (from RV hypertrophy in cor pulmonale). Elevated BE = chronic kidney compensation was present. Now pH ↓ because acute-on-chronic exacerbation overwhelms compensation. Both tests point to the same disease.

### Q: SBA 3 — 30-year-old with T1DM, drowsy, RR 32. ECG shows sinus tachycardia at 115, peaked T waves. ABG: pH 7.10, PaCO₂ 2.5, BE −25, PaO₂ 13. K⁺ 6.3. Which ECG feature is explained by which ABG finding?
- **A.** Peaked T waves from hypokalaemia
- **B. Peaked T waves from hyperkalaemia in DKA** ✓
- **C.** Sinus tachy from hypoxia
- **D.** Tall T waves from acidaemia directly
- **E.** Unrelated

**Answer: B.** DKA causes hyperkalaemia (insulin deficiency → K⁺ shifts extracellular). Peaked T waves are the classic ECG sign. Sinus tachy here is from dehydration. Acidaemia doesn't cause peaked T waves directly. *(Note: hypokalaemia causes flat T and U waves — reverse pattern.)*

### Q: SBA 4 — Patient with chronic AF on warfarin presents with acute neuro deficit. What mechanism links the two?
- **A.** AF causes acid-base disturbance
- **B. Atrial stasis → left atrial appendage clot → embolic stroke** ✓
- **C.** AF causes hypoxaemia directly
- **D.** Warfarin causes stroke
- **E.** Unrelated

**Answer: B.** This is an L1-internal link (AF mechanism) but worth pairing with L2 because the post-stroke patient will likely have an ABG. Ischaemic brain tissue contributes to lactic acidosis if severe. Anticoagulation is prevention; acute stroke still happens.

### Q: SBA 5 — Severe asthma attack. Initial ABG: pH 7.48, PaCO₂ 3.8, BE −1, PaO₂ 8. An hour later: pH 7.30, PaCO₂ 5.8, BE −2, PaO₂ 7. What has changed?
- **A.** Compensation has worsened
- **B.** Patient is improving
- **C. Patient is tiring — normalising/rising CO₂ is an ominous sign** ✓
- **D.** Now has metabolic acidosis
- **E.** Sample error

**Answer: C.** Severe asthma → hyperventilation → respiratory alkalosis + hypoxaemia (initial). As the patient tires, CO₂ clearance fails. A "normal" CO₂ in obvious respiratory distress = failing ventilation = pre-arrest. Often a trigger for ITU/intubation.

🟥 **Trap.** In obstructive respiratory distress, **normal or rising CO₂ = worsening**, not improvement. Numbers look "better" on the way to catastrophe.

### Q: SBA 6 — Young patient, sudden onset pleuritic chest pain, breathless. ECG: sinus tachy 115. ABG: pH 7.48, PaCO₂ 3.5, BE 0, PaO₂ 8.5. Most likely diagnosis?
- **A.** Panic attack
- **B. Pulmonary embolism** ✓
- **C.** Acute MI
- **D.** Pneumonia
- **E.** Asthma

**Answer: B.** Respiratory alkalosis (hyperventilation from pain/hypoxia) + hypoxaemia. Panic attack would give similar alkalosis but **normal PaO₂**. MI typically normal gases unless complicated. The ECG may also show S1Q3T3 or RAD in PE.

### Q: SBA 7 — Unconscious patient found at scene. ECG: sinus brady 50. ABG: pH 7.22, PaCO₂ 8.5, BE 0, PaO₂ 9. What is happening?
- **A.** Chronic respiratory acidosis
- **B.** DKA
- **C. Acute respiratory acidosis from hypoventilation** ✓ (consider opioid OD)
- **D.** PE
- **E.** Lactic acidosis

**Answer: C.** Acute — BE hasn't moved (no time). Low HR + low RR + CO₂ retention = central depression. Opioid overdose fits. Chronic would have elevated BE. DKA would have low BE and Kussmaul (↓CO₂).

### Q: SBA 8 — Patient with chronic COPD (baseline PaCO₂ 7.5, BE +8) presents unwell. New ABG: pH 7.20, PaCO₂ 9.5, BE +8, PaO₂ 6. What has changed and why?
- **A.** New metabolic acidosis
- **B. Acute-on-chronic respiratory acidosis — compensation unable to keep up with worsening ventilation** ✓
- **C.** PE
- **D.** Incorrect sample
- **E.** Improved

**Answer: B.** Baseline BE +8 indicates long-standing chronic resp acidosis with renal compensation. Acute rise in CO₂ exceeds the compensation → pH crashes. BE unchanged because kidneys need days to respond to this new acute insult.

### Q: SBA 9 — During cardiac arrest with pulseless VT, the team delivers shocks and adrenaline. ABG taken mid-arrest shows pH 7.10, PaCO₂ 7.0, BE −12. How do you interpret?
- **A.** Respiratory acidosis only
- **B.** Metabolic acidosis only
- **C. Mixed acidosis — respiratory from inadequate ventilation during arrest + metabolic from lactate** ✓
- **D.** No acid-base disturbance
- **E.** Fully compensated respiratory acidosis

**Answer: C.** Arrest causes both: hypoperfusion → lactic acidosis (↓BE) and failed gas exchange → CO₂ retention (↑PaCO₂). Classic mixed pattern. Both axes move in the acidosis direction.

### Q: SBA 10 — Patient with massive GI bleed, tachycardic (sinus 125). ABG: pH 7.22, PaCO₂ 3.0, BE −18, PaO₂ 12. Most likely primary cause of the acidosis?
- **A.** CO₂ retention
- **B. Lactic acidosis from hypovolaemic hypoperfusion** ✓
- **C.** Renal failure
- **D.** DKA
- **E.** Vomiting

**Answer: B.** Severe blood loss → hypoperfusion → tissues go anaerobic → lactate → metabolic acidosis. ↓PaCO₂ = respiratory compensation. E wrong — vomiting causes metabolic *alkalosis*.

### Q: SBA 11 — Cardiac axis +105°, peaked P waves >2.5 mm in II. Most likely ABG pattern?
- **A.** Respiratory alkalosis
- **B.** Metabolic acidosis
- **C. Fully compensated respiratory acidosis (chronic COPD / cor pulmonale)** ✓
- **D.** Metabolic alkalosis
- **E.** Normal ABG

**Answer: C.** RAD + P pulmonale = right heart strain from chronic lung disease. Fits chronic CO₂ retention with full renal compensation.

### Q: SBA 12 — Patient with sinus tachycardia 120 and ABG showing pH 7.30, PaCO₂ 3.0, BE −15, PaO₂ 14. Calculate anion gap context — most likely one of these diagnoses. Which?
- **A.** Vomiting
- **B.** Anxiety attack
- **C. DKA / lactic acidosis (high anion gap metabolic acidosis)** ✓
- **D.** COPD
- **E.** PE

**Answer: C.** pH ↓, BE ↓, CO₂ ↓ = partially compensated metabolic acidosis. Sinus tachy fits dehydration / sympathetic response. Vomiting → alkalosis. Anxiety → alkalosis with normal PO₂. COPD → resp acidosis. PE → resp alkalosis.

### Q: SBA 13 — Meta-skill question. What is the single best habit for not getting caught out by either an ECG or an ABG SBA?
- **A.** Memorise all possible answers
- **B.** Trust the machine report
- **C. Walk the numbered algorithm every time, pre-check the sample, then integrate with the clinical story** ✓
- **D.** Start with the most-likely diagnosis and work backwards
- **E.** Always pick the most serious option

**Answer: C.** Lecturer-flagged across both sessions: don't interpret holistically, don't trust numbers blindly, don't skip the pre-check. Algorithm + story = reliable answer even on rhythms or ABGs you've never seen.

### Q: SBA 14 — 40-year-old ingested high dose aspirin 30 minutes ago. Initial ABG? Later ABG?
- **A.** Early: respiratory acidosis. Late: metabolic alkalosis
- **B. Early: respiratory alkalosis (direct CNS stimulation). Late: mixed metabolic acidosis + resp alkalosis** ✓
- **C.** Early: metabolic acidosis only. Late: normal
- **D.** No ABG change
- **E.** Metabolic alkalosis throughout

**Answer: B.** Aspirin stimulates the respiratory centre first → hyperventilation. Later, salicylate drives a high anion gap metabolic acidosis while hyperventilation persists → classic **mixed picture**. Advanced but a documented exam theme.

### Q: SBA 15 — A patient has ECG showing ST elevation in V2–V4. While awaiting PCI, an ABG is taken. What acid-base pattern would suggest the infarct is causing haemodynamic compromise?
- **A.** Respiratory alkalosis from pain
- **B.** Normal ABG
- **C. Metabolic acidosis (from lactate) with respiratory compensation** ✓
- **D.** Pure respiratory acidosis
- **E.** Metabolic alkalosis

**Answer: C.** An anteroseptal STEMI compromising LV function → cardiogenic shock → hypoperfusion → lactate. Metabolic acidosis with ↓CO₂ compensation is a bad sign — flags haemodynamic failure. Pain-only hyperventilation would give respiratory alkalosis without metabolic component.

## Reusable cross-lecture pattern

### Q: What is the single habit the bundle trains?
- **Build a numbered algorithm for every bedside test, walk it the same way every time, and tie the findings back to the patient.**
- Same approach: ECG (7 steps), ABG (4 steps), and whatever test you meet next.
- Abnormalities come in groups: a territory change on one test predicts a pattern on another test.

### Q: What is the deepest integration idea across the bundle?
- Cardiorespiratory problems leave fingerprints on **both** tests.
- Hypoxia + hyperventilation → PE (alkalosis + hypoxaemia on ABG, RAD on ECG).
- Hypoperfusion → lactic acidosis on ABG, arrhythmia / ST change on ECG.
- Chronic lung disease → compensated acidosis + RAD.
- Don't read investigations in isolation — ask what **one disease** produces both findings.

### Q: How do I prioritise revision across L1 and L2?
- **L1**: mechanism spine + territory mapping + arrhythmia atlas + shockable-vs-non-shockable.
- **L2**: 4-step algorithm + master grid + four archetypes + worked examples.
- **Cross**: pair each disease with its expected ECG + ABG fingerprint.
- Practise SBAs that mix both — that's the exam-style skill.
