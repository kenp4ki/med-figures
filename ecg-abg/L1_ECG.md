## The unifying intuition

### Q: What is the one idea that makes every ECG finding derivable?
- The ECG is the **shadow of a moving arrow**, cast from 12 different angles.
- The arrow = net depolarisation vector at that instant.
- Toward a lead → tall positive. Away → deep negative. Perpendicular → nothing.
- Amplitude = muscle mass. Width = conduction speed. Polarity = direction vs camera.

## Section 1 — Mechanism spine

📎 **Figure L1.4 — Cardiac conduction cascade**

```
SAN ──► atria ──► AVN ──► Bundle of His ──► L & R bundle branches ──► Purkinje ──► repolarisation
 |        |        |              |                     |                |              |
invisible P wave  isoelectric   invisible              Q wave         R then S        T wave
          (~80ms) (PR segment)                        (septal)      (apex then          
                                                                     lat walls)          
```

### Q: Why does the P wave exist at all and why is it short and rounded?
- Depolarisation of **thin atrial muscle** (low mass → small amplitude).
- Conducted through ordinary myocardium, no fast path (→ rounded, ~80 ms wide).
- Net vector points from SAN toward AVN = toward lead II's + electrode → **upright in II**.

### Q: Why does the AV node deliberately slow conduction?
- Gives ventricles time to **fill with blood** before contracting.
- Also preserves diastolic window for **coronary perfusion** (coronaries are squeezed shut during systole).
- AVN delay = flat ECG (PR segment, no activity).

### Q: Why is the Q wave small and negative rather than tall and positive?
- It's **septal depolarisation**: left bundle branch fires first, running left→right across thin septum.
- Small mass → small amplitude.
- Vector points away from lead II + electrode → **negative deflection**.

### Q: Why is the R wave the tallest and narrowest feature?
- Ventricular apex fires through **Purkinje network** (fast → narrow).
- Left ventricle is the thickest muscle in the heart (big mass → tall).
- Vector points down-left toward lead II + electrode → **tall positive**.

### Q: Why does the S wave exist after the R wave?
- Last to depolarise = lateral ventricular walls.
- Wavefront now points upward and away from lead II + electrode.
- Small negative deflection after R.

### Q: Where is atrial repolarisation on the ECG?
- **Hidden inside the QRS** — a small event swamped by a louder one.
- Exists, but not visible. Not clinically useful.

🟦 **Reusable.** Every ECG abnormality maps to one step of this cascade. "Prolonged PR" = AVN slow. "Wide QRS" = Purkinje bypassed. "Deep Q" = septal depolarisation into dead tissue.

## Section 2 — The 12 cameras

📎 **Figure L1.5 — Electrode placement and Einthoven's triangle**

### Q: Electrodes, cables, and leads — what's the difference?
- **Electrodes** = sticky pads on the skin.
- **Cables/wires** = the physical copper connection.
- **Leads** = *views* of the heart (12 leads from 10 cables).
- Not 1:1. Leads are a mathematical construction, not a physical thing.

### Q: What are the two ECG planes and how many leads in each?
- **Coronal plane** (6 leads): I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF — view from front.
- **Horizontal plane** (6 leads): V1–V6 — cross-section view.
- Bipolar limb leads = I, II, III. Unipolar augmented = aVR, aVL, aVF. Unipolar chest = V1–V6.

### Q: What is the Rule of L's for bipolar limb leads?
- **Lead I** = one L → Left Arm involved once (runs between arms).
- **Lead II** = two L's → Left Leg (L-L) (RA → LL).
- **Lead III** = three L's → Left Arm + Left Leg (LA → LL).

### Q: What is the Rule of Reading for negative electrodes?
- English reads **left→right, top→bottom**. Polarity too.
- Draw triangle: RA top-left, LA top-right, LL bottom.
- The first electrode you reach reading L→R then top→bottom = **negative**.
- So: Lead I → RA −ve. Lead II → RA −ve. Lead III → LA −ve.

### Q: What does the "aV" mean and how do you remember each lead's direction?
- **a** = augmented. **V** = voltage. Letter = **positive** electrode.
- **aVR** = Right shoulder positive (−150°) → mostly inverted waveforms.
- **aVL** = Left shoulder positive (−30°) → high lateral view.
- **aVF** = Foot positive (+90°) → inferior view from below.

### Q: What are the three perpendicular lead pairs and how do you memorise them?
- Stack the limb leads in two columns: I / II / III next to aVF / aVL / aVR.
- Each row is a perpendicular pair:
  - **I ⟂ aVF**, **II ⟂ aVL**, **III ⟂ aVR**.
- Why it matters: perpendicular pairs let you build right-angled triangles for axis trig.

### Q: Where do V1 to V6 sit anatomically?
- **V1** — right sternal border, **4th ICS**.
- **V2** — left sternal border, 4th ICS.
- **V4** — mid-clavicular line, **5th ICS**.
- **V3** — halfway between V2 and V4 (no fixed landmark).
- **V5** — anterior axillary line, V4 level. **V6** — mid-axillary line, V4 level.

🟨 **High-yield.** V3 having **no fixed landmark** is lecturer-flagged. Classic exam distractor: "V3 is placed at…" with a specific rib space that doesn't exist.

### Q: How do you find the 4th intercostal space on a real patient?
- Find the **sternal angle** (angle of Louis) — palpable ridge on sternum.
- The rib articulating there = **2nd rib**.
- Space just below = 2nd ICS. Count down: 2 → 3 → 4.

📎 **Figure L1.6 — 12-lead territories and coronary supply**

### Q: Which leads look at which anatomical territory and artery?

| Leads | Territory | Artery |
|---|---|---|
| I, aVL, V5, V6 | Lateral | LCx |
| II, III, aVF | **Inferior** | **RCA** |
| V1, V2 | Septal | LAD |
| V3, V4 | Anterior | LAD |

aVR has no territory.

### Q: Memory hook for lead → territory mapping?
- **"All V1–V4 is LAD"** (septal + anterior).
- **"The rest of lateral is LCx"** (I, aVL, V5, V6).
- **"Inferior is RCA"** (II, III, aVF).
- RCA wraps round the back and underneath → inferior wall.

🟨 **Highest-yield grouping.** II, III, aVF = inferior = RCA. Lecturer drilled this.

## Section 3 — Cardiac axis

📎 **Figure L1.8 — Hexaxial reference system**

### Q: What is the cardiac axis and what is its normal range?
- Net direction of the **R-wave vector** — summed ventricular depolarisation.
- Dominated by left ventricle (biggest mass).
- **Normal range: −30° to +90°.**
- Measured on the hexaxial reference (clock face of limb leads).

### Q: Eyeball method — which two leads and what do the patterns mean?

| Lead I | aVF | Axis |
|---|---|---|
| + | + | **Normal** (0° to +90°) |
| + | − | Possible **LAD** (confirm with II) |
| − | + | **RAD** |
| − | − | **Extreme** |

If aVF is slightly negative but lead II is still positive, axis is between −30° and 0° → still normal. True LAD needs lead II also negative.

### Q: What causes left and right axis deviation?
- **LAD**: LV hypertrophy, LBBB, inferior MI (dead inferior tissue can't fire, axis shifts up-left).
- **RAD**: RV hypertrophy, PE, COPD/cor pulmonale, left posterior fascicular block.
- **Extreme**: rare, usually serious.

📎 **Figure L1.9a — Cardiac axis trigonometry worked example**

### Q: Cardiac axis by trigonometry — what's the 5-step method?
1. Pick a **perpendicular pair** (easiest: I and aVF).
2. Measure **net QRS deflection** in each (positive mm − negative mm).
3. Treat the two nets as adjacent and opposite sides of a right-angled triangle.
4. **tan θ = opposite / adjacent**, then θ = tan⁻¹(that value).
5. Orient relative to reference lead bearing; sanity-check with eyeball rule.

### Q: Why is the lecturer's example axis = 36°?
- Lead II net = +4.5 mm (bearing +60°). Lead aVL net = +2 mm (bearing −30°).
- tan θ = 2 / 4.5 = 0.444 → θ = 24°.
- Axis sits 24° toward aVL from lead II → **60° − 24° = +36°**.
- Sanity: both I and aVF would be positive → normal axis ✓.

🟨 **Examiner signal.** Lecturer called this *"full mark exam question."* Stem will give you mm amplitudes and expect numerical answer.

🟦 **Practical tip.** In exam, use **lead I and aVF** whenever possible. Their bearings (0° and +90°) give trivial arithmetic — no awkward subtraction from +60° or −30°.

## Section 4 — Reporting procedure & intervals

📎 **Figure L1.10 — ECG reporting procedure**

### Q: What are the pre-checks before interpreting any ECG?
- Correct recording? (right patient, right date — sneaky trap)
- Signal quality / leads readable?
- Voltage calibration = 10 mm/mV, paper speed = 25 mm/s?
- Patient background / clinical context?

### Q: What are the 7 steps of ECG reporting?
1. **Rate & rhythm**
2. P wave, PR interval, P:QRS ratio
3. QRS duration
4. QRS axis
5. ST segment
6. QT interval *(de-emphasised at Phase 1B)*
7. T wave *(de-emphasised at Phase 1B)*

Then **double-check** abnormal findings.

### Q: What are the normal ECG intervals to memorise?

| Interval | Normal value |
|---|---|
| PR | **120–200 ms** |
| QRS | **<120 ms** |
| QT | ~420 ms |
| HR | 60–100 bpm |

### Q: Two ways to calculate heart rate from a strip?
- Big-square method: count big squares between R-R → **300 / (big squares)**.
- Small-square method: count small squares between R-R → 1500 / (small squares).
- Rhythm-strip method: if ≥10 seconds, count beats × 6.

## Section 5 — Arrhythmia atlas

### Territory map

```
Normal ─────────┬──── Sinus rhythm (60–100, 1:1 P:QRS)
                ├──── Sinus bradycardia (<60)
                ├──── Sinus tachycardia (>100)
                └──── Sinus arrhythmia (R-R varies with breathing)

Atrial ─────────┬──── Atrial fibrillation (irregularly irregular, no P)
                └──── Atrial flutter (sawtooth II/III/aVF, 2:1 / 3:1)

AV blocks ──────┬──── 1° (PR >200, always conducts)
                ├──── 2° Mobitz I / Wenckebach (PR lengthens, beat drops)
                ├──── 2° Mobitz II (PR fixed, random drops)
                └──── 3° complete (AV dissociation)

Ventricular ────┬──── VT (regular, broad-complex, 100–200)
                ├──── VF (chaotic, ≥250, no output — ARREST)
                └──── Asystole (flat line — ARREST)

ST changes ─────┬──── ST elevation (infarction)
                └──── ST depression (ischaemia / reciprocal / hypoK+)
```

### Sinus rhythms

📎 **Figure L1.12 — Sinus rhythm**

### Q: Define sinus rhythm in 3 criteria.
- Every P followed by a QRS (**1:1**).
- Regular R-R intervals.
- Rate **60–100 bpm**.
- P upright in lead II (and inverted in aVR).

📎 **Figure L1.13 — Sinus bradycardia**

### Q: What is sinus bradycardia and what are the 4 main causes?
- Sinus rhythm with rate **<60 bpm**.
- Causes: **athletic** (↑ stroke volume), **drugs** (β-blockers, CCBs, digoxin), **vagal stimulation**, **disease** (sick sinus, hypothyroid).

📎 **Figure L1.14 — Sinus tachycardia**

### Q: Why is sinus tachycardia rarely the "answer" on its own?
- Sinus rhythm with rate **>100 bpm** — usually **secondary**.
- Points at underlying cause: pain, fever, hypovolaemia, anxiety, hypoxia, PE, hyperthyroid.
- Manage the cause, not the rhythm.

📎 **Figure L1.15 — Sinus arrhythmia**

### Q: What is sinus arrhythmia and why is it normal?
- R-R varies with breathing: **inspiration → HR↑, expiration → HR↓**.
- Mechanism: brainstem inspiratory signal reduces vagal tone → less brake on SAN.
- Very common in young healthy people. Treatment = reassurance.

### Atrial arrhythmias

📎 **Figure L1.16 — Atrial fibrillation**

### Q: How is atrial fibrillation identified on ECG?
- **No discernible P waves** — oscillating/wavy baseline.
- **Irregularly irregular** QRS complexes.
- Rate variable (slow, normal, or fast).

### Q: Why does AF cause stroke, and what's the management principle?
- Chaotic atria → stagnant blood in left atrial appendage → **clot** → embolic stroke.
- Management: **rate/rhythm control** (β-blocker, CCB, cardioversion) + **anticoagulation** (stroke prevention, CHA₂DS₂-VASc).

📎 **Figure L1.17 — Atrial flutter**

### Q: How do you distinguish atrial flutter from AF on ECG?
- Flutter = **regular**, **sawtooth** baseline (best in **II, III, aVF**), fixed atrial:ventricular ratio (2:1, 3:1, 4:1).
- AF = irregularly irregular, no P waves, chaotic baseline.
- Atrial rate in flutter ~300 bpm; 2:1 block → ventricular 150 bpm.

🟨 **Classic stem.** Regular narrow-complex tachycardia at 150 bpm with sawtooth in inferior leads = flutter with 2:1 block.

### Heart blocks — the ladder

📎 **Figure L1.18 — First degree heart block**
📎 **Figure L1.19 — Mobitz I (Wenckebach)**
📎 **Figure L1.20 — Mobitz II**
📎 **Figure L1.21 — Third degree heart block**

### Q: What are the 4 types of heart block in one ladder sentence each?
- **1°** — PR long, every beat conducts.
- **Mobitz I** — PR *progressively lengthens*, then one beat drops.
- **Mobitz II** — PR constant, beats drop suddenly (2:1, 3:1).
- **3°** — complete AV dissociation, P and QRS independent.

### Q: Compare the heart blocks on 4 axes.

| Feature | 1° | Mobitz I | Mobitz II |
|---|---|---|---|
| PR interval | Long, constant | Lengthens → drops | Constant → drops |
| P:QRS | 1:1 | 1:1 until drop | Fixed ratio (2:1) |
| Danger | Benign | Usually benign | **Can progress to 3°** |

3° block sits separately: P regular, QRS regular, no P-QRS relationship. Ventricular escape rate 30–50.

### Q: Why does 3° heart block not kill the patient immediately?
- **Back-up pacemaker** kicks in: AV junction or ventricular escape focus.
- Rate is slow (30–50), but cardiac output is preserved enough to survive.
- Hierarchy: SAN → if fails, AVN takes over → if fails, ventricles (slowest).

🟥 **Trap.** Mobitz II can **rapidly deteriorate into 3° block**. Needs pacemaker. Do not dismiss as "just a block."

### Q: Rhyme for heart blocks?
- *"If the R is far from P, then you have a first degree."*
- *"Longer, longer, longer, drop, then you have a Wenckebach."*
- *"If some Ps just don't get through, then you have a Mobitz II."*
- *"If Ps and Qs don't agree, then you have a third degree."*

### Ventricular arrhythmias & arrest

📎 **Figure L1.22 — Ventricular tachycardia**
📎 **Figure L1.23 — Ventricular fibrillation**
📎 **Figure L1.11 — Asystole (from "acute responses" slide)**

### Q: What is VT on ECG and how is it managed?
- **Regular, broad-complex** tachycardia (QRS >120 ms, rate 100–200).
- P waves hidden or dissociated.
- **Shockable** if pulseless → defibrillate. Pulsed & unstable → synchronised cardioversion. Stable → amiodarone.

### Q: What is VF on ECG and what does it mean clinically?
- **Chaotic, irregular**, rate ≥250, no identifiable waves.
- **No cardiac output** — this is cardiac arrest.
- **Shockable** → immediate defibrillation.

### Q: What is asystole and what is the trap?
- **Flat line**, no electrical activity.
- Cardiac arrest.
- **Non-shockable** — defib does nothing.
- Management: **CPR + IV adrenaline** + find reversible causes (4 Hs / 4 Ts).

🟥 **Biggest exam trap in this lecture.** *"Defibrillate asystole"* is the film-and-TV answer. Lecturer: *"If you take nothing from today, take away that nonsense."*

### Q: Shockable vs non-shockable — which is which?
- **Shockable**: VT (pulseless), VF.
- **Non-shockable**: asystole, PEA.
- Memory: shockable rhythms have **shape** (VT = tall regular, VF = chaotic); asystole has no shape to reset.

### ST segment changes

📎 **Figure L1.24 — ST elevation**
📎 **Figure L1.25 — ST depression**

### Q: What causes ST elevation and why does it localise?
- **Myocardial infarction (STEMI)** — transmural tissue death.
- The leads showing elevation tell you the territory → the artery.
- Emergency: primary PCI (or thrombolysis if PCI unavailable) within time window.

### Q: What are the 3 causes of ST depression?
- **Myocardial ischaemia** (subendocardial).
- **Reciprocal changes** — mirror image of elevation in the opposite territory.
- **Hypokalaemia** (also causes flat T waves and U waves).

🟦 **Discrimination.** Elevation = one diagnosis (infarct). Depression = three differentials. Stem clues: dynamic + chest pain = ischaemia; matched with elevation elsewhere = reciprocal; low K on bloods = hypokalaemia.

## Section 6 — Cross-lecture links

### Q: How does sinus arrhythmia relate to ABG physiology?
- Both reflect the **vagus-ventilation coupling**: inspiration → ↓vagal tone.
- In L1 this manifests as HR variability with breathing.
- In L2 the same brainstem-driven ventilation changes rapidly compensate for acid-base disturbance.
- See also: L2 — *Respiratory vs renal compensation*.

### Q: How do the 7-step ECG procedure and the 4-step ABG procedure relate?
- Both are **numbered checklists** for bedside tests — never interpret holistically.
- Same meta-skill: pre-checks → numbered steps → integrate with clinical story → double-check abnormalities.
- Transferable to future investigations (CXR, PFTs, echo).
- See also: L2 — *4-step interpretation procedure*.

### Q: Which arrhythmia-producing pathology would also produce a metabolic acidosis on ABG?
- **VT or VF** → poor cardiac output → tissue hypoperfusion → **lactic acidosis**.
- ECG and ABG are two fingerprints of the same pathology.
- See also: L2 — *Metabolic acidosis causes*.

### Q: Which ECG finding would you expect in a patient with long-standing COPD and why?
- **Right axis deviation** (>+90°) from RV hypertrophy / cor pulmonale.
- P pulmonale (peaked P waves) may also be present.
- Pairs with: L2 — *Fully compensated respiratory acidosis* pattern on ABG.
- See also: L2 — *Archetypes of disturbance → respiratory acidosis*.

## Section 7 — Reusable reasoning pattern

### Q: The transferable skill from L1 — what is the habit?
- **Pre-check** the recording before reading it.
- Walk the **7 steps** in order, every time — never "glance and call it."
- Every abnormality maps to a step of the **conduction cascade** (Section 1).
- Abnormalities come in groups — ST elevation in one territory → look for **reciprocal depression** elsewhere.
- Integrate with the **clinical story**. An ECG alone is rarely the full answer.

## Section 8 — Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 68-year-old collapses on the ward. He is pulseless, not breathing. Monitor shows a flat line with no discernible waves. Most appropriate next step?
- **A.** Defibrillate at 200 J
- **B. Administer IV adrenaline and commence high-quality CPR** ✓
- **C.** Synchronised cardioversion
- **D.** IV amiodarone
- **E.** Transcutaneous pacing

**Answer: B.** Flat line = asystole = **non-shockable**. CPR + adrenaline + search 4 Hs/4 Ts. A is the film-and-TV trap. C is for tachyarrhythmias with a pulse. D is used after 3rd shock in shockable rhythms. E is for symptomatic bradycardia with pulse.

### Q: SBA 2 — ECG shows ST elevation in II, III, aVF with reciprocal depression in I and aVL. Which artery is occluded?
- **A.** LAD
- **B.** LCx
- **C. RCA** ✓
- **D.** Left main stem
- **E.** Posterior descending only

**Answer: C.** II/III/aVF = inferior = RCA (dominant in ~85%). Reciprocal depression in lateral leads (I, aVL) is expected. LAD gives V1–V4 elevation; LCx gives lateral (I, aVL, V5, V6).

### Q: SBA 3 — P waves at 80 bpm, QRS at 40 bpm, no fixed relationship. Diagnosis?
- **A.** 1° heart block
- **B.** Mobitz I
- **C.** Mobitz II
- **D. 3° complete heart block** ✓
- **E.** AF with slow response

**Answer: D.** Complete AV dissociation — P and QRS independent. Ventricular escape at 40. A has 1:1. B/C still conduct some beats. E has no P waves, irregularly irregular.

### Q: SBA 4 — Lead I net negative, aVF net positive, Lead II net negative. Axis?
- **A.** Normal
- **B.** LAD
- **C. RAD** ✓
- **D.** Extreme axis
- **E.** Need trig

**Answer: C.** I negative + aVF positive = right-axis quadrant. II negative confirms axis >+90°. Eyeball method decides it.

### Q: SBA 5 — Regular narrow-complex tachycardia at 150 bpm, sawtooth baseline best in II/III/aVF. Diagnosis?
- **A.** Sinus tachycardia
- **B.** AF
- **C. Atrial flutter with 2:1 block** ✓
- **D.** VT
- **E.** 1° heart block

**Answer: C.** Sawtooth in inferior leads is the flutter signature. Atrial rate ~300, 2:1 → ventricular 150. Sinus tachy has clear Ps; AF is irregularly irregular; VT is broad-complex.

### Q: SBA 6 — Young athlete, HR 48, 1:1 P:QRS, normal intervals. Management?
- **A.** Urgent pacemaker
- **B. Reassurance — physiological** ✓
- **C.** Start β-blocker
- **D.** Stop any β-blocker they're on
- **E.** ECHO urgently

**Answer: B.** Sinus bradycardia in a trained athlete = physiological (high stroke volume → fewer beats needed). No symptoms, no pathology, no intervention.

### Q: SBA 7 — PR interval 320 ms (constant), every P followed by QRS. Diagnosis and action?
- **A. 1° heart block — reassurance and review culprit drugs** ✓
- **B.** Mobitz I — reassurance
- **C.** Mobitz II — pacemaker
- **D.** 3° block — pacemaker
- **E.** Normal variant

**Answer: A.** Long PR but 1:1 = 1° block. Benign. Check β-blockers, CCBs, digoxin. Mobitz I has *progressively* lengthening PR.

### Q: SBA 8 — V3 is placed at the 5th intercostal space, left sternal border. True or false?
- **False.** V3 has **no fixed anatomical landmark** — it sits **halfway between V2 and V4**. V2 is at 4th ICS left sternal border; V4 is at 5th ICS mid-clavicular.

### Q: SBA 9 — Irregularly irregular rhythm, no P waves, wavy baseline. Most important chronic management concern?
- **A.** Heart rate control
- **B. Stroke prevention via anticoagulation** ✓
- **C.** Rhythm conversion
- **D.** ICD
- **E.** Exercise restriction

**Answer: B.** AF. Stagnation in left atrial appendage → clots → embolic stroke. CHA₂DS₂-VASc guides anticoagulation. Rate control matters but stroke risk kills patients.

### Q: SBA 10 — Patient with 20-minute VT runs at 180 bpm, pulseless. Action?
- **A.** Synchronised cardioversion
- **B.** IV amiodarone
- **C. Defibrillation** ✓
- **D.** Adrenaline only
- **E.** Pacing

**Answer: C.** Pulseless VT is a shockable arrest rhythm. Unsynchronised defibrillation. If pulse had been present and unstable → synchronised cardioversion (A). Amiodarone used after 3rd shock.
