## Painless haematuria workup (cross-cutting)

📎 Figure L1.1 — Diagnosis and management of incidentally discovered microscopic haematuria

```
Painless haematuria
│
├── VISIBLE                       → 2-week-wait referral
│      Flexible cystoscopy + CT urogram + renal function + FBC
│
├── PERSISTENT NON-VISIBLE        (≥3 RBC/HPF on repeat dipstick)
│      ≥50 yr: same workup as visible
│      <50 yr: flexible cystoscopy + US KUB
│
└── In all cases also: urine culture, urine cytology, BP, urinalysis for proteinuria
```

### Q: Why "top end + bottom end" for every haematuria workup?

- Bleeding can come from anywhere along the urinary tract.
- **Imaging (CT / US)** sees the kidneys, ureters and gross bladder pathology = **top end**.
- **Cystoscopy** sees small / flat bladder mucosal lesions that imaging misses = **bottom end**.
- One modality alone leaves a blind spot.

### Q: Pre-test probability of serious pathology — visible vs non-visible haematuria?

- **Visible**: up to **50%** in older patients → 2-week-wait.
- **Non-visible**: ~**5%** → still investigate ≥50 yr.
- Pattern: visibility scales the urgency, not the workup.

### Q: When does ultrasound replace CT urogram in the haematuria pathway?

- Lower pre-test probability scenarios (non-visible haematuria, younger patients).
- Avoids radiation and contrast.
- US KUB picks up hydronephrosis and large renal masses well, misses small ureteric / bladder lesions.

⭐ **Field change:** transitional epithelium runs from renal calyces to proximal urethra. Smoking / aromatic amines damage the whole tract. Bladder TCC patients are at risk of upper-tract TCC and vice versa.

## CARD 1 — KIDNEY CANCER (RCC)

### Q: Kidney cancer epidemiology in 4 lines?

- **13,100** UK cases/yr.
- **7th** most common UK cancer.
- Incidence and mortality **rising** (contrast: bladder declining, prostate mortality declining).
- 80% of cases in patients >55 yr.

### Q: Histological breakdown of kidney cancer?

- **85% RCC** (adenocarcinoma) — the default answer.
- 10% TCC (renal pelvis, behaves like bladder cancer).
- 5% sarcoma / Wilms / other.
- Within RCC: **clear cell ~75%**, papillary ~15%, chromophobe ~5%.

### Q: Top 5 risk factors for RCC?

- **Smoking** (commonest modifiable).
- **Obesity**.
- **Hypertension**.
- **Long-term dialysis** for CKD.
- **Inherited syndromes** — especially **von Hippel-Lindau** (bilateral, multifocal RCC).

### Q: Classic clinical triad of RCC?

- **Loin pain + palpable mass + haematuria**.
- Present in only ~10% of patients but a strong exam pointer.
- ~50% of RCCs are now **incidental** findings on CT done for other reasons.

### Q: Two paraneoplastic syndromes specific to RCC?

- **PTH-related peptide → hypercalcaemia** (stones, bones, groans, moans).
- **Erythropoietin → polycythaemia** (raised Hb / Hct).
- Counter-intuitive: cancer of the EPO-producing organ raises Hb instead of dropping it.

📎 Figure L1.2 — Renal cell carcinoma at lower pole of kidney with renal vessels and IVC

### Q: Investigation pathway for suspected RCC?

- **CT renal triple phase** — characterises mass enhancement (RCC is vascular and lights up).
- **Staging CT chest** — looks for lung mets.
- **Bone scan only if symptomatic** — bone mets in RCC are typically painful lytic lesions.
- Biopsy rarely done pre-op — diagnosis is made on imaging characteristics.

### Q: Why CT triple phase rather than single-phase CT?

- RCC is a **vascular tumour**.
- Arterial phase shows it lighting up brightly; delayed phase shows washout.
- That enhancement pattern distinguishes a malignant solid mass from a simple cyst.

### Q: TNM staging for RCC — T-stages?

- **T1 ≤ 7 cm**, confined to kidney.
- **T2 > 7 cm**, confined to kidney.
- **T3** — extends outside kidney into renal vein/IVC, perinephric fat, or ipsilateral adrenal — but within Gerota's fascia.
- **T4** — beyond Gerota's fascia.

### Q: Fuhrman grading system?

- 1 = well differentiated.
- 2 = moderate.
- 3 + 4 = poorly differentiated.
- Less detail-heavy than Gleason; just remember "Fuhrman = kidney, 1–4, higher = worse".

### Q: Indications for partial (not radical) nephrectomy?

- **T1 tumour ≤ 7 cm**.
- **Single functioning kidney**.
- **Bilateral tumours**.
- **Multifocal RCC in VHL**.
- Poor contralateral function (would otherwise need dialysis).

### Q: Treatment ladder for RCC by scenario?

- Localised, fit, ≤7 cm or special indication → **partial nephrectomy**.
- Localised, fit, larger/central → **radical nephrectomy** (kidney + Gerota's).
- Small tumour, unfit for surgery → **cryosurgery**.
- Metastatic → **TKIs** (sunitinib, pazopanib) + **immunotherapy** (checkpoint inhibitors).

### Q: Why is RCC treated with TKIs and immunotherapy in metastatic disease, not chemo?

- RCC is famously **chemo- and radio-resistant**.
- Pathway: VHL gene loss → HIF stabilisation → VEGF upregulation → angiogenesis.
- TKIs target VEGF signalling; immunotherapy harnesses host response.

⚠️ **Trap:** elderly, incidentally found 3 cm renal mass on CT, good contralateral kidney → answer is **partial nephrectomy** (or surveillance/cryo if frail), **not** radical nephrectomy.

## CARD 2 — BLADDER CANCER

### Q: Bladder cancer epidemiology in 4 lines?

- **10,200** UK cases/yr.
- **11th** most common UK cancer.
- Incidence and mortality **declining** (smoking decline + occupational regulation).
- M:F ratio ~3:1.

### Q: Histological breakdown of bladder cancer?

- **>90% TCC** (transitional / urothelial).
- **1–7% SCC** — but **75% in schistosomiasis-endemic areas**.
- 2% adenocarcinoma (rare).
- TCC is the universal default unless the stem hints at chronic irritation.

### Q: Risk factors for TCC of the bladder?

- **Smoking** — dominant risk factor.
- **Aromatic amines** (β-naphthylamine) — chemical dyes, rubber, textile, paint, petroleum, hairdressers, painters.
- **Cyclophosphamide** (acrolein metabolite).
- Pelvic radiotherapy, phenacetin, pioglitazone >1 yr.

### Q: When should you suspect SCC rather than TCC of the bladder?

- **Schistosomiasis** exposure (Egypt, Africa, Middle East).
- **Long-term indwelling catheter** (especially spinal cord injury).
- Chronic UTIs, bladder stones.
- Pattern: chronic irritation → squamous metaplasia → SCC.

### Q: Cardinal symptom of bladder cancer + 3 supportive features?

- **Painless visible haematuria** = the red flag.
- **LUTS** (frequency, urgency, dysuria) — irritative cluster.
- **Recurrent UTIs** — tumour acts as nidus.
- **Hydronephrosis with renal impairment** — if tumour overlies ureteric orifice.

📎 Figure L1.3 — Axial pelvic CT: contrast-filling defect (left) and bladder mass with clot (right)

### Q: Investigation pathway for suspected bladder cancer?

- **Flexible cystoscopy** — only modality that reliably sees small/flat bladder lesions.
- **CT urogram** — images upper tract and characterises mass.
- **Renal function + FBC**.
- **TURBT** = both diagnostic (depth = stage) and curative for NMI disease.

### Q: Why TURBT rather than just biopsy for bladder lesions?

- Depth of muscle invasion = the single most important prognostic question.
- Simple biopsy may not include muscularis propria.
- TURBT resects through the lesion deep enough to sample muscle.
- Pathologist explicitly reports: "is muscularis propria present?"

📎 Figure L1.5 — Cystoscopic view: papillary bladder tumour with resection loop

### Q: TNM T-stages for bladder cancer?

- **Ta** — non-invasive papillary.
- **Tis** — carcinoma in situ (flat, high-grade dysplasia in urothelium).
- **T1** — invades subepithelial connective tissue (lamina propria).
- **T2** — invades **muscularis propria** ← fork point.
- **T3** — perivesical fat. **T4** — adjacent organs.

🧠 **The bladder cancer fork:** Ta/Tis/T1 = non-muscle-invasive (NMI). T2+ = muscle-invasive (MI). These are essentially two different diseases with different management. If a stem gives the T-stage, this is the first decision.

### Q: Management of non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer (NMI)?

- **TURBT** removes visible tumour.
- **Intravesical mitomycin C** post-TURBT for low-grade NMI — reduces 1-yr recurrence ~12–14%.
- **Intravesical BCG** for **CIS** and high-risk NMI — 6-week induction + maintenance.
- **Cystoscopic surveillance** every 3–6 months / yearly.

### Q: Management of muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MI)?

- **Radical cystectomy** ± neoadjuvant cisplatin-based chemo.
- Urinary diversion: ileal conduit (stoma) or neobladder reconstruction.
- **Radiotherapy** if unfit for or declining cystectomy.
- Palliation if metastatic and frail.

### Q: Why BCG (a TB vaccine) for bladder CIS?

- BCG induces a strong local immune response in the bladder mucosa.
- CIS represents urothelial **field change** — the whole lining is at risk.
- Immunotherapy treats the whole field, not just one lesion.
- Mitomycin (chemo) is for low-grade non-CIS NMI.

⚠️ **Trap:** "high-grade Tis (CIS)" → **BCG**, not mitomycin. Different drug class for different disease.

## URETERIC TCC — short card

📎 Figure L1.6 — Ureteric TCC: CT urogram filling defect (left), ureteroscopy procedure (right)

### Q: Presentation, diagnosis and operation for upper-tract TCC?

- Same urothelium as bladder — same risk factors (smoking dominant).
- Presentation: painless haematuria + **hydronephrosis** (ureter obstructed by tumour).
- Imaging: CT urogram filling defect in renal pelvis or ureter.
- Diagnosis: **ureteroscopy + biopsy**.
- Operation: **nephroureterectomy** — whole kidney + ureter (field change risk).

⚠️ **Trap:** stem with "haematuria + hydronephrosis + filling defect in ureter or renal pelvis" → upper-tract TCC, **not** RCC.

## CARD 3 — PROSTATE CANCER

### Q: Prostate cancer epidemiology in 4 lines?

- **48,500** UK cases/yr.
- **Most common cancer in UK men**.
- Incidence rising; mortality declining.
- >**95% adenocarcinoma**.

### Q: Top 4 risk factors for prostate cancer?

- **Age >50** (most cases).
- **Ethnicity** — black men have **~4× higher mortality** than Asian men.
- **Family history** — brother/father <60 yrs (2–4× risk); BRCA-associated breast cancer in close female relative.
- Obesity, high-calcium diet (weak).

### Q: How does prostate cancer typically present?

- **Usually asymptomatic** — picked up on raised PSA.
- LUTS are **not** typical (those suggest BPH).
- When symptomatic: **bone pain** (sclerotic mets), urinary retention from bulky disease, spinal cord compression.
- Pattern: symptoms = advanced/metastatic disease.

### Q: What is PSA and what produces it?

- **PSA = prostate-specific antigen**, a serine protease.
- Produced by prostatic glandular tissue; liquefies semen.
- Detectable levels only from prostate tissue.
- **Prostate-specific, NOT prostate-cancer-specific.**

### Q: Three things (not instrumentation) that elevate PSA?

- **BPH** (volume-driven).
- **Infection** (UTI, prostatitis).
- **Prostate cancer**.
- Plus instrumentation: recent biopsy, cystoscopy, vigorous DRE.

### Q: Age-specific PSA upper limits?

- **<50 yr → 2.5** ng/mL.
- **50–59 yr → 3.5** ng/mL.
- **60–69 yr → 4.5** ng/mL.
- **70–79 yr → 6.5** ng/mL.

### Q: Why is PSA a poor screening test?

- ~25% of men with normal age-specific PSA still have prostate cancer.
- ~1000 men screened to save 2 lives → cost-controversy.
- PSA neither rules in nor rules out — raises index of suspicion only.

### Q: Modern diagnostic pathway for raised PSA?

- **Multiparametric MRI (mpMRI) BEFORE biopsy** — paradigm shift.
- If focal lesion seen → **MRI-targeted transperineal biopsy**.
- If MRI clear → can avoid biopsy or do template biopsy for reassurance.
- Replaces random TRUS biopsy (under-detected high-grade, over-detected low-grade).

### Q: Why transperineal rather than transrectal biopsy?

- **Lower infection risk** (skin cleaner than rectum).
- **Better access to anterior prostate** (TRUS misses anterior lesions).
- Systematic template sampling.

### Q: Prostate cancer T-stages?

- **T1** non-palpable, not visible on imaging (T1c = found via biopsy after raised PSA — modern presentation).
- **T2** palpable, confined to prostate (a/b/c = half/more than half/both sides).
- **T3** through capsule into periprostatic fat (T3b = into seminal vesicles).
- **T4** fixed to adjacent structures (bladder, rectum, pelvic side wall).

### Q: M-staging in prostate cancer — what's M1b and why does it matter?

- **M1a** = non-regional LN.
- **M1b** = **bone** (commonest met site).
- **M1c** = other organs.
- Bone scan is the staging tool for high-risk disease because bone is the dominant target.

### Q: How is the Gleason score constructed?

- Pathologist looks at biopsy under microscope.
- Identifies architectural patterns 1 (nearly normal) to 5 (completely abnormal).
- Prostate cancer is multifocal → take **most common pattern + second most common pattern**.
- Add them: e.g. 3+4 or 4+3.

📎 Figure L1.13 — Gleason grading patterns (1–5) and ISUP Grade Group / Risk Group table

### Q: ISUP Grade Group system?

| Grade Group | Gleason | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | ≤6 (3+3) | Low / very low |
| 2 | 7 (3+4) | Intermediate (favourable) |
| 3 | 7 (4+3) | Intermediate (unfavourable) |
| 4 | 8 | High |
| 5 | 9–10 | Very high |

### Q: Why is 4+3 worse than 3+4 even though both = 7?

- Order matters: first number = **dominant** pattern.
- 4+3 = pattern 4 dominant → more aggressive architecture.
- 3+4 = pattern 3 dominant → less aggressive.
- Maps to Grade Group 3 vs 2 = different management.

### Q: Prostate cancer management — the 2×2?

| | **Low grade** | **High grade / metastatic** |
|---|---|---|
| **Young + fit** | Active surveillance | Prostatectomy / RT / focal therapy |
| **Old / unfit** | Watchful waiting | Hormone therapy (ADT) |

### Q: Active surveillance vs watchful waiting — what's the difference?

- **Active surveillance**: young, fit, low-grade. Regular PSA + MRI ± biopsy. **Curative intent** if progression.
- **Watchful waiting**: old, unfit, low-grade. PSA only. **Non-curative** — manage symptoms if they arise.
- Pattern: same monitoring concept, different intent.

### Q: How does hormone therapy work in prostate cancer?

- Prostate cancer cells depend on **testosterone** for growth.
- **LHRH agonists** (goserelin) downregulate pituitary → ↓ testosterone (chemical castration).
- ± **Anti-androgens** (bicalutamide) block androgen receptor.
- Used for metastatic, locally advanced, or high-grade disease in unfit patients.

⚠️ **Tumour flare:** GnRH agonists cause an initial testosterone surge (~2–4 weeks) before downregulation. Worsening bone pain post-initiation → flare. Co-prescribe an anti-androgen for the first 2–4 weeks to cover.

### Q: Post-prostatectomy PSA monitoring?

- Should be **undetectable or <0.01 ng/mL** (no prostate tissue left).
- **>0.2 ng/mL = biochemical relapse**.
- 6-monthly PSA testing.
- Negative staging + relapse → salvage RT to prostate bed.

### Q: Two commonest side effects of radical prostatectomy and why?

- **Erectile dysfunction** — damage to **cavernous nerves** (run posterolateral to prostate).
- **Urinary incontinence** — disruption of **proximal urethral sphincter** (sits at bladder neck within prostate).
- Nerve-sparing surgery preserves erectile function in ~70%.

### Q: Adverse effects of hormone therapy (chemical castration profile)?

- Hot flashes, fatigue.
- Hyperlipidaemia, insulin resistance, cardiovascular disease.
- Anaemia, osteoporosis.
- ED, cognitive deficits.

⚠️ **Trap:** PSA elevation in a man recently catheterised, biopsied, or with a UTI 2 weeks ago — **don't act on it**. Repeat after 4–6 weeks once the prostate has settled.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: TNM logic — what does each T-step represent anatomically across the three cancers?

| Boundary | Kidney | Bladder | Prostate |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1→T2 | 7 cm size cut-off | Lamina propria → muscularis propria | Non-palpable → palpable |
| T2→T3 | Out of kidney into perinephric fat / vein | Muscularis → perivesical fat | Within capsule → through capsule |
| T3→T4 | Beyond Gerota's fascia | Perivesical → adjacent organ | Through capsule → fixed adjacent organ |

### Q: Imaging choice rules across urological cancers?

- **Bladder mucosa** → cystoscopy (CT misses small lesions).
- **Kidney mass character** → CT triple phase (enhancement pattern).
- **Prostate lesion** → mpMRI (replaces random biopsy as first step).
- **Bone mets** → bone scan if symptomatic / high-risk.

### Q: When does curative intent disappear?

- Distant metastases (mostly).
- Patient too unfit to tolerate curative treatment.
- Disease invading vital structures unresectably.

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern — the urological cancer 2×2:** every treatment question reduces to (1) how aggressive is the disease and (2) how fit is the patient. Aggressive disease + fit patient = cut it out or radiate. Indolent disease + frail patient = surveillance or watchful waiting. Metastatic disease = systemic therapy (TKI/IO for kidney; chemo/IO for bladder; ADT for prostate). Apply this grid to any urological stem you can't immediately classify.

### Q: Field change principle — when does it apply?

- Applies to **TCC** (urothelium runs from renal calyces to proximal urethra).
- Bladder TCC → at risk of upper-tract TCC; bladder CIS = bladder field change.
- Upper-tract TCC → nephroureterectomy (remove entire ipsilateral urothelium).
- **Does NOT apply** to RCC or prostate cancer (different cell lineages).

## Interleaved SBAs — discrimination across all three cancers

### Q: SBA 1 — A 55-year-old hairdresser with a 30-pack-year smoking history has painless visible haematuria. CT urogram shows a 2 cm filling defect in the bladder. Most likely histological subtype?

- A) Squamous cell carcinoma
- B) Transitional cell carcinoma
- C) Adenocarcinoma
- D) Renal cell carcinoma
- E) Small cell carcinoma

**Answer: B — TCC.** Smoking + occupational dye exposure (hairdressers handle aromatic amines) → bladder TCC. SCC requires schistosomiasis or chronic catheter context.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 60-year-old man has a palpable left flank mass, weight loss, and Hb 19.0 g/dL. CT shows a 9 cm enhancing left renal mass with renal vein extension. Best management?

- A) Partial nephrectomy
- B) Radical nephrectomy
- C) Cryotherapy
- D) Sunitinib
- E) Active surveillance

**Answer: B — radical nephrectomy.** T3a RCC (renal vein extension, >7 cm) in a fit patient. Partial neph is for ≤7 cm or special indications. Sunitinib is for metastatic disease.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 72-year-old man has biochemical relapse 18 months post-prostatectomy with PSA 0.4 ng/mL. CT and bone scan are negative for distant mets. Next step?

- A) Repeat radical prostatectomy
- B) Salvage radiotherapy to the prostate bed
- C) Active surveillance
- D) Hormone therapy alone
- E) Watchful waiting

**Answer: B — salvage RT.** PSA >0.2 = biochemical relapse. Negative distant disease → likely local recurrence in prostate bed → salvage RT is curative-intent.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 50-year-old smoker has right loin pain. CT urogram shows right hydronephrosis and a filling defect in the right proximal ureter. Most likely diagnosis?

- A) Renal cell carcinoma
- B) Transitional cell carcinoma of the ureter
- C) Renal pelvis adenocarcinoma
- D) Renal artery stenosis
- E) Wilms tumour

**Answer: B — ureteric TCC.** Filling defect within the lumen + smoker + obstruction. RCC is a parenchymal mass, not a luminal filling defect.

### Q: SBA 5 — A 46-year-old man (father died of prostate cancer at 58) has PSA 4.0 ng/mL. mpMRI: 7 mm PI-RADS 4 lesion. Next step?

- A) Repeat PSA in 12 months
- B) Transrectal ultrasound-guided random biopsy
- C) MRI-targeted transperineal biopsy
- D) Radical prostatectomy
- E) Hormone therapy

**Answer: C — MRI-targeted transperineal biopsy.** Modern paradigm replaces random TRUS. Don't operate or hormone-treat without histology.

### Q: SBA 6 — A 68-year-old paraplegic man with a 15-year suprapubic catheter has worsening haematuria. Cystoscopy: fungating bladder mass. Most likely histology?

- A) Transitional cell carcinoma
- B) Squamous cell carcinoma
- C) Adenocarcinoma
- D) Sarcoma
- E) Lymphoma

**Answer: B — SCC.** Long-term catheter + SCI = chronic urothelial irritation → squamous metaplasia → SCC.

### Q: SBA 7 — A 65-year-old fit man has Gleason 3+3 prostate cancer confined to the prostate. He's anxious about treatment side effects. Best management?

- A) Radical prostatectomy
- B) External beam radiotherapy
- C) Active surveillance
- D) Watchful waiting
- E) Hormone therapy

**Answer: C — active surveillance.** Young, fit, low-grade = curative-intent monitoring. Many men die *with* not *of* prostate cancer. Watchful waiting is non-curative (frail patients).

### Q: SBA 8 — A 60-year-old fit man has biopsy-confirmed T2N0M0 muscle-invasive bladder cancer. Staging negative for mets. Best management?

- A) Intravesical mitomycin and surveillance
- B) Intravesical BCG induction
- C) TURBT alone with quarterly cystoscopy
- D) Radical cystectomy ± neoadjuvant chemotherapy
- E) Palliative radiotherapy

**Answer: D — radical cystectomy ± neoadjuvant chemo.** T2 = muscle-invasive. Intravesical agents are for NMI only. Standard MIBC pathway = neoadjuvant cisplatin → cystectomy.

### Q: SBA 9 — A 70-year-old man with metastatic prostate cancer (M1b — bone) is started on goserelin. Two weeks later: worsening bone pain. Most likely explanation?

- A) Disease progression
- B) Tumour flare effect
- C) Hypercalcaemia of malignancy
- D) Spinal cord compression
- E) Osteoporotic fracture

**Answer: B — tumour flare.** GnRH agonists cause initial testosterone surge before downregulation (~2–4 weeks). Co-prescribe anti-androgen to prevent.

### Q: SBA 10 — A 48-year-old woman with VHL has bilateral renal masses (largest 4 cm right lower pole, smallest 1.5 cm left upper pole). Best management?

- A) Bilateral radical nephrectomy with dialysis
- B) Right radical, left active surveillance
- C) Bilateral partial nephrectomies
- D) Pazopanib
- E) Active surveillance of all lesions

**Answer: C — bilateral partial nephrectomies.** VHL = multifocal bilateral RCCs. Bilateral radical = lifelong dialysis. Nephron-sparing on each side, accepting future re-excisions.

### Q: SBA 11 — A 75-year-old reasonably mobile man has PSA 32, Gleason 4+5 (Grade Group 5), MRI shows extracapsular extension, bone scan: multiple sclerotic lumbar/pelvic foci. Stable angina. First-line management?

- A) Radical prostatectomy
- B) Salvage radiotherapy
- C) Hormone therapy (ADT)
- D) Active surveillance
- E) Cryotherapy

**Answer: C — ADT.** M1b bone mets = metastatic. Curative-intent local therapy is no longer the answer. ADT (LHRH agonist ± anti-androgen) is first-line.

### Q: SBA 12 — A 62-year-old fit man has a 4 cm enhancing renal mass. He has a single functioning kidney (other removed for trauma 20 years ago). Best management?

- A) Radical nephrectomy + lifelong dialysis
- B) Partial nephrectomy
- C) Active surveillance with serial CT
- D) Pazopanib
- E) Cryotherapy

**Answer: B — partial nephrectomy.** Single functioning kidney is an explicit indication for nephron-sparing. Cryotherapy reserved for unfit patients.
