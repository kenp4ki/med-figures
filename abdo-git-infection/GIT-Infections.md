## Unifying intuition

🧠 Diarrhoea is the symptom; the question every case asks is **which mechanism explains it**. Two main culprits in this lecture: hospital-acquired antibiotic-driven toxin-mediated colitis (*C. difficile*) and community-onset chronic immune-mediated colitis (UC). Both produce a 3-tier severity ladder, both have treatment ladders that mirror severity, and both can converge on toxic megacolon → perforation. The single rule that ties everything together: **always rule out *C. difficile* before escalating immunosuppression**.

## Card 1 — Clostridioides difficile colitis

### Q: Typical patient archetype for *C. difficile* colitis.
- Elderly, hospitalised or institutionalised
- Recent antibiotics (especially the "4 Cs": clindamycin, cephalosporins, co-amoxiclav, ciprofloxacin)
- Post-operative or post-procedural
- New-onset watery diarrhoea + abdominal pain + raised inflammatory markers

### Q: *C. difficile* — what kind of organism, and what's the toxic mechanism?
- Gram-positive, **spore-forming**, anaerobic bacillus
- Produces **toxin A** (enterotoxin) and **toxin B** (cytotoxin)
- Toxins damage colonic epithelium → inflammation → diarrhoea ± pseudomembranes
- Antibiotics wipe out protective microbiota → C. diff overgrows

### Q: Differential diagnoses for new-onset post-op diarrhoea — split by category.
- **Infectious**: *C. difficile*, *Klebsiella oxytoca*, *Clostridium perfringens*, *Salmonella spp*
- **Non-infectious**: antibiotic side effect (direct), post-infectious IBS, IBD, microscopic colitis, ischaemic colitis, coeliac
- Always rule out *C. difficile* first

### Q: First-line investigations for suspected *C. difficile*.
- **Stool sample for *C. difficile* toxin** (first-line, confirms active toxin production)
- Stool culture (excludes other infectious causes)
- AXR if concerned about colonic dilatation
- Endoscopy avoided in active fulminant disease (perforation risk)

📎 Figure GASTRO-L7.1 — AXR Day 4: early colonic dilatation

⚠️ Endoscopy is **deferred** in active fulminant *C. diff* colitis — the colonic wall is thin and inflamed, and insufflation risks perforation. Diagnose on stool toxin and image with AXR/CT instead.

### Q: *C. difficile* severity stratification — the thresholds.

| Severity | WCC | Creatinine | Other |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-severe | < 15 | < 150 | No systemic toxicity |
| **Severe** | **> 15** | **> 150** | Systemic toxicity |
| Fulminant | Either | Either | Hypotension/shock, ileus, **toxic megacolon** |

⭐ The **15 / 150** numbers are classic SBA fodder. WCC and creatinine together stratify severity in non-fulminant disease; shock or megacolon override and define fulminant.

### Q: *C. difficile* — universal management steps regardless of severity.
- **Infection control**: side room, soap-and-water hand wash (alcohol gel does not kill spores)
- **Stop the inciting antibiotic**
- IV fluids, electrolyte correction, nutritional support

### Q: *C. difficile* — antibiotic therapy by severity.
- **Non-severe → severe**: oral **vancomycin** OR **fidaxomicin** OR metronidazole
- **Severe / fulminant**: antibiotic + supportive + early surgical consult
- **Recurrent**: consider faecal microbiota transplantation (FMT)

### Q: Why oral, not IV, vancomycin for *C. difficile*?
- *C. difficile* lives in the colonic lumen
- Oral vancomycin is **poorly absorbed** systemically → stays in lumen at high concentration
- IV vancomycin doesn't reach the colonic lumen reliably
- Counter-intuitive: poor bioavailability is the point

### Q: *C. difficile* — 5 indications for surgery (disease-agnostic).
- **Colonic perforation**
- **Necrosis or full-thickness ischaemia**
- Intra-abdominal hypertension or **abdominal compartment syndrome**
- **Peritonitis or worsening abdominal exam despite adequate medical therapy**
- **End-organ failure**

📎 Figure GASTRO-L7.3 — Erect CXR: free air under right hemidiaphragm (perforation)

### Q: Toxic megacolon — definition and AXR features.
- Severe colonic inflammation + transverse colon dilatation **> 6 cm** + systemic toxicity
- Loss of haustral pattern
- "Mucosal islands" — surviving mucosa pokes into dilated lumen
- Wall thinning → perforation risk

### Q: Pseudomembranous colitis — recognition.
- Most often associated with *C. difficile* infection
- Manifestation of severe colonic disease
- **Yellow-white plaques** on the mucosa (fibrin, mucus, dead epithelium, neutrophils)
- Confirmed on endoscopy ± biopsy (in stable patients)

## Card 2 — Ulcerative colitis

### Q: Typical patient archetype for ulcerative colitis.
- Young adult (peak 15–30, second peak 50–70)
- Chronic (months) bloody mucousy diarrhoea + urgency
- Recent smoking cessation is a classic UC clue (smoking is **protective** for UC)
- No travel; otherwise healthy

### Q: UC pathology — defining features.
- **Continuous** inflammation (no skip lesions)
- **Mucosal/submucosal only** (not transmural)
- **Always involves the rectum**, extends proximally a variable distance
- **No granulomas** on histology

### Q: UC vs Crohn's — the SBA discrimination.

| Feature | UC | Crohn's |
|---|---|---|
| Distribution | Continuous, rectum upwards | Skip lesions, anywhere mouth-to-anus |
| Depth | Mucosal/submucosal | **Transmural** |
| Granulomas | Absent | **Present** |
| Smoking | Protective | Risk factor |

📎 Figure GASTRO-L7.5 — UC on colonoscopy: continuous erythema, friability, loss of vascular pattern

### Q: First-line investigations for chronic bloody diarrhoea.
- Stool *C. difficile* toxin (rule out before any escalation)
- Stool culture (exclude infectious mimics)
- **Faecal calprotectin** — distinguishes IBD from IBS
- **FIT** (faecal immunochemical test) — screens for colorectal pathology
- Endoscopy (flex sig or colonoscopy) with biopsy — confirms diagnosis, defines extent

### Q: UC severity — Truelove-Witts cut-offs.

| Severity | BMs/day | Systemic | Anaemia | ESR/CRP |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mild | 4 | None | None | Normal |
| Moderate | > 4 | Minimal | Mild | Mild rise |
| **Severe** | **> 6** | **Yes** | **Significant** | **Raised**, weight loss |

⭐ **> 6 BMs/day with systemic toxicity = severe.** This single threshold drives the entire acute severe UC algorithm.

### Q: UC management ladder — escalate as severity escalates.
- **5-ASA** (mesalazine) — first-line for mild-moderate; PR for distal, PO for proximal, both in combination if needed
- **Corticosteroids** (prednisolone PO; IV hydrocortisone for severe) — induce remission acutely
- **Immunomodulators** — azathioprine (steroid-sparing maintenance)
- **Biologics** — infliximab (anti-TNFα) when steroids/immunomodulators fail
- **Colectomy** — failure of medical therapy or complications; curative for UC

### Q: Pre-azathioprine workup — what test is essential and why?
- **TPMT** (thiopurine methyltransferase) — enzyme that metabolises azathioprine
- Patients with low/absent TPMT → profound myelosuppression on standard doses
- Check TPMT first → dose-reduce or avoid if deficient
- Plus baseline FBC, LFT, U&E, CRP

### Q: Pre-biologic (anti-TNF) workup — what tests are essential?
- **Tuberculosis screen** (CXR + IGRA) — anti-TNF reactivates latent TB
- **Hepatitis B/C, HIV** — reactivation risk
- **Varicella status** — disseminated VZV risk in immunosuppressed
- **Vaccinations updated** — live vaccines contraindicated AFTER immunosuppression

### Q: Acute severe UC algorithm — the steps.
- **Rule out infection / *C. difficile*** before escalation
- **Imaging** (AXR/CT) to exclude toxic megacolon and perforation
- **Surgical consult early** — they need to know the patient exists
- **IV corticosteroids × 3 days** (the "3-day rule")
- **No response → infliximab rescue** (or ciclosporin) while continuing azathioprine
- **Still no response → colectomy**

### Q: Anti-TNF biologic — what are the major side effect categories?
- **Infection** — especially **TB reactivation**, opportunistic infections
- **Malignancy / lymphoma** — especially with combination immunomodulator therapy
- **Demyelinating disease, including PML** (JC virus reactivation)
- **Bone marrow suppression**, hepatotoxicity, **CHF** worsening
- Autoimmunity / immunogenicity (anti-drug antibodies, drug-induced lupus)
- Infusion / injection-site reactions

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: The 3-tier severity ladder — what's the universal scaffold?
- Count **stool frequency** per day
- Check **inflammatory markers** (WCC, CRP) and **renal function** (creat, AKI)
- Look for **systemic toxicity** (HR, BP, fever)
- Treatment intensity mirrors severity tier
- Same scaffold for *C. diff*, UC, and most colitides

### Q: The diarrhoea DDx scaffold — what are the two columns?
- **Infectious**: *C. difficile*, *Salmonella*, *Shigella*, *E. coli*, *Klebsiella oxytoca*, *Clostridium perfringens*, *Campylobacter*
- **Non-infectious**: IBD, microscopic colitis, ischaemic colitis, coeliac, post-infectious IBS, antibiotics (direct), haemorrhoids
- Same scaffold every case; epidemiology and symptoms tick boxes

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern**: when a colitis patient deteriorates, walk this checklist — (1) is *C. difficile* excluded? (2) is there imaging evidence of megacolon or perforation? (3) does the abdomen show new peritonism (worse on movement, generalised tenderness, lying still)? (4) which surgical indication is now met? Same checklist whether the underlying disease is *C. diff* or severe UC. The 5 surgical indications are disease-agnostic.

### Q: Why is the *C. diff* toxin test relevant in UC?
- Rules **out** *C. diff* before escalating immunosuppression
- Adding steroids and biologics on top of unrecognised *C. diff* worsens it
- The test does opposite jobs in the two cards: confirms diagnosis (Card 1); excludes superinfection before escalation (Card 2)

## SBAs

### Q: SBA — 75M day 6 post-laparotomy on co-amoxiclav develops 5×/day watery diarrhoea, WCC 13, Creat 130. First investigation?
- **Stool sample for *C. difficile* toxin**
- Recent antibiotics + post-op + watery diarrhoea + raised WCC = *C. diff* until proven otherwise
- CT identifies complications but the patient is stable
- Colonoscopy contraindicated in active fulminant disease

### Q: SBA — Confirmed *C. diff*, WCC 22, Creat 240, BP 90/55, HR 130, AXR shows colonic dilatation. Severity?
- **Fulminant**
- Hypotension + likely toxic megacolon override the WCC/Creat alone
- WCC > 15 + Creat > 150 alone would be severe
- Mild/recurrent are not applicable here

### Q: SBA — 28F starting azathioprine for steroid-dependent UC. Most important pre-treatment test?
- **TPMT level**
- Identifies patients at risk of azathioprine-induced myelosuppression
- Hepatitis serology and TB screen are more critical pre-biologics
- Calprotectin is for IBD activity monitoring, not pre-treatment

### Q: SBA — 26F with UC, 8 bloody BMs/day, anaemic, tachycardic, CRP 90. *C. diff* negative, AXR no megacolon. Started IV hydrocortisone. After 3 days, no improvement. Next step?
- **Commence infliximab** (rescue therapy)
- Acute severe UC unresponsive to IV steroids = textbook trigger for infliximab/ciclosporin
- Colectomy comes after rescue therapy fails, not before
- Continuing or downgrading steroids is futile

### Q: SBA — Histology: continuous mucosal/submucosal inflammation rectum to splenic flexure, no granulomas. Diagnosis?
- **Ulcerative colitis**
- Continuous + rectum + mucosal-only + no granulomas = UC
- Crohn's would have skip lesions, transmural inflammation, granulomas
- Pseudomembranous would show pseudomembranes acutely, not chronic continuous inflammation

### Q: SBA — UC patient develops pneumonitis 3 months after starting infliximab. CXR concerning for TB. Which pre-treatment test would have identified the risk?
- **Tuberculosis screen (CXR + IGRA)**
- Anti-TNF biologics reactivate latent TB
- TPMT is for azathioprine, not biologics
- Hep B and HIV are also part of pre-biologic workup but don't address TB specifically

### Q: SBA — Severe *C. diff* colitis. Most appropriate antibiotic?
- **Oral vancomycin**
- Reaches colonic lumen at high concentration; poorly absorbed systemically
- IV vancomycin doesn't reach the lumen
- Co-amoxiclav and ciprofloxacin are common *C. diff*-precipitating agents

### Q: SBA — Confirmed *C. diff* now with peritonism and free air under R hemidiaphragm. Next step?
- **Urgent surgical consultation for laparotomy / colectomy**
- Two of the 5 surgical indications met: perforation and peritonitis
- Escalating antibiotics doesn't fix a perforation
- FMT is for recurrent disease in stable patients, never in perforation

### Q: SBA — Cross-lecture: 78F day 4 post-SBO repair, on co-amoxiclav, now with 6×/day watery diarrhoea, central crampy pain, distension. Which two framework points combine to drive first management?
- **L4 SOCRATES** — central colicky pain in a patient with bowel pathology, no signs of obstruction recurrence
- **L7 epidemiology** — recent antibiotic + post-op + nursing home + watery diarrhoea = *C. diff*
- First step: stool toxin assay + isolation + stop the inciting antibiotic
- Pattern: every colitis case starts with both frameworks together

### Q: SBA — UC patient on azathioprine flares with 12 bloody BMs/day, generalised tenderness, lying still, CRP 200. Single most important investigation before escalating?
- **Stool *C. difficile* toxin**
- Cross-lecture rule: rule out *C. diff* before escalating immunosuppression
- Lying still and generalised tenderness signal possible perforation — image in parallel
- Negative *C. diff* gives permission to escalate; positive changes whole management
