## Unifying intuition

🧠 Three principles derive almost every fact: **embryology → location**, **structural type → character**, **anatomy → radiation**. Visceral pain reports to one of three midline zones until parietal peritoneum is touched, when it switches to somatic and localises. Hollow tubes peristalse → colicky; solid organs distend → constant. Memorising lists without these principles is what makes the topic feel shapeless.

## SOCRATES framework

### Q: What does SOCRATES stand for?
- **S**ite, **O**nset, **C**haracter, **R**adiation
- **A**ssociations, **T**ime course, **E**xacerbating/relieving, **S**everity

## Visceral vs somatic + embryology

### Q: How does visceral pain differ from somatic pain?
- **Visceral**: stretch / inflammation / ischaemia → autonomic afferents → poorly localised, midline, dull
- **Somatic**: parietal peritoneum and abdominal wall → spinal nerves → sharp, localised, worse on movement
- **Pattern**: visceral first, then somatic when peritoneum is involved

### Q: Map foregut / midgut / hindgut to limits, blood supply, and referral zone.

| Division | Limits | Artery | Refers to |
|---|---|---|---|
| Foregut | Distal oesophagus → proximal half 2nd duodenum | Coeliac trunk | Epigastrium (T5–T9) |
| Midgut | Distal half 2nd duodenum → proximal 2/3 transverse colon | SMA | Umbilicus (T10) |
| Hindgut | Distal 1/3 transverse colon → rectum | IMA | Suprapubic (T11–L1) |

### Q: Why does appendicitis pain start umbilical and shift to RIF?
- Appendix is midgut → visceral pain refers to T10 (umbilical)
- Inflammation reaches parietal peritoneum → somatic afferents fire
- Pain localises sharply over the appendix in RIF
- Migration is the diagnostic stamp of appendicitis

### Q: Pain referred to the suprapubic region — which embryological division?
- **Hindgut** — distal 1/3 transverse colon to rectum, supplied by IMA
- Includes sigmoid colon, rectum
- Hypogastric pain in a young woman: also consider bladder, uterus, adnexae (not embryological hindgut, but same surface region)

## Surface anatomy — 9 regions

### Q: List the organs producing pain in each abdominal region.
- **R hypochondrium**: gallbladder, liver
- **Epigastrium**: stomach, duodenum, pancreas
- **L hypochondrium**: pancreas (tail), spleen
- **R/L lumbar (flank)**: kidney
- **Umbilical**: small bowel, caecum, retroperitoneal
- **R iliac fossa**: appendix, caecum; **Hypogastric**: bladder, uterus, adnexae; **L iliac fossa**: sigmoid

### Q: Pain in the LIF — what's likely?
- **Sigmoid colon** pathology
- Classical: diverticulitis (older patient, fever, change in bowel habit)
- Also: left-sided UC (chronic, bloody, urgency)

### Q: Epigastric pain that bores through to the back — which organs?
- **Pancreas (head)** — retroperitoneal, directly anterior to the lumbar spine
- Posterior penetrating duodenal or gastric ulcer
- AAA — must exclude in elderly with cardiovascular collapse

## Pain character — colicky vs constant

### Q: What's the rule for predicting colicky vs constant?
- Hollow muscular tubes that peristalse around obstruction → **colicky**
- Solid organs or capsules that stretch → **constant**
- Same anatomical region can give either — depends on which structure (e.g. ureter vs kidney parenchyma)

### Q: Group the abdominal organs by pain character.
- **Colicky**: ureter (stone), bowel (any obstruction), biliary tree
- **Constant**: liver, spleen, kidney parenchyma/capsule
- Single rule: ask "is it a tube or a capsule?"

⚠️ Kidney appears in both groups: **ureter (the tube) = colicky**; **kidney capsule = constant**. Pyelonephritis stem says "constant loin pain"; stone stem says "writhing colicky pain, cannot lie still."

### Q: How do you distinguish ureteric colic from pyelonephritis on history?
- Both: loin pain
- **Ureteric colic**: colicky, sudden, radiates loin-to-groin, cannot lie still
- **Pyelonephritis**: constant, fever, dysuria, lies still

## Pain radiation

### Q: Map radiation by organ.

| Organ | Radiates to | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Gallbladder | Back/right + R shoulder tip | Phrenic nerve C3–C5 |
| Pancreas (head) | Straight to back | Retroperitoneal |
| Pancreas (tail) | Back and **left** | Tail sits at splenic hilum |
| Kidney/ureter | Loin to groin | Genitofemoral nerve, T10–L1 |

### Q: Why does gallbladder pain refer to the right shoulder tip?
- Gallbladder sits under the right hemidiaphragm
- Diaphragmatic irritation travels via the **phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5)**
- Phrenic refers to dermatomes of the shoulder tip
- **Pattern:** any subdiaphragmatic irritation (gallbladder, hepatic abscess, splenic rupture) → ipsilateral shoulder

### Q: Why "loin to groin" for ureteric stones?
- Ureter passes from kidney through retroperitoneum to bladder
- Shares dermatomes (T10–L1) with the genitofemoral nerve
- Genitofemoral supplies groin, scrotum/labia
- The pain follows the ureter's anatomical path

## Atlas — Patient 1: appendicitis

### Q: Appendicitis — full SOCRATES profile.
- **S** central → RIF; **O** gradual; **C** constant dull ache
- **R** none; **A** anorexia, nausea, fever
- **T** first episode; **E** worse on movement (peritonism); **S** builds gradually

### Q: Appendicitis — what's the underlying mechanism?
- Luminal obstruction (faecolith, lymphoid hyperplasia)
- Mucus and bacterial overgrowth → wall inflammation
- If untreated: perforation → abscess or generalised peritonitis

📎 Figure GASTRO-L4.1 — Appendix positions and frequencies

### Q: Appendix positions and frequencies — what's the breakdown?
- **Retrocecal — 64%** (commonest)
- **Pelvic — 32%**
- Paracecal 2%, preileal 1%, postileal 0.5%

⭐ Retrocecal 64% means atypical presentations are common: less anterior tenderness, positive **psoas sign** (pain on passive right hip extension because the inflamed appendix overlies psoas major).

## Atlas — Patient 2: bowel obstruction

### Q: Bowel obstruction — full SOCRATES profile.
- **S** central; **O** gradual; **C** colicky
- **R** none; **A** vomiting, **absolute constipation**, distension
- **T** previous colicky episodes; **E** flatus relieves; **S** moderate

### Q: What are the 4 cardinal features of bowel obstruction?
- **Pain** (colicky, central — midgut/hindgut visceral)
- **Vomiting** (early in SBO, late in LBO)
- **Distension** (early in LBO, late in SBO)
- **Absolute constipation** — no stool, no flatus

### Q: SBO vs LBO — vomiting vs distension timing.
- **SBO**: early vomiting (proximal, no reservoir); distension is late
- **LBO**: early distension (large reservoir); vomiting is late, eventually faeculent
- Competent ileocaecal valve makes LBO a closed-loop emergency

## Atlas — Patient 3: ureteric colic

### Q: Ureteric colic — full SOCRATES profile.
- **S** loin; **O** **sudden**; **C** colicky
- **R** **to groin** (loin-to-groin); **A** vomiting, ± haematuria
- **T** previous colicky episodes; **E** **cannot find comfortable position**; **S** 10/10

### Q: Where does a ureteric stone classically impact?
- **Pelvi-ureteric junction** (top, kidney → ureter)
- **Pelvic brim** (ureter crosses iliac vessels)
- **Vesico-ureteric junction** (bottom; commonest distal lodging point)

## Atlas — Patient 4: biliary colic

### Q: Biliary colic — full SOCRATES profile.
- **S** RUQ; **O** sudden; **C** colicky
- **R** **right shoulder** + back/right; **A** nausea, indigestion
- **T/E** postprandial, **fatty foods** (CCK → gallbladder contraction); **S** up to 10/10

### Q: Three named gallstone locations and why each matters.
- **Cholelithiasis** — stone in gallbladder → biliary colic, cholecystitis if persistent
- **Hepatolithiasis** — stone in intrahepatic ducts → uncommon, can cause cholangitis
- **Choledocholithiasis** — stone in CBD → obstructive jaundice, ascending cholangitis, gallstone pancreatitis

## Cross-cutting discrimination matrix

### Q: Compare the 4 cases on the discriminating SOCRATES axes.

| Feature | Appendicitis | Bowel obstruction | Ureteric colic | Biliary colic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Site → final | Central → RIF | Central | Loin | RUQ |
| Onset / character | Gradual / constant | Gradual / colicky | Sudden / colicky | Sudden / colicky |
| Radiation | None | None | Loin to groin | R shoulder + back |
| Body language | Lies still | Indifferent | Cannot lie still | Indifferent |

### Q: What's the single best discriminator between appendicitis and ureteric colic?
- **Body language**: appendicitis lies still (peritonism); ureteric colic writhes
- Both can present as right lower abdominal pain
- Both can have nausea, vomiting, severe pain
- Stillness vs restlessness is the cleanest exam discriminator

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern**: read every abdominal pain stem in this order — (1) which embryological zone fits the location? (2) hollow or solid → colicky or constant? (3) where does it radiate, and via which dermatome or nerve? (4) does the body language fit peritonism (still) or colic (writhing)? Four answers usually pin the diagnosis before any lab value.

## SBAs

### Q: SBA — 22F with 12h umbilical pain that has localised to RIF, anorexic, T 37.8, lying still. Most likely diagnosis?
- **Acute appendicitis**
- Migration (umbilical → RIF) + anorexia + low-grade fever + peritonism
- Right ureteric colic gives loin-to-groin and the patient writhes
- Ovarian torsion is sudden severe RIF without the migration pattern

### Q: SBA — 70M, 24h central crampy pain, vomited ×5, no BO ×3 days, distended; previous laparotomy. Cause?
- **Adhesional small bowel obstruction**
- All 4 cardinal features in a patient with prior abdominal surgery
- Adhesions are the commonest cause of SBO post-laparotomy
- Sigmoid volvulus has massive distension with less prominent vomiting

### Q: SBA — 50F with severe RUQ pain after dinner, radiating to R shoulder, restless. Which structure?
- **Gallbladder** (biliary colic from cholelithiasis)
- RUQ + postprandial + fatty trigger + R shoulder via phrenic
- Liver gives constant capsular pain, not postprandial colic
- Right kidney gives loin pain with groin radiation, not shoulder

### Q: SBA — 28M, 2h severe L flank pain radiating to L groin, cannot keep still, vomited ×2. Diagnosis?
- **Left ureteric stone**
- Triad: loin-to-groin + restlessness + sudden severe colicky
- Pyelonephritis is constant with fever and the patient lies still
- Splenic infarct gives LUQ constant pain, not loin-to-groin

### Q: SBA — Best feature distinguishing appendicitis from right ureteric colic?
- **Whether the patient is restless or still**
- Both can share location, severity, and nausea
- Peritonism (appendicitis with parietal involvement) → lies still
- Ureteric colic → writhes, cannot find comfortable position

### Q: SBA — Patient with epigastric pain boring through to the back, raised lipase. Most likely?
- **Acute pancreatitis**
- Pancreas is retroperitoneal → pain bores straight through
- Lipase confirms (more specific than amylase)
- AAA can radiate back but presents with cardiovascular collapse and pulsatile mass
