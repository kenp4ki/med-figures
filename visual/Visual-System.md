## Foundations: retina, photoreceptors, optics

### Q: What is the order of neurons in the retina, from sclera inward?

- **1st-order: photoreceptors** (rods, cones) — outer layer, closest to sclera.
- **2nd-order: bipolar cells** — middle layer, local signal processing.
- **3rd-order: retinal ganglion cells** — inner layer, axons form the optic nerve.

⚠️ Light travels through all retinal layers *before* hitting photoreceptors at the back.

📎 Figure L1.1 — Retinal layered structure: photoreceptors → bipolar → ganglion cells

### Q: Rods vs cones — how do they differ?

| Feature | Rods | Cones |
|---|---|---|
| Sensitivity | 100× higher | Lower |
| Speed | Slow | Fast |
| Function | Night vision, peripheral, movement | Day, fine detail, colour |
| Count / location | 120M, everywhere except fovea | 6M, concentrated in fovea |

📎 Figure L1.2 — Rod and cone photoreceptor structure

### Q: Why does macular damage cause poor reading but preserved navigation?

- Macula = highest cone density → loss kills central detail and colour.
- Peripheral retina (rod-rich) is intact → patient can still see shapes and movement.
- This is why **visual acuity** (central) and **visual field** (peripheral) are tested separately.

### Q: Where is the macula in relation to the optic disc?

- Macula sits **temporal** to the optic disc.
- Fovea = the very centre of the macula = highest cone density.
- Optic disc = blind spot (no photoreceptors).

### Q: In myopia, where do parallel rays focus and how is it corrected?

- Focus **anterior** to the retina.
- Cause: **excessive long globe** (axial myopia, more common) or excessive refractive power.
- Correction: **concave (diverging) lens**.

📎 Figure L1.3 — Myopia: focus anterior to retina, long globe

### Q: In hyperopia, where do parallel rays focus and how is it corrected?

- Focus **posterior** to the retina.
- Cause: **excessive short globe** (axial hyperopia, more common) or insufficient refractive power.
- Correction: **convex (converging) lens**.

📎 Figure L1.4 — Hyperopia: focus posterior to retina, short globe

### Q: What is presbyopia and how is it corrected?

- Age-related (~40+) loss of lens elasticity → loss of accommodation.
- Distance vision intact; near vision blurred.
- Corrected with **convex reading glasses**.

### Q: What are the 3 components of the near response triad?

1. **Pupillary miosis** (sphincter pupillae contracts) → increases depth of field.
2. **Convergence** (medial recti contract) → eyes align on near object.
3. **Accommodation** (ciliary muscle contracts) → lens rounds up, more refractive power.

All three are driven by **parasympathetic fibres riding along CN III**.

📎 Figure L1.5 — Near response triad anatomy

### Q: What happens to the ciliary muscle, zonules, and lens during accommodation?

- Ciliary muscle **contracts** (the muscle ring shrinks in diameter).
- Zonules **slacken** (releases tension on the lens).
- Lens **rounds up** → more refractive power for near vision.

⚠️ Counter-intuitive: ciliary muscle contraction *releases* the lens — does not squeeze it. Slide-30 MCQ tests exactly this.

📎 Figure L1.6 — Accommodation: ciliary muscle contracted vs relaxed

### Q: Symptoms of myopia vs hyperopia?

- **Myopia:** blurred distance vision; squinting at distance; headache.
- **Hyperopia:** near blur, worse when tired or in dim light; **asthenopic symptoms** — eye pain, frontal headache, burning eyes.

## Cascade 1 — Visual pathway

```
Visual field (world)
       ↓   (lens inverts image L↔R, U↔D)
Retina  (nasal half + temporal half of each eye)
       ↓
(1) Optic nerve (CN II)              ← one eye's fibres only
       ↓
(2) Optic chiasm                     ← nasal fibres CROSS, temporal fibres DON'T
       ↓
(3) Optic tract                      ← each tract = opposite half of visual world
       ↓
(4) Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) ← thalamic relay; synapse to 4th-order
       ↓
(5) Optic radiation                  ← fans through parietal & temporal lobes
       ↓
(6) Primary visual cortex            ← occipital, striate
       ↓
(7) Extra-striate cortex             ← higher processing
```

📎 Figure L1.7 — Visual pathway anatomy from eye to cortex

### Q: Which retinal fibres cross at the chiasm and what visual field do they carry?

- **Nasal retina fibres cross** → carry the **temporal visual field**.
- **Temporal retina fibres do not cross** → carry the **nasal visual field**.
- 53% of all ganglion fibres cross.

📎 Figure L1.8 — Optic chiasm: nasal retina crosses, temporal does not

### Q: Why does a pituitary tumour cause bitemporal hemianopia?

- Pituitary sits directly **under the chiasm**.
- Tumour grows up, pushes into the **midline of the chiasm** where crossing fibres lie.
- Crossing fibres = nasal retinas of both eyes = **temporal fields of both eyes** lost.

📎 Figure L1.9 — Sagittal MRI: pituitary lesion compressing chiasm

### Q: What field defect does an optic nerve lesion cause and why?

- **Monocular field loss** (one eye only).
- No fibres have crossed yet — only one eye's worth of fibres in the optic nerve.

### Q: What field defect does an optic tract lesion cause?

- **Contralateral homonymous hemianopia.**
- Each tract carries the opposite visual world; tract lesion takes out the opposite half of vision in both eyes.
- **No** macular sparing (distinguishes from cortical lesion).

### Q: Why does a PCA stroke cause homonymous hemianopia *with macular sparing*?

- Cortical macular representation at the occipital pole has **dual blood supply** from posterior cerebral arteries on **both sides**.
- Unilateral PCA stroke cannot fully infarct the macular cortex.
- Result: contralateral hemianopia with central vision (macula) preserved.

📎 Figure L1.10 — Homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing: cortical lesion

🧠 **Reusable pattern — localise any visual field defect in three questions.** (1) One eye or both? — one eye = pre-chiasmal. (2) If both, bitemporal or homonymous? — bitemporal = chiasm; homonymous = post-chiasmal. (3) If homonymous, macular sparing? — yes = visual cortex (PCA stroke); no = tract or radiation.

### Q: Lesion site → field defect map

| Lesion | Defect | Classic cause |
|---|---|---|
| Optic nerve | Monocular field loss | Optic neuritis, compression |
| Chiasm (central) | Bitemporal hemianopia | Pituitary tumour |
| Optic tract | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia | Stroke, tumour |
| Visual cortex | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia *with macular sparing* | PCA stroke |

📎 Figure L1.11 — Visual pathway lesions (1–7) and corresponding field defects

### Q: Why do post-chiasmal lesions spare the pupillary light reflex?

- Pupil fibres branch off the optic tract **before the LGN**.
- Lesions in radiations or cortex are downstream of this branch point.
- Visual field is lost; pupils still react normally.

## Cascade 2 — Pupillary light reflex

```
Light enters eye
      ↓
Retinal ganglion cells (pupil-specific subset)
      ↓
Optic nerve (CN II)
      ↓
Optic chiasm (partial decussation)
      ↓
Optic tract → fibres branch OUT before LGN (posterior third)
      ↓
Pretectal nucleus (midbrain)
      ↓
   SPLITS  → LEFT  Edinger-Westphal nucleus
           → RIGHT Edinger-Westphal nucleus
      ↓                          ↓
CN III (parasympathetic out)    CN III (parasympathetic out)
      ↓                          ↓
Ciliary ganglion                Ciliary ganglion
      ↓                          ↓
Short posterior ciliary nerves
      ↓                          ↓
Sphincter pupillae → BOTH pupils constrict
```

📎 Figure L1.12 — Pupillary reflex arc: afferent and efferent pathways

### Q: Why do both pupils constrict when light is shone in only one eye?

- The pretectal nucleus projects to **both** Edinger-Westphal nuclei (bilateral).
- Both EW nuclei fire → both CN III efferents activate → both pupils constrict.
- This is the anatomical basis of the consensual reflex.

### Q: What is the difference between the direct and consensual reflex?

- **Direct:** pupil of the eye into which light is shone constricts.
- **Consensual:** pupil of the *other* eye constricts.
- Both happen simultaneously in a normal patient.

📎 Figure L1.13 — Direct vs consensual light reflex

### Q: Pattern recognition for afferent vs efferent defect

| Scenario | Light in R | Light in L |
|---|---|---|
| Normal | Both constrict | Both constrict |
| **R afferent defect** | Neither constricts | Both constrict |
| **R efferent defect** | L only | L only |

📎 Figure L1.14 — Normal vs efferent vs afferent defect pupil responses

🧠 **Reusable pattern — afferent vs efferent.** Ask two questions. (1) Is one pupil *always* broken regardless of which eye is lit? → that pupil's efferent is broken. (2) Does light in one eye fail to make *anything* happen, while the other eye works normally for both pupils? → that eye's afferent is broken.

### Q: What is the swinging torch test and what does it detect?

- Alternate light between eyes; watch both pupils each time.
- Detects a **relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)** — partial unilateral optic nerve / retinal lesion.
- Pathognomonic: **both pupils paradoxically dilate** when light swings to the affected eye.
- Most sensitive, only objective test for unilateral optic nerve lesion.

📎 Figure L1.15 — Swinging torch test: paradoxical dilation in RAPD

### Q: Why is RAPD absent if both eyes are equally affected?

- The test relies on a **difference** between the two sides.
- Symmetrical bilateral disease → both eyes drive the same (weak) constriction → no relative change → no RAPD.

⚠️ "No RAPD" does not mean "normal optic nerves". Bilateral symmetric optic neuropathy is invisible to this test.

### Q: Causes of an RAPD?

- **Optic nerve lesion** — optic neuritis (MS), traumatic optic neuropathy, glaucoma, ischaemic optic neuropathy.
- **Severe retinal disease** — large retinal detachment, central retinal artery/vein occlusion.
- Anything along the **anterior visual pathway** that's asymmetric.

## Atlas — extraocular muscles + cranial nerve palsies

### Territory map

- **6 muscles, 3 nerves.** Mnemonic **LR6 SO4** — Lateral Rectus by VI, Superior Oblique by IV, everything else by III.
- **CN III (oculomotor):** SR, IR, MR, IO + levator palpebrae superioris + parasympathetic to pupil/ciliary.
- **CN IV (trochlear):** SO only.
- **CN VI (abducens):** LR only.

📎 Figure L1.16 — Extraocular muscles of the right eye (anterior view)

📎 Figure L1.17 — Extraocular muscles with cranial nerve innervation

### Q: Attachment and isolated action of each rectus muscle?

| Muscle | Attachment | Isolated action | Nerve |
|---|---|---|---|
| Superior rectus | 12 o'clock | Elevates | III |
| Inferior rectus | 6 o'clock | Depresses | III |
| Medial rectus | Nasal | Adducts | III |
| Lateral rectus | Temporal | Abducts | VI |

### Q: Why do the obliques have counter-intuitive actions?

- **Superior oblique** attaches high-temporal but runs through the **trochlea** (a nasal pulley). Effective line of pull is from the nose → depresses + abducts ("down and out").
- **Inferior oblique** attaches low-nasal, no pulley → elevates + abducts ("up and out").
- On H-test the eye is **adducted** to align oblique pull vertically: SO depresses adducted eye; IO elevates adducted eye.

### Q: Cardinal positions of gaze and the muscle for each?

| Direction | Main muscle | Nerve |
|---|---|---|
| Up-out | Superior rectus | III |
| Up-in | Inferior oblique | III |
| Down-out | Inferior rectus | III |
| Down-in | Superior oblique | IV |

Plus: straight out = LR (VI); straight in = MR (III).

📎 Figure L1.18 — Cardinal positions of gaze (H-test) with muscle and CN

### Q: CN III palsy — what stops working and what's left?

- **Out:** SR, IR, MR, IO, levator palpebrae, parasympathetic to pupil + ciliary.
- **Working:** LR (VI) and SO (IV) only.

### Q: CN III palsy — clinical signs?

- Eye position: **down and out** (unopposed LR + SO).
- Lid: **complete ptosis** (levator paralysed).
- Pupil: potentially **dilated, unreactive** (parasympathetic out).
- Diplopia; impaired accommodation.

📎 Figure L1.19 — Right 3rd nerve palsy: muscles knocked out (LR6 SO4)

### Q: Pupil-involving vs pupil-sparing CN III palsy — anatomical basis?

- **Parasympathetic fibres run on the OUTSIDE** of the nerve, supplied by surface pial vessels.
- **Motor fibres run in the CORE**, supplied by penetrating vasa nervorum.
- External compression hits outside first → pupil involved.
- Internal ischaemia hits core first → pupil spared.

⚠️ Pupil-involving CN III palsy = surgical emergency. Think **PCom artery aneurysm**. Urgent CT/MR angiography, neurosurgical referral.

📎 Figure L1.20 — Pupil-involving (surgical) vs pupil-sparing (medical) 3rd nerve palsy

### Q: Medical vs surgical CN III palsy — causes and management

| Feature | Surgical | Medical |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | External compression | Microvascular ischaemia |
| Classic cause | PCom aneurysm | Diabetes, hypertension |
| Pupil | Dilated, unreactive | Spared, normal-reactive |
| Action | Urgent CTA/MRA, neurosurgery | Glycaemic/BP control, watch |

📎 Figure L1.21 — Circle of Willis: PCom artery next to oculomotor nerve

### Q: CN IV palsy — clinical signs?

- Eye drifts **up and in** at rest (SO out; IO + IR + MR pulls dominate).
- Vertical diplopia worst on **looking down and in** — reading, going downstairs.
- Compensatory **head tilt to opposite shoulder** to align the eyes.
- Common causes: trauma (long intracranial course), congenital, microvascular.

### Q: CN VI palsy — clinical signs?

- Affected eye **adducted** at rest (LR out; MR unopposed).
- **Cannot abduct** the affected eye on H-test.
- Horizontal diplopia, worst on looking toward the affected side.
- Common causes: **raised intracranial pressure** (false localising sign — long intracranial course), microvascular, pontine lesion.

📎 Figure L1.22 — Clinical appearance: 3rd, 4th, 6th nerve palsy (composite)

### Q: "Down and out" — two different uses?

- **Isolated SO action:** the muscle on its own depresses + abducts → "down and out".
- **3rd nerve palsy resting position:** the eye is held down-and-out by SO + LR (the only muscles still working).
- Same vector, different context — read the question carefully.

### Q: Why is CN VI the most common nerve affected in raised ICP?

- Longest intracranial course → stretches first when pressure pushes the brainstem down.
- Bilateral CN VI palsy in a headache patient = raised ICP until proven otherwise.
- Called a **false localising sign** — the lesion isn't local to CN VI itself.

🧠 **Reusable pattern — derive any nerve palsy from first principles.** (1) Name what the nerve innervates. (2) In palsy, those muscles are silent — antagonists pull unopposed. (3) The resting eye position is the vector sum of what's still working. Don't memorise palsy positions — derive them.

## Interleaved SBAs — discriminate between palsies and pupil defects

### Q: SBA 1 — 58yo man, ptosis right, eye down-and-out, dilated right pupil, normal consensual when light shone right. Most likely cause?

A) Diabetic microvascular CN III  
B) Right PCom aneurysm  
C) Right CN IV  
D) Right CN VI  
E) Right Horner's

**Answer: B.** Pupil-involving CN III palsy = surgical until proven otherwise. Urgent angiography, neurosurgical opinion.
- A: diabetic CN III is pupil-sparing.
- C: CN IV gives up-and-in eye with head tilt, no ptosis or pupil change.
- D: CN VI gives adducted eye, no ptosis.
- E: Horner's gives **miosis** + mild ptosis + anhidrosis — opposite pupil sign.

### Q: SBA 2 — 45yo woman, vertical diplopia worst going downstairs, head tilted right, left eye slightly elevated and adducted. Diagnosis?

A) Left CN III  
B) Left CN IV  
C) Left CN VI  
D) Left Horner's  
E) Left CN VII

**Answer: B.** Up-and-in eye + diplopia on downgaze + compensatory head tilt = trochlear (CN IV) palsy.
- A: would cause down-and-out + ptosis + pupil sign.
- C: would cause adduction at rest + horizontal diplopia.
- D: Horner's = miosis + mild ptosis, no diplopia.
- E: facial nerve doesn't move the eye.

### Q: SBA 3 — 72yo with pituitary macroadenoma; left eye cannot abduct past midline; pupils equal and normal, no ptosis. Affected nerve?

A) Left CN III  
B) Left CN IV  
C) Left CN VI  
D) Left optic nerve  
E) Right CN VI

**Answer: C.** Isolated abduction failure with no other findings = LR palsy = CN VI. Macroadenoma raises ICP and CN VI is most vulnerable.
- A: CN III would cause ptosis, pupil change, multiple movement defects.
- B: CN IV gives vertical diplopia.
- D: optic nerve = vision loss, not movement.
- E: right CN VI = right eye fails to abduct on rightward gaze.

### Q: SBA 4 — 65yo diabetic, sudden right ptosis, eye down-and-out, pupil 3mm and reactive (equal to left). Cause and next step?

A) PCom aneurysm; urgent CTA  
B) Microvascular CN III; optimise glucose, review  
C) Myasthenia gravis  
D) Horner's syndrome  
E) Graves' ophthalmopathy

**Answer: B.** Pupil-sparing CN III in a diabetic = classic medical microvascular palsy. Most resolve in 3–6 months.
- A: only if pupil were involved.
- C: MG never affects the pupil; tends to fluctuate during the day.
- D: Horner's has miosis, not down-and-out eye.
- E: Graves' causes lid retraction + proptosis, not ptosis.

### Q: SBA 5 — Right pupil dilated, fixed; light in right eye → left pupil constricts, right does not; light in left eye → left constricts, right does not. Lesion?

A) Right afferent only  
B) Right efferent only  
C) Combined defect  
D) Left afferent  
E) Normal

**Answer: B.** Right pupil always broken regardless of which eye is lit = right efferent. Left pupil works regardless of which eye is lit = afferent intact bilaterally.
- A would mean light in right eye does nothing to either pupil.
- C: afferent is clearly working (left pupil responds to right-eye stimulation).

### Q: SBA 6 — 28yo with MS, reduced vision right eye; on swinging torch from L to R both pupils dilate. What is this?

A) Right efferent defect  
B) Left afferent defect  
C) Right CN III palsy  
D) Right RAPD  
E) Post-chiasmal lesion

**Answer: D.** Paradoxical bilateral dilation on swing TO an eye = RAPD on that side. Classic for optic neuritis in MS.
- A: efferent defects don't paradoxically dilate.
- C: CN III gives a fixed dilated pupil regardless of light source.
- E: post-chiasmal lesions don't affect pupils (fibres branch off the tract before LGN).

### Q: SBA 7 — Sudden right homonymous hemianopia, preserved acuity, normal pupils. Site?

A) Left optic nerve  
B) Chiasm  
C) Left visual cortex (PCA stroke)  
D) Right optic tract  
E) Left Meyer's loop

**Answer: C.** Right field loss + macular sparing (preserved acuity) + normal pupils = left occipital cortex; PCA stroke.
- A: optic nerve = monocular.
- B: chiasm = bitemporal.
- D: right tract gives left field loss, not right.
- E: Meyer's loop gives quadrantanopia, not full hemianopia.

### Q: SBA 8 — 50yo with bitemporal hemianopia, amenorrhoea, headache. Mechanism of field defect?

A) Compression of temporal retinal fibres  
B) Compression of crossing fibres at chiasm  
C) Optic tract infiltration  
D) Cortical infarct  
E) RAPD

**Answer: B.** Pituitary macroadenoma compresses the chiasm midline → crossing fibres (from nasal retinas) damaged → temporal fields lost in both eyes. Amenorrhoea suggests prolactinoma.

### Q: SBA 9 — Which lesion gives homonymous hemianopia WITHOUT macular sparing?

A) Visual cortex pole  
B) Optic tract  
C) PCA stroke  
D) Optic radiation Meyer's loop  
E) Chiasm

**Answer: B.** Optic tract lesion gives homonymous hemianopia without macular sparing — there is no dual blood supply protection at this level.
- A, C: macular sparing is the cortical signature.
- D: gives quadrantanopia.
- E: gives bitemporal.

### Q: SBA 10 — In a complete CN III palsy, why is the pupil dilated rather than constricted?

A) Sympathetic damage  
B) Loss of parasympathetic via CN III; sympathetic unopposed  
C) Direct iris damage  
D) RAPD  
E) Horner's mechanism

**Answer: B.** Parasympathetic via CN III drives sphincter pupillae (constriction). Lose it → only dilator pupillae (sympathetic, intact) acts → pupil dilates.

### Q: SBA 11 — 70yo post-thyroid surgery presents with mild ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis on the right side of the face. Diagnosis?

A) Right CN III  
B) Right CN IV  
C) Right Horner's syndrome  
D) Right RAPD  
E) Right CN VI

**Answer: C.** Ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis = Horner's. Sympathetic chain damage. Mild ptosis (not complete — Müller's muscle is sympathetic, not levator). Pupil is **constricted**, not dilated — fastest discriminator from CN III.

### Q: SBA 12 — Patient with sudden-onset homonymous superior quadrantanopia after a temporal lobe stroke. Anatomical structure damaged?

A) Optic chiasm  
B) Optic tract  
C) Meyer's loop (lower optic radiation fibres in temporal lobe)  
D) Primary visual cortex  
E) Lateral geniculate nucleus

**Answer: C.** Lower radiation fibres carrying upper visual field pass forward through the temporal lobe (Meyer's loop). Damage here gives homonymous **superior** quadrantanopia ("pie in the sky"). Parietal lobe lesions damage upper radiation fibres → inferior quadrantanopia.
