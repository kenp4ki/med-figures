## Spoke 1 — Sigmoid volvulus → Hartmann's procedure

> 🧠 **Plugs into L1 Card 1 — Bowel Obstruction.** Specifically: aetiology table (slide 14), SBO vs LBO presentation (slide 15), surgical indications (slide 22), closed-loop concept.

### Cascade skeleton

```
History → SBO vs LBO?
  ↓ (LBO clues: absolute constipation early, no vomiting, marked distension)
Exam → hernial orifices clear, no scars, empty rectum
  ↓
AXR → coffee bean / bent inner-tube sign
  ↓
First-line: rigid sigmoidoscopy + flatus tube (left lateral position)
  ↓ FAILS
Risk untreated: necrosis (closed-loop, mesenteric blood supply cut)
  ↓
Hartmann's procedure: sigmoid colectomy + closed rectal stump + end colostomy
  ↓ ~6 months later
Hartmann's reversal
```

### Q: SBO vs LBO clue cluster — what locks "low" obstruction in Case 1?

- **Absolute constipation since onset** (no stool, no flatus)
- **No vomiting** so far
- **Marked distension**
- All three together → LBO

> 🧠 **Pattern:** Three-feature lock. Each individually isn't decisive; together they triangulate.

### Q: Why does the lecturer make a point of "no scars + clear hernial orifices"?

- Rules out the **2 commonest SBO causes**: adhesions, hernia
- Combined with LBO clinical pattern → case is settled as LBO
- Empty rectum on PR = obstruction is upstream, not at the rectum

### Q: Coffee bean / bent inner-tube sign — where, why, and what does it mean?

- AXR finding for **sigmoid volvulus**
- Enormously distended oval gas shadow looped on itself
- Both names interchangeable
- Haustra do not cross full width → confirms large bowel

📎 Figure L2.1 — Sigmoid volvulus AXR (erect + supine) — coffee bean sign

### Q: Sigmoid volvulus mechanism — in two parts?

- Sigmoid attached at narrow base to its mesentery → twists around base
- (1) Lumen obstructed at both ends of twist → **closed-loop obstruction**
- (2) Mesenteric vessels through twisted base compressed → **ischaemia of loop**

📎 Figure L2.2 — Sigmoid volvulus mechanism diagram

### Q: First-line management of sigmoid volvulus?

- Patient in **left lateral position**
- Pass rigid sigmoidoscope
- Advance large, well-lubricated **soft rubber rectal tube** along the scope
- Volvulus untwists → vast quantities of flatus + liquid faeces released

### Q: Conservative trial fails — why does this become a closed-loop emergency?

- Both ends of segment obstructed → fluid builds up with no exit
- Mesenteric vessels twisted → blood supply cut
- → necrosis → perforation
- No more conservative trial — straight to theatre

### Q: Hartmann's procedure — three steps?

- Resect the necrotic/twisted sigmoid colon
- Close the rectal stump (sutures or staples) — leave in place
- Bring the descending colon out as an **end colostomy**

📎 Figure L2.3 — Sigmoid colectomy resection line
📎 Figure L2.4 — Hartmann's procedure: gross specimen + post-op anatomy

### Q: Why no anastomosis at the time of Hartmann's?

- Bowel was **ischaemic** + likely **contaminated**
- Joining ischaemic or infected bowel ends → **high leak rate**
- Stoma now, rejoin later (Hartmann's reversal at ~6 months)

> 🧠 **Pattern:** Universal surgical principle — don't anastomose ischaemic, infected, or contaminated bowel. Recurs in Spoke 2 (damage control laparotomy) and L1 perforation surgery.

### Q: Anastomosis healing hierarchy — what heals best?

- **SB-SB > SB-LB > LB-LB**
- Small bowel is the most forgiving
- Large-to-large anastomosis is structurally weakest
- Heals worse with: infection, ischaemia, diabetes, smoking

### Q: SBA — 80-yr-old woman, sigmoid volvulus on AXR, decompressed successfully with flatus tube, comfortable for 24 hr. Next step?

- Options: discharge / Hartmann's now / right hemicolectomy / **elective sigmoid colectomy** / endoscopic stent
- **Elective sigmoid colectomy.** Decompression unbinds the volvulus but doesn't fix the redundant sigmoid. Recurrence is common.
- Wrong: Hartmann's (was the answer when conservative *failed*; here it succeeded), right hemi (wrong anatomy), stent (palliative low-cancer use)

## Spoke 2 — Acute mesenteric ischaemia → emergency laparotomy

> 🧠 **Plugs into L1 Card 2 — Bowel Ischaemia.** Specifically: AMI vs ischaemic colitis (slide 5), CT angio interpretation (slide 6), surgical indications + revascularisation (slide 8).

### Cascade skeleton

```
History → unwell + breathless + ex-smoker
  ↓
Exam → pale/sweaty, soft abdomen with guarding (PAIN OUT OF PROPORTION)
  ↓
Bloods → ↑ WCC, ↑ urea, ↑ creatinine, LACTATE 4.36, ↑ CRP, amylase normal
  ↓
AXR → dilated central SB loops (SBO pattern WITHOUT mechanical cause)
  ↓
Lock diagnosis → AMI clue cluster
  ↓
Investigation → triple-phase / arterial-phase CT
  ↓
CT findings → SMA thrombus distal to MCA; non-enhancing fluid-filled SB
  ↓
Emergency laparotomy
  ↓
(1) Restore SMA flow (embolectomy / endovascular / bypass)
(2) Resect non-viable bowel
(± damage control laparotomy if unstable)
```

### Q: AMI clue cluster — name the 6 features that lock the diagnosis.

- Elderly + smoker → ↑ CV risk
- Short, sudden history
- Central pain with guarding
- Absent bowel sounds; no scars/hernia for SBO cause
- ↑ serum lactate; sepsis profile
- **Pain out of proportion to clinical signs**

> ⭐ **High-yield:** This is THE AMI exam pattern. See it → answer AMI before reading options.

### Q: Why does the AXR in Case 2 show SBO pattern with no mechanical cause?

- SMA blood supply cut → small bowel ischaemic + paralysed
- Paralysed bowel dilates and fills with fluid → SBO appearance on AXR
- No previous surgery + no hernia → no mechanical cause → think AMI
- 📎 Figure L2.5 — AXR with dilated, centrally located small bowel loops in AMI

### Q: Why triple-phase / arterial-phase CT specifically for AMI?

- Need **arterial phase** to see SMA contrast cut-off (not just venous)
- Plain or oral contrast = lumen only (misses vessels and wall)
- IV-contrast CT = vasculature + bowel wall enhancement together

### Q: What CT findings confirm AMI in Case 2?

- Axial: SMA contrast normal at origin → suddenly cut off mid-course (thrombus)
- Coronal: thrombus **distal to origin of middle colic artery (MCA)**
- Bowel wall enhancement: transverse colon enhances; **small bowel non-enhancing, fluid-filled**
- 📎 Figure L2.6 — Axial CT: following the SMA from origin to thrombus
- 📎 Figure L2.7 — Coronal CT (SMA thrombus distal to MCA) + axial (ischaemic SB vs perfused TC)

### Q: Why is the transverse colon spared in Case 2?

- MCA supplies transverse colon
- Thrombus is **distal to MCA origin** → MCA still gets blood from SMA proximal segment
- Small bowel + ascending colon (supplied distal to thrombus) = ischaemic

> 🧠 **Pattern:** Location of thrombus relative to branches tells you which bowel is dead. Anatomy = prognosis.

### Q: Foregut / midgut / hindgut blood supply — which artery, which bowel?

- **Coeliac** (foregut): lower oesophagus → mid-D2
- **SMA** (midgut): mid-D2 → splenic flexure (incl. all small bowel + ascending + transverse colon)
- **IMA** (hindgut): splenic flexure → upper rectum
- Rectum below anastomoses with systemic circulation

### Q: Why no conservative management for AMI?

- Watching → operating on perforation/peritonitis later instead of disease itself
- Bowel necrosis is irreversible once established
- Window is hours

### Q: Two surgical goals at emergency laparotomy for AMI?

- (1) **Restore SMA blood flow** — embolectomy (embolic), endovascular (thrombotic), arterial bypass (thrombotic)
- (2) **Resect non-viable bowel**
- Revascularisation only saves bowel that's still alive
- 📎 Figure L2.8 — Extensive small bowel necrosis from AMI

### Q: What is damage control laparotomy and when is it used?

- Patient too unstable for definitive operation
- Staple bowel ends, leave in **discontinuity** inside abdomen
- ICU resuscitation + physiological correction
- Return to theatre 24–48 h later for reassessment + reconstruction

> 🧠 **Pattern:** Same Hartmann's principle — no early anastomosis on a sick patient. See also: L2 — Spoke 1 (Hartmann's).

### Q: Why does the lecturer flag AMI as potentially terminal?

- SMA supplies the entire small bowel
- **You cannot live without small bowel**
- All-bowel necrosis at laparotomy → close + palliate

## AMI causes — atlas

### Q: AMI causes — frequency and broad mechanism?

- **Embolic (50%)** — embolus lodges in SMA
- **Thrombotic (20–35%)** — in-situ atherosclerotic thrombus at SMA origin
- **Non-occlusive / NOMI (<5%)** — inadequate flow without mechanical block
- **Venous / SMV thrombosis (10–15%)** — bowel can't drain, gets congested

### Q: Embolic AMI — what are the embolic sources?

- **AF (left auricle)** — top source
- Mural thrombus post-MI
- Aortic atheroma or aneurysm
- Endocarditis vegetations
- Left atrial myxoma

### Q: Thrombotic AMI — distinguishing feature?

- Atherosclerosis usually affects **all 3 splanchnic vessels** — coeliac, SMA, IMA
- Often see disease at the **origin** of all three
- Single-vessel cut-off → think embolic, not thrombotic

### Q: NOMI — triggers?

- Hypotension or hypoperfusion in shock
- Vasospasm in septic shock
- **Vasopressor use** (vasoconstriction reduces splanchnic flow)
- Dialysis with large-volume fluid removal

### Q: Venous AMI — risk factors and imaging difference?

- SMV thrombosis from: portal hypertension, **portal pyaemia**, sickle cell, hypercoagulable state
- Bowel can't drain → **congested/swollen**, not hypo-enhancing
- Different visual pattern from arterial AMI

### Q: Stem cue → AMI cause type?

- AF → **embolic**
- Smoker / atherosclerosis → **thrombotic** (often all 3 splanchnic vessels)
- Septic shock + vasopressors → **NOMI**
- Hypercoagulable / portal hypertension → **venous (SMV thrombosis)**

> ⭐ **High-yield:** Treatment differs (embolectomy vs endovascular vs vasodilators vs anticoagulation), so the cause matters clinically.

## Portal pyaemia (pylephlebitis)

### Q: Portal pyaemia — definition and triggers?

- Septic, often suppurative thrombophlebitis of portal venous system
- Both names equivalent: portal pyaemia = pylephlebitis
- Complication of intra-abdominal sepsis: **diverticulitis, appendicitis**

### Q: Portal pyaemia — imaging hallmark?

- Air in **SMV + intrahepatic portal venous system**
- Pathognomonic on CT
- Listed under causes of SMV thrombosis (venous AMI)
- 📎 Figure L2.9 — Portal venous gas in pylephlebitis

## SBA block — cross-bundle discrimination

### Q: SBA — adhesional SBO patient, day 2 conservative, develops constant pain + HR 120 + T 38.2 + lactate 3.8. Next step?

- Options: continue conservative / gastrograffin / repeat AXR / **emergency laparotomy** / sigmoidoscopy
- **Emergency laparotomy.** Strangulation features (constant pain, tachycardia, fever, ↑ lactate) → ischaemic bowel within obstructed segment. 2-day cut-off doesn't apply — strangulation triggers theatre regardless of timing.
- Wrong: continue (now failed), gastrograffin (uncomplicated SBO only), AXR (no decision value, wastes time), sigmoidoscopy (sigmoid volvulus only)

### Q: SBA — Diverticular perforation 5 days ago. New RUQ pain + fever + deranged LFTs + air in intrahepatic portal venous branches. Diagnosis?

- Options: cholangitis from stone / hepatic abscess / **portal pyaemia (pylephlebitis)** / gas gangrene / cholecystitis
- **Portal pyaemia.** Air in portal venous system + intra-abdominal sepsis source = pylephlebitis. Portal vein drains gut → bacterial seeding from colonic source.
- Wrong: cholangitis (would show dilated CBD + stone), abscess (discrete fluid collection, not portal gas), gas gangrene (not the pattern), cholecystitis (no gallbladder findings)

### Q: SBA — 78-yr-old, AF off anticoagulation, sudden severe central pain 3 hr ago, agony with soft abdomen, lactate 4.8. Next investigation?

- Options: erect CXR / diagnostic laparoscopy / **CT abdo/pelvis with arterial-phase contrast** / mesenteric angiography / repeat AXR in 4 hr
- **CT with arterial-phase contrast.** AMI clue cluster locked. CT angio = both diagnostic + plans operation (embolectomy vs endovascular).
- Wrong: CXR (no perforation features), diagnostic lap (loses time), angiography (historical first-line, replaced by CT angio), watching (bowel necroses in hours)

### Q: SBA — 75-yr-old on dialysis, sudden pain after 4 L fluid removal, hypotensive on vasopressor. CT shows ischaemia but NO thrombus. Diagnosis?

- Options: embolic / thrombotic / **NOMI** / venous / ischaemic colitis
- **NOMI.** Triggers fit: dialysis ultrafiltration → hypovolaemia, vasopressor → splanchnic vasoconstriction, CT shows ischaemia without vessel block.
- Wrong: embolic (no AF/MI/endocarditis source; no thrombus), thrombotic (no in-situ thrombus on CT), venous (would show congested bowel + visible SMV thrombus), ischaemic colitis (colon-only + gradual)

### Q: SBA — Highest risk for embolic (vs thrombotic) AMI?

- Options: 70-yr smoker with PVD + aortic atherosclerosis / 65-yr in septic shock on noradrenaline / 50-yr with sickle cell + portal HTN / **78-yr with new AF off anticoagulation** / 60-yr with Crohn's
- **AF off anticoagulation.** Left atrial appendage thrombus throws emboli to SMA.
- Wrong: atherosclerosis (thrombotic), septic shock + pressor (NOMI), sickle cell + portal HTN (venous), Crohn's (unrelated)

### Q: SBA — 84-yr-old, 3-day Hx severe distension + absolute constipation + no vomiting, no scars/hernias, AXR coffee bean sign. Successfully decompressed. Best definitive plan?

- Options: emergency Hartmann's now / continue conservative indefinitely / **elective sigmoid colectomy** / left hemicolectomy / endoscopic stent
- **Elective sigmoid colectomy.** Decompression worked → no emergency op needed. But redundant sigmoid → high recurrence risk → plan elective definitive surgery.
- Wrong: emergency Hartmann's (only if conservative failed or perforation), continue conservative (recurrence rate too high), left hemi (wrong scope), stent (palliative low cancer use)
