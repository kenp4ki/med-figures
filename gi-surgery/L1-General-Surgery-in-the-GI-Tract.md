# L1 — General Surgery in the GI Tract

> 📘 **How to use:** Each `Q:` is a Notion toggle — read it, force an answer aloud, then expand. This is **L1 of the *Surgery in the GI Tract* bundle (the hub)**. L2 = applied cases that plug back into specific cards here.

## Lecturer's voice

**Flagged as exam-critical**

- Examine **hernial orifices** in any obstruction stem — direct examiner alert
- "**Pain out of proportion to clinical signs**" = AMI hallmark
- **Caecum >9 cm** = imminent perforation = emergency theatre
- **Charcot's triad** (jaundice + RUQ pain + fever) = acute cholangitis
- Cholecystectomy timing — **<72 hr or 4–6 weeks** (never in between)

**De-emphasised**

- Endoscopic stenting — palliative only, low colonic cancer with poor prognosis
- Gallstone "ileus" — "very rare, I've only seen one"

**Spent disproportionate time on**

- "**In lumen / wall / outside wall**" reusable rule (universal for any obstruction)
- Bag analogy for AXR supine vs erect
- Mechanism of pain migration in appendicitis (visceral → parietal)
- Mechanism of **gastrograffin** in adhesional SBO

## Territory map

Five conditions, framed by what's failing in the GI tract:

- **Lumen blocked** → Bowel obstruction (distension + vomiting + absolute constipation; previous surgery or hernia)
- **Blood supply cut** → Bowel ischaemia (pain out of proportion + AF/atherosclerosis + ↑ lactate)
- **Wall gives way** → GI perforation (sudden severe pain + rigid abdomen + free air on erect CXR)
- **Focal RLQ inflammation** → Acute appendicitis (migratory periumbilical→RLQ + anorexia + low-grade fever)
- **Focal RUQ/epigastric** → Biliary & pancreatic (RUQ + biliary findings; or epigastric→back + amylase)

## Cross-cutting frameworks

### Q: Order of approach for any acute abdomen patient?

- Resuscitate first (**ABCDE**), diagnose second
- Pain history: **SOCRATES**
- Bloods: VBG, FBC, CRP, U&Es, LFTs, **amylase**
- Imaging: erect CXR, AXR, CT (CTAP / CT angio), USS
- Pregnancy test in women of reproductive age

### Q: The "in lumen / wall / outside wall" rule — what is it and where does it apply?

- Universal rule for any obstructed lumen: bowel, biliary, ureter, vessel
- **In lumen:** stone, faecal impaction, foreign body, bezoar
- **In wall:** tumour, stricture, inflammatory (Crohn's, diverticulitis)
- **Outside wall:** hernia, volvulus, adhesions, mass effect

> 🧠 **Pattern:** When asked "what is the cause of obstruction?", run the three buckets — answer is in one of them.

### Q: What is "drip and suck"?

- Initial conservative bundle for any bowel emergency
- NBM + large-bore IV cannula + IV fluid resuscitation
- NG tube for decompression
- Urinary catheter (output monitoring), IV analgesia, antiemetics
- Correct electrolytes

### Q: What did the lecturer single out as the highest-yield exam expectation in obstruction?

- **Examine hernial orifices** (and look for abdominal scars, including laparoscopic portholes)
- Direct quote-level alert: "everyone is waiting for you to examine for hernias"
- Missing a strangulated hernia kills patients

> ⭐ **High-yield:** Hernia exam is testable in any obstruction stem.

### Q: Common hernial sites and what makes each a defect?

- **Epigastric & umbilical:** linea alba (midline weak point)
- **Inguinal & femoral:** natural defects in the groin
- **Incisional:** previous surgical site
- 📎 Figure L1.3 — Hernia anatomy: sites, neck of sac, strangulated, Richter's

### Q: What is a Richter's hernia and why does it matter?

- Only **part of the bowel circumference** herniates through the defect
- Strangulation of that segment **without complete obstruction**
- Patient is sick (ischaemic bowel) without classic obstruction picture
- Easy to miss

### Q: What does a raised lactate mean in any acute abdomen?

- Ischaemia somewhere — bowel, sepsis hypoperfusion, strangulated obstruction
- **Suggestive, not diagnostic** — pair with clinical picture
- Normal lactate does not exclude AMI early on

### Q: What pattern unlocks "acute mesenteric ischaemia"?

- **Pain out of proportion to clinical signs** — patient in agony, abdomen soft
- Bowel dying without peritoneal inflammation yet
- Combined with: AF or atherosclerosis + ↑ lactate + elderly

### Q: How does pain location filter the DDx by quadrant?

| Quadrant | Top differentials |
| --- | --- |
| **RUQ** | Cholecystitis, cholangitis, biliary colic, hepatitis |
| **Epigastrium** | Peptic ulcer, pancreatitis, **AAA, dissection, MI** |
| **RLQ** | Appendicitis; Crohn's (terminal ileum); gynae |
| **LLQ** | Diverticulitis; IBD; gynae |

> 🧠 **Pattern:** Epigastric crushing in older patient → always include AAA, dissection, MI in DDx, not just GI causes.

## Card 1 — Bowel obstruction

**Unifying intuition:** SBO and LBO differ on **size and timing**. Large bowel can hold litres before complaining → distension early, vomiting late. Small bowel can't → vomiting early, distension mild. Everything else follows.

### Q: Two main categories of bowel obstruction, and how is mechanical sub-classified?

- Categories: **paralytic ileus** vs **mechanical**
- Mechanical sub-classification: speed (acute/chronic), site (high/low ≈ small/large), nature (simple vs strangulating), aetiology (in lumen / wall / outside)

### Q: What are the strangulation features of a bowel obstruction?

- Pain changes from **colicky → continuous**
- Tachycardia, pyrexia, peritonism
- Bowel sounds reduced/absent
- Leucocytosis, ↑ CRP, ↑ lactate

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Strangulating obstruction with peritonitis = up to **15% mortality**. Surgical emergency.

### Q: Top causes of small bowel obstruction with frequencies?

- **Adhesions (60%)** — Hx of previous abdominal surgery
- Neoplasia (20%) — primary, metastatic, extraintestinal
- Incarcerated hernia (10%) — external or internal
- Crohn's (5%) — acute oedema or chronic strictures
- Other (5%) — intussusception, foreign body, bezoar

### Q: Top causes of large bowel obstruction?

- **Colorectal carcinoma** (top in adults)
- Volvulus (sigmoid, caecal)
- Diverticulitis (inflammation, strictures)
- Faecal impaction
- Hirschsprung's (infants/children)

### Q: SBO vs LBO presentation — how do they differ?

| Feature | SBO | LBO |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Pain | Colicky, central | Colicky or constant |
| Vomiting | **Early**, large, bilious | **Late**; eventually faecal |
| Constipation | Late sign | **Early sign** |
| Distension | Less significant | **Marked, early** |

### Q: What does "absolute constipation" mean specifically?

- **No stool AND no flatus**
- Just "constipated" doesn't qualify
- Patients still passing wind are not in absolute constipation

### Q: Bowel sounds in obstruction — what do they tell you?

- **Tinkling, high-pitched (early)** — bowel fighting against the obstruction
- **Absent (late)** — bowel exhausted/paralysed
- Progression from one to the other = deterioration

### Q: VBG patterns in bowel obstruction — two opposite pictures?

- **Persistent vomiting:** loses gastric HCl → **hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis**
- **Strangulation:** ischaemic bowel → **metabolic acidosis with ↑ lactate**
- Same disease, opposite VBGs depending on complication

### Q: AXR diameter cut-offs in bowel obstruction?

- SBO: dilated small bowel **>3 cm**, central
- LBO: dilated large bowel **>6 cm**, peripheral
- Caecum **>9 cm** = imminent perforation = emergency theatre

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Caecum >9 cm flips an LBO from "manage conservatively" to "go now". Hard examiner cut-off.

### Q: Pattern recognition on AXR — SBO vs LBO?

- **SBO:** central loops, **valvulae conniventes** crossing **full width** (circular folds go all the way round)
- **LBO:** peripheral loops, **haustrations** from taeniae coli — **partial-width** markings only
- Erect view shows air-fluid levels; supine doesn't
- 📎 Figure L1.4 — AXR comparison: SBO vs LBO, supine vs erect

### Q: What does CT add over plain AXR in bowel obstruction?

- Identifies the **transition point** — distended bowel proximal, collapsed distal
- Localises cause (tumour, mass)
- Detects unusual hernias (e.g. obturator)
- 📎 Figure L1.5 — CT bowel obstruction: SBO transition point + LBO sigmoid stricture

### Q: What does intraoperative bowel show at the transition point?

- Bowel proximal to obstruction: dusky, dilated, often discoloured
- Bowel distal: pink, collapsed, healthy
- Visual confirmation of the transition point
- 📎 Figure L1.6 — Intraoperative transition point in bowel obstruction

### Q: What does gastrograffin do, mechanistically, in adhesional SBO?

- Hyperosmolar iodinated contrast
- Pulls fluid from bowel wall into lumen → **reduces wall oedema**
- Adds intraluminal fluid that helps push past obstruction
- Both **diagnoses and treats** adhesional SBO

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Avoid in suspected perforation — don't flood peritoneum with hyperosmolar fluid.

### Q: 5 indications for surgery in bowel obstruction?

- Haemodynamic instability or sepsis
- Complete obstruction with ischaemia signs
- **Closed loop obstruction**
- Persistent obstruction **>2 days** despite conservative
- Strangulation features

### Q: What is closed-loop obstruction and why is it surgical without delay?

- Both ends of a bowel segment obstructed simultaneously
- No exit for fluid → pressure rises rapidly → ischaemia → perforation
- No conservative trial — straight to theatre
- Sigmoid volvulus is the textbook example

### Q: When is endoscopic stenting used in bowel obstruction?

- Very rarely
- Only in **low colonic cancer** (sigmoid/rectal) with poor prognosis
- Palliative, QoL only
- Clinical weight: **Minor**

> 🧠 **Cross-ref — How does L2 apply this card?** L2 Case 1 = sigmoid volvulus → closed-loop → conservative trial fails → **Hartmann's procedure**. Demonstrates "no anastomosis on ischaemic/contaminated bowel". See also: L2 — Spoke 1.

## Card 2 — Bowel ischaemia

**Unifying intuition:** Two diseases pretending to be one. **AMI** = sudden vascular catastrophe; small bowel infarcts in hours; agony with soft abdomen. **Ischaemic colitis** = slow under-perfusion of the colon; gradual onset; often resolves conservatively.

### Q: AMI vs ischaemic colitis — what differs?

| Feature | AMI | Ischaemic colitis |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bowel | Small | Large |
| Mechanism | Occlusive thromboembolism | Non-occlusive low flow / atherosclerosis |
| Onset | **Sudden** | **Gradual** (80–85% mild and gradual) |
| Default Mx | Always surgical | Conservative if mild–moderate |

### Q: What risk factors point to AMI in a stem?

- Age >65
- **AF** (top embolic source)
- Atherosclerosis (top thrombotic; affects all splanchnic vessels)
- Hypercoagulability/thrombophilia
- Vasculitis, sickle cell disease
- Profound shock (non-occlusive)

### Q: Why does bowel ischaemia drive a high WCC?

- Two mechanisms working together:
- (1) Necrosis → cytokine release
- (2) Damaged mucosa → bacterial translocation into systemic circulation

### Q: What test confirms AMI?

- **CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast (CT angiogram)**
- Shows: vessel cut-off, vascular stenosis, *pneumatosis intestinalis* (late), *thumbprint sign*
- Endoscopy only for mild ischaemic colitis (oedema, cyanosis, ulceration)
- 📎 Figure L1.1 — AXR thumbprinting + CT angio with mesenteric vessel cut-off

### Q: Conservative management of bowel ischaemia — when does it apply?

- **Mild–moderate ischaemic colitis only** (NOT small bowel ischaemia)
- Drip and suck + broad-spectrum Abx (covers translocation)
- Anticoagulation
- Treat underlying cause (e.g. fix the shock state)
- Serial abdominal exam + repeat imaging

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Small bowel ischaemia goes **straight to theatre**. Conservative is colon-only.

### Q: Surgical management of bowel ischaemia — indications and operations?

- Indications: small bowel ischaemia, peritonitis/sepsis, instability, massive bleeding, **toxic megacolon**
- Exploratory laparotomy → resect necrotic bowel
- ± open surgical embolectomy or mesenteric arterial bypass
- **Endovascular revascularisation** only if no signs of established ischaemia
- 📎 Figure L1.2 — Intraoperative ischaemic small bowel

> 🧠 **Cross-ref — How does L2 apply this card?** L2 Case 2 = AMI in 84-yr-old ex-smoker → triple-phase CT → emergency laparotomy + revascularisation. Demonstrates the AMI clue cluster + embolic vs thrombotic decision. See also: L2 — Spoke 2.

## Card 3 — Acute appendicitis

**Unifying intuition:** Two pain phases because there are two kinds of pain. Inflamed appendix → **visceral pain** (poorly localised, periumbilical, T10). Inflammation reaches parietal peritoneum → **somatic pain** (sharp, localised, RLQ). Hence migration. Every clinical sign just provokes the parietal peritoneum.

### Q: Why does appendicitis pain migrate periumbilical → RLQ?

- Phase 1: visceral pain from inflamed appendix → poorly localised, central
- Phase 2: parietal peritoneum inflames → somatic pain → sharp, RLQ
- Migration usually within 24 hours

### Q: McBurney's point — exact definition?

- Tenderness at the **lateral 1/3 of a line drawn from right ASIS to the umbilicus**
- NOT ASIS to pubic tubercle (that line is the inguinal ligament)

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Lecturer corrects this exact mistake live in Q&A. Anchor: ASIS → **umbilicus**.

### Q: Name the 5 clinical signs of appendicitis and what each provokes?

- **McBurney's point** — RLQ tenderness at the 1/3 mark
- **Blumberg sign** — rebound tenderness in RIF
- **Rovsing sign** — press LLQ → pain in RLQ
- **Psoas sign** — flex right hip against resistance → RLQ pain
- **Obturator sign** — passive internal rotation of flexed hip → RLQ pain (pelvic appendix)

### Q: Why does pelvic appendicitis cause urinary findings?

- Inflamed pelvic appendix sits next to bladder
- Causes mild **pyuria/haematuria**
- Retrocaecal is the most common position; **pelvic is 2nd most common**

> ⚠️ **Trap:** RLQ pain + leucocytes in urine ≠ UTI. Pelvic appendicitis is the classic distractor.

### Q: Imaging choice for appendicitis — by patient type?

- **CT** = gold standard in adults, especially **age >50**
- **USS** in children, pregnancy, breastfeeding (avoid radiation)
- **MRI** in pregnancy if USS inconclusive
- **Diagnostic laparoscopy** if imaging inconclusive but suspicion remains

### Q: Why CT specifically for patients >50?

- Acute appendicitis in older patients can be caused by **appendiceal malignancy** obstructing the lumen
- CT excludes this differential

### Q: Why do you take out a normal-looking appendix at diagnostic laparoscopy?

- **30% return microscopic appendicitis on histology**
- Plus removes a future diagnostic uncertainty

### Q: Alvarado score components and cut-offs?

- 10 points total
- 2-point items: **RLQ tenderness**, **WCC >10,000**
- 1-point items: fever >37.3°C, rebound, pain migration, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, neutrophilia (left shift)
- ≤4 unlikely; 5–6 possible; **≥7 likely**

### Q: When is appendicitis managed conservatively, and what's the recurrence rate?

- Complicated appendicitis (abscess, phlegmon, sealed perforation)
- IV fluids + IV Abx + percutaneous drainage if collection
- Avoids operating on friable inflamed mass (caecum tears)
- **Recurrence:** slide says 12–24%; lecturer updates to **30% post-Covid**

> ⭐ **High-yield:** Lecturer overrides slide — prefer 30% post-Covid figure.

### Q: Gold standard surgery for appendicitis and key steps?

- **Laparoscopic appendicectomy** (less pain, lower SSI, shorter stay, better QoL)
- Steps: 3 trocars → identify appendix → divide mesoappendix (with appendicular artery) → endoloop base → divide → retrieve in bag → **pelvic wash-out** → close

## Card 4 — GI perforation

**Unifying intuition:** Once GI lumen breaches the wall, **the patient becomes septic** (bacteria) AND **gas + fluid escape into peritoneum**. The clinical picture is the same regardless of where; only contextual clues differ by site.

### Q: Site-specific clue clusters for perforation?

- **Peptic ulcer:** sudden epigastric pain; **referred shoulder pain**; NSAID/steroid Hx
- **Diverticulum:** LLQ pain, constipation, elderly
- **Appendix:** migratory pain, anorexia, gradual worsening
- **Malignancy:** constitutional Sx — weight loss, anorexia, PR bleeding, change in bowel habit

### Q: Why does perforated peptic ulcer give referred shoulder pain?

- Diaphragm is dome-shaped
- Fluid from a high upper-abdominal perforation pools at the back/top of the abdomen
- Diaphragmatic irritation refers via **C3–C5** to the shoulder
- Diverticular perforation doesn't cause this — fluid sits low

### Q: Diagnostic imaging finding for GI perforation?

- **Erect CXR → subdiaphragmatic free air (pneumoperitoneum)**
- CT abdo/pelvis: pneumoperitoneum + free GI content + mesenteric fat stranding
- CT also excludes pancreatitis as DDx
- 📎 Figure L1.7 — Pneumoperitoneum: erect CXR + axial CT

### Q: Same physics, two views — explain pneumoperitoneum in supine vs erect?

- Erect CXR: air rises to under the diaphragm
- Supine CT: air rises **anteriorly** (because patient lies flat)
- Both demonstrate the same thing — free intraperitoneal gas

### Q: Conservative management of GI perforation — when?

- Rare. Only for **localised peritonitis without sepsis** in **contained/sealed** perforations
- Drip and suck + broad-spectrum Abx + **IV PPI** (esp. upper GI)
- IR-guided drainage of any collection
- "If fluid is leaking, surgical management"

### Q: Graham's omental patch — technique?

- For perforated peptic ulcer
- **Three sutures** placed *under* the perforation
- **Omentum** laid over the defect
- Sutures tied **on top of the omentum** (omentum acts as biological seal)
- 📎 Figure L1.8 — Graham's omental patch repair: defect + repaired

### Q: Surgical management of GI perforation — what beyond patching?

- Generalised peritonitis ± sepsis: exploratory lap, close defect, **lavage peritoneum**
- Resect perforated segment + primary anastomosis or temporary stoma
- Appendiceal: appendicectomy
- Malignancy: intraoperative biopsies
- Slide emphasis: peritoneal lavage **++++**

## Card 5 — Biliary & pancreatic

**Unifying intuition:** Four conditions, all RUQ/epigastric, distinguished by *what's blocked or inflamed and where*. Stone in cystic duct (colic); inflamed gallbladder (cholecystitis); infected bile **duct** (cholangitis); pancreas auto-digestion (pancreatitis, often from stone at ampulla).

### Q: Biliary colic — presentation, Ix, Mx?

- **Symptoms:** postprandial RUQ pain → shoulder; nausea
- **Bloods:** normal
- **Imaging:** USS — cholelithiasis
- **Management:** analgesia, antiemetics, spasmolytics → elective cholecystectomy

### Q: Acute cholecystitis — presentation, Ix, Mx?

- **Symptoms:** acute severe RUQ + fever + **Murphy's sign**
- **Bloods:** ↑ WCC/CRP
- **Imaging:** USS — thickened gallbladder wall
- **Management:** fluids, **Abx**, analgesia, blood cultures → cholecystectomy **<72 hr or 4–6 weeks**

### Q: Murphy's sign — how to elicit and what does it mean?

- Patient takes a **deep breath** while you palpate the RUQ
- Inflamed gallbladder hits your fingers → **inspiratory arrest** from pain
- Specific for acute cholecystitis

### Q: Cholecystectomy timing — what's the rule and why?

- **<72 hours OR 4–6 weeks** — never in between
- Days 3 to 4–6 weeks: tissue is friable and inflamed
- Operating in that window risks tearing structures

### Q: Acute cholangitis — presentation, Ix, Mx?

- **Symptoms:** **Charcot's triad** — jaundice + RUQ pain + fever
- **Bloods:** ↑ LFTs, WCC, CRP, +ve blood cultures
- **Imaging:** USS — biliary dilatation
- **Management:** fluids, **IV Abx**, analgesia → **ERCP within 72 hr** for clearance/stenting

### Q: Why ERCP for acute cholangitis?

- Bile duct is infected because something obstructs drainage
- ERCP clears the stone or stents the duct open
- Drainage = source control = antibiotics actually work

### Q: Acute pancreatitis — presentation, Ix, Mx?

- **Symptoms:** severe epigastric pain → back; nausea/vomiting; Hx gallstones or alcohol
- **Bloods:** ↑ amylase/lipase; ↑ WCC; **↓ Ca²⁺** (saponification with released fatty acids)
- **Imaging:** CT/US for cause and complications
- **Management:** **Glasgow-Imrie** score; aggressive fluid resus, O₂; ITU/HDU if severe

### Q: Acute pancreatitis aetiology — country-specific examiner trap?

- **#1 in UK = gallstones**
- **#1 in US = alcohol** (#2 in UK)
- Read the geography of the stem

## Discrimination patterns — stem cue → most likely card

### Q: Stem cue → most likely card (Part 1 — obstruction & ischaemia)

- Previous surgery + colicky central pain + early bilious vomiting → **adhesional SBO**
- Elderly + change in bowel habit + LLQ + distension + mass → **LBO from CRC**
- AF + sudden severe pain + soft abdomen + ↑ lactate → **AMI**
- Pain out of proportion to clinical signs → **AMI** (pattern lock)
- Caecum >9 cm on AXR → **imminent perforation, emergency theatre**

### Q: Stem cue → most likely card (Part 2 — appendicitis, perforation, biliary)

- Migratory periumbilical→RLQ + anorexia + low fever → **acute appendicitis**
- Sudden epigastric + rigid abdomen + free air on CXR → **perforated peptic ulcer**
- LLQ pain + diverticular Hx + sepsis → **perforated diverticulum**
- RUQ + jaundice + fever → **acute cholangitis (Charcot's triad)**
- Epigastric → back + ↑ amylase + Hx gallstones/alcohol → **acute pancreatitis**

### Q: Reusable decision rules across all 5 cards?

- **2-day cut-off** for failed conservative obstruction → theatre
- **Closed loop** → straight to theatre, bypass conservative trial
- **Strangulation features** → straight to theatre
- **No anastomosis on ischaemic/infected/contaminated bowel** — stoma instead
- **Examine hernial orifices** in every obstruction patient

## SBA block — interleaved discrimination

### Q: SBA — 78-yr-old, AF (off warfarin), sudden severe central pain, agony but soft abdomen, lactate 5.2. Diagnosis?

- Options: SBO / perforated PUD / **AMI** / sigmoid volvulus / ischaemic colitis
- **AMI.** AF (embolic source) + sudden + agony with soft abdomen + ↑ lactate. Bowel hasn't perforated yet → no peritonism.
- Wrong: SBO (colicky + early vomiting + distension), perforation (rigid abdomen + free air), volvulus (LBO + coffee bean AXR), ischaemic colitis (gradual + bloody diarrhoea)

### Q: SBA — 67-yr-old, 3 days distension + absolute constipation, no surgery, no hernias, AXR caecum 10 cm. Next step?

- Options: gastrograffin / CT abdo/pelvis / sigmoidoscopy / **emergency laparotomy** / NG + IV fluids 48h
- **Emergency laparotomy.** Caecum >9 cm = imminent perforation = surgical emergency. Threshold flips priority to theatre regardless of cause.
- Wrong: gastrograffin (for adhesional SBO not LBO with imminent perf), CT (good *next* test in absence of caecum threshold), sigmoidoscopy (only if confirmed sigmoid volvulus without ischaemia), conservative (contraindicated by caecum size)

### Q: SBA — 28-yr-old woman, 18 hr migratory periumbilical→RLQ pain, anorexia, T 37.6, WCC 14, CRP 60, trace urinary leucocytes. Next investigation?

- Options: CT / **USS pelvis** / MRI / diagnostic laparoscopy / erect CXR
- **USS pelvis.** First-line in young women — avoid radiation; ovarian cyst/ectopic statistically more common than appendicitis. Trace leucocytes ≠ UTI (pelvic appendicitis can cause this).
- Wrong: CT (reasonable but not first-line in young women), MRI (pregnancy after inconclusive USS), diagnostic lap (if imaging inconclusive), CXR (no perforation features)

### Q: SBA — 72-yr-old, severe RUQ + 39.1°C + jaundice, hypotensive. Bili 95, ALP 410, ALT 90. Definitive Mx?

- Options: IV Abx alone / urgent cholecystectomy / **ERCP within 72 hr** / Glasgow-Imrie + ITU / erect CXR
- **ERCP within 72 hr.** Charcot's triad locks acute cholangitis. Antibiotics necessary but not sufficient — must clear/stent obstructed bile duct for source control.
- Wrong: Abx alone (not source control), cholecystectomy (for cholecystitis), Glasgow-Imrie (for pancreatitis), CXR (no perforation features)

### Q: SBA — 55-yr-old, sudden epigastric pain → right shoulder, NSAID Hx, rigid abdomen, lucent line under both hemidiaphragms on erect CXR. Diagnosis?

- Options: pancreatitis / cholecystitis / **perforated peptic ulcer** / AMI / inferior MI
- **Perforated peptic ulcer.** Sudden epigastric + NSAID Hx + rigid abdomen + free subdiaphragmatic air. Shoulder radiation = diaphragmatic irritation pattern (C3–C5 referral).
- Wrong: pancreatitis (pain → back not shoulder; no free air), cholecystitis (more localised RUQ; no free air), AMI (soft abdomen not rigid), MI (mimics upper-GI Sx but no free air)
