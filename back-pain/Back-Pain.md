## Territory map

```
BACK PAIN
│
├── MECHANICAL  (95%, benign, self-limiting)
│   ├── Muscular tension / poor posture
│   ├── Acute muscle sprain / spasm
│   ├── Degenerative disc disease
│   ├── Facet joint osteoarthritis
│   └── (+ sciatica) Disc herniation onto exiting nerve root
│
├── SERIOUS  (must not miss)
│   ├── Tumour      → metastases, myeloma
│   ├── Infection   → discitis, osteomyelitis, paraspinal abscess
│   │                 (Staph, Strep, TB)
│   ├── Inflammatory→ AS, psoriatic arthritis, IBD-associated
│   ├── Fracture    → traumatic OR atraumatic (osteoporotic)
│   └── Large disc prolapse → cord/cauda equina compression
│
└── REFERRED  (not from the back at all — exam trap)
    └── Pancreas, kidneys, AAA
```

🧠 **The one idea that makes the rest derivable:** mechanical pain is lumbar, episodic, worse on movement, better at rest, no systemic features, no neurology, in middle age. Every red flag is a **deviation** from that signature. Memorise the deviations, not ten unrelated bullets.

## Red flags — the decision-rule spine

```
Patient with back pain
        │
   Red flags?
   /        \
  NO         YES
   │          │
   ▼          ▼
Conservative  Investigate
- No imaging  - Bloods (ESR/CRP, FBC,
- NSAIDs        ALP, Ca²⁺, PSA)
- Keep moving - Imaging (usually MRI
- Physio        for spine)
- Safety-net  - Treat the cause
  3–4 weeks
```

⭐ Safety-net review at **3–4 weeks** is the NICE timeframe — examinable.

### Q: List the red flag symptoms for back pain.

- Pain at night / worse when supine
- Constant or progressive pain
- Thoracic pain
- Weight loss
- Previous malignancy
- Fever / night sweats
- Immunosuppressed
- Bladder/bowel disturbance (sphincter dysfunction)
- Leg weakness or sensory loss
- Age <20 or >55

### Q: For each red flag cluster, what is it pointing at?

- **Night/supine pain, constant/progressive, weight loss, previous malignancy** → tumour
- **Fever/night sweats, immunosuppressed** → infection (incl. TB)
- **Thoracic pain** → atypical location → tumour, infection, fracture
- **Bladder/bowel, saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg pain** → cauda equina
- **Age extremes** → shifts base rates toward serious causes

### Q: Why is thoracic back pain itself a red flag?

Mechanical back pain almost always presents in the lumbar (or cervical) spine because that's where most movement and load happens. Thoracic spine is splinted by the rib cage and rarely produces mechanical pain — so thoracic pain is **disproportionately likely** to mean fracture, tumour, or infection.

### Q: What is the NICE rule for imaging non-specific low back pain?

- **No** routine spinal X-ray or imaging in primary care.
- Safety-net at **3–4 weeks**: review and reassess if persistent or worsening.
- Imaging only if red flags appear or symptoms progress.

⚠️ Sciatica without weakness, sensory loss, or sphincter symptoms is **not** an indication for imaging on its own. The pain itself doesn't earn you an MRI.

## Mechanical back pain

📎 Figure L1.1 — Lateral spine: cervical & lumbar lordosis, thoracic kyphosis

### Q: Define mechanical back pain by its history pattern.

- **Worse with movement**, better or absent at rest.
- No systemic features (no fever, no weight loss).
- No neurology.
- Lumbar (or cervical) location.

### Q: Name the four common causes of mechanical back pain.

- Muscular tension (chronic poor posture, weak core)
- Acute muscle sprain or spasm
- Degenerative disc disease
- Facet joint osteoarthritis

### Q: Treatment of low back pain without red flags.

- **Time** — most resolve in days; 96% by 6 weeks
- **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen) ± paracetamol ± codeine
- **Avoid bed rest** — keep moving
- **Physiotherapy** — soft tissue + core (side plank, "bird dog")
- **Safety-net** review at 3–4 weeks

⚠️ "Bed rest" is a wrong answer in an SBA — it actively worsens outcomes. The slide bolded **AVOID**.

## Sciatica (mechanical pain with a radicular twist)

📎 Figure L1.3 — Intervertebral disc: nucleus pulposus inside annulus fibrosus
📎 Figure L1.4 — Disc herniation contacting exiting nerve root
📎 Figure L1.5 — Dermatome map

### Q: What is sciatica and what causes it?

- **Unilateral** leg pain in a dermatomal distribution.
- Caused by **disc herniation** contacting the exiting lumbar nerve root.
- Pain location is determined by the level of the herniated disc.
- Character: electric shock, pins and needles, tingling.

### Q: What are the two pathophysiological mechanisms by which a herniated disc causes pain?

- **Mechanical**: nucleus pulposus extrudes through a tear in the annulus fibrosus and physically compresses the exiting nerve root.
- **Chemical**: the nucleus pulposus is locally inflammatory; leaked material irritates the nerve root even without much physical contact.

🧠 The chemical mechanism is **why steroid nerve root injections work** — they treat the inflammatory arm.

### Q: Treatment ladder for herniated disc / sciatica.

- **1.** Conservative — NSAIDs + physiotherapy (core strength, treat muscle spasm)
- **2.** **CT-guided nerve root injection** (local anaesthetic + glucocorticoid) if persistent
- **3.** Surgery — only if neurological compromise or persistent symptoms

### Q: What does the surgery vs conservative trial evidence show for sciatica?

- Surgery shortens **time-to-recovery** slightly.
- **No long-term advantage** for surgery.
- Two-thirds of herniated discs partially or completely resolve by **6 months** without specific treatment.

📎 Figure L1.12 — Spontaneous regression of herniated disc over 5 months

⚠️ Stem to remember: 45-y-o, sciatica, 2 weeks off work, no neurology → **no imaging, conservative**. The tempting wrong answer is "MRI now."

## Cauda equina syndrome — the emergency

📎 Figure L1.2 — Termination of cord at L2; conus medullaris and cauda equina

⭐ Same-day MRI lumbar spine. No exceptions. Untreated = permanent paralysis and incontinence.

### Q: What is the cauda equina and where does it sit?

- Bundle of lumbosacral nerve roots **below** spinal cord termination at L2.
- Cord ends at L2; below that is conus medullaris, then cauda equina.
- Lumbar puncture is performed at L3/4 to avoid the cord.

### Q: Pentad of cauda equina syndrome.

- **Saddle anaesthesia** (perineum, inner thighs, genitals)
- Bladder / bowel incontinence (or retention)
- **Loss of anal tone on PR exam**
- Radicular leg pain — often **bilateral**
- Ankle jerks may be absent

📎 Figure L1.6 — Saddle anaesthesia distribution

### Q: Investigation and management of cauda equina syndrome.

- **Investigation: same-day MRI lumbar spine**.
- Treatment depends on cause: surgical decompression for disc; drainage + antibiotics for abscess; radiotherapy ± surgery for tumour.

### Q: What are the classic causes of cauda equina syndrome?

- Large disc herniation
- Bony metastases
- Myeloma
- Tuberculosis
- Paraspinal abscess

🧠 Notice these are the **same** five causes that produce most of the "serious" presentations in this lecture. Same enemies, different presentations.

⚠️ Bilateral leg pain in a back-pain stem = cauda equina until ruled out. Sciatica is unilateral.

## Osteoporotic vertebral collapse (Case 1)

📎 Figure L1.8 — Osteoporotic wedge fracture (anterior height loss, "pancake" deformity)

### Q: Classic presentation of osteoporotic vertebral collapse.

- Post-menopausal woman (or older patient on long-term steroids).
- **Acute** focal back pain, often thoracic.
- Pain may radiate around the chest wall.
- Focal vertebral tenderness on exam.
- Often after trivial or no trauma.

### Q: What does a wedge fracture look like on imaging and why is it called that?

- Lateral X-ray shows a vertebra "squashed like a pancake" into a triangular shape.
- Anterior vertebral height is lost while posterior height is preserved → wedge profile.
- Adjacent vertebrae are square/normal for comparison.

### Q: Management priorities for an osteoporotic wedge fracture.

- **Analgesia and mobilisation** in the short term.
- **Treat the underlying osteoporosis** — the fracture is a sentinel event.
- **Consider MRI** to exclude pathological fracture from underlying malignancy.

## Spinal infection / TB spine (Case 2)

📎 Figure L1.10 — TB spine: vertebral destruction, discitis, paraspinal abscess on MRI

### Q: Who is at risk of spinal infection?

- Immunosuppressed (drugs, HIV)
- IV drug users
- Patients from TB-endemic regions
- Diabetes, end-stage renal disease

### Q: How does spinal infection present?

- **Subacute** worsening back pain over weeks
- **Constant pain — present at all times** (including at rest)
- Systemic features: fever, night sweats, **weight loss**
- Raised ESR / CRP

### Q: What three pathologies typically coexist in TB spine, and what is the term?

- **Discitis** + **vertebral osteomyelitis** + **paraspinal abscess** — frequently coexist as infection spreads from disc to adjacent vertebra and tracks paraspinally.
- Common bugs in vertebral osteomyelitis: Staph, Strep; classic insidious cause: **TB** (Pott's disease).

### Q: Management of TB spine.

- Drainage of the paraspinal abscess.
- Prolonged anti-TB therapy (>1 year for spinal TB).

⚠️ Stem clue: young patient + endemic country origin + night sweats + weight loss + back pain → must include TB in your differential, even if patient looks otherwise well.

## Spinal malignancy

### Q: Which primaries metastasise to the spine?

- Breast
- Prostate
- Lung
- Kidney
- Thyroid
- Plus **myeloma** (originates in bone marrow)

### Q: Blood test pattern in suspected spinal malignancy.

- ↑ ESR (especially myeloma)
- ↑ ALP (bone) **with normal liver enzymes**
- ↑ Ca²⁺ (myeloma, bony mets)
- Anaemia (myeloma, chronic disease)
- ↑ PSA (prostate Ca, men)

### Q: Why ALP with normal liver enzymes points to bone?

ALP is released by both liver and bone. If the liver enzymes (ALT, GGT) are normal but ALP is up, the source is bone — usually metastatic deposits or osteoblastic activity.

🧠 **Reusable pattern — the inflammatory vs mechanical history split.** Single most transferable pattern in this lecture.

| Feature | Mechanical | Inflammatory |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Acute, with trigger | Insidious, gradual |
| Movement | Worse | Better |
| Rest | Better | Worse / same |
| Morning stiffness | <30 min | >30 min, often hours |

## Ankylosing spondylitis — clinical features

📎 Figure L1.16 — Ankylosing spondylitis: loss of lumbar flexion (left) and severe fixed-flexion deformity (right)

### Q: Classic AS clinical pattern.

- Young adult (teens to 30s), often male
- Insidious back / **buttock** pain >3 months
- **Morning stiffness >30 min** (often hours)
- Pain **better with movement, worse at rest** — inverse of mechanical
- NSAID-responsive

### Q: What unifies the spondyloarthritides (SpA) family?

- **Seronegative** — no rheumatoid factor (distinguishes from rheumatoid arthritis).
- Immune-mediated inflammatory diseases.
- Inflame: spine (spondylitis), SI joints (sacroiliitis), entheses.
- Members: **AS, psoriatic arthritis, IBD-associated arthritis**.

### Q: What is enthesitis and why does it cause dactylitis?

- **Enthesitis** = inflammation at sites where tendons/ligaments insert onto bone — the defining feature of AS.
- **Dactylitis** ("sausage finger") = whole-digit swelling because inflammation tracks along the tendon insertions, not just the joint.
- Contrast rheumatoid arthritis where swelling is centred on the joint capsule.

📎 Figure L1.14 — Dactylitis (whole-finger swelling from enthesitis)

### Q: Extra-articular manifestations of AS — the "4 A's".

- **A**nterior uveitis (iritis) — painful red eye
- **A**pical lung fibrosis
- **A**ortitis / aortic regurgitation
- **A**myloidosis — chronic ↑ serum amyloid A → organ deposition

📎 Figure L1.13 — Anterior uveitis (red, painful eye)
📎 Figure L1.15 — Psoriasis on extensor surface

### Q: Why do you lose lumbar lordosis in long-standing AS?

- Chronic spinal inflammation drives ossification of ligaments → bridging syndesmophytes → fusion.
- Loss of intervertebral motion produces fixed flexion deformity.
- Patient ends up with the head tilted forward, lumbar lordosis flattened, sometimes "head almost on chest."

## Ankylosing spondylitis — pathophysiology

📎 Figure L1.17 — Physiological role of MHC class I: peptide presentation to CD8⁺ T cells

⭐ HLA-B27 — the highest-yield genetic biomarker in this entire course. Know the numbers and the trap.

### Q: What is the defining histopathological process in AS?

**Enthesitis** — chronic inflammation at sites where tendons and ligaments insert onto bone.

### Q: What is HLA-B27 and where does it sit?

- HLA = region on **chromosome 6** encoding **MHC molecules**.
- HLA-B27 is a **class I** MHC molecule.
- Class I MHC molecules present peptides to **CD8⁺ T cells**.

### Q: HLA-B27 prevalence in AS vs general population, and the trap.

- ~**90% of AS** patients are HLA-B27 +ve.
- ~**10% of general population** are HLA-B27 +ve.
- Used as a diagnostic biomarker.

⚠️ **HLA-B27 +ve alone ≠ AS.** Most B27-positive people never develop AS. It's a risk marker, not a diagnosis.

### Q: Three pathogenic cytokines in AS (and therapeutic targets).

- **TNF-α**
- **IL-17**
- **IL-23**

### Q: What other genetic abnormality contributes to AS pathogenesis?

Aberrant peptide processing pathways — abnormal **aminopeptidases** in the endoplasmic reticulum — linked to abnormal peptide presentation by HLA-B27.

## Ankylosing spondylitis — natural history (cascade)

```
Step 1: Spinal enthesitis
        (chronic inflammation at tendon/ligament insertions)
            │
            ▼
Step 2: Bridging syndesmophytes
        (new bone growth between adjacent vertebrae)
            │
            ▼
Step 3: Spinal fusion
        (rigid spine, "bamboo spine" radiographically)
```

📎 Figure L1.20 — Bridging syndesmophytes / spinal fusion in untreated AS

### Q: Walk through the AS natural history cascade.

- **Step 1** — enthesitis at vertebral corners and ligament insertions
- **Step 2** — chronic inflammation drives ossification → syndesmophytes bridge adjacent vertebrae
- **Step 3** — once enough syndesmophytes form, motion is lost → spinal fusion → fixed flexion deformity

### Q: Why does early diagnosis matter so much in AS?

- Treatment goal is to interrupt at **Step 1** (active inflammation), before structural damage.
- Once fusion occurs (Step 3), it's irreversible.
- This is why MRI (sees Step 1 inflammation) beats X-ray (only sees Steps 2–3 structural change) for early diagnosis.

## Ankylosing spondylitis — investigation

📎 Figure L1.18 — Radiographic sacroiliitis on AP pelvis (irregularity, sclerosis)
📎 Figure L1.19 — Sacroiliitis on MRI: bone marrow oedema at SIJs
📎 Figure L1.21 — "Shiny corners" sign on MRI (vertebral corner oedema, L4/L5/S1)

### Q: Why is MRI preferred over X-ray for diagnosing AS?

- **MRI detects bone marrow oedema** = active inflammation.
- This shows up **before** X-ray structural changes (syndesmophytes, fusion).
- Lets you treat in the early window before damage is fixed.

### Q: What two MRI signs of active AS should you recognise?

- **Sacroiliitis** on MRI — bone marrow oedema at the SI joints, lighting up either side of the joint line.
- **"Shiny corners" sign** — vertebral corner oedema at L4, L5, S1 on sagittal MRI.

### Q: What does X-ray sacroiliitis look like and why is it a late sign?

- AP pelvis: SI joint irregularity, sclerosis, eventual erosion or fusion.
- These are **structural** changes, occurring after years of active inflammation — too late for early treatment.

## Ankylosing spondylitis — management

```
Step 1 — Physiotherapy + life-long exercise programme
            │
            ▼
Step 2 — NSAIDs (1st-line drug)
         ibuprofen / naproxen / diclofenac
         (try at least 2 NSAIDs first)
            │
            ▼
Step 3 — Biologics (2nd-line drug)
         Anti-TNF-α: -mab suffix
         Anti-IL-17: secukinumab
```

⭐ Trigger to escalate to biologics: **inadequate disease control after trying 2 NSAIDs**.

### Q: Mechanism of NSAIDs in AS.

- Inhibit **COX-1 and COX-2** → block conversion of arachidonic acid to prostaglandins.
- Reduces prostaglandin-mediated **pain and inflammation**.
- Cardinal first-line drug in AS axial disease.

### Q: NSAID side effect profile — derive from the COX pathway.

| Lost effect | Resulting risk |
|---|---|
| Gastric mucosa protection | Peptic ulcer / GI bleed |
| Renal blood flow | Acute kidney injury |
| (Shunt to leukotrienes) | Bronchoconstriction → asthma exacerbation |
| Long-term COX inhibition | ↑ atherothrombosis (MI, stroke) |

🧠 **Reusable pattern:** know one mechanism (COX → prostaglandins → multiple downstream effects) and you can derive every NSAID side effect from it. Don't memorise the side effects as a list.

### Q: What does a selective COX-2 inhibitor (celecoxib) reduce, and what does it not reduce?

- **Reduces:** GI ulcer risk (spares COX-1 in gastric mucosa).
- **Does NOT reduce:** cardiovascular (atherothrombosis) risk.

### Q: Biologics in AS — class identity, not drug names.

- **Anti-TNF-α** monoclonal antibodies — adalimumab, certolizumab, infliximab, golimumab (the "-mab" suffix).
- **Anti-IL-17** monoclonal antibody — secukinumab.
- Used after failed trial of 2 NSAIDs.

⚠️ The lecturer explicitly de-emphasised individual biologic names — **class identity** is what's testable.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: Imaging choice rules across back pain.

- **Bony detail** (fracture, mets) → CT or X-ray
- **Soft tissue / cord / nerve roots / discs / marrow oedema** → MRI
- **Suspected cauda equina** → same-day MRI lumbar spine
- **Early AS** → MRI SI joints/spine
- **Mechanical, no red flags** → no imaging

### Q: Blood test logic across causes of back pain.

- ESR / CRP up → inflammation or infection
- ALP up + normal liver enzymes → bone (mets)
- Calcium up + back pain → myeloma or bony mets
- PSA up + older man + back pain → prostate Ca with mets
- ↓ Hb, ↑ WCC → myeloma / chronic inflammation / infection

### Q: When to escalate from first-line to biologic in immune-mediated rheumatic disease.

- Trial **first-line agent** (NSAIDs in AS, methotrexate in RA).
- If inadequate after a defined trial (2 NSAIDs in AS), escalate to **monoclonal antibody** targeting the cytokine driver (TNF-α, IL-17, IL-23).
- Recurring rheumatology pattern — recognise it across lectures.

### Q: How to spot an inflammatory back pain stem in 5 seconds.

- Young adult
- Buttock or low back pain >3 months
- Morning stiffness ≥1 hour
- Better with movement, worse at rest
- NSAIDs help
- Possibly HLA-B27 +ve, ESR/CRP raised

🧠 The single signature: **better with movement, worse at rest** is the inverse of mechanical pain. If the stem says this, it's signalling AS or a related SpA.

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 67-y-o woman with prior breast cancer presents with 6 weeks of progressive thoracic back pain, worse at night. No neurology. Single best next investigation?

**Options:** A) Lumbar X-ray  B) MRI whole spine  C) CT thoracic spine  D) ESR alone  E) Reassure, review in 4 weeks

**Answer: B — MRI whole spine.**

- Multiple red flags: prior malignancy, age >55, thoracic pain, progressive, night pain.
- MRI is the best modality for cord, marrow infiltration, multilevel mets.
- A: X-rays insensitive for early metastatic deposits.
- C: CT inferior for soft tissue and marrow.
- D: bloods support but don't replace imaging here.
- E: never safety-net a patient with multiple red flags.

### Q: SBA 2 — 24-y-o man, 3 days lumbar pain after lifting, worse on bending, eased lying flat, normal neuro exam. Best management?

**Options:** A) Bed rest  B) Urgent MRI  C) NSAIDs + activity + safety-net  D) Neurosurgery referral  E) Oral prednisolone

**Answer: C — NSAIDs, keep moving, review.**

- Classic mechanical back pain, no red flags.
- A: bed rest worsens outcomes (slide bolded "AVOID").
- B: imaging without red flags is unhelpful (false positives common).
- D: no neurological compromise.
- E: not standard for mechanical back pain.

### Q: SBA 3 — 38-y-o woman, 36 h severe LBP, bilateral leg pain, urinary hesitancy, reduced perineal sensation. Single best next step?

**Options:** A) NSAIDs + physio  B) MRI in 2 weeks  C) Lumbar X-ray  D) Same-day MRI lumbar spine  E) Bladder catheter + conservative

**Answer: D — same-day MRI lumbar spine.**

- Cauda equina pentad: bilateral leg pain + saddle anaesthesia + urinary symptoms.
- Untreated → permanent paralysis.
- A and B delay imaging — dangerous.
- C: X-ray cannot show nerve root compression.
- E: treats a symptom while cause progresses.

### Q: SBA 4 — 28-y-o man from Pakistan, 6 weeks worsening thoracolumbar pain, weight loss, night sweats, ESR markedly raised. Most likely diagnosis?

**Options:** A) AS  B) Mechanical  C) Spinal TB  D) Myeloma  E) Metastatic prostate Ca

**Answer: C — spinal TB.**

- Endemic country + constitutional symptoms + subacute progressive back pain + raised ESR.
- A: AS would give morning stiffness and NSAID-responsive pain, not night sweats + weight loss.
- B: red flags rule it out.
- D: myeloma rare at 28 (typically >65).
- E: prostate Ca rare under 50.

### Q: SBA 5 — 26-y-o man, 9 months LBP + buttock pain, morning stiffness >1 h, improved by exercise, reduced lumbar flexion, HLA-B27 +ve. Best investigation to confirm active disease?

**Options:** A) Lumbar X-ray  B) MRI sacroiliac joints  C) CT lumbar spine  D) Repeat HLA-B27  E) Synovial biopsy

**Answer: B — MRI sacroiliac joints.**

- AS pattern + B27 +ve. MRI shows active sacroiliitis (bone marrow oedema) before X-ray changes.
- A: X-ray changes are late.
- C: CT poor for soft tissue/marrow.
- D: B27 already known.
- E: not how AS is diagnosed.

### Q: SBA 6 — 30-y-o woman with AS on long-term naproxen develops asthma exacerbation. Best mechanism?

**Options:** A) Stimulates IgE  B) Shunts arachidonic acid to leukotrienes → bronchoconstriction  C) Depletes gastric prostaglandins  D) Blocks β2-adrenergic receptors  E) Displaces salbutamol from albumin

**Answer: B — leukotriene shunt.**

- COX inhibition redirects arachidonic acid down the leukotriene pathway → bronchoconstriction.
- A, D, E: fictional pharmacology.
- C: real but causes ulcers, not asthma.

### Q: SBA 7 — 72-y-o man with prostate cancer, 4 weeks progressive LBP worse at night. Hb 10.2, Ca²⁺ 2.7, ↑ALP, PSA 350. Most likely diagnosis?

**Options:** A) Mechanical  B) AS  C) Vertebral metastases  D) Vertebral osteomyelitis  E) Disc herniation

**Answer: C — vertebral metastases.**

- Known prostate Ca, age >55, night pain, anaemia, hypercalcaemia, raised ALP, enormous PSA.
- A and B fail on age and red flags.
- D: would need fever and inflammation pattern.
- E: doesn't explain biochemistry.

### Q: SBA 8 — 24-y-o man, 6 months LBP waking him in second half of night, relieved by getting up. Morning stiffness >1 h, SI joint tenderness. Most likely diagnosis?

**Options:** A) Disc herniation  B) Mechanical  C) AS  D) Vertebral osteomyelitis  E) Cauda equina

**Answer: C — ankylosing spondylitis.**

- Inflammatory pattern: nocturnal pain relieved by movement, prolonged stiffness, SI joint tenderness in young man.
- A: would give radicular leg pain.
- B: wrong direction on movement/rest axis.
- D: needs fever and ↑CRP.
- E: needs sphincter symptoms.

### Q: SBA 9 — 55-y-o woman with RA on methotrexate + prednisolone, 3 weeks severe localised lumbar pain + fever, CRP 180, ↑WCC. Best initial imaging?

**Options:** A) Lumbar X-ray  B) MRI lumbar spine  C) CT abdomen  D) Bone scan  E) No imaging

**Answer: B — MRI lumbar spine.**

- Immunosuppressed + fever + ↑inflammatory markers + focal back pain → vertebral osteomyelitis / discitis / paraspinal abscess.
- MRI visualises infection, abscess, cord.
- A: misses early disease.
- C: wrong territory.
- D: non-specific, not first-line.
- E: ignores red flags.

### Q: SBA 10 — 35-y-o man, acute right-sided sciatica for 10 days. Power 5/5, sensation intact, no sphincter symptoms. Best management?

**Options:** A) Same-day MRI  B) Surgical discectomy  C) NSAIDs + physio + safety-net  D) Anti-TNF biologic  E) CT-guided nerve root injection now

**Answer: C — conservative.**

- Sciatica without red flags = no imaging, conservative.
- A: over-investigation.
- B: over-treatment without neurological compromise.
- D: for inflammatory disease.
- E: step 2 of the ladder, only after failed conservative.

### Q: SBA 11 — 68-y-o woman fell from standing, acute mid-thoracic pain radiating round chest wall, focal vertebral tenderness. Most likely diagnosis?

**Options:** A) Disc herniation T7  B) Osteoporotic wedge fracture  C) Cauda equina  D) AS  E) Aortic dissection

**Answer: B — osteoporotic wedge fracture.**

- Post-menopausal, low-energy trauma, focal thoracic tenderness, chest-wall radiation.
- A: rare in thoracic spine (rib cage splints).
- C: needs sphincter/saddle features.
- D: young person's disease.
- E: tearing chest pain, not focal tenderness.

### Q: SBA 12 — 40-y-o man, 24 h bilateral leg weakness, urinary retention, reduced perineal sensation, reduced tone, absent ankle reflexes. Best next step?

**Options:** A) Outpatient neurology  B) Lumbar X-ray  C) Same-day MRI lumbar spine  D) Reassure, review 2 weeks  E) Oral dexamethasone

**Answer: C — same-day MRI lumbar spine.**

- Cauda equina pentad complete. Anything other than urgent MRI is wrong.

### Q: SBA 13 — 45-y-o man with AS on naproxen 500 BD for 18 months with persisting morning stiffness and night pain. Tried ibuprofen previously. Best next step?

**Options:** A) Add paracetamol  B) Switch to celecoxib for CV safety  C) Refer for anti-TNF biologic  D) Increase naproxen  E) Oral prednisolone

**Answer: C — refer for anti-TNF biologic.**

- Failed two NSAIDs → criterion for biologic escalation.
- A: doesn't address inflammation.
- B: celecoxib reduces GI not CV risk.
- D: ignores threshold rule.
- E: not standard for axial AS.

## Reusable reasoning patterns

🧠 **The whole lecture in one move:** every back pain stem is testing whether you can detect red flags. No flags → conservative + safety-net. Flags present → identify which flag → match to the cause it points at → choose the imaging that visualises that cause.

### Q: Decision algorithm to apply to any back pain SBA stem.

- Identify all red flags in the stem.
- Match flag pattern → which serious cause is it pointing at?
- Use cause to predict best investigation (MRI for cord/inflammation/infection; CT for bone; bloods for systemic).
- For management: follow the ladder of the matched cause.

### Q: How to differentiate sciatica from cauda equina in 10 seconds.

- **Sciatica** = unilateral leg pain, no sphincter or saddle symptoms, normal neuro exam → conservative.
- **Cauda equina** = bilateral leg symptoms ± saddle anaesthesia ± sphincter dysfunction → same-day MRI.

### Q: How to derive any NSAID side effect from one mechanism.

- NSAIDs inhibit COX → block prostaglandins.
- Lost prostaglandin function → predict the consequence (gastric, renal, platelet, atherothrombotic).
- Arachidonic acid shunts to leukotrienes → bronchoconstriction → asthma trigger.
