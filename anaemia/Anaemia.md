## Territory map

```
ANAEMIA
├── REDUCED RED CELL SURVIVAL  (haemolytic — retics ↑, LDH ↑, unconj bili ↑, haptoglobin ↓)
│   ├── Inherited
│   │   ├── Membrane              → HEREDITARY SPHEROCYTOSIS  (Case 1)
│   │   ├── Haemoglobin           → SICKLE CELL  (not in this lecture)
│   │   └── Enzyme (HMP shunt)    → G6PD DEFICIENCY  (Case 3)
│   └── Acquired
│       ├── Immune (DAT+)         → AIHA  (Case 2)
│       ├── Mechanical            → MAHA, HUS
│       ├── Oxidant (drugs)       → dapsone, primaquine — often + G6PD
│       └── Microbiological       → malaria
│
└── REDUCED RED CELL PRODUCTION  (retics low/normal)
    ├── Microcytic   — defect in Hb synthesis
    │   ├── Haem ↓                → IRON DEFICIENCY (Case 4) | ANAEMIA OF CHRONIC DISEASE (Case 5)
    │   └── Globin ↓              → THALASSAEMIA
    ├── Normocytic                → recent bleed | early IDA | BM failure/infiltration | hypersplenism
    └── Macrocytic                → MEGALOBLASTIC (B12/folate, Case 6) | drugs | liver/alcohol | haemolysis
```

🧠 **Three-question reasoning pattern for any anaemia stem.** (1) Production failure or excess loss/destruction — answered by the reticulocyte count. (2) MCV — micro/normo/macro narrows the differential. (3) Iron studies / B12-folate / DAT — nails the cause. Apply this before reaching for memorised lists.

## Case 1 — Hereditary Spherocytosis

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.01 — HS blood film: spherocytes lacking central pallor

### Q: What is hereditary spherocytosis and what's the molecular defect?

- Inherited red cell membrane disorder → spherical, less deformable RBCs → trapped and destroyed in spleen (extravascular haemolysis)
- **Defect:** disruption of **vertical linkages** in the membrane skeleton, usually **ankyrin** or **spectrin**
- Result: chronic compensated haemolysis with low-grade jaundice from infancy/childhood

### Q: What makes a spherocyte recognisable on a film?

- Round and dense, slightly smaller than normal
- **Loss of central pallor** (normal RBCs are biconcave discs → pale centre on smear)
- Sit darker against the background

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.02 — SEM: spherocyte (sphere) vs normal RBC (biconcave disc)
📎 Figure HAEM-L1.03 — Red cell membrane skeleton: ankyrin/spectrin vertical linkages

### Q: How does the bone marrow respond to chronic haemolysis?

- Reticulocytosis — young RBCs released early to compensate
- On film: **polychromatic macrocytes** (slightly bluer, larger)
- Reticulocyte count high (e.g. >150 ×10⁹/L; in HS often 200+)

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.04 — Polychromatic macrocytes (arrowed)

### Q: Lab signature of haemolysis (any cause)?

- **LDH raised** — released from lysed cells
- **Unconjugated bilirubin raised** — pre-hepatic, from Hb breakdown
- **Haptoglobin reduced** — mops up free Hb, gets depleted
- **Reticulocytes raised** — marrow compensation

⭐ **High-yield.** Anaemia + raised unconjugated bilirubin + raised LDH + reticulocytosis = haemolysis until proven otherwise.

### Q: Why does HS cause pigment gallstones — and at young age?

- Chronic Hb breakdown → high bilirubin turnover
- Excess bilirubin precipitates in bile → **pigment gallstones**
- Can present as obstructive jaundice in adolescence/young adult

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.05 — Pigment gallstones in chronic haemolysis

### Q: Unconjugated vs conjugated bilirubin — three discriminators?

| Property | Unconjugated | Conjugated |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Solubility | Insoluble (albumin-bound) | Water-soluble |
| Where it accumulates | Pre-hepatic jaundice (haemolysis) | Post-hepatic (cholestasis) |
| Urine | Cannot be excreted | Excreted → dark urine |

### Q: Treatment of HS?

- **Folic acid** — increased erythropoiesis demand
- **Splenectomy** if severe — removes the destruction site, prolongs RBC lifespan
- Cholecystectomy if symptomatic gallstones

⚠️ **Trap.** Long-standing HS patient + sudden RUQ pain + a switch to **CONJUGATED** hyperbilirubinaemia → think obstructive jaundice from a pigment stone in the CBD, not a haemolytic crisis.

## Case 2 — Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia (AIHA)

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.06 — AIHA blood film: spherocytes + reactive lymphocyte/neutrophil

### Q: What is AIHA and what test confirms it?

- Acquired haemolysis from autoantibodies binding the RBC membrane → splenic destruction
- **DAT (Direct Antiglobulin Test) positive** confirms immune mechanism
- Film also shows **spherocytes** — Ig-coated patches stripped by the spleen → sphering

### Q: How does the DAT actually work?

- Patient's RBCs are already coated with human IgG (autoantibody)
- Add **rabbit anti-human Ig** in a test tube
- Rabbit antibody bridges adjacent IgG-coated RBCs → visible agglutination
- Agglutination = positive DAT

### Q: Three categories of cause for AIHA?

- **Idiopathic** — no underlying cause found
- **Systemic autoimmune disease** — classically **SLE**
- **Underlying lymphoid malignancy** — classically **lymphoma** (also CLL)

### Q: Spherocytes appear in TWO conditions — how do you tell them apart?

- **Hereditary spherocytosis** — DAT NEGATIVE, family history, lifelong
- **AIHA** — DAT POSITIVE, acquired, often older patient with autoimmune/lymphoma clue

🧠 **Pattern.** Spherocytes on film = HS or AIHA. **DAT decides which.**

### Q: Why was Case 2's MCV mildly raised (104 fL) despite haemolytic — not megaloblastic — anaemia?

- Reticulocytes are **~20% larger** than mature RBCs
- Brisk reticulocytosis pulls up the average MCV
- This is non-megaloblastic macrocytosis — different mechanism from B12/folate (impaired DNA synthesis)

⚠️ **Trap.** Macrocytosis with HIGH retics → haemolysis. Macrocytosis with LOW retics → megaloblastic / liver / drugs. Don't conflate.

## Case 3 — G6PD Deficiency

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.07 — Glycolysis and pentose (HMP) shunt; G6PD generates NADPH

### Q: What does G6PD do, and why does deficiency matter?

- **G6PD** is the rate-limiting enzyme of the **HMP / pentose phosphate shunt**
- Generates **NADPH** → reduces glutathione → neutralises oxidative stress in RBCs
- Without it, oxidants damage Hb and membrane → haemolysis when oxidant load is high

### Q: Inheritance and epidemiology of G6PD?

- **X-linked** — males more severely affected
- Very common in **Africa, Mediterranean, Middle East, South & Southeast Asia** (up to 26%)
- The geography is a stem clue ("Sardinian man", "West African child")

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.08 — Global distribution of G6PD deficiency

### Q: What triggers a haemolytic crisis in G6PD?

- **Oxidant drugs** — dapsone, primaquine, sulfonamides, nitrofurantoin
- **Fava beans** (broad beans) — favism
- **Naphthalene** (mothballs)
- **Infection** — endogenous oxidative stress

### Q: Blood film features in a G6PD crisis?

- **Heinz bodies** — precipitated oxidised haemoglobin (small inclusions)
- **Bite cells / irregularly contracted cells** — spleen has bitten Heinz bodies out
- **Ghost cells** — empty membrane shells = intravascular haemolysis
- **Hemighosts** — half-empty cells (Hb pushed to one side)

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.09 — G6PD crisis film: Heinz bodies, ghosts, hemighosts, contracted cells
📎 Figure HAEM-L1.10 — Heinz bodies on standard Romanowsky stain

### Q: Management of G6PD?

- Confirm with **enzyme assay** — do AFTER the acute phase (young retics still have enzyme → false-normal during crisis)
- **Avoid** oxidant drugs, fava beans, naphthalene
- Warn patient that infection can precipitate haemolysis
- Supportive during crisis (transfusion if severe)

⭐ **Exam pattern.** Mediterranean/African/Asian background + acute jaundice + irregularly contracted cells/Heinz bodies + recent oxidant drug or fava bean = G6PD until proven otherwise.

## Case 4 — Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA)

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.11 — IDA blood film: microcytic, hypochromic, pencil cells

### Q: Iron-study pattern in IDA?

- **Hb low**, **MCV low**, **MCH/MCHC low** (microcytic, hypochromic)
- **Ferritin LOW** — storage iron empty
- **Transferrin (TIBC) HIGH** — body upregulates carrier to grab iron
- **Transferrin saturation LOW**

### Q: What does the IDA film look like?

- Small (microcytic), pale (hypochromic) — wide central pallor
- **Pencil cells** (elongated elliptocytes)
- Occasional target cells
- Reactive thrombocytosis often present

### Q: Causes of iron deficiency — three buckets?

- **Increased loss (blood loss)** — commonest in adults; menstrual; GI (often occult); **hookworm = commonest worldwide**
- **Insufficient intake/absorption** — dietary (vegetarian/vegan); coeliac disease; *H. pylori* gastritis
- **Increased requirement** — pregnancy, infancy

### Q: Clinical signs of IDA?

- **Koilonychia** — spoon-shaped nails
- **Glossitis** — smooth, sore tongue
- **Angular stomatitis** — cracked mouth corners
- Pallor, fatigue, exertional dyspnoea, ankle oedema (high-output state)

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.12 — IDA clinical signs: koilonychia, glossitis, angular stomatitis

### Q: History questions to nail the cause of IDA?

- **Diet** — vegetarian/vegan
- **GI symptoms** — dysphagia, dyspepsia, abdominal pain, change in bowel habit, haematemesis/melaena/rectal bleeding
- **Menstrual / post-menopausal bleeding**
- **Weight loss** (red flag)
- **Medications** — aspirin, NSAIDs

### Q: Investigations for IDA in an adult without an obvious cause?

- **FIT** (faecal immunochemical test) — blood in stool
- **Upper GI endoscopy** + **duodenal biopsy** (also catches coeliac)
- **Colonoscopy**
- **Coeliac antibodies** (anti-TTG)

⚠️ **Trap.** Post-menopausal woman — or any adult man — with IDA: assume GI malignancy until excluded by endoscopy + colonoscopy. **Weight loss + IDA = sigmoid carcinoma until proven otherwise.**

### Q: How does hepcidin behave in IDA?

- Low ferritin → **hepcidin LOW**
- Low hepcidin → gut iron absorption ↑ AND macrophage iron release ↑
- Body grabs every available iron molecule

### Q: Treatment of IDA?

- Treat the cause
- **Iron replacement** — **ferrous sulphate** PO (or IV iron if intolerant/malabsorption)

## Case 5 — Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)

### Q: When do you suspect ACD?

- Anaemia in someone who is **chronically unwell** — **NOT** bleeding, **NOT** haemolysing, **NOT** marrow-infiltrated, **NOT** iron/B12/folate deficient
- Common causes: **chronic infection** (TB, HIV), **autoimmune** (RA), **malignancy**
- Single-word mechanism: **inflammation**

### Q: Lab pattern in ACD?

- **Hb low**, **MCV normal or mildly low**
- **Ferritin HIGH** — acute-phase reactant + iron trapped in stores
- **Transferrin (TIBC) low/normal**
- **Transferrin saturation normal**
- **ESR/CRP high**

### Q: Why rouleaux on the film?

- Inflammation raises plasma proteins (fibrinogen, immunoglobulins)
- These bridge RBCs → stack like coins
- Same mechanism explains the high ESR

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.13 — Rouleaux ("stack of coins")

### Q: Pathophysiology — what does hepcidin do in inflammation?

- Inflammation → **hepcidin HIGH**
- Hepcidin **blocks gut absorption** of iron AND **blocks macrophage release** of stored iron
- Iron locked in storage → ferritin rises, serum iron falls
- Marrow can't get iron to make Hb → microcytic-leaning anaemia

🧠 **Mirror image.** IDA = low hepcidin, low ferritin, high transferrin. ACD = high hepcidin, high ferritin, low transferrin. Same axis, opposite ends.

### Q: ACD vs IDA — the discriminator table?

| Index | ACD | IDA |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ferritin | HIGH | LOW |
| Transferrin (TIBC) | Low/normal | HIGH |
| Transferrin saturation | Normal | LOW |
| ESR | High | May be high (low Hct) |

⚠️ **Trap.** A **NORMAL ferritin does NOT exclude iron deficiency.** When ACD and IDA coexist, ferritin sits in the normal range (acute-phase mask). Look at **transferrin saturation** and the clinical picture.

### Q: Treatment of ACD?

- **Treat the underlying cause** — the only durable fix
- Iron supplements alone don't work (hepcidin still blocks delivery)
- EPO ± transfusion in selected cases

## Case 6 — Megaloblastic Anaemia (B12 / Folate)

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.14 — Megaloblastic blood film: oval macrocyte + hypersegmented neutrophil

### Q: Megaloblastic vs macrocytic — what's the difference?

- **Macrocytic** = RBCs larger than normal (high MCV) — many causes
- **Megaloblastic** = subset where the bone marrow shows **megaloblasts** — large erythroid precursors with **asynchronous nucleocytoplasmic maturation** (cytoplasm matures, nucleus lags)
- Caused by impaired DNA synthesis: B12, folate, antifolate drugs, MDS

### Q: Why does B12/folate deficiency cause megaloblasts?

- Both are required for **DNA synthesis**
- Without them, the nucleus can't divide on schedule, but cytoplasm keeps making haemoglobin and growing
- Result: large cell with immature nucleus and mature cytoplasm = nucleocytoplasmic dissociation

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.15 — Normal vs megaloblastic bone marrow side-by-side
📎 Figure HAEM-L1.16 — Folate–B12 biochemistry: dietary folate → methyl-THF → (B12) → DNA precursors

### Q: Blood film features of megaloblastic anaemia?

- **Oval macrocytes** (egg-shaped, large)
- **Hypersegmented neutrophils** (≥5–6 lobes)
- Tear-drop poikilocytes
- Often pancytopenic (low WBC, low platelets too)
- Retics low/normal — marrow ineffective

### Q: Causes of B12 deficiency by site, and how to treat each?

| Site | Cause | Treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Diet | Veganism | Oral B12 |
| Stomach (intrinsic factor) | Gastrectomy/bariatric; **pernicious anaemia** (anti-GPC, anti-IF Abs) | **IM hydroxocobalamin** |
| Terminal ileum | Crohn's, ileal resection | **IM hydroxocobalamin** |

### Q: Causes of folate deficiency?

- **Dietary** — poverty, alcoholism
- **Malabsorption** — coeliac disease, jejunal resection (proximal small bowel)
- **Increased demand** — pregnancy, lactation, chronic haemolysis
- Treatment: oral folate supplement

### Q: Pernicious anaemia — what's the autoimmunity and why does it matter?

- Autoantibodies against **gastric parietal cells (anti-GPC)** and **intrinsic factor (anti-IF)**
- Parietal cells make IF; IF binds B12 in stomach; complex absorbed in terminal ileum
- No IF → no B12 absorption → deficiency
- Often associated with other autoimmune disease — vitiligo, autoimmune thyroid

⭐ **Classic stem.** Older woman + vitiligo + macrocytic anaemia + "walking on cotton wool" + low B12 → pernicious anaemia. Treat with **IM hydroxocobalamin**, NOT oral B12.

⚠️ **Severe trap.** Neurological complications of B12 deficiency are **severe and can be irreversible**: dementia and **subacute combined degeneration (SACD) of the cord**. Folate deficiency in pregnancy → **neural tube defects**. **Replace B12 BEFORE folate** — giving folate alone in B12 deficiency can precipitate SACD.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: When should I suspect haemolytic anaemia (4 prompts)?

- **Otherwise unexplained anaemia** — normochromic, usually normocytic or macrocytic
- **Morphologically abnormal RBCs** on film (spherocytes, sickle cells, schistocytes, irregularly contracted cells)
- **Increased red cell breakdown** — raised LDH, unconjugated bilirubin, low haptoglobins
- **Increased marrow activity** — reticulocytosis, polychromasia

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.17 — Suspecting haemolytic anaemia: morphological clues
📎 Figure HAEM-L1.18 — Sickle cells and schistocytes/fragments
📎 Figure HAEM-L1.19 — Clinical signs in chronic haemolysis: pigment stones + scleral icterus

### Q: Intravascular vs extravascular haemolysis?

- **Intravascular** — acute, severe damage inside vessels → free Hb in plasma → **ghost cells, haemoglobinuria, very low haptoglobin** (e.g. G6PD crisis, MAHA, ABO incompatibility)
- **Extravascular** — defective RBCs cleared by spleen → spherocytes, less dramatic Hb release (e.g. HS, AIHA)
- Often mixed in practice

### Q: Anaemia by cell size — first-pass differential?

| MCV | Common causes |
| --- | --- |
| Microcytic | IDA, ACD, thalassaemia |
| Normocytic | Acute bleed, early IDA, BM failure/infiltration, hypersplenism, sickle splenic sequestration |
| Macrocytic | B12/folate deficiency, drugs (DNA synthesis), liver/alcohol, haemolysis (high retics) |

### Q: Why do haemolytic anaemias raise the MCV?

- Reticulocytes are **~20% larger** than mature RBCs
- Brisk reticulocytosis raises the average cell volume
- Mechanism is reticulocyte size, NOT impaired DNA synthesis
- Result: "non-megaloblastic" macrocytosis

📎 Figure HAEM-L1.20 — Microcyte vs normal RBC vs macrocyte (single-cell view)
📎 Figure HAEM-L1.21 — Haemoglobin: 2α + 2β globin chains, each binding one haem (Fe²⁺)

### Q: Why is microcytic anaemia about Hb synthesis?

- Microcytosis = reduced Hb per cell
- Hb = **haem (Fe²⁺ + protoporphyrin) + globin chains** (2α + 2β)
- Reduced **haem** → IDA (no iron) or ACD (iron locked away)
- Reduced **globin** → thalassaemia (genetic chain defect)

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern.** When you see microcytic anaemia, ask: "Haem problem or globin problem?" Iron studies answer the haem question; **Hb electrophoresis (HbA2)** answers the globin question. Both unhelpful → think early IDA or ACD evolving.

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1. A 10-year-old girl: scleral icterus, Hb 98 g/L, MCV 92 fL, unconjugated bili 30 µmol/L, normal LFTs otherwise, retics 230. Film shows round cells without central pallor. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Hereditary spherocytosis**
- Spherocytes + chronic haemolysis pattern + child
- Confirm with osmotic fragility / EMA binding; family history; **DAT will be NEGATIVE** (distinguishes from AIHA)

### Q: SBA 2. A 38-year-old woman with SLE: Hb 87, MCV 104, unconjugated bili 42, LDH 650, DAT +++. Diagnosis and mechanism?

- **AIHA secondary to SLE**
- Autoantibodies coat RBCs → splenic destruction (extravascular)
- Spherocytes on film come from Ig-coated patches stripped by spleen
- DAT positive = the discriminator from HS

### Q: SBA 3. Previously healthy Sardinian man develops jaundice 2 days after starting an antimalarial. Hb 77, retics 320, irregularly contracted cells and Heinz bodies on film. Diagnosis?

- **G6PD deficiency** triggered by primaquine
- Mediterranean ancestry + oxidant drug + Heinz/contracted cells
- Confirm with G6PD enzyme assay AFTER the crisis (retic-rich blood gives false-normal during crisis)

### Q: SBA 4. A 58-year-old woman: Hb 80, MCV 70, MCH low, ferritin low, transferrin high, TfSat low. Most likely cause and next investigation?

- **Iron deficiency anaemia**
- Most concerning cause in this demographic = **GI malignancy**
- Next step: **upper GI endoscopy + colonoscopy** (+ coeliac antibodies, FIT)

### Q: SBA 5. A 45-year-old man with severe RA: Hb 85, MCV 70, **ferritin HIGH**, transferrin LOW, TfSat normal, ESR 79. Diagnosis?

- **Anaemia of chronic disease**
- The ferritin–transferrin pattern is the mirror image of IDA
- Mechanism: hepcidin upregulated by inflammation → iron locked in stores

### Q: SBA 6. A 62-year-old woman: pale + yellow tinge, vitiligo, Hb 45, MCV 128, low WBC and platelets, B12 90 ng/L, folate normal. Best treatment?

- **IM hydroxocobalamin** (pernicious anaemia until proven otherwise)
- Vitiligo + pancytopenia + very high MCV + low B12 = autoimmune pernicious anaemia
- Oral B12 won't work — no intrinsic factor to absorb it

### Q: SBA 7. Both HS and AIHA can show spherocytes. Which single test discriminates them?

- **DAT (Direct Antiglobulin Test)**
- POSITIVE → AIHA (immune)
- NEGATIVE → HS (hereditary)

### Q: SBA 8. Patient with anaemia, ferritin in normal range, transferrin saturation 8%. Iron deficient or not?

- **Cannot exclude IDA on ferritin alone**
- A normal ferritin can mask IDA when ACD coexists (ferritin is an acute-phase reactant)
- Low TfSat 8% strongly supports iron-restricted erythropoiesis → likely IDA ± ACD

### Q: SBA 9. Patient with macrocytic anaemia. Reticulocytes are HIGH. Megaloblastic or not?

- **Not megaloblastic** — high retics drive non-megaloblastic macrocytosis
- Look for haemolysis (LDH, unconjugated bili, haptoglobin, blood film)
- Megaloblastic anaemia has LOW/normal retics — marrow can't make cells fast enough

### Q: SBA 10. Patient with chronic haemolysis develops acute RUQ pain and a switch to **CONJUGATED** hyperbilirubinaemia. Most likely cause?

- **Pigment gallstone obstructing the common bile duct** → obstructive jaundice
- Liver still conjugates bilirubin, but it can't reach the duodenum → conjugated bili rises
- Distinguishes from a haemolytic crisis (which would be unconjugated)

### Q: SBA 11. Treatment of B12 deficiency in ileal Crohn's vs in dietary veganism?

- **Ileal Crohn's** — IM hydroxocobalamin (terminal ileum diseased, can't absorb)
- **Veganism** — oral B12 supplements (gut works fine, just no dietary intake)

### Q: SBA 12. Patient has B12 AND folate deficiency. Replace folate first?

- **NO. Replace B12 first.**
- Folate alone in B12 deficiency can precipitate or worsen **subacute combined degeneration of the cord** (irreversible)

### Q: SBA 13. Microcytic anaemia, normal ferritin, normal iron studies, raised HbA2. Diagnosis?

- **β-thalassaemia trait**
- Normal iron studies but microcytosis = think globin problem
- Hb electrophoresis showing raised **HbA2** confirms

### Q: SBA 14. Most common cause of iron deficiency worldwide vs in UK adults?

- **Worldwide:** hookworm infestation
- **UK adults:** menstrual blood loss (women) and occult GI blood loss (men + post-menopausal women)

### Q: SBA 15. Pyruvate kinase deficiency — high-yield exam topic?

- **No.** Per the slide: "Rare, don't worry about it. Just for completeness."
- Recognise the name as a glycolytic-pathway enzyme defect; don't memorise the detail
