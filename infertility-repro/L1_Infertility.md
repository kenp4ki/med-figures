## The unifying intuition

🧠 Every endocrine cause of infertility sits at one of three levels — hypothalamus, pituitary, gonad. Each gives a signature LH/FSH/steroid pattern. Pattern → level → 3–4 named syndromes.

## Part A — HPG axis and three-level framework

📎 Figure L1.5 — HPG axis

### The axis skeleton

```
Hypothalamus
├─ Kisspeptin neurons  ← feedback lands HERE only
└─ GnRH neurons        → pulsatile GnRH → portal circulation
        ↓
Anterior Pituitary
└─ Gonadotrophs        → LH & FSH → systemic circulation
        ↓
Gonads
├─ Men:   LH → Leydig cells → testosterone
│         FSH → Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis
└─ Women: LH + FSH → follicle + ovulation
          → oestradiol (pre-ov) + progesterone (corpus luteum)
        ↑
Feedback: T (aromatised to E2) + E2 + progesterone → kisspeptin only
```

### Q: Why can't we measure GnRH directly?

> - Released into hypophyseal-portal circulation (tiny private bloodstream)
> - Too dilute in systemic circulation to detect
> - LH and FSH are surrogate markers

### Q: Which neurons carry the oestrogen receptor?

> **Kisspeptin neurons only.** GnRH neurons do not. All feedback acts via kisspeptin.

### Q: Why does this matter pharmacologically?

> Every drug that modulates the axis — letrozole, clomiphene, COCP, HRT — acts through the kisspeptin feedback route.

### Q: Why does pulsatility matter clinically?

> - GnRH pulses ~every 90 min → LH, FSH, T/E2 all pulsatile
> - Testosterone highest in morning → morning fasting sample standard

### Q: Pulsatile vs continuous GnRH — same drug, opposite effects?

> - **Pulsatile** (pump) → stimulates pituitary
> - **Continuous** (agonist) → desensitises, suppresses
> - L2: pulsatile for HA; continuous for trans women

### Q: State the three-level framework.

> - **Hypothalamus:** ↓LH ↓FSH ↓steroid → hypogonadotrophic
> - **Pituitary:** ↓LH ↓FSH ↓steroid → hypogonadotrophic (same bloods)
> - **Gonad:** ↑LH ↑FSH ↓steroid → hypergonadotrophic

🧠 Can't separate hypothalamic from pituitary on bloods alone. Use MRI pituitary + clinical context.

### Q: Why is PCOS not hypogonadal?

> - Dysregulation, not failure
> - ↑LH:FSH ratio, ↑androgens, **normal or mildly low E2**
> - Consequence: chronic unopposed oestrogen → endometrial Ca risk

### Q: Why does hyperprolactinaemia fit the "hypothalamic" tier?

> - PRL binds kisspeptin neurons → inhibits kisspeptin
> - Whole axis shuts down downstream
> - Bloods identical to hypothalamic lesion unless you measure PRL

⭐ Explicitly flagged as frequently examined.

### Kallmann syndrome

### Q: Kallmann — cause?

> Congenital. Mutation blocks **migration of GnRH neurons** from olfactory placode to hypothalamus during embryogenesis. GnRH neurons share the migratory path with olfactory fibres.

### Q: Kallmann — phenotype and bloods?

> - **Anosmia** + failure of puberty + infertility
> - Normosmic variant exists (migration ok, function impaired)
> - ↓GnRH (not measurable), ↓LH, ↓FSH, ↓T or ↓E2

📎 Figure L1.8 — Kallmann: failed GnRH neuron migration

🧠 **See also: L2 — hCG + FSH for sperm induction** — treatment pathway if fertility wanted.

### Functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea (FHA)

### Q: FHA — cause?

> Low energy availability shuts down kisspeptin pulsatility. Drivers: **low BMI, excessive exercise, stress**. Mechanism: low leptin + high cortisol.

### Q: FHA — phenotype and bloods?

> - Amenorrhoea in thin, highly active, or highly stressed woman
> - Low bone density from prolonged low E2
> - Same process in men (anorexia → near-undetectable T)
> - ↓LH, ↓FSH, ↓E2 (or ↓T)

### Q: FHA treatment handle?

> Lifestyle reversal first. Pulsatile GnRH pump or FSH if needed (L2).

🧠 **See also: L2 — HA ovulation induction ladder** — and why clomiphene is poor in HA.

### Hyperprolactinaemia

### Q: Hyperprolactinaemia — mechanism?

> PRL binds PRL receptors on kisspeptin neurons → inhibits kisspeptin → whole axis shuts down.

📎 Figure L1.9 — PRL binding kisspeptin; X across whole axis.

### Q: Hyperprolactinaemia — causes?

> - Prolactinoma (commonest endocrine cause)
> - Dopamine antagonists (antipsychotics — dopamine normally suppresses PRL)
> - Physiological: pregnancy, breastfeeding, nipple stimulation

### Q: Hyperprolactinaemia — sex-specific phenotype?

> - **Women:** oligomenorrhoea (>35-day cycles) or amenorrhoea (3–6 months), ± galactorrhoea, low libido, infertility, low bone density
> - **Men:** low libido dominant; galactorrhoea rare (no oestrogenised breast tissue)
> - **Mass effect:** headache, bitemporal hemianopia

### Q: Hyperprolactinaemia treatment?

> Dopamine agonist (**cabergoline**) — restores dopaminergic brake on PRL.

⚠️ Trap — ↓LH ↓FSH ↓steroid looks identical to Kallmann/FHA/hypopituitarism until you measure prolactin.

### Hypopituitarism

### Q: Hypopituitarism — causes?

> Tumour, infiltration (haemochromatosis, sarcoidosis), apoplexy (Sheehan's = postpartum infarction), surgery, radiation.

### Q: Hypopituitarism — bloods and how to separate from hypothalamic lesion?

> - ↓LH ↓FSH ↓steroid — identical to hypothalamic
> - **MRI pituitary** + cause-specific clues (postpartum haemorrhage → Sheehan's; headache/visual fields → tumour)
> - Other pituitary axes often also deficient (TSH, ACTH, GH, ADH)

### Klinefelter syndrome (47XXY)

### Q: Klinefelter — cause and epidemiology?

> - Extra X chromosome
> - 1:1100 live male births
> - ~25% undiagnosed
> - Accounts for up to 3% of male infertility

### Q: Klinefelter — phenotype?

> - **Tall stature**, mildly impaired IQ, gynaecomastia
> - Reduced facial/chest hair, female-pattern pubic hair
> - Narrow shoulders, wide hips
> - Small penis and testes, low bone density, infertility

### Q: Klinefelter — bloods?

> ↑LH, ↑FSH, ↓T — hypergonadotrophic hypogonadism.

### Q: Klinefelter — why tall?

> Low T → no aromatisation to E2 → growth plates don't fuse → plates stay open longer.

### Q: Klinefelter — treatment handle?

> - Testosterone for symptoms
> - **Surgical sperm retrieval (micro-TESE) + ICSI** for fertility — gonadotrophins don't help (gonad itself has failed)

📎 Figure L1.11 — labelled Klinefelter body diagram

🧠 **See also: L2 — testosterone replacement protocol.**

### Turner syndrome (45X0)

### Q: Turner — cause and epidemiology?

> Missing one X chromosome. 1:2500 live female births. Female mirror of Klinefelter.

### Q: Turner — phenotype?

> - Short stature, characteristic facies, webbed neck, low hairline
> - Shield chest, wide-spaced nipples, coarctation of aorta
> - Poor breast development, cubitus valgus, short 4th metacarpal
> - Small fingernails, brown nevi, underdeveloped reproductive tract, primary amenorrhoea

### Q: Turner — bloods and treatment handle?

> - ↑LH, ↑FSH, ↓E2
> - HRT for oestrogen replacement; donor-egg IVF for fertility

📎 Figure L1.12 — labelled Turner body diagram

⚠️ Slide fidelity — L1 slide 34 labels the gonadal steroid "↓T" for Turner. Slide error. Always use ↓E2 for a woman.

🧠 **See also: L2 — HRT in POI/young women.**

### Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)

### Q: POI — diagnostic criteria?

> **FSH >25 iU/L on ≥2 samples ≥4 weeks apart**, plus low oestradiol.

### Q: POI — why two samples?

> - Mid-cycle LH/FSH surge can transiently raise FSH
> - Simultaneously low E2 + high FSH distinguishes true failure (in a surge, E2 peaks too)

### Q: POI — causes and phenotype?

> - Autoimmune; genetic (Turner); iatrogenic (prior chemo/radiotherapy)
> - Early menopausal symptoms before age 40
> - Conception still possible in 20% (hence rename from "failure")

### Q: POI — treatment handle?

> HRT — especially important young (CVD + bone benefit). Donor-egg IVF if fertility desired.

⭐ The ≥2 samples ≥4 weeks apart rule and rationale.

🧠 **See also: L2 — MHT/HRT benefits and risks.**

### PCOS

### Q: PCOS — Rotterdam criteria?

> 2 of 3, after excluding other causes:
> 1. Oligo/anovulation (cycles <8–9/year)
> 2. Hyperandrogenism (clinical OR biochemical)
> 3. Polycystic morphology (≥20 follicles in one ovary OR ovarian volume >10 mL)

### Q: PCOS — clinical and biochemical hyperandrogenism?

> - **Clinical:** acne, hirsutism, alopecia
> - **Biochemical:** raised androgens (testosterone, androstenedione, DHEAS)

⚠️ "Polycystic" is a misnomer — these are arrested antral follicles, not cysts.

### Q: PCOS — bloods and central driver?

> - ↑LH:FSH ratio, raised androgens, normal/mildly ↓E2, raised fasting insulin
> - **Insulin resistance is central** — high insulin stimulates ovarian androgens and reduces SHBG (freeing more active androgen)

### Q: PCOS — epidemiology?

> - 5–15% of reproductive-age women
> - Most common endocrine disorder in women
> - Most common cause of female infertility

### Q: PCOS — complications?

> - Irregular menses, infertility, hirsutism
> - **Endometrial cancer ×2–6** (chronic unopposed E)
> - Impaired glucose tolerance / T2DM / GDM
> - Insulin resistance

### Q: PCOS treatment — irregular menses or unopposed E?

> COCP, or progesterone courses 3–4×/year (shed endometrium), or Mirena IUS.

### Q: PCOS treatment — infertility?

> Lifestyle → metformin → **letrozole** (first-line) → clomiphene → FSH (L2).

### Q: PCOS treatment — hirsutism?

> - COCP (↑SHBG → ↓free androgen)
> - Topical removal
> - Spironolactone (teratogenic — pair with contraception)

### Q: PCOS treatment — insulin resistance?

> Diet/lifestyle, metformin.

🧠 **See also: L2 — PCOS ovulation induction ladder and letrozole mechanism.**
🧠 **Compare: L2 — HRT must include progestogen if uterus present** — same unopposed E mechanism.

## Part C — Anatomical female differential

📎 Figure L1.2 — female reproductive tract with site breakdown

### Q: Six anatomical categories of female infertility — sites and percentages?

> - **Ovarian (40%):** anovulation (endocrine differential); corpus luteum insufficiency
> - **Tubal (30%):** infection, endometriosis, trauma/surgery
> - **Uterine (10%):** congenital malformations, infection/inflammation/scarring, fibroids
> - **Cervical (5%):** infection/inflammation, anti-sperm antibodies
> - **Pelvic (5%):** endometriosis, adhesions
> - **Unexplained (10%)**

⚠️ Always pregnancy test first in any reproductive-age woman with amenorrhoea.

### Q: Endometriosis — definition and mechanism?

> - Functioning endometrial tissue **outside** the uterus (fallopian tube, ovary, peritoneum, cul-de-sac)
> - Oestrogen-responsive — grows and sheds cyclically
> - Blood has nowhere to go → inflammation + adhesions

### Q: Endometriosis — phenotype and age pattern?

> - 5% of women; leans younger than fibroids
> - ↑menstrual pain, menstrual irregularities, **deep dyspareunia**, infertility

📎 Figure L1.3 — endometrial deposits at extrauterine sites

### Q: Fibroids — what and where?

> - Benign myometrial tumours; oestrogen-responsive
> - 1–20% of pre-menopausal women; frequency rises with age
> - Three locations: submucosal (most likely to cause bleeding/infertility), intramural, subserosal (can be pedunculated)

### Q: Fibroids — clinical pattern?

> - Usually asymptomatic
> - When symptomatic: mimics endometriosis + mechanical obstruction → infertility
> - Leans older than endometriosis

📎 Figure L1.4 — fibroid types

### Q: Cryptorchidism — anatomy and significance?

> - Undescended testis; 90% lodged in the inguinal canal
> - Fails to develop outside cooler scrotal environment
> - Primary gonadal failure pattern + ↑testicular cancer risk in adulthood

## Part E — Clinical workup

### Q: Male infertility — history components?

> - Duration, previous children, pubertal milestones
> - T-deficiency symptoms (low libido, loss of morning erections, ↓shaving)
> - PRL symptoms (headache, visual fields)
> - CHH features (anosmia)
> - Meds/drugs (sulfasalazine, antipsychotics, chemo, recreational)

### Q: Male infertility — examination?

> BMI; secondary sexual characteristics; testicular volume (~15 mL normal); anosmia test.

### Q: Semen analysis — WHO 5th edition thresholds?

> - Volume ≥1.5 mL
> - Sperm concentration ≥15 million/mL
> - Total motility ≥40%
> - Azoospermia = no sperm; oligospermia = reduced

### Q: Male infertility — bloods and imaging?

> - **Bloods:** LH, FSH, PRL, morning fasting testosterone, karyotyping
> - **Imaging:** scrotal US/Doppler; MRI pituitary if ↓LH/FSH or ↑PRL

### Q: Female infertility — what changes vs male workup?

> - **History:** adds menstrual history
> - **Examination:** adds hyperandrogenism signs
> - **Always first:** pregnancy test
> - **Bloods:** adds E2, androgens, **day-21 progesterone**
> - **Imaging:** transvaginal US, hysterosalpingogram (tubal patency)

### Q: Why day 21 progesterone?

> - Cycle day 1 = first day of bleeding; ovulation ≈ day 14; day 21 = mid-luteal peak
> - Progesterone made only by corpus luteum (exists only post-ovulation)
> - Raised day-21 P = ovulation happened; undetectable = no ovulation

⚠️ "Regular periods" predicts ovulation ~95%, not 100%. Day-21 P is the objective test.

## Part F — Cross-cutting pattern

### Q: Three-level triage — step 1–2?

> 1. Measure LH, FSH, gonadal steroid (+ prolactin)
> 2. Steroid ↓ + gonadotrophins ↑ → **gonadal** (Klinefelter, Turner, POI, cryptorchidism, post-chemo)

### Q: Three-level triage — step 3–4?

> 3. Steroid ↓ + gonadotrophins ↓ → **hypothalamic or pituitary** (add PRL, BMI/exercise, MRI)
> 4. Androgens ↑ + ↑LH:FSH + PCO morphology → **PCOS** (not hypogonadal)

### Q: Three-level triage — step 5 (narrowing)?

> - Anosmia → Kallmann
> - Tall + 47,XXY → Klinefelter
> - Short + webbed neck + 45,X → Turner
> - Post-chemo → iatrogenic primary
> - Low BMI → FHA
> - Postpartum haemorrhage → Sheehan's

🧠 This triage solves every endocrine infertility stem and transfers directly into L2 treatment decisions.

### Q: Common traps — same bloods, different diseases?

> Kallmann, FHA, hyperprolactinaemia (if PRL not measured), hypopituitarism all give ↓LH ↓FSH ↓steroid. Separate with anosmia test, BMI, prolactin, MRI pituitary.

### Q: "Inappropriately normal" LH/FSH — meaning?

> With low T, normal LH/FSH is functionally low — compensation should raise them.

### Q: Klinefelter vs Kallmann — how to tell apart?

> - **Klinefelter:** ↑LH/FSH + 47,XXY + tall
> - **Kallmann:** ↓LH/FSH + 46,XY + anosmia

## Part G — SBAs

### Q: SBA — 28M, low libido, ↓shaving. T 4.2, LH 14, FSH 22. Most likely site?

> **C. Testes.** Low T + raised LH/FSH = hypergonadotrophic pattern. Klinefelter, post-chemo, cryptorchidism. A/B would give everything down together.
>
> A) Hypothalamus · B) Anterior pituitary · C) Testes · D) Liver (SHBG) · E) Thyroid

### Q: SBA — 19F elite runner, BMI 16, 8 months amenorrhoea. LH 1.1, FSH 2.0, E2 low. Mechanism?

> **C.** FHA. Low fat → low leptin; high cortisol; kisspeptin/GnRH shuts down. A would give raised gonadotrophins; B raised androgens; D raised PRL; E wouldn't affect LH/FSH.
>
> A) Ovarian failure · B) Autonomous ovarian androgens · C) Hypothalamic shutdown via low leptin + high cortisol · D) Prolactinoma · E) Endometrial adhesions

### Q: SBA — 32F with PCOS, 6 months amenorrhoea, not sexually active. Why periodic progesterone?

> **C.** Chronic anovulation → no corpus luteum → no P → endometrium sees E alone → hyperplasia → Ca risk (×2–6). Same rule reappears in HRT.
>
> A) Restore fertility · B) Suppress LH/androgens · C) Induce withdrawal bleed, reduce endometrial Ca risk from unopposed E · D) Raise SHBG · E) Reverse insulin resistance

### Q: SBA — 42F, 8 months oligomenorrhoea + hot flushes. LH 48, FSH 42, E2 low, PRL normal, βhCG neg. Next step?

> **B.** Suspected POI — requires FSH >25 on ≥2 samples ≥4 weeks apart. C is correct management but premature.
>
> A) Reassure · B) Repeat FSH in 4–6 weeks · C) Start combined HRT · D) MRI pituitary · E) Karyotype

### Q: SBA — 26M, small testes (4 mL), lifelong anosmia. T 3.8, LH 1.2, FSH 1.5, PRL normal. Karyotype 46,XY. Diagnosis?

> **B. Kallmann.** Lifelong anosmia + hypogonadotrophic + 46,XY = Kallmann. A would give ↑LH/FSH + 47,XXY + tall. C would show raised PRL. D → ↑LH/FSH.
>
> A) Klinefelter · B) Kallmann · C) Prolactinoma · D) Primary testicular failure · E) Hypothalamic shutdown from under-nutrition
