## The unifying intuition

🧠 Every treatment manipulates the HPG axis in one of three directions — turn it on (fertility), turn it off (contraception; trans women), or replace the gonadal output (HRT; testosterone). Before picking a drug: where is the lesion, and am I stimulating, suppressing, or bypassing?

## Four reusable rules

### Q: Rule 1 — Why can't you give testosterone for male fertility?

> - Exogenous T → negative feedback → ↓GnRH/LH/FSH → spermatogenesis worsens
> - Spermatogenesis needs **intratesticular T ~100× serum** — only local LH stimulation of Leydig cells gets there
> - Injected serum T can't reach that concentration and shuts off the system upstream

### Q: Rule 1 corollary — when is testosterone okay?

> If fertility NOT wanted → testosterone replacement for symptoms is fine. If fertility wanted → gonadotrophins (hCG ± FSH).

### Q: Rule 2 — When must progestogen oppose oestrogen?

> Whenever exogenous oestrogen meets a functioning endometrium. Unopposed E → hyperplasia → endometrial cancer.

### Q: Rule 2 — Three contexts where the progestogen rule applies?

> - HRT in post-menopausal woman with uterus → add progestogen
> - PCOS with chronic unopposed E (L1) → periodic progesterone / Mirena / COCP
> - Trans man on T with intact uterus → add progesterone (15% otherwise hyperplasia)

### Q: Rule 2 — Exception?

> Hysterectomised women on HRT — oestrogen-only is safer (avoids breast Ca + VTE from P component).

### Q: Rule 3 — Why is transdermal oestrogen safer than oral?

> - Oral → first-pass liver metabolism → ↑clotting factor synthesis → ↑VTE, ↑stroke
> - Transdermal bypasses liver → no increase in clotting factors

📎 Figure L2.7 — oral vs transdermal oestrogen route

### Q: Rule 3 — When to default to transdermal?

> Whenever VTE/stroke risk is in the picture: BMI >30, migraine history, prior VTE, high-dose E in trans women.

### Q: Rule 4 — GnRH delivery duality?

> - **Pulsatile** (pump, ~every 90 min) → stimulates pituitary → LH/FSH release
> - **Continuous** (long-acting agonist) → desensitises → shuts axis down
> - Biology only responds to rhythm

## Territory 1 — Inducing spermatogenesis

### Q: Man with low T + low/inappropriately normal LH/FSH + male-factor infertility — treatment ladder?

> - Step 1: **hCG injections** (act on LH receptors → intratesticular T ×100 → spermatogenesis)
> - Step 2: if no response at 6 months, add **FSH injections** (Sertoli cell stimulation)
> - Do NOT give testosterone

### Q: Why hCG rather than LH?

> Same LH/hCG receptor binding; longer half-life; cheaper.

⚠️ "Inappropriately normal" LH/FSH in a man with low T = problem is above the testis. LH/FSH should be high if the testis had failed.

🧠 **See also: L1 — Kallmann, hyperprolactinaemia, hypopituitarism** — all hypogonadotrophic, all treated with this ladder if fertility wanted.

## Territory 2 — Inducing ovulation

📎 Figure L2.1 — PCOS vs HA side-by-side

### Q: Ovulation induction — aim?

> - Develop **one** follicle per cycle
> - >1 → multiple pregnancy → maternal and fetal risks
> - Small FSH rise only; cancel cycle if too many follicles develop

### Q: Letrozole — mechanism?

> - Aromatase inhibitor (ovary)
> - Blocks T → E2 conversion → ovarian E2 falls
> - Hypothalamus senses low E2 → ↓negative feedback → ↑GnRH → ↑FSH
> - E2 actually low

### Q: Clomiphene — mechanism?

> - Selective ER modulator at hypothalamus
> - Blocks ER on kisspeptin neurons → hypothalamus "sees" low E2
> - Same downstream cascade → ↑FSH
> - E2 is normal but perceived as low

### Q: Gonadotrophin injections — mechanism?

> - Direct FSH ± LH supply
> - Bypasses hypothalamus and pituitary
> - Normal feedback intact

📎 Figure L2.3 — ovulation induction three-panel mechanism

⚠️ Clomiphene is poor in HA — E2 is already low, so there's no negative feedback to remove.

### Q: PCOS ovulation induction ladder?

> 1. Lifestyle — 5% weight loss restores ovulation in many
> 2. Metformin (↓insulin)
> 3. **Letrozole** (first-line pharmacological)
> 4. Clomiphene
> 5. FSH

### Q: HA ovulation induction ladder?

> 1. Lifestyle — weight gain + reduce exercise
> 2. **Pulsatile GnRH pump** (first-line in theory)
> 3. FSH injections (used in practice — pump often unavailable)
> 4. Letrozole
> 5. Clomiphene

⭐ Letrozole first in PCOS; pulsatile GnRH first in HA.

🧠 **See also: L1 — PCOS (Rotterdam) and FHA mechanism** — diagnosis underlies this choice.

## Territory 3 — IVF

📎 Figure L2.4 — IVF 4-step process

### Q: IVF four steps?

> 1. Oocyte retrieval — high-dose FSH for multiple follicles; transvaginal needle retrieval
> 2. Fertilisation — IVF (sperm + egg in dish) or ICSI (single sperm injected)
> 3. Embryo incubation — few days
> 4. Embryo transfer — 1–2 embryos returned

### Q: Why high-dose FSH in IVF (unlike ovulation induction)?

> Drop-off at every step: not every follicle yields an egg, not every egg fertilises, not every embryo survives. Starting with many compensates.

### Q: When is ICSI used instead of standard IVF?

> Male factor infertility — low sperm count/motility/function. Single sperm injected directly into egg cytoplasm.

### Q: IVF benchmarks?

> Natural conception 85%/year; IVF ~30% per cycle; UK ~70,000 cycles/year.

🧠 **See also: L1 — Klinefelter, cryptorchidism** — gonadal failure cases where gonadotrophins don't help; ICSI or donor sperm.

## Territory 4 — Contraception

📎 Figure L2.6 — contraception efficacy bar chart

### Q: Five contraceptive categories?

> - Barrier (condoms M/F, diaphragm, cap + spermicide)
> - COCP
> - POP / mini-pill
> - LARC (IUD, IUS, depot, implant)
> - Emergency
> - Plus permanent: vasectomy, female sterilisation

### Q: Condoms — one positive, one trap?

> - **Positive:** only method that protects against STIs; free from clinics; no hormonal contraindications
> - **Trap:** two condoms are NOT better than one — friction increases rupture risk

### Q: COCP — three mechanisms that prevent pregnancy?

> - E+P negative feedback at hypothalamus (↓GnRH) and pituitary (↓LH/FSH) → **anovulation**
> - Progesterone thickens cervical mucus → sperm can't cross
> - Progesterone thins endometrium → reduces implantation

📎 Figure L2.5 — OCP mechanism on HPG axis

### Q: COCP — key positives?

> - Easy (one pill, any time)
> - Doesn't interrupt sex
> - Back-to-back packs avoid bleeds
> - **Reduces endometrial and ovarian cancer risk**
> - Weight-neutral in 80%

### Q: COCP — key negatives?

> - Difficult to remember
> - No STI protection
> - P450 inducers reduce efficacy
> - Not ideal in breastfeeding
> - Rare: VTE ~2/10,000

### Q: COCP non-contraceptive uses?

> Endometriosis/fibroids; dysmenorrhoea; menorrhagia; bleed regulation; PCOS (↓LH, ↑SHBG → ↓free androgen).

⚠️ COCP reduces ovarian/endometrial Ca. HRT increases them. Opposite signs.

### Q: COCP — absolute contraindications?

> - **Migraine with aura** (regardless of route)
> - Smoking >15/day at age >35
> - History of stroke or CVD
> - Current breast cancer

### Q: POP — when preferred over COCP?

> - Cannot take oestrogen (VTE risk, migraine with aura, breast Ca)
> - Breastfeeding
> - Caveat: must be taken at same time each day (shorter-acting)

### Q: Copper IUD vs progesterone IUS (Mirena) — distinction?

> - **IUD (copper):** device, no hormones, mechanical. Lasts 5–10y. Can cause heavy periods. Usable as emergency contraception.
> - **IUS (progesterone, Mirena):** local P → thins endometrium + thickens mucus. Lasts 5y. Treats heavy bleeding. Minimal systemic P.

### Q: Depot progesterone and subdermal implant?

> - **Depot:** 3-monthly injection; slow recovery — avoid if desiring fertility soon
> - **Implant:** 3+ years; lowest real-world failure (<1%)

⚠️ Coils rarely cause pregnancy, but if one occurs it's more likely **ectopic** (intrauterine implantation blocked; tubal is not).

### Q: Emergency contraception — most effective option?

> **Copper IUD** — up to 5 days after unprotected sex, <1% failure, BMI-independent.

### Q: Emergency contraception — ulipristal acetate (ellaOne 30 mg)?

> - Blocks progesterone receptor → prevents ovulation
> - 5-day window; 1–2% failure
> - Fails if ovulation already occurred

### Q: Emergency contraception — levonorgestrel (Levonelle 1.5 mg)?

> - Synthetic progesterone → prevents ovulation
> - 3-day window; 1–3% failure
> - Less effective if **BMI >26**
> - P450 inducers reduce efficacy
> - Vomit within 2–3h → re-dose

### Q: Real-world failure rates — most effective tier?

> Implant / IUS / IUD: <1%. Depot progesterone: 3%. Nothing user-dependent to remember.

### Q: Real-world failure rates — user-dependent tier?

> Female condoms 5% · COCP/POP 8% · Male condoms 15% · Withdrawal 27% · No contraception 85%.

🧠 **See also: L1 — PCOS treatment of symptoms** — COCP is often the non-contraceptive choice.

## Territory 5 — MHT / HRT

### Q: Peri vs menopause vs post — definitions?

> - Peri-menopause: years leading up (symptoms + irregular cycles)
> - **Menopause: 12 months after LMP**
> - Post-menopause: after menopause

### Q: Benefits of HRT?

> - Symptom relief: hot flushes, sweats, disturbed sleep, joint pains, brain fog, ↓libido, low mood, vaginal dryness
> - Reduction in osteoporosis-related fractures

### Q: HRT VTE risk — mechanism and rule?

> - Oral E → first-pass → ↑clotting factors → ↑VTE
> - Transdermal bypasses liver — safer
> - Avoid oral E if BMI >30

### Q: HRT breast cancer risk?

> - Slight ↑ with combined (E+P)
> - Duration-dependent; reduces after stopping
> - **Continuous combined > sequential** for risk

### Q: HRT endometrial cancer risk — core rule?

> If uterus present → MUST give progestogen with oestrogen. Unopposed E → hyperplasia → cancer.

📎 Figure L2.8 — normal vs hyperplastic endometrium

### Q: HRT and cardiovascular disease — the "window of opportunity"?

> - Improved risk if started young / soon after menopause
> - Risk ↑ if started >10 years post-menopause or >60
> - Clear benefit in POI

### Q: HRT stroke risk?

> Small ↑. Oral > transdermal. Combined > E-only.

### Q: HRT risks per 1000 women after 10 years — the biggest jumps?

> - **Endometrial Ca + unopposed E: +32** (never do this with a uterus)
> - **Continuous combined + breast Ca: +40**
> - VTE + combined: +13

📎 Figure L2.9 — HRT risks table (NICE 2015)

### Q: Vaginal oestrogen — safety profile?

> Transvaginal E treats local symptoms without meaningful systemic absorption.

⚠️ Post-menopausal bleeding = red flag for endometrial Ca. Exception: first few months of starting HRT (breakthrough expected).

🧠 **See also: L1 — POI** — young POI women benefit clearly from HRT (CVD + bone).
🧠 **Compare: L1 — PCOS unopposed E rule** — same mechanism, different context.

## Territory 6 — Testosterone replacement

### Q: Indications before prescribing T?

> - ≥2 low morning fasting T measurements
> - Cause investigated (L1 primary vs secondary)
> - Symptoms present (low libido, loss of morning erections, ↓energy, ↓shaving)
> - Fertility NOT wanted

### Q: Testosterone preparations?

> - Daily gel (don't contaminate partner/children's skin)
> - IM injection (Sustanon) — 3-weekly
> - IM injection (Nebido) — 3-monthly
> - Oral, implants — less common

### Q: Testosterone safety monitoring?

> - **Haematocrit** — T stimulates erythropoiesis → hyperviscosity → stroke risk
> - **PSA** — T can drive prostate tissue
> - Symptom response

⭐ Two safety bloods: Hct and PSA.

## Territory 7 — Transgender care

### Q: Sex vs gender — biological vs social?

> - **Sex:** biologically defined (male, female, intersex)
> - **Gender:** social construct (how someone identifies)

### Q: Cisgender, non-conforming, dysphoria — terminology?

> - **Cisgender:** birth sex + gender identity aligned
> - **Gender non-conforming:** gender doesn't match assigned sex
> - **Gender dysphoria:** distress caused by mismatch

### Q: Transgender men vs women — which term?

> - **Trans man:** female sex at birth, male gender
> - **Trans woman:** male sex at birth, female gender
> - Prevalence: trans women ~3× more common than trans men

⚠️ Someone with ambiguous genitalia at birth is **DSD**, not transgender. Transgender relates to gender identity.

### Q: Prepubertal transgender care?

> GnRH agonists historically used to delay puberty (continuous → desensitisation). No longer routine in UK. Specialist waits ~4y; regret rates 1–2%.

### Q: Trans men — hormonal regimen?

> - **Testosterone** (injections/gels)
> - ± progesterone to suppress menstrual bleeding (15% risk of endometrial hyperplasia without it)

### Q: Trans women — hormonal regimen?

> - Reduce T via GnRH agonist (continuous → desensitisation) OR anti-androgen
> - Plus oestrogen (transdermal, oral, IM)
> - **High-dose E 4–5 mg/day → VTE risk ~2.6%**

🧠 Same drug, opposite direction by delivery: pulsatile GnRH stimulates (HA); continuous GnRH agonist suppresses (trans women, prostate Ca).

🧠 **See also: L1 — PCOS unopposed E rule** — trans men on T still have ovaries; same progestogen-protection logic.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: Reusable pattern — fertility needs gonadotrophins, never sex steroids?

> - Men: hCG ± FSH (not testosterone)
> - Women: letrozole / clomiphene / FSH / pulsatile GnRH (not oestrogen)
> - T/E feed back negatively and suppress what fertility needs

### Q: Reusable pattern — progestogen rule in three contexts?

> - HRT if uterus present → add P
> - PCOS with chronic unopposed E → add P courses / Mirena / COCP
> - Trans men on T with intact uterus → add P

### Q: Reusable pattern — transdermal > oral oestrogen when?

> Whenever VTE risk matters: BMI >30, migraine, prior VTE, trans women on high-dose E. Rationale: oral first-pass → ↑clotting factors.

### Q: Reusable pattern — GnRH delivery duality in three clinical uses?

> - Pulsatile → stimulate → HA
> - Continuous (agonist) → desensitise → trans women
> - Continuous (agonist) → desensitise → prostate cancer

### Q: Common traps — COCP vs HRT and cancer risk?

> COCP reduces ovarian/endometrial Ca. HRT increases them. Opposite signs. Different physiology.

### Q: Common traps — post-menopausal bleeding?

> Red flag for endometrial Ca, unless first few months of HRT (breakthrough expected).

### Q: Common traps — migraine with aura and COCP?

> Absolute CI regardless of route. The oestrogen is the problem, not the route.

### Q: Common traps — Levonelle and BMI?

> Loses efficacy above BMI 26. Copper IUD works regardless of BMI.

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA — 34M with Kallmann on T gel, wants to conceive. Semen: azoospermia. Next step?

> **B.** Kallmann = hypogonadotrophic; T suppresses the axis and worsens spermatogenesis. hCG drives intratesticular T; FSH added if no response at 6 months. Clomiphene (E) needs intact GnRH neurons — absent in Kallmann.
>
> A) Continue T + sildenafil · B) Stop T; start hCG; add FSH at 6 months · C) COCP in partner · D) Donor sperm · E) Clomiphene

### Q: SBA — 29F PCOS, BMI 32, lost 3 kg. Wants to conceive. Best next step?

> **B. Letrozole** — first-line ovulation induction in PCOS. A/D prevent pregnancy. E is HA first-line.
>
> A) COCP · B) Letrozole · C) Testosterone · D) POP · E) Pulsatile GnRH

### Q: SBA — IVF uses high-dose FSH (unlike ovulation induction). Why?

> **B.** Drop-off at each IVF step. Ovulation induction aims for one because implantation happens naturally.
>
> A) Severe ovarian insensitivity · B) Generate multiple follicles to compensate for drop-off · C) Uterine receptivity · D) Suppress LH · E) Prevent ectopic

### Q: SBA — 38F BMI 34, migraine with aura, family history DVT. Safest option?

> **E. Copper IUD** — no hormones, no clotting impact. A and B both contraindicated (migraine with aura = absolute CI to oestrogen regardless of route).
>
> A) Oral COCP · B) COCP patch · C) POP · D) Depot progesterone · E) Copper IUD

### Q: SBA — 52F 14 months post-LMP, hot flushes, intact uterus, BMI 31. Best HRT?

> **C.** Uterus → must have P. BMI 31 → avoid oral E. Transdermal E + P route.
>
> A) Oral E-only · B) Oral combined sequential · C) Transdermal E + oral P (or Mirena) · D) Transdermal E-only · E) Vaginal E only

### Q: SBA — 26M on T gel, Hct 56% at 3 months. Main concern?

> **B.** T stimulates erythropoiesis → ↑Hct → hyperviscosity → stroke risk. Prostate monitored via PSA separately. D is a JAK2 disorder.
>
> A) Prostate hypertrophy · B) Hyperviscosity-related stroke · C) Liver toxicity · D) Polycythaemia vera · E) Hypercalcaemia

### Q: SBA — 19F, unprotected sex 4 days ago, BMI 29. Most effective emergency contraceptive?

> **C.** 5-day window, <1% failure, BMI-independent. A out of window + BMI-sensitive. B in window but less effective if ovulation already occurred.
>
> A) Levonorgestrel · B) Ulipristal · C) Copper IUD · D) COCP · E) Depot progesterone

### Q: SBA — 27F trans woman on 5 mg oral E. Calf swelling + pain. Mechanism?

> **B.** Same mechanism as oral HRT VTE. Transdermal would avoid this.
>
> A) Progesterone hypercoagulability · B) First-pass liver metabolism of oral E → ↑clotting factors · C) T polycythaemia · D) GnRH agonist vasculitis · E) Anti-androgen thrombocytosis

### Q: SBA — 35F, 4 days post unprotected sex, on rifampicin, BMI 30. Best emergency contraception?

> **C. Copper IUD** robust to all three factors: 5-day window, non-hormonal (rifampicin-resistant), BMI-independent.
>
> A) Levonorgestrel · B) Ulipristal · C) Copper IUD · D) POP today · E) COCP today

### Q: SBA — PCOS woman, chronic amenorrhoea, no fertility plans. Why periodic progesterone?

> **C.** Same rule as HRT in a woman with a uterus. Without P, chronic E in anovulatory PCOS → hyperplasia → cancer (×2–6). Single most transferable rule between L1 and L2.
>
> A) ↓insulin resistance · B) Suppresses LH · C) Sheds endometrium, preventing hyperplasia from unopposed E · D) Corrects hyperandrogenism · E) Restores ovulation
