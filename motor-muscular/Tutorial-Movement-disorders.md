# Tutorial — Movement disorders (MND case)

## Lecturer's voice

This tutorial has no separate transcript — the answer slide deck is the source. The discrimination skill it trains is repeatedly examined in clinical neurology.

**Flagged as exam-critical**
- Mixed UMN+LMN signs in motor-only distribution + preserved sensation + preserved cognition → MND.
- Absent superficial (abdominal) reflexes alongside brisk deep tendon reflexes = pure corticospinal tract lesion.
- Fasciculation = involuntary firing of one motor unit; visible to the eye.

---

## The one idea

A neurological case is just two questions: *which sign cluster are you looking at?* and *where in the nervous system can lesions produce that cluster?* In MND, both UMN and LMN clusters appear simultaneously — that combination, in a motor-only distribution with preserved sensation and cognition, is almost diagnostic on its own.

---

## The case

> 📘 **How to use.** Read the case once. Then run each finding through the UMN vs LMN table from L1 / L2.

### Q: Summarise the history.

- **57-year-old right-handed policeman.** 1 year of:
- Tiredness on walking (rest every 2 miles instead of his usual several); **stiff legs**, foot drag, tripping, **calf cramps**.
- Difficulty carrying a cup, lifting arms (combing hair, reaching cupboards).
- Lost 2 stone with normal appetite. 6 months of slurred speech.
- Dysphagia — long meals, food liquidised, fluids triggering cough.
- Memory and sphincters normal. **No alcohol, no family history.**

### Q: Summarise the examination.

- Thin (66 kg @ 1.85 m). **Spastic gait + bilateral foot drop**, uses stick.
- Speech slurred but grammatically correct.
- **Tongue wasted bilaterally, slow protrusion, fasciculating.**
- Difficulty drinking — slow, double-swallow, triggers cough.
- Palate elevates poorly with phonation BUT **gag reflex pathologically brisk**. **Jaw jerk increased.**
- Wasting of shoulders/arms/forearms/legs. **Widespread fasciculations.**
- Spasticity in upper and lower limbs.
- Weakness — arms (R>L, proximal), legs (L>R, distal).
- All deep tendon reflexes brisk. **Plantars extensor. Abdominal reflexes absent.**
- **Sensation preserved.**

---

## Q1 — Which anatomical part of the nervous system is affected?

### Q: What's the diagnosis and which neurons are involved?

- **Both UMN and LMN are affected → motor neuron disease (ALS).**
- Almost no other diagnosis produces this combination in a motor-only, sensory-spared, cognitively-spared adult.

### Q: Bedside rule for distinguishing UMN from LMN findings.

| Sign | UMN | LMN |
|---|---|---|
| Tone | ↑ spastic, clonus | Flaccid, no clonus |
| Reflexes | Brisk | Reduced/absent |
| Plantar | Extensor | Flexor |
| Wasting/fasciculations | No | Possible |

> 🧠 **See also.** L1 — "UMN lesion" + "LMN lesion" tables. L2 — "Hyper-reflexia" / "Hypo-reflexia" mechanism.

## Q3 — Which pathways are affected?

### Q: Name them.

- **Pyramidal tracts** = corticospinal + corticobulbar.
- Voluntary motor commands to limbs/trunk and head/neck respectively.
- Both pathways carry both UMN-side findings (spastic limbs, brisk jaw jerk, dysarthria) and LMN-side findings at the brainstem/cord (tongue fasciculations, palatal weakness, limb wasting).

> 🧠 **See also.** L1 — "Corticospinal tract" + "Corticobulbar tract".

---

## Q4 — Why are abdominal reflexes absent while deep tendon reflexes are brisk?

### Q: Why this dissociation?

- It is the **hallmark of a pure corticospinal tract lesion**.
- **Superficial (cutaneous) reflexes** (e.g. abdominal) require descending corticospinal input → drop out when CST damaged.
- **Deep tendon reflexes** depend on the spinal arc — freed of descending inhibition, they amplify.

> ⚠️ **Trap.** Two reflex types moving in opposite directions sounds paradoxical but is the *signature* of UMN/CST disease. Don't reject the diagnosis because of it.

> 🧠 **See also.** L2 — "Descending control of reflexes — decerebration" — the mechanism of brisk deep tendon reflexes.

---

## Q5 — What is a fasciculation? What is its significance?

### Q: Define and localise.

- **Involuntary, abnormal firing of a single motor neuron + all its muscle fibres.**
- A whole motor unit twitching spontaneously, **visible** through the skin.
- **LMN sign.**

### Q: Fasciculation vs fibrillation — distinguish them.

- **Fasciculation** — whole motor unit, visible to the eye.
- **Fibrillation** — single muscle fibre, **not visible** — needle EMG only.

---

## Q6 — How would you summarise the abnormalities in motor unit structure and function?

### Q: What is happening at motor-unit level in MND?

- A motor neuron dies → its fibres are **denervated**.
- Surviving neighbouring axons sprout new branches → **reinnervate** orphaned fibres.
- The new motor unit is **larger and less stable** → ectopic APs in the distal axon → spontaneous contraction = **fasciculation**.

### Q: Why does this make MND a fasciculation-rich disease?

- Ongoing motor neuron death → continuous denervation–reinnervation cycle.
- Steady stream of unstable, oversized motor units = visible fasciculations everywhere.
- Eventually motor neurons die faster than reinnervation compensates → atrophy + weakness.

> 🧠 **See also.** L2 — "Motor unit" — the unit being remodelled. L2 — "Neurotrophic factors" — why losing the nerve causes the muscle to waste.

## SBAs

### Q: SBA 1. A 60-year-old has bilateral wasting of small hand muscles with widespread fasciculations, brisk knee jerks, and extensor plantars. Sensation is normal throughout. The most likely diagnosis is:

A. Cervical spondylotic myelopathy
B. **Motor neuron disease**
C. Multiple sclerosis
D. Guillain-Barré syndrome
E. Parkinson's disease

**Answer: B.** Mixed UMN signs (brisk reflexes, extensor plantars) + LMN signs (wasting, fasciculations) in a motor-only distribution with preserved sensation = MND. Cervical myelopathy usually has sensory involvement; MS has sensory/visual signs; GBS is acute LMN-only with preserved plantars; PD is BG.

### Q: SBA 2. In a corticospinal tract lesion, which combination is characteristic?

A. Brisk deep tendon reflexes + present abdominal reflexes
B. **Brisk deep tendon reflexes + absent abdominal reflexes**
C. Absent deep tendon reflexes + present abdominal reflexes
D. Absent deep tendon reflexes + absent abdominal reflexes
E. Resting tremor + rigidity

**Answer: B.** The dissociation — deep brisk, superficial absent — is the CST hallmark.

### Q: SBA 3. Which is NOT expected in a pure LMN lesion?

A. Hypotonia
B. Hyporeflexia
C. Muscle atrophy
D. Fasciculations
E. **Babinski sign**

**Answer: E.** Babinski is a UMN sign. LMN lesions give flaccid tone, hypo/areflexia, atrophy, fasciculations — but plantars stay flexor.

### Q: SBA 4. A patient with MND develops increasing dysphagia and a brisk gag reflex but a wasted, fasciculating tongue. Which tract is principally responsible?

A. Spinothalamic
B. Corticospinal
C. **Corticobulbar**
D. Vestibulospinal
E. Rubrospinal

**Answer: C.** Bulbar muscle innervation: corticobulbar UMN (brisk gag/jaw) → cranial-nerve LMN (XII for tongue → wasting/fasciculation when LMN dies). Mixed UMN+LMN in face/throat = corticobulbar.

### Q: SBA 5. A 65-year-old man has fasciculations in his calves but normal strength, normal reflexes, normal sensation, and a normal examination otherwise. He is worried about MND. Which feature most strongly argues *against* MND?

A. Age of the patient
B. Fasciculations confined to calves
C. **Normal strength, reflexes, and absence of any UMN signs**
D. The presence of fasciculations
E. Male sex

**Answer: C.** Benign (idiopathic) fasciculations are common, especially in calves. MND requires *progressive weakness* and ideally *both* UMN and LMN signs — not isolated fasciculations. **Pattern matters; isolated fasciculations ≠ MND.**
