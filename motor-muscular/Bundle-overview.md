# Bundle — Motor control

> 📘 **How to use this page.** L1 + L2 are upstream lectures; the **MND tutorial is the named downstream convergence** that depends on both. Read this overview to see the full clinical workflow. Drill detail on individual lecture pages.

## Bundle shape

**HYBRID — sequential + shared outcomes.** L1 → L2 is sequential (L2's "loss of descending inhibition" requires L1's UMN concept). Both lectures then converge on the **bedside UMN-vs-LMN sign cluster**, which the **MND tutorial** uses as a worked clinical case.

## The unifying intuition

Motor control is a leashed system. The cortex commands voluntary movement; the basal ganglia and cerebellum *sit on the side* and adjust; the brainstem and cord execute; the cord's reflex circuits **would run hot on their own** but for tonic descending inhibition. Every disorder in the bundle is one of three things:
- The cortex/tract is broken → execution lost AND the leash is cut → **UMN signs**.
- The motor unit is broken → no efferent leg of the reflex arc, no trophic input to muscle → **LMN signs**.
- The side-loops (basal ganglia, cerebellum) fail → abnormal movement without paralysis.

## The decision spine

**Step 1 — Is there weakness?** If yes, sort the signs:

| Cluster | Tone | Reflexes | Plantar | Wasting / fasciculations | Lesion site |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **UMN** | ↑ spastic | Brisk | Up (+ Babinski) | No | Cortex / capsule / brainstem / descending cord |
| **LMN** | ↓ flaccid | ↓ / absent | Down (normal) | Yes | Anterior horn / root / nerve / NMJ / muscle |

**Step 2 — Both clusters in the same body? Motor-only? Sensation preserved?** → **MND**.

**Step 3 — No weakness but movement is wrong?**
- Slow + rigid + resting tremor + masked face → BG hypokinetic (Parkinson's).
- Excess movement (chorea, ballism) → BG hyperkinetic (Huntington's, ballism).
- On-movement only: ataxia + dysmetria + intention tremor → cerebellum.
- Skilled-action loss with intact strength → apraxia (SMA / premotor).

## Cross-lecture interleaved SBAs

> ⭐ Highest-yield section for exam application. Each SBA forces you to choose between UMN, LMN, BG, cerebellar, or convergence (MND) interpretations.

### Q: SBA 1. A 50-year-old develops a 4 Hz tremor in his right hand at rest, which disappears when he reaches for a cup. Which structure is most likely affected?

A. Right cerebellar hemisphere
B. **Substantia nigra (left)**
C. Left primary motor cortex
D. Right anterior horn
E. Left subthalamic nucleus

**Answer: B.** Resting tremor (disappears on movement) = Parkinsonian. SN dopaminergic loss; symptoms contralateral. Cerebellar tremor is on movement. STN lesion → ballism, not tremor. *Discriminator: tremor at rest vs on movement.*

### Q: SBA 2. A 28-year-old presents 3 weeks after a viral illness with progressive ascending weakness. Examination shows flaccid tone, areflexia, and downgoing plantars. Sensation is intact. Which best explains the reflex pattern?

A. Loss of descending inhibition
B. **LMN damage breaking the efferent limb of the reflex arc**
C. Cerebellar damage
D. Substantia nigra degeneration
E. Cortical apraxia

**Answer: B.** Areflexia + flaccid + downgoing plantars = LMN. Mechanism: efferent limb of reflex arc broken (Guillain-Barré). *Discriminator: reflex direction reveals UMN vs LMN.*

### Q: SBA 3. A 62-year-old has dysarthria, dysphagia, brisk jaw jerk, tongue fasciculations and wasting, brisk knee jerks, and extensor plantars. Sensation and cognition are normal. The most likely diagnosis is:

A. Cervical myelopathy
B. **Motor neuron disease**
C. Multiple sclerosis
D. Bulbar stroke
E. Myasthenia gravis

**Answer: B.** Mixed UMN + LMN, motor-only, preserved sensation/cognition = MND. *Convergence diagnosis.*

### Q: SBA 4. A patient cannot mime brushing her teeth despite intact strength, sensation, and reflexes. The most likely lesion is:

A. **Supplementary motor area**
B. Substantia nigra
C. Anterior horn
D. Cerebellar vermis
E. Lateral corticospinal tract

**Answer: A.** Apraxia — disorder of skilled movement with intact strength. SMA / premotor / inferior parietal. *Discriminator: weakness absent → not pyramidal.*

### Q: SBA 5. A 45-year-old with chronic alcoholism has a wide-based, staggering gait. Which cerebellar region is most likely affected?

A. Vestibulocerebellum
B. **Spinocerebellum (vermis)**
C. Cerebrocerebellum (lateral)
D. Dentate nucleus alone
E. Tonsils alone

**Answer: B.** Spinocerebellar (vermis) degeneration — classic alcohol-related pattern, mainly legs → wide-based gait. *Pattern recognition.*

### Q: SBA 6. A 70-year-old with stroke has hyper-reflexia, spasticity, and an upgoing plantar on his left side. Which mechanism best explains the hyper-reflexia?

A. LMN damage
B. Cerebellar damage
C. **Loss of inhibitory descending input to the spinal reflex arc**
D. Excess substantia nigra dopamine
E. Demyelination of peripheral nerves

**Answer: C.** UMN stroke removes descending inhibition → cord arc runs hot → hyper-reflexia + spasticity + Babinski. *Chains across L1 (sign) → L2 (mechanism).*

### Q: SBA 7. A patient with MND has fasciculations in the tongue. Which best explains them at the cellular level?

A. Dopamine deficiency
B. Cerebellar dysmetria
C. Loss of descending inhibition unmasking spinal reflexes
D. **Motor neuron death → reinnervation creates oversized, unstable motor units that fire ectopic APs**
E. Antibody-mediated NMJ blockade

**Answer: D.** Denervation/reinnervation produces enlarged, unstable motor units prone to ectopic firing. *Convergence: requires L2 motor unit + Tutorial mechanism.*

### Q: SBA 8. A 38-year-old with autosomal dominant family history develops jerky involuntary movements of hands and face, plus a personality change. Genetic testing finds an expanded CAG repeat. Which neurons are degenerating?

A. Dopaminergic neurons of the substantia nigra
B. **GABAergic neurons of the striatum**
C. Cholinergic neurons of nucleus basalis
D. Lower motor neurons of the cord
E. Upper motor neurons of the cortex

**Answer: B.** Huntington's: GABAergic striatal loss; chromosome 4 CAG repeat; chorea + cognitive decline. *BG over-movement disorder.*

## The reusable pattern this bundle trains

> **Lesion localisation by pattern matching.** Take any neuro exam, sort each finding into UMN, LMN, BG, cerebellar, or sensory bins. Then ask: where in the nervous system does a single lesion produce *that* combination? If impossible from one lesion, suspect multi-focal disease (MND, MS).

This algorithm carries forward into stroke localisation, MS, peripheral neuropathies, NMJ disorders, and primary muscle disease — the entire neurological examination.
