## The one idea

Movement is built top-down. The cortex *plans and decides*; the basal ganglia and cerebellum *don't drive movement themselves — they sit on the side and adjust* what the cortex commands; the brainstem and cord *execute*. Every motor disorder in this lecture is a story of one of those layers being knocked out, with the others over- or under-running as a consequence.

## Section 1 — Two organising principles

> 📘 **How to use this section.** Memorise the two words first; they are the scaffolding the rest of the lecture hangs on.

### Q: What two principles organise the motor system?

- **Hierarchical organisation** — higher centres do harder things (planning, deciding); lower centres execute.
- **Functional segregation** — different brain areas do different aspects of movement (M1, premotor, SMA, BG, cerebellum each have a distinct job).

### Q: Where do the basal ganglia and cerebellum sit in the hierarchy?

- Neither projects directly to spinal cord.
- Both form **side-loops** that project back up to cortex *via thalamus*.
- Implication: their disorders cause **abnormal movement, not paralysis**.

## Section 2 — Major descending tracts

> ⭐ **High-yield discriminator.** Voluntary = pyramidal (corticospinal + corticobulbar). Everything else listed = extrapyramidal = involuntary/automatic.

### Q: Pyramidal vs extrapyramidal — what is the defining anatomical difference?

- **Pyramidal** = corticospinal + corticobulbar; pass through **medullary pyramids**; from **motor cortex**.
- **Extrapyramidal** = vestibulospinal, tectospinal, reticulospinal, rubrospinal; do **not** pass through pyramids; from **brainstem nuclei**.

### Q: Which descending tracts do what?

| Tract | Origin | Function | Clinical weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| Corticospinal | Motor cortex | Voluntary, body | Major |
| Corticobulbar | Motor cortex | Voluntary, face/head/neck | Major |
| Vestibulospinal | Vestibular nuclei | Head stabilisation, head–eye coord, postural adjustment | Mid |
| Reticulospinal | Reticular formation | Muscle tone with voluntary movement, postural stability | Mid |
| Tectospinal | Superior colliculus | Head/neck orientation during eye movements | Minor |
| Rubrospinal | Red nucleus | UL flexor drive — largely taken over by corticospinal in humans | Minor |

> ⭐ **Pattern.** "Voluntary or involuntary?" — voluntary iff corticospinal or corticobulbar. Everything else is automatic.

## Section 3 — Cortical motor areas

![Cortical motor areas](figures/L1_2_cortical-motor-areas.jpg)
**Figure L1.2** — Cortical motor areas in the frontal lobe: M1, premotor, SMA.

### Q: Where is the primary motor cortex (M1) and what does it do?

- Located in the **precentral gyrus**, anterior to central sulcus.
- Controls **fine, discrete, precise voluntary movements** — especially hand.
- Sends descending signals that *execute* the movement.

### Q: External vs internal cue — premotor or SMA?

- **External cue → premotor area** (anterior to M1). E.g. seeing an apple → reaching for it.
- **Internal cue → SMA** (anterior + medial to M1). E.g. recalling and saying a phrase.
- SMA also fires **before** voluntary movement begins (readiness potential).

### Q: Compare the three cortical motor areas.

| Area | Location | Job |
|---|---|---|
| M1 | Precentral gyrus | Execute fine voluntary movement |
| Premotor | Anterior to M1 | Plan **externally** cued movement |
| SMA | Anterior + medial to M1 | Plan **internally** cued / complex movement; speech |

## Section 4 — Corticospinal tract

![Corticospinal tract anatomy](figures/L1_3_corticospinal-tract.jpg)
**Figure L1.3** — The corticospinal tract: 85–90% of fibres decussate at the medullary pyramids forming the lateral corticospinal tract (limb muscles); 10–15% remain ipsilateral as the anterior corticospinal tract (trunk muscles).

### Q: Trace the corticospinal tract from cortex to muscle.

- M1 UMN → internal capsule → cerebral peduncle (midbrain) → pons → **pyramids** of medulla.
- **Decussation** at medullo-cervical junction → **85–90% cross** = lateral CST → limb (distal) muscles.
- **10–15% don't cross** = anterior CST → trunk (proximal) muscles.
- Both synapse onto LMN in ventral horn → LMN axon out via ventral root.

### Q: Why does a unilateral cortical lesion give contralateral *limb* weakness but relatively preserved trunk?

- Lateral CST (limb) is mostly crossed → lesion on one side denervates the opposite limb.
- Anterior CST (trunk) is uncrossed → trunk receives ipsilateral *and* contralateral input → bilateral support survives a one-sided lesion.

### Q: What does the motor homunculus tell you clinically?

![Motor homunculus and somatotopy](figures/L1_4_homunculus-somatotopy.jpg)
**Figure L1.4** — Motor homunculus and somatotopic representation in M1: cortical area is proportional to fineness of motor control.

- M1 is **somatotopically mapped** — disproportionate area for hand, lips, tongue.
- Layout lateral→medial: tongue → face → hand → arm → trunk → leg → foot (foot wraps medially).
- A focal infarct in a vascular territory produces a **focal pattern** (e.g. hand-only weakness), not whole-body.

## Section 5 — Corticobulbar tract

![Corticobulbar tract](figures/L1_5_corticobulbar-tract.jpg)
**Figure L1.5** — Corticobulbar tract: cortical projections to brainstem cranial-nerve motor nuclei controlling face, jaw, tongue, and extraocular muscles.

### Q: What does the corticobulbar tract do?

- Same architecture as CST but synapses onto **brainstem cranial-nerve motor nuclei** (III, IV, V-motor, VI, VII, XII).
- Principal motor pathway for **voluntary movements of face and neck**.
- Damaged in MND → dysarthria, dysphagia, brisk jaw jerk.

## Section 6 — UMN lesion: positive and negative signs

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern.** UMN lesion takes the **brakes off** the cord. Voluntary control is lost (negative) AND the unsupervised cord runs hot (positive). L2 will give you the cellular mechanism for the "running hot" half.

### Q: What are the negative signs of an UMN lesion?

- **Loss of voluntary motor function.**
- **Paresis** = graded weakness.
- **Paralysis (plegia)** = complete loss of voluntary muscle activity.

### Q: What are the positive signs of an UMN lesion, and why do they appear?

- Caused by **loss of inhibitory descending input** → cord reflex arc runs hot.
- **Spasticity** — increased muscle tone.
- **Hyper-reflexia** — exaggerated tendon reflexes.
- **Clonus** — abnormal oscillatory contractions.
- **Babinski's sign** — upgoing big toe.

> 🧠 **See also.** L2 — "Decerebrate rigidity" — the experimental demonstration of *why* removing descending input produces these signs.

## Section 7 — Apraxia

![Bilateral SMA infarct](figures/L1_6_bilateral-SMA-infarct.jpg)
**Figure L1.6** — Bilateral SMA infarct (T1 sagittal MRI): hyperintense lesion just anterior to precentral gyrus, medial surface.

### Q: What is apraxia?

- **Disorder of skilled movement** — patient is **not paretic** but has lost *how* to perform skilled actions.
- Lesion: inferior parietal lobe, premotor cortex, or SMA.
- Most common causes: **stroke, dementia**.

### Q: How would apraxia present at the bedside?

- Full strength, full sensation, normal reflexes.
- Cannot mime a skilled action (e.g. brushing teeth) on command.
- They know what they want to do; they cannot sequence it.

## Section 8 — LMN lesion + UMN/LMN comparison

> ⭐ **The single most important table in clinical neurology.** Every neuro exam reduces to which column you are looking at.

### Q: What are the signs of an LMN lesion?

- **Weakness** (no signal getting through).
- **Hypotonia** — flaccid muscle.
- **Hyporeflexia** — efferent limb of reflex broken.
- **Muscle atrophy** — loss of trophic input from nerve.
- **Fasciculations** — visible twitches of damaged motor units.
- **Fibrillations** — twitches of *individual* fibres, only seen on EMG.

### Q: UMN vs LMN — give the bedside table.

| Sign | UMN | LMN |
|---|---|---|
| Tone | ↑ (spastic) | ↓ (flaccid) |
| Reflexes | ↑ (hyper, brisk) | ↓ (hypo / absent) |
| Plantar | Up (Babinski +) | Down (normal) |
| Wasting / fasciculations | No | Yes |

## Section 9 — Motor neuron disease (MND / ALS)

![MND sites of involvement](figures/L1_7_MND-sites-of-involvement.jpg)
**Figure L1.7** — Sites of motor-neuron loss in MND: both upper motor neurons (cortex) and lower motor neurons (brainstem and cord) degenerate.

### Q: What is MND and what makes it diagnostically distinctive?

- Progressive **neurodegenerative** disorder of the motor system.
- Most common form: **amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)**.
- Kills **both UMN and LMN simultaneously** — almost no other disease does this.
- **Sensation preserved**, **cognition preserved** (Stephen Hawking).

### Q: List the UMN signs in MND.

- Spasticity (limbs and tongue).
- Brisk limb and **jaw** reflexes.
- Babinski's sign.
- Loss of dexterity.
- Dysarthria, dysphagia.

### Q: List the LMN signs in MND.

- Weakness.
- Muscle wasting.
- **Tongue fasciculations and wasting** — very characteristic.
- Nasal speech.
- Dysphagia.

### Q: What is the prognosis in MND?

- Incurable, eventually fatal.
- Typically 12–18 months from diagnosis.
- Death usually from respiratory muscle failure.

## Section 10 — Basal ganglia: structure and function

### Q: Name the basal ganglia structures.

- **Caudate nucleus**.
- **Lentiform nucleus** = putamen + external globus pallidus.
- **Caudate + putamen = striatum**.
- **Substantia nigra** (midbrain).
- **Subthalamic nuclei**.

### Q: What is the function of the basal ganglia?

- Decision to move at all.
- Elaborating **associated movements** — arm-swing, facial expression matching emotion.
- **Suppressing unwanted movements** (the brake).
- Performing movements in order.

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern.** BG = motor system's *moderator*. Disease comes in two flavours: **too much suppression → under-movement (Parkinson's)**; **too little suppression → over-movement (Huntington's, ballism)**.

## Section 11 — Basal ganglia disorders

![Basal ganglia circuitry with disease overlays](figures/L1_9_basal-ganglia-circuitry.jpg)
**Figure L1.9** — Basal-ganglia circuitry: cortex → striatum → GPi → thalamus → SMA, with disease overlays at affected nodes.

### Q: Parkinson's — pathology in one sentence?

- Degeneration of **dopaminergic neurons** of the **substantia nigra** projecting to the **striatum**.

![Substantia nigra in PD vs normal](figures/L1_10_substantia-nigra-PD.jpg)
**Figure L1.10** — Substantia nigra in normal (top) vs Parkinson's (bottom): loss of dopaminergic, neuromelanin-containing neurons.

### Q: Five cardinal features of Parkinson's.

- **Bradykinesia** — slowness of small movements.
- **Hypomimic face** — mask-like, expressionless.
- **Akinesia** — difficulty *initiating* movement (cannot internally generate).
- **Rigidity** — increased tone, "lead-pipe" / cogwheel.
- **Resting tremor** — 4–7 Hz, "pill-rolling"; disappears on movement.

### Q: Huntington's — pathology and genetics?

- Degeneration of **GABAergic neurons** in **striatum** (caudate first, then putamen).
- **Autosomal dominant**, **chromosome 4**, expanded **CAG repeat** in huntingtin gene.

![Huntington's coronal](figures/L1_11_huntingtons-coronal.jpg)
**Figure L1.11** — Coronal brain in Huntington's (right) vs normal (left): striking caudate atrophy with enlarged lateral ventricles.

### Q: Clinical features of Huntington's?

- **Choreic** movements — rapid jerky involuntary; hands/face first, then legs/body.
- Speech impairment, dysphagia.
- Unsteady gait.
- Late: cognitive decline, dementia.

### Q: Ballism — cause and presentation?

- Usually **stroke** affecting the **subthalamic nucleus**.
- **Sudden uncontrolled flinging** of extremities.
- **Contralateral** to lesion (pyramidal pathways cross).

![Hemiballism](figures/L1_12_hemiballism.jpg)
**Figure L1.12** — Hemiballism: sudden, uncontrolled, contralateral flinging of limb extremities from a subthalamic-nucleus lesion (typically stroke).

### Q: Compare the three BG disorders along the under-/over-movement axis.

| Disorder | Node lost | Movement |
|---|---|---|
| Parkinson's | SN dopaminergic → striatum | UNDER |
| Huntington's | Striatum (GABAergic) | OVER (chorea) |
| Ballism | Subthalamic nucleus | OVER (flinging) |

## Section 12 — Cerebellum

![Cerebellum location](figures/L1_13_cerebellum-anatomy.jpg)
**Figure L1.13** — Cerebellum: posterior cranial fossa location (sagittal MRI, left), posterior view (middle), antero-inferior view (right).

### Q: What does the cerebellum do, in one phrase?

- **Coordinator and predictor of movement** — compares what you intend with what you actually do, corrects in real time.
- Located in **posterior cranial fossa**, separated from cerebrum by **tentorium cerebelli**.

### Q: Three functional regions of the cerebellum and their damage patterns.

| Region | Function | Damage pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Vestibulocerebellum | Gait, posture, equilibrium; head–eye coord | Falls *even when sitting eyes open* (e.g. tumour) |
| Spinocerebellum (vermis) | Speech, muscle tone, limb coord | **Wide-based gait** — classic chronic alcohol pattern |
| Cerebrocerebellum (lateral) | Skilled movement; cognition, language | Intention tremor + speech (mainly arms) |

> ⭐ **Pattern.** Wide-based gait + alcohol → spinocerebellum. Falls when still → vestibulocerebellum. Intention tremor on finger–nose → cerebrocerebellum.

## Section 13 — Cerebellar dysfunction signs

> ⭐ **Hallmark.** Every cerebellar sign is **apparent only on movement**. Resting tremor → think Parkinson's, not cerebellum.

### Q: Five signs of cerebellar dysfunction.

- **Ataxia** — wide-based, "drunken" gait.
- **Dysmetria** — wrong force/distance for target (knocks cup over instead of grabbing).
- **Intention tremor** — limb oscillates as it nears target (nose–finger test).
- **Dysdiadochokinesia** — can't perform rapid alternating movements (fast pronation/supination).
- **Scanning speech** — staccato, syllable-by-syllable.

### Q: Cerebellar tremor vs Parkinsonian tremor — one-line discriminator?

- **Cerebellar = on movement (intention tremor).**
- **Parkinsonian = at rest (pill-rolling); disappears on movement.**

## SBAs

### Q: SBA 1. A 55-year-old man develops sudden right-sided weakness affecting his arm more than his leg, with brisk reflexes and an upgoing right plantar. Sensation is preserved. Which structure is most likely affected?

A. Right basal ganglia
B. **Left primary motor cortex**
C. Right cerebellar hemisphere
D. Left anterior horn of spinal cord
E. Left subthalamic nucleus

**Answer: B.** Right-sided signs from a left-sided lesion (CST decussation). UMN signs (brisk reflexes, Babinski). Arm > leg = MCA territory (lateral cortex). Anterior horn (D) = LMN signs.

### Q: SBA 2. A patient is asked to mime brushing her teeth. She has full strength, full sensation, and normal reflexes, but cannot perform the action even though she clearly understands the instruction. The most likely lesion is in:

A. Substantia nigra
B. Lateral corticospinal tract
C. **Supplementary motor area**
D. Vestibulocerebellum
E. Anterior horn

**Answer: C.** Apraxia — disorder of skilled movement, intact strength. SMA / premotor / inferior parietal.

### Q: SBA 3. A 68-year-old woman has slowness of movement, expressionless face, 5 Hz resting tremor in the right hand, and increased tone in the right arm. Which neurotransmitter system is most directly affected?

A. GABAergic neurons in the striatum
B. **Dopaminergic neurons of the substantia nigra**
C. Acetylcholine at the NMJ
D. Glutamatergic neurons of the subthalamic nucleus
E. Noradrenergic neurons of the locus coeruleus

**Answer: B.** Classic Parkinson's: bradykinesia + hypomimic face + resting tremor + rigidity → SN dopaminergic loss.

### Q: SBA 4. A 40-year-old man with chronic heavy alcohol use presents with a wide-based, staggering gait. The most likely site of pathology is:

A. Vestibulocerebellum
B. **Spinocerebellum**
C. Cerebrocerebellum
D. Substantia nigra
E. Lateral corticospinal tract

**Answer: B.** Spinocerebellar (vermis) degeneration — classic chronic-alcoholism pattern, mainly legs → wide-based gait.

### Q: SBA 5. Which feature distinguishes a cerebellar tremor from a Parkinsonian tremor?

A. Frequency of the tremor
B. Symmetry
C. **Whether it is present at rest or on movement**
D. Whether it affects the face
E. Patient age

**Answer: C.** Parkinsonian = resting (disappears on movement); cerebellar = intention (worsens on approach).
