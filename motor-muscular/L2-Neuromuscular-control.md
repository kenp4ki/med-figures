## The one idea

The spinal cord is not a passive relay. It contains its own reflex circuits that *would* run hot on their own, and the brain's job is to keep them on a leash. Cut the leash (UMN lesion) → reflexes amplify. Kill the runner (LMN lesion) → reflexes vanish.

## Section 1 — Alpha motor neuron

![Spinal cord cross-section](figures/L2_1_spinal-cord-cross-section.jpg)
**Figure L2.1** — Spinal-cord cross-section: α-motor neurons in the ventral horn, organised somatotopically (flexor/extensor, proximal/distal).

### Q: What is an alpha motor neuron?

- The **lower motor neuron** of brainstem (cranial nerve nuclei) and spinal cord (ventral horn).
- Innervates **extrafusal** muscle fibres of skeletal muscle.
- Activation → muscle contraction.

### Q: What is a motor neuron pool?

- The full population of α-MNs that innervate a **single muscle**.
- E.g. "the biceps motor neuron pool" = all α-MNs whose axons reach biceps fibres.

> 🧠 **See also.** L1 — "Lesions of the motor tract — LMN" — the α-motor neuron *is* the LMN.

## Section 2 — Motor unit

![Motor unit diagram](figures/L2_2_motor-unit.jpg)
**Figure L2.2** — The motor unit: one motor neuron + all the muscle fibres it innervates.

### Q: Define the motor unit.

- A **single motor neuron + all the muscle fibres it innervates**.
- The **smallest functional unit** with which to produce force.
- Stimulation → all fibres in that unit contract together.

### Q: Numbers worth remembering for motor units in humans.

- ~420,000 motor neurons.
- ~250 million skeletal muscle fibres.
- Average **~600 fibres per motor neuron**.

### Q: In healthy muscle, can one fibre be innervated by more than one neuron?

- **No.** One fibre = one motor neuron's branch.
- This rule is *violated* after denervation/reinnervation — see Tutorial Q6.

## Section 3 — Three types of motor unit

![Motor unit fibre type mosaic](figures/L2_3_motor-unit-fibre-mosaic.jpg)
**Figure L2.3** — Distribution of motor-unit fibre types throughout a muscle: chequerboard staining shows that fibres of different types are mosaically intermixed.

### Q: Compare the three motor unit types.

| Type | Force | Speed | Fatigue | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Slow (S, type I) | Low | Slow | Holds 100% ≥60 min | Postural (standing all day) |
| Fast fatigue-resistant (FR, IIA) | Mid | Fast | Drops over ~6 min | Walking, running |
| Fast fatigable (FF, IIB) | High | Fast | Drops to ~0 in ~2 min | Sprinting, lifting |

### Q: Are fibre types regionally segregated within a muscle?

- **No** — they are **mosaically intermixed** throughout each muscle (chequerboard staining).
- A single motor unit's fibres are scattered across the muscle's cross-section.

![Motor unit properties graphs](figures/L2_4_motor-unit-properties.jpg)
**Figure L2.4** — Motor-unit properties: single-twitch tension (left) and fatigue under repeated stimulation (right). FF = high force, fatigues in minutes. S = low force, fatigue-resistant for ≥60 min.

## Section 4 — Regulation of muscle force

![Recruitment and rate coding](figures/L2_5_recruitment-rate-coding.jpg)
**Figure L2.5** — Recruitment (left): later units join at higher force and drop out first as force declines. Rate coding (right): firing rate increases with force; slow units recruited first.

### Q: What two mechanisms grade muscle force?

- **Recruitment** — turn on more motor units when more force is needed.
- **Rate coding** — once on, increase the firing frequency.

### Q: State the size principle.

- **Smaller (slow) motor units recruited first.**
- Larger (fast) units added as force demand rises.
- On de-recruitment, **larger units drop out first** — slow units stay on longest.
- Allows fine control at low forces (handwriting).

### Q: How does rate coding work?

- Once recruited, a motor unit fires at ~8 Hz baseline.
- Firing rate climbs to ~20 Hz with rising demand.
- Higher rate → **summation** (APs arrive faster than muscle can relax) → more force.

## Section 5 — Neurotrophic factors + plasticity

![Cross-innervation experiment](figures/L2_6_cross-innervation.jpg)
**Figure L2.6** — Cross-innervation experiment: a slow-muscle nerve transplanted into a fast muscle makes it slower, and vice versa — the motor neuron determines fibre properties.

### Q: What are neurotrophic factors?

- Greek *trophē* = food.
- A type of **growth factor** delivered along the nerve.
- Prevent neuronal death; promote growth after injury; **maintain muscle fibre properties**.

### Q: What does the cross-innervation experiment show?

- Swap the nerves to soleus (slow) and FDL (fast).
- **Soleus becomes faster, FDL becomes slower.**
- Conclusion: the **motor neuron dictates fibre type** — the nerve is a chemical instructor, not just a wire.

### Q: Why does muscle waste when its nerve is cut?

- Loss of **neurotrophic input**, not just loss of contraction.
- Disuse alone (with intact nerve and blood supply) does not cause this degree of atrophy.

### Q: How do fibre types change with training, deconditioning, and ageing?

- **Training**: most common shift is **IIB → IIA**.
- **Severe deconditioning / spinal cord injury / microgravity**: I → II shift possible.
- **Ageing**: loses both types but **preferentially type II** → relative dominance of type I → slower contraction times → slower movements.

## Section 6 — Reflex function

### Q: Define a reflex.

- **Automatic** response to a stimulus.
- Receptor → nerve centre → effector, **without reaching consciousness**.
- Magnitude/timing set by stimulus intensity/onset.
- **Once released, cannot be stopped** (unlike voluntary movement).

## Section 7 — Monosynaptic stretch reflex

![Monosynaptic stretch reflex](figures/L2_7_monosynaptic-stretch-reflex.jpg)
**Figure L2.7** — The monosynaptic stretch reflex: spindle stretch → Ia afferent → direct excitation of α-MN to homonymous muscle, with simultaneous inhibition of antagonist motor neuron.

### Q: Trace the patellar (knee-jerk) reflex.

- Tendon tap stretches quadriceps → activates **muscle spindle**.
- **Ia sensory afferent** fires → enters cord via dorsal root.
- **One synapse** (monosynaptic) onto α-MN of quadriceps → quadriceps contracts.
- Branch excites inhibitory interneuron → inhibits α-MN of antagonist (hamstrings) → **reciprocal inhibition** relaxes hamstrings.

## Section 8 — Descending control: the leash

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern.** Reflexes look automatic but are actively *dampened* by tonic descending inhibition. Cut that inhibition (decerebrate, UMN lesion, stroke) → cord runs hot → spasticity, hyper-reflexia, clonus.

![Jendrassik manoeuvre](figures/L2_8_jendrassik-manoeuvre.jpg)
**Figure L2.8** — The Jendrassik manoeuvre: pulling against locked fingers reduces descending inhibition and enhances the patellar reflex.

### Q: What does the Jendrassik manoeuvre demonstrate?

- Have the patient clench teeth, make a fist, or pull against locked fingers while you tap the patellar tendon.
- The **reflex becomes larger**.
- Demonstrates that descending input modulates reflex magnitude — they are not purely automatic.

![Decerebrate rigidity](figures/L2_9_decerebrate-rigidity.jpg)
**Figure L2.9** — Decerebrate rigidity: section above the brainstem removes inhibitory descending control, revealing tonic excitatory drive — the stretch reflex becomes phasic + tonic and limbs become extensor-rigid.

### Q: What does decerebration reveal about descending control?

- Surgically separate brain above brainstem from cord below.
- Stretch reflex becomes **3–4× larger** with sustained tonic component.
- Limbs become rigidly extended ("decerebrate rigidity").
- Conclusion: **inhibitory descending control normally dominates**; removing it unmasks brainstem excitatory drive.

### Q: How does the brain modulate the cord — five routes?

![Descending control routes](figures/L2_10_descending-control-routes.jpg)
**Figure L2.10** — Five sites at which descending tracts modulate spinal motor output: α-MN, inhibitory interneuron, propriospinal neuron, γ-MN, afferent terminal.

| Route | Effect |
|---|---|
| 1. Activate α-MN | Drive voluntary movement |
| 2. Activate inhibitory interneuron | Suppress reflexes (the leash) |
| 3. Propriospinal / γ-MN / afferent terminal | Coordinate segments, set spindle sensitivity, gate sensory input |

> ⭐ **Pattern.** "Spasticity in UMN lesion is due to loss of which descending input?" — answer is route 2 (inhibitory).

> 🧠 **See also.** L1 — "UMN lesion: positive signs" — decerebration is the experimental version of an UMN lesion. The cat's rigid limbs are spasticity in animal form.

## Section 9 — Hyper-reflexia, clonus, Babinski

![Hyper-reflexia](figures/L2_11a_hyperreflexia.jpg)
**Figure L2.11a** — Hyper-reflexia in a stroke patient: knee jerk dramatically larger on the affected side.

### Q: What is hyper-reflexia and what does it mean?

- **Overactive tendon reflexes.**
- Mechanism: **loss of descending inhibition** → cord reflex arc unleashed.
- **UMN sign.**

![Clonus](figures/L2_11b_clonus.jpg)
**Figure L2.11b** — Clonus: involuntary rhythmic contractions triggered by sustained stretch.

### Q: What is clonus?

- **Involuntary rhythmic muscle contractions** elicited by sustained stretch.
- Mechanism: reflex arc so excitable that each contraction stretches the muscle slightly and triggers the next → self-sustaining oscillation.
- **UMN sign.**

![Babinski](figures/L2_11c_babinski.jpg)
**Figure L2.11c** — Babinski sign: stroke the sole; pathological response is upgoing big toe.

### Q: How is the Babinski sign elicited and interpreted?

- Stroke sole with blunt instrument.
- **Big toe down → normal adult.**
- **Big toe up → positive Babinski → UMN lesion in adults.**

> ⚠️ **Trap.** **Upgoing toe is normal in infants** (corticospinal tract not yet developed). It only becomes pathological once the tract has matured (around the time of walking).

---

## Section 10 — Hypo-reflexia

### Q: What is hypo-reflexia and what does it indicate?

- **Below-normal or absent reflexes.**
- Mechanism: **efferent limb of reflex arc broken** — LMN, axon, NMJ, or muscle.
- **LMN sign.**

> 🧠 **The single most useful pairing in clinical neurology.** Hyper-reflexia = UMN lesion (descending leash cut). Hypo-reflexia = LMN lesion (efferent limb broken).

> 🧠 **See also.** L1 — "LMN lesion" — same disease pattern, anatomical view. Tutorial Q5/Q6 — fasciculation as a further LMN sign with cellular mechanism.

## SBAs

### Q: SBA 1. A patient has a complete spinal cord transection at T10. Below the lesion (chronic phase, after spinal shock has resolved), you would expect:

A. Hypotonia, hyporeflexia, fasciculations
B. **Spasticity, hyper-reflexia, upgoing plantars**
C. Normal tone, normal reflexes, downgoing plantars
D. Resting tremor and rigidity
E. Wide-based gait and intention tremor

**Answer: B.** Cord transection separates leg-controlling segments from descending inhibition but leaves their LMNs intact → UMN signs below the lesion. *Chains across L1→L2*: L1 listed positive UMN signs; L2 explains the mechanism (loss of descending inhibition).

### Q: SBA 2. Which best describes the size principle?

A. Larger motor units are recruited first to maximise force
B. **Smaller motor units are recruited first, with larger units added as demand rises**
C. Recruitment is random
D. Type IIB units are always recruited before type I
E. Recruitment depends on conscious choice

**Answer: B.** Slow (type I) units first; fast units join as force needed. Gives fine control at low forces.

### Q: SBA 3. A man has an exaggerated knee jerk. The most likely mechanism is:

A. Damage to the muscle spindle
B. Damage to the alpha motor neuron
C. **Loss of inhibitory descending input from higher CNS centres**
D. Excess dopamine in the striatum
E. Cerebellar intention tremor

**Answer: C.** Hyper-reflexia = UMN sign = loss of descending inhibition. (B) gives *hypo*-reflexia.

### Q: SBA 4. A 70-year-old man moves more slowly than at 30. The most likely fibre-type change is:

A. Selective loss of type I fibres
B. **Selective (preferential) loss of type IIB fibres**
C. Conversion of type I to type II
D. Increased proportion of type IIB
E. No change

**Answer: B.** Ageing loses both but preferentially type II → relative dominance of type I → slower contraction.

### Q: SBA 5. A patient has a peripheral nerve transection 6 weeks ago. The denervated muscle now shows:

A. Spasticity and brisk reflexes
B. Hypertrophy from compensatory work
C. **Atrophy and absent reflexes**
D. Resting tremor
E. Babinski sign

**Answer: C.** Cutting the nerve removes neurotrophic input + the LMN efferent limb → atrophy + absent tendon reflexes. *Chains across L1→L2*: explains L1's "LMN lesion → muscle atrophy + hypo-reflexia" listing.
