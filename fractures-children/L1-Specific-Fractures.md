## Module 1 — How to think about a fracture

### Q: What is a fracture, viewed as a system?

- Injury to a system, not just to a bone.
- Two levers determine healing: **mechanical environment** (movement, forces) and **biological environment** (blood supply, nutrition, infection, immunity).
- Surgery never heals bone — it creates the conditions that let bone heal itself.

### Q: ATLS priority — when can the fracture be addressed?

- **A**irway → **B**reathing → **C**irculation → **D**isability before the fracture.
- Fracture treated as part of "C" only when it is the bleeding source (long bone, pelvis).
- Otherwise belongs in the secondary survey.

### Q: What four findings define a fracture clinically?

- Pain
- Swelling (= bleeding — bone is vascular)
- Crepitus
- Deformity

⭐ **High-yield: collateral damage check.** Document **neurovascular status BEFORE AND AFTER any reduction or manipulation**. Limb perfusion + medico-legal protection. This is the single most repeated examiner signal.

### Q: When is each imaging modality used?

- **X-ray (AP + lateral)** — default, two orthogonal views.
- **CT** — to make diagnosis or assess pattern (e.g. tibial plateau, pilon).
- **MRI** — when clinical suspicion exceeds X-ray finding (occult **scaphoid**, occult **NoF**).

## Module 2 — Describing a fracture

📎 Figure L1.2 — Fracture patterns: impacted, avulsion, open, simple, transverse, comminuted, spiral.

### Q: Six elements of describing a fracture radiograph (in order).

- **Location** (which bone, which part)
- **Pieces** (simple vs multifragmentary/comminuted)
- **Pattern** (transverse / oblique / spiral / avulsion / impacted)
- Displaced or undisplaced
- Translated and/or angulated
- X / Y / Z plane

### Q: How does fracture pattern hint at injury energy?

- **Comminuted, multifragmentary, transverse with butterfly fragment in good bone** → high energy → expect soft-tissue damage, periosteal stripping, NV injury, distant injuries.
- **Simple, oblique or spiral in older bone** → low energy → soft tissues usually fine.
- **Spiral pattern** → rotational mechanism (twist injury).

📎 Figure L1.3 — Translation vs angulation with X/Y/Z reference axes.
📎 Figure L1.4 — Four primary displacement types.

### Q: Four types of displacement, with directional terms.

| Type | Direction terms |
|---|---|
| **Translation** | Medial / lateral; dorsal / volar; anterior / posterior |
| **Angulation** | Varus / valgus; dorsal / volar |
| **Rotation** | Internal / external |
| **Impaction** | Shortening + compression |

### Q: Quick mental model — varus vs valgus.

- **Valgus** = distal segment points away from midline (knock-knee at the joint).
- **Varus** = distal segment points toward midline (bow-leg).
- For wrist, equivalents are **dorsal/volar**.

## Module 3 — Fracture healing

📎 Figure L1.5 — Indirect (secondary) healing cascade with collagen-type histology.

### Q: Two pathways of fracture healing — which produces callus?

- **Direct (primary)** — anatomical reduction + absolute stability + compression → **NO callus**. Bone "thinks it isn't broken" and just remodels.
- **Indirect (secondary)** — sufficient reduction + micromotion → **callus**.

### Q: Three phases of indirect healing — what happens in each?

- **Inflammation:** haematoma → cytokines → granulation tissue + new vessels.
- **Repair:** soft callus (**Type II collagen — cartilage**) → hard callus (**Type I collagen — bone**).
- **Remodelling:** Wolff's law reshapes; excess bone removed.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern:** if the construct rigidly compresses (plate + lag screws) → direct healing → no callus expected. If the construct allows micromotion (IM nail, plaster, ex-fix) → indirect → callus expected. Callus on X-ray is a *construct* finding, not pathology.

### Q: Mnemonic for collagen types in healing.

- **Type One = Owen = bOne** (hard callus, mature bone).
- **Type Two = cartilage** (soft callus).

📎 Figure L1.6 — Paediatric humerus fracture healing timeline (day 1 → 6 months).

### Q: Approximate time to union by site (adult).

- **Phalanges 3 wks · metacarpals 4–6 wks · distal radius 4–6 wks**
- **Forearm 8–10 wks · tibia 10 wks · femur 12 wks**
- **Pattern: upper limb < lower limb. "6 weeks" is convention; actual range 3–12 wks.**
- Signs of healing on X-ray from **7–10 days**.

### Q: Mechanical vs biological factors affecting healing.

- **Mechanical:** movement (too little → won't form callus unless rigidly compressed; too much → non-union), forces.
- **Biological:** blood supply, immune function, infection, nutrition, smoking.

## Module 4 — Management framework

📎 Figure L1.7 — Reduction options decision tree.

### Q: The 3R framework for any fracture.

- **Reduce** (closed: manipulation/traction; open: mini-incision/full exposure)
- **Hold** (plaster/splint, external fix, internal fix)
- **Rehabilitate** (early/late mobilisation, weight-bearing, physio)

### Q: What is ligamentotaxis and why does it matter for closed reduction?

- Traction on the limb tightens surrounding ligaments and muscles.
- Tight ligaments push the fragments back into rough alignment.
- This is why pulling on a limb often reduces a fracture without opening it.

### Q: Trade-off of open reduction.

- **Gain:** anatomical reduction, direct visualisation.
- **Cost:** strips periosteum and disturbs the haematoma — both biological assets for healing.
- **Use only when anatomy matters** (intra-articular fractures; failed closed reduction).

📎 Figure L1.8 — Fixation types: plaster, plate+screws (extramedullary), IM nail, external mono- and multiplanar.

### Q: Five fixation modalities and their healing pathway.

- **Plaster / cast** — indirect.
- **Internal extramedullary** (plate + screws) — direct if compressed; otherwise indirect.
- **Internal intramedullary** (nail) — indirect (relative stability).
- **External mono- or multiplanar fixator** — indirect.

⚠️ **Trap:** an IM nail is *internal* but the fixation is *relative*, so callus is expected. Do not call this construct "absolute stability" — that description applies only to compression plating.

### Q: Why is open reduction with compression plating the only construct producing direct healing?

- It uniquely combines the three required conditions: anatomical reduction, absolute stability, and compression at the fracture line.
- Every other construct allows some micromotion and so heals via callus.

## Module 5 — Complications

### Q: General fracture complications (early or late).

- **Fat embolus** (early — sudden death risk)
- **DVT, PE**
- **Infection**
- **Prolonged immobility** — UTI, chest infection, pressure sores

### Q: Specific local fracture complications.

- **Neurovascular injury**
- Muscle/tendon injury
- **Non-union / malunion**
- Local infection (osteomyelitis)
- **Degenerative change** (intra-articular → post-traumatic OA)
- **Reflex sympathetic dystrophy** (= CRPS)

⭐ **Key:** intra-articular fractures cause OA via joint surface step-off. This is why anatomical reduction matters for any joint fracture (tibial plateau, distal radius, ankle).

## Module 6 — Atlas card: Fractured Neck of Femur (NoF)

📘 **Hub of the lecture.** Lecturer flags this as "the most important fractures you will be asked about." Whole card is high-yield.

📎 Figure L1.9 — Femoral neck anatomy: head, neck, greater + lesser trochanter, capsule.

### Q: Three blood supply sources to the femoral head, in order of importance.

- **Medial and lateral circumflex arteries (capsular)** — dominant supply.
- Nutrient artery up the femoral shaft.
- Artery in ligamentum teres — minor in adults.

### Q: Why do we obsess about intracapsular vs extracapsular?

- **Intracapsular** disrupts the capsular vessels → AVN (~25% in displaced) and **non-union** (a bigger practical problem than AVN).
- **Extracapsular** preserves blood supply → head will survive → just stabilise mechanically.

📎 Figure L1.10 — NoF fracture types labelled (subcapital, transcervical, basicervical, intertrochanteric, subtrochanteric).

### Q: NoF fracture types — which are intracapsular?

- **Subcapital + transcervical = intracapsular** (standard anatomy).
- **Basicervical = borderline, treated as extracapsular**.
- **Intertrochanteric + subtrochanteric = extracapsular**.

⚠️ **Conflict flag:** the slide labels transcervical as "extracapsular" — this contradicts standard anatomy and the lecturer's own verbal teaching. Treat transcervical as **intracapsular** — that's what makes the AVN logic work.

### Q: Classic clinical presentation of displaced NoF.

- Shortened, externally rotated leg.
- Unable to weight-bear.
- Groin/hip pain.
- Often elderly fall from standing height.

### Q: What is Shenton's line and what does it tell you?

- Smooth arc from the inferior femoral neck across the superior border of obturator foramen.
- **Should be smooth and continuous; broken in displaced NoF.**
- Useful for spotting subtle displacement on plain pelvic AP.

📎 Figure L1.11 — NoF management decision tree.

### Q: NoF management — extracapsular fractures.

- **Always fix.**
- **Intertrochanteric** → **DHS (dynamic hip screw)** — plate + sliding hip screw. Sliding action converts displacement into compression.
- **Subtrochanteric / reverse oblique** → **IM nail** — shorter lever arm, tolerates unstable patterns.

### Q: NoF management — intracapsular fractures.

- **Undisplaced** → fix with cannulated screws.
- **Displaced, <55 yrs** → reduce + fix with screws (preserve native head).
- **Displaced, >65 yrs** → **replace**:
  - Fit and mobile → **THR (total hip replacement)**.
  - Less fit → **hemiarthroplasty**.

⭐ **Pattern:** 55–65 is the grey zone (clinical judgement). Default ≥65 to replace, default ≤55 to fix.

### Q: What is "tip-apex distance" and why does it matter?

- Distance from the tip of the DHS lag screw to the apex (subchondral bone) of the femoral head, summed on AP and lateral.
- **Smaller TAD = lower cut-out failure rate.**
- Aim: tip as close to subchondral bone as possible.

## Module 7 — Atlas card: Shoulder dislocation

📎 Figure L1.12 — Shoulder anatomy.
📎 Figure L1.13 — Anterior shoulder dislocation annotated.

### Q: Classic presentation of anterior shoulder dislocation.

- Severe pain after trauma or FOOSH.
- Restricted movement.
- **Loss of normal shoulder contour** — "squared-off" deltoid because the humeral head is no longer beneath it.
- >95% of dislocations are anterior.

📎 Figure L1.14 — Dislocation overview, clinical contour photo.

### Q: Which nerve is at risk and how is it tested?

- **Axillary nerve** — wraps around surgical neck of humerus.
- **Sensory:** lateral upper arm over deltoid (the "regimental badge area").
- **Motor:** deltoid contraction (clinically more important).
- Document **before AND after reduction**.

### Q: Why must X-ray be done BEFORE manipulation?

- To exclude associated fracture: **humeral neck, greater tuberosity avulsion, glenoid rim**.
- A fracture-dislocation changes management — don't manipulate without senior input.
- Views: AP + scapular-Y view (or modified axillary).

### Q: Which reduction technique is safest, and which carries axillary nerve risk?

- **Safest:** traction–counter-traction +/- gentle internal rotation to disimpact humeral head.
- **Most-used:** Kocher's (traction + internal then external rotation).
- **If alone:** Stimson method (prone with weight on arm).
- **Avoid:** Hippocratic method (foot in axilla) — risks axillary nerve compression. Not first-line.

### Q: What relaxation/sedation is used for reduction?

- Adequate analgesia (often morphine).
- **Entonox** for relaxation.
- **Benzodiazepines** for sedation when needed.
- Safe environment — call senior/anaesthetics, especially in elderly.

📎 Figure L1.15 — Hill-Sachs defect and Bankart lesion.

### Q: Hill-Sachs vs Bankart — what and where?

- **Hill-Sachs:** impaction defect on **posterolateral humeral head** (humerus banged against anterior glenoid rim during dislocation).
- **Bankart:** avulsion of **anteroinferior glenoid labrum** (± bony rim).
- Both predispose to **recurrent dislocation** if large.
- Memory: Hill-Sachs on the Humerus; Bankart on the Bone-of-the-glenoid.

🧠 **L1 → L2 cross-reference: AVN of the femoral head.** Same retrograde blood supply (medial/lateral circumflex arteries) underlies traumatic NoF AVN here AND idiopathic Perthes AVN in the limping child. See also: **L2 — Perthes disease**.

## Module 8 — Atlas card: Distal radius fracture

📎 Figure L1.16 — Distal radius fracture with dorsal angulation.

### Q: Classic mechanism and presentation.

- FOOSH (fall on outstretched hand).
- Pain, swelling, "**dinner fork deformity**" if dorsally displaced.
- Usually low-energy in elderly osteoporotic bone.

### Q: Colles vs Smith — which is stable, which needs ORIF?

- **Colles:** **dorsal** angulation/displacement. Strong wrist flexors *hold* it in place → inherently more stable → often non-operative.
- **Smith:** **volar** angulation/displacement. Strong wrist flexors *worsen* it → unstable → ORIF with **volar buttress plate**.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern:** wrist flexors > extensors. Where the deforming muscle pull *helps* the fragment stay reduced, you can cast. Where it *worsens* displacement, you must fix.

### Q: Three management options for distal radius fracture.

- **Cast/splint** — minimally displaced, extra-articular, OR temporary holding.
- **MUA + K-wires** — extra-articular but unstable, especially in children. Wires removed in clinic later.
- **ORIF (volar locking plate)** — displaced unstable; intra-articular involvement; Smith fractures.

## Module 9 — Atlas card: Scaphoid fracture

📎 Figure L1.17 — Scaphoid fracture annotated (PA).

### Q: Classic stem and clinical sign.

- FOOSH, often young person who has been drinking; presents next day with persistent wrist pain.
- **Anatomical snuffbox tenderness** + pain on axial loading of thumb.

### Q: Why is the scaphoid notorious for AVN and non-union?

- Blood supply enters the **distal pole** and runs proximally (retrograde).
- A fracture at the waist or proximal pole interrupts this supply.
- Same logic as intracapsular NoF — proximal fragment is at risk.

### Q: What if X-ray is negative but clinical suspicion high?

- **MRI** (or repeat X-ray at 10–14 days, or CT).
- Treat as occult scaphoid fracture in the meantime — thumb-spica cast.
- Same principle as occult NoF: clinical suspicion outweighs single negative X-ray.

⚠️ **Trap:** "negative X-ray means no fracture" is wrong for scaphoid. Snuffbox tenderness + FOOSH = scaphoid until imaging proves otherwise.

## Module 10 — Atlas card: Tibial plateau fracture

📎 Figure L1.18 — Tibial plateau fracture annotated with lipohaemarthrosis.

### Q: Mechanism — why does the plateau fracture?

- Extreme **valgus / varus force** or axial loading.
- Hard distal femoral condyles impact the relatively **soft cancellous bone of the plateau** → split or depression.
- Concomitant ligamentous or meniscal injury is common.

### Q: What is lipohaemarthrosis and what does it mean?

- Fat–fluid level on a **horizontal-beam (cross-table) lateral knee X-ray**.
- Marrow fat from broken cancellous bone leaks into the joint and floats on blood.
- **Pathognomonic of intra-articular fracture.**

### Q: Why is CT essential for tibial plateau fractures?

- Quantifies depression depth.
- Identifies splits not visible on plain film.
- Plans fixation (which plate, which approach, whether bone graft needed).
- Changes management.

### Q: Tibial plateau management.

- **Non-operative** — only **truly undisplaced** with good joint-line congruency on CT.
- **Operative (majority)** — restore articular surface with plate + screws; **bone graft or cement** to fill voids and prevent re-depression.

## Module 11 — Atlas card: Ankle fractures

📎 Figure L1.19 — Trimalleolar fracture annotated.

### Q: Three structures that stabilise the ankle ring.

- **Lateral ligament complex** (ATFL, CFL, PTFL).
- **Deltoid ligament** (medial — strong fan shape).
- **Syndesmosis** (between distal tibia and fibula).
- **Rule of the ring:** lose one — works. Lose two — problematic. Lose three — unstable.

### Q: What is a trimalleolar fracture?

- Fracture of **medial malleolus** + **lateral malleolus** (distal fibula) + **posterior malleolus** (posterior tibial lip — not a true malleolus).
- Disrupts the ring at multiple sites → unstable → operative.

📎 Figure L1.20 — Weber A/B/C classification.

### Q: Weber A/B/C — what is the classifying feature?

- **Level of the fibular fracture relative to the syndesmosis.**
- **A:** below syndesmosis (avulsion/traction) — syndesmosis intact — **stable**.
- **B:** at the level of the syndesmosis (rotational) — syndesmosis variably injured — **stable or unstable**.
- **C:** above syndesmosis — syndesmosis disrupted — **unstable**.

### Q: Which sign decides whether a Weber B is stable or unstable?

- **Talar shift** on AP/mortise view (talus moved laterally relative to tibia).
- Plus medial malleolus or posterior malleolus fracture.
- Either of these = unstable Weber B → operative.

### Q: Ankle fracture management — non-operative criteria.

- **Below-knee cast, non-weight-bearing, 6–8 weeks**, then walking boot, then physio.
- Indications: **Weber A** (always stable) or **Weber B with NO talar shift, NO medial mall fracture, NO posterior mall fracture**.

### Q: Ankle fracture management — operative criteria.

- **Soft-tissue dependent timing** — strict elevation pre-op as ankles swell rapidly.
- ORIF +/- syndesmosis repair (transverse screw or tightrope).
- Indications: **Weber B unstable** (talar shift / medial or posterior mall fracture); **Weber C** (always unstable).

⭐ **Goal of any ankle fixation:** restore the ankle mortise — talar dome and tibial plafond parallel and centred. Ankles tolerate this well long-term; lose the mortise, and OA follows.

🧠 **L1 → L2 cross-reference: PAIDS framework.** Adult fracture description + Salter-Harris = paediatric description. The "I" in PAIDS (Intra/Extra-articular) is exactly the same logic applied to ankle fractures here. See also: **L2 — Paediatric fracture description (PAIDS)**.

## Module 12 — Cross-cutting patterns across the atlas

### Q: What three structural questions predict almost every management decision?

- Is the **blood supply** at risk? (NoF, scaphoid)
- Is the **joint surface** involved? (tibial plateau, ankle, intra-articular distal radius)
- Is the **physis** at risk? (paediatric — see L2)
- These three questions explain the NoF tree, lipohaemarthrosis sign, scaphoid AVN risk, syndesmosis logic, and Salter-Harris.

### Q: Three questions to ask first in any fracture stem.

- **What is the patient stable?** (ATLS first)
- **Where is it and what is involved?** (intra/extra-articular, blood supply)
- **Reduce → Hold → Rehab — by what method?**

🧠 **L1 → L2 cross-reference: Wolff's law.** L1 uses Wolff's law to explain remodelling after fracture. L2 extends it to morphogenesis (DDH, OI). Same biological principle, two timescales. See also: **L2 — Developmental dysplasia of the hip; Osteogenesis imperfecta**.

## Interleaved SBA block

### Q: SBA 1. A 24-year-old motorcyclist arrives in resus with a deformed right thigh. He is GCS 15, RR 22, sats 96% on air, HR 130, BP 92/58. Right thigh is shortened and externally rotated; distal pulses intact, sensation normal. Most appropriate immediate next step?

- **Answer: insert two large-bore IVs and start resuscitation, alongside identifying the femoral fracture as the bleeding source.**
- Why: ATLS — the femoral fracture is haemorrhagic shock's likely source, treated as part of "C". Splinting reduces further blood loss but resuscitation and IV access come first.
- Wrong-answer logic: X-ray and CT delay resuscitation; splint alone without IV access is incomplete; theatre commits an unstable patient prematurely.

### Q: SBA 2. A 78-year-old woman falls and is found to have a displaced subcapital femoral neck fracture. She walks independently with a stick, lives alone, manages own shopping. Most likely operative management?

- **Answer: total hip replacement (THR).**
- Why: displaced intracapsular + age >65 + fit and mobile → replace, with THR (better long-term function in fit patients). Hemiarthroplasty would be the answer if she were less mobile/frail.
- Wrong-answer logic: cannulated screws would be appropriate for undisplaced or for under-55 displaced; DHS is for extracapsular, not intracapsular.

### Q: SBA 3. Patient post-anterior-shoulder-dislocation reduction. Pre-reduction sensation in the regimental badge area was normal. Post-reduction the patient cannot abduct the arm against gravity. Which structure is most likely injured?

- **Answer: axillary nerve.**
- Why: it supplies deltoid (motor → abduction) and the regimental badge area (sensory). Motor deficit is the more clinically important sign and is part of the standard pre/post documentation.
- Wrong-answer logic: suprascapular nerve supplies supraspinatus/infraspinatus; musculocutaneous gives elbow flexion; long thoracic gives serratus anterior (winged scapula).

### Q: SBA 4. A 62-year-old woman trips and twists her ankle. AP/mortise X-rays show oblique fibular fracture starting at the tibial plafond level + transverse medial malleolar fracture + 5 mm lateral talar shift. She is neurovascularly intact. Most appropriate management?

- **Answer: ORIF — fibular plate + medial mall screw, soft-tissue dependent timing.**
- Why: fibular fracture at syndesmosis = Weber B; medial mall fracture + talar shift = unstable Weber B → operative. Wait for swelling to settle before formal ORIF.
- Wrong-answer logic: below-knee cast appropriate only for stable fractures; MUA + K-wires is for distal radius (not ankle); external fixator is a damage-control bridge, not definitive.

### Q: SBA 5. A 50-year-old falls onto his right hand. He has snuffbox tenderness and pain on axial thumb loading. PA + lateral X-rays of the wrist are reported as normal. Most appropriate next step?

- **Answer: thumb-spica cast and outpatient MRI (or repeat X-ray) at 10–14 days.**
- Why: classic occult scaphoid fracture — clinical suspicion outweighs single negative X-ray. Treat as fractured until imaging proves otherwise. Same principle as occult NoF.
- Wrong-answer logic: discharge with "no fracture" misses ~10% scaphoid fractures with serious AVN/non-union risk; fixation without imaging is premature; CT is reasonable but MRI is the lecturer's named modality.

### Q: SBA 6. A 9-year-old falls off a trampoline with a closed displaced midshaft femoral fracture. Treated with flexible IM nailing. At 4 weeks: comfortable, partial weight-bearing, X-ray shows abundant external callus. Mother is alarmed. Best explanation?

- **Answer: indirect (secondary) healing with callus formation, as expected for this construct.**
- Why: flexible nails provide relative stability — micromotion is by design — and callus is the expected radiological signature. This is normal healing.
- Wrong-answer logic: hypertrophic non-union requires lack of progress over multiple time points; direct healing produces no callus and requires absolute stability (incompatible with flexible nails); malunion is about alignment, not healing type; osteomyelitis would have systemic features.
