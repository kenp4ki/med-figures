## Module 1 — The four things that make a child's skeleton different

📎 Figure L2.1 — Paediatric skeleton: 270 bones, growth plates at long bone ends.

### Q: Single sentence framing — how does the paediatric skeleton differ from the adult?

- **Children's skeleton is NOT a miniaturised version of an adult.**
- It is a continuously changing system because the child is growing.
- Four distinguishing features: **elasticity, physis, speed of healing, remodelling**.

### Q: Skeletal maturity ages — when do physes close?

- **Girls 15–16, boys 18–19.**
- Closure is gradual, influenced by puberty, menarche, parental height.
- Once closed, longitudinal growth stops.

🧠 **L2 → L1 cross-reference: same biology, different tempo.** Both age groups heal by the identical two pathways (direct vs indirect) — children just heal faster and remodel more aggressively because the physis is open and metabolism is higher. See also: **L1 — Fracture healing**.

## Module 2 — Bone development

### Q: Two mechanisms of bone development.

- **Intramembranous** — mesenchymal cells → bone directly. **Cranial bones and clavicle.**
- **Endochondral** — mesenchymal → cartilage → bone. **All other long bones.**

📎 Figure L2.2 — Intramembranous ossification cascade.
📎 Figure L2.3 — Endochondral primary ossification.

### Q: Primary vs secondary ossification centres — what and where?

- **Primary OC:** pre-natal, in the diaphysis (central shaft) of a long bone.
- **Secondary OC:** post-natal, in epiphyses — these become **the physes**.
- Long bones often have several secondary OCs (femoral head, greater trochanter, etc.).

## Module 3 — The physis (cascade detail — high-yield)

📎 Figure L2.4 — Physis zones histology.

### Q: The five zones of the physis, top to bottom.

```
EPIPHYSIS
   │
   ├─ 1. Reserve zone        → cells resting; matrix production
   ├─ 2. Proliferative zone  → mitosis (achondroplasia disrupts here)
   ├─ 3. Maturation/         → cells enlarge; lipids, glycogen,
   │     hypertrophy zone      alkaline phosphatase; matrix calcifies
   ├─ 4. Calcified matrix    → chondrocyte apoptosis
   └─ 5. Zone of ossification → vascular invasion; true bone deposited
       (metaphysis)
   │
METAPHYSIS
```

### Q: Mental model for the physis.

- **One-way conveyor belt** producing new bone at the metaphyseal end.
- Cells start at the top (epiphyseal side), do their job in zones, emerge as bone at the bottom.
- **Damage anywhere on the belt → growth slows or stops** → length discrepancy or angular deformity (if partial).

📎 Figure L2.6 — Healing timeline + physis growth contributions.

### Q: Which physes contribute most to growth, and what proportion?

- **Knee dominates the leg:** distal femur **~70%**, proximal tibia **~70%**.
- **Extremes of upper limb:** proximal humerus **~40%**, distal radius **~40%**.
- **Hip ~12% · elbow ~20% each · ankle ~18%.**
- **Pattern:** "knee grows most; extremes of UL grow most." Damage near the knee = biggest growth loss.

⭐ **High-yield: physis at the knee = ~70% of leg growth.** Single most likely SBA recall fact in this lecture.

## Module 4 — Elasticity (paediatric-specific fracture patterns)

📎 Figure L2.5 — Buckle, plastic deformation, greenstick.

### Q: Why is children's bone more elastic than adult bone?

- Higher density of haversian canals.
- More collagen relative to mineralisation.
- Bone bends before it breaks — adult bone is too brittle for this.

### Q: Three paediatric-specific fracture patterns adults don't get.

- **Buckle (torus)** — compression-side cortex crumples ("like a column").
- **Plastic deformation** — bone bends without visible cortical break (PVC pipe analogy).
- **Greenstick** — one cortex breaks (tension side); opposite cortex bends but stays intact.

### Q: Test yourself — which paediatric pattern is each scenario?

- "Bone bowed on X-ray, no fracture line visible" → **plastic deformation**.
- "Wrinkled cortex on the dorsal side of distal radius after FOOSH" → **buckle (torus)**.
- "One cortex broken, opposite cortex intact and bent" → **greenstick**.

## Module 5 — Speed of healing and remodelling

### Q: Approximate healing times by age.

- **Newborn:** 5–6 days.
- **5–6 year old:** 2–3 weeks.
- **Adolescent:** approaches adult.
- **Adult:** 6 weeks (convention; actual 3–12 wks site-dependent).

### Q: Why do children remodel away malalignment that an adult never could?

- Growth + Wolff's law operate simultaneously.
- The bone elongates around the new mechanical axis, gradually correcting angulation.
- The younger the child and the closer to a fast-growing physis, the more malalignment can be accepted.

⚠️ **Trap:** remodelling potential is age- and site-dependent. A 13-year-old with 2 years of growth left will *not* remodel like a 2-year-old. Don't treat all paediatric malunions as equivalent.

## Module 6 — Atlas: congenital conditions (4 cards)

📘 **Card template** — each condition uses: definition / mechanism / demographics / examination / imaging / treatment.

### Card 6a — Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

📎 Figure L2.7 — DDH X-ray: dislocated right hip.

### Q: What is DDH and how should you think about it?

- Spectrum of disorders where the femoral head is unstable or incongruous with the acetabulum.
- **Spectrum:** dysplasia → subluxation → dislocation.
- **Principle:** ball must be in socket for both to develop normally (Wolff's law applied to morphogenesis).

### Q: DDH risk factors.

- **Female 6:1**, first-born, breech, family history.
- Oligohydramnios.
- Swaddling cultures (Native American, Lapland).
- Rare in African American/Asian populations.

### Q: DDH examination — what is the key finding, and why are Barlow/Ortolani limited?

- **Limited hip abduction** is the key sign on baby check.
- Galeazzi sign — apparent leg length discrepancy with knees flexed.
- **Barlow and Ortolani are non-sensitive after 3 months** (femoral head no longer reducible).

### Q: DDH imaging — why ultrasound under 4 months?

- Femoral head is **cartilaginous** until ~4 months, so doesn't show on X-ray.
- **Ultrasound (birth–4 mo)** sees cartilage.
- **X-ray (>4 mo)** when head ossifies.

📎 Figure L2.8 — Pavlik harness.

### Q: DDH treatment ladder.

- **<6 months + reducible** → **Pavlik harness** (92% effective). Holds hips flexed and abducted, locking head into socket.
- **Failed Pavlik or 6–18 months** → **MUA + closed reduction + hip spica cast**.
- Older → operative open reduction (beyond scope).

🧠 **L2 → L1 cross-reference: Wolff's law in two directions.** L1 uses Wolff's law to explain *remodelling after fracture*; L2 uses it to explain *morphogenesis* — DDH bones grow misshapen because the mechanical signal is wrong. Same principle, two timescales. See also: **L1 — Indirect fracture healing**.

### Card 6b — Clubfoot (Congenital Talipes Equinovarus, CTEV)

📎 Figure L2.9 — Bilateral clubfoot clinical photo.

### Q: Clubfoot demographics.

- **Most common MSK birth defect, 1:1000.**
- Highest in Hawaiians; M:F = 2:1; **50% bilateral**.
- ~5% sibling risk; 25% familial; **PITX1 gene** (polygenic).

### Q: The CAVE deformity — what does each letter mean?

- **C**avus — high arch (tight intrinsics, FHL, FDL).
- **A**dductus of foot (tight tib post + tib ant).
- **V**arus — hindfoot turned in (tight tendoachilles, tib post).
- **E**quinus — ankle plantarflexed (tight tendoachilles).

📎 Figure L2.10 — Ponseti method serial casting + boots and bar.

### Q: Ponseti method (gold standard) — four steps.

- **Serial weekly casts**, gradual stretch (~6 weeks).
- **Percutaneous tendoachilles release** (heel-cord tenotomy) — most need this.
- **Boots and bar brace** — full-time first 3 months, then nights/naps to age 5.
- Some need late surgery for residual deformity.

### Card 6c — Achondroplasia

📎 Figure L2.11 — Achondroplasia clinical phenotype.

### Q: Achondroplasia genetics and mechanism.

- **Autosomal dominant**; **G380 mutation in FGFR3**.
- FGFR3 inhibits chondrocyte proliferation in the **proliferative zone of the physis**.
- Defective endochondral ossification → long bones don't grow.

### Q: Achondroplasia phenotype — what does "rhizomelic" mean?

- **Rhizomelic** = root-limbed: the proximal limb segment is shorter than the distal.
- Humerus < forearm; femur < tibia; trunk normal.
- Adult height ~125 cm; cognition normal.

### Q: Dangerous complications of achondroplasia.

- **Foramen magnum stenosis** (cervicomedullary compression — high-yield).
- Spinal stenosis; lumbar lordosis.
- Lower-limb varus.
- Ligamentous laxity.
- ~30% need orthopaedic intervention in their lifetime.

⭐ **SBA triad:** AD inheritance + FGFR3 + proliferative zone of physis. Plus foramen magnum stenosis as the dangerous complication.

### Card 6d — Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI)

📎 Figure L2.12 — Blue sclera in OI.
📎 Figure L2.13 — OI: bowed femurs pre-op vs telescoping rods post-op.

### Q: OI mechanism — one-line summary.

- Hereditary defect (AD or recessive) of **Type I collagen**.
- Either decreased secretion or abnormal collagen produced.
- Affects everything containing Type I collagen: bone, sclera, dentine, tendon, skin.

### Q: OI bony effects.

- **Fragility fractures.**
- Short stature.
- Scoliosis.
- Bones bow under their own weight (Wolff's law fails because bone can't respond normally).

### Q: OI non-bony manifestations to recognise.

- **Blue sclera** (thin sclera shows underlying choroid).
- **Dentinogenesis imperfecta** — brown soft teeth.
- **Wormian skull bones** (extra small bones at sutures).
- Heart defects.
- Hypermetabolism.

### Q: OI medical treatment options — old and new.

- **Bisphosphonates** — inhibit osteoclasts → reduce resorption → increase density.
- **Setrusumab (UX143)** — anti-sclerostin monoclonal antibody (anabolic).
- **Denosumab** — anti-RANKL monoclonal antibody.
- **Surgical:** mechanical alignment + stabilisation, often **telescoping rods** that grow with the child.

## Module 7 — Paediatric fracture description (PAIDS)

### Q: PAIDS — five elements of paediatric fracture description.

- **P**attern (transverse/oblique/spiral/comminuted/avulsion)
- **A**natomy (proximal/middle/distal third)
- **I**ntra- or extra-articular
- **D**isplacement (displaced/angulated/shortened/rotated)
- **S**alter-Harris classification

🧠 **L2 → L1 cross-reference: PAIDS = adult description + Salter-Harris.** L1 teaches the location/pattern/displacement/intra-articular vocabulary; PAIDS is that framework with the physis layer added on top. See also: **L1 — Describing a fracture**.

## Module 8 — Salter-Harris (cascade — physeal injury)

📎 Figure L2.14 — Salter-Harris I–V.

### Q: Salter-Harris path skeleton — how does each type cross the physis?

```
TYPE I   ─── physis only (separation)
TYPE II  ─┬─ physis → exits into metaphysis (Above)        ← MOST COMMON
TYPE III ─┴─ physis → exits into epiphysis (Lower)         ← intra-articular
TYPE IV  ─── crosses epiphysis + physis + metaphysis (Through all) ← intra-articular
TYPE V   ─── crush injury to physis itself (cRush)         ← worst prognosis
```

Mnemonic: **SALTR** (Separation, Above, Lower, Through all, cRush).

### Q: Salter-Harris — which types are intra-articular, and which has the worst growth-arrest risk?

- **Intra-articular: III and IV** (fracture line enters the joint via epiphysis).
- **Worst growth-arrest risk: V** (crush kills the physeal cells directly).
- **Most common: II.**
- Risk of growth arrest **increases I → V**.

### Q: Why are types III and IV particularly bad?

- **Two problems at once:**
  - Joint surface incongruity → post-traumatic OA.
  - Physeal disruption → growth arrest.
- Almost always need anatomical reduction + internal fixation.

⚠️ **Trap:** Salter-Harris V is often **missed on initial X-ray** because there's no displacement — just a crushed physis. If a child later develops growth arrest after a "minor" injury, V is often the retrospective diagnosis.

## Module 9 — Paediatric fracture management (4 Rs)

### Q: The 4 R framework — what's added vs the adult 3 R?

- **Resuscitate** (added — explicit ATLS step)
- **Reduce** (closed: traction/manipulation; open: incision)
- **Restrict** (renamed from "Hold" — plaster, splint, internal/external fixation)
- **Rehabilitate** (play is a great rehabilitator; stiffness is rarely an issue)

🧠 **L2 → L1 cross-reference: Resuscitate = ATLS.** L2's 4 Rs add the explicit Resuscitate step that L1 puts on a separate slide as A/B/C/D. Same content. See also: **L1 — ATLS priority**.

📎 Figure L2.15 — Gallows traction (infant-specific).

### Q: Gallows traction — what is it and who is it for?

- **Infant lower-limb fracture management.**
- Baby supine, both legs hung vertically by skin traction.
- Body weight provides counter-traction.
- Only safe in babies/toddlers (light enough that body weight doesn't compromise circulation).

### Q: Two paediatric-specific operative considerations.

- **Avoid crossing the physis with metalwork** where possible — risk of growth arrest.
- **Plan for metalwork removal** — child grows past implants; what fitted at age 8 is in the wrong position at 14.

## Module 10 — The limping child (atlas — 4 differentials)

📘 **Lecturer's drum:** any limping child has **septic arthritis until proven otherwise** — repeated four times on the final slide of the lecture.

### Q: Differential diagnosis of the limping child — 4 entities by typical age.

| Condition | Typical age | Fever / unwell? | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Septic arthritis** | Any (often 4–8) | **Yes** | Surgical washout — emergency |
| **Transient synovitis** | 4–8 | Mild / absent | Supportive — diagnosis of exclusion |
| **Perthes** | **4–8**, M:F 4:1 | No (chronic) | Containment; sometimes surgery |
| **SUFE** | **12–13**, obese male | No | Operative — single screw in situ |

---

### Card 10a — Septic arthritis

### Q: Why is septic arthritis in a child an orthopaedic emergency?

- Infection enzymatically degrades cartilage within hours.
- Pus tracking to the metaphysis can damage the **physis** → growth arrest.
- Both joint AND growth at risk → **lifetime consequences if missed**.
- Treatment: **surgical washout** (open or arthroscopic) + antibiotics.

### Q: Kocher's classification — four criteria.

- **Non-weight-bearing**
- **Temperature >38°C**
- **WBC >12,000**
- **ESR >40**

Probabilities: 0/4 <0.5% · 1/4 ~20% · 2/4 ~40% · 3/4 ~80% · **4/4 >90%**.

### Q: Definitive diagnostic step for septic arthritis.

- **Joint aspiration** + microscopy + PCR + culture.
- Pus + organism = septic arthritis → washout.
- Modern PCR panels (e.g. BioFire) ID organism in ~20 min vs hours for Gram stain.

⚠️ **Trap:** "Kocher 1/4 means it's not SA" is wrong. 1/4 still ~20% probability. **Aspirate before discharge** if any criterion met and the child is irritable.

---

### Card 10b — Transient synovitis

### Q: How does transient synovitis differ from septic arthritis?

- **Diagnosis of exclusion** — only after SA aspirate is negative.
- Reactive synovitis to recent (often viral) infection — URTI, otitis media.
- Child not systemically unwell; mild joint inflammation.
- **Supportive treatment ± antibiotics**; resolves over days.

---

### Card 10c — Perthes disease

📎 Figure L2.16 — Perthes: idiopathic AVN of right proximal femoral epiphysis.

### Q: Perthes — mechanism and demographics.

- **Idiopathic avascular necrosis of the proximal femoral epiphysis.**
- Aetiology unknown; ?vascular, ?coagulopathic.
- **Age 4–8**; M:F **4:1**; ~500/year UK.
- Smoking environment + lower socioeconomic class.

### Q: Perthes presentation — what distinguishes it from septic arthritis?

- **Chronic limp** (weeks–months) rather than acute.
- **No fever, no systemic illness.**
- Hip/groin/knee pain (referred via obturator nerve).
- X-ray: flattened, sclerotic, fragmented femoral epiphysis.

### Q: Why is Perthes treated with containment rather than replacement?

- **Child is still growing — the head can remodel** while it dies and is gradually replaced.
- Goal: keep the ball spherical and contained in the socket while it remodels (Play-Doh ice cream analogy).
- **Physio + activity modification first**; surgery sometimes needed.

🧠 **L2 → L1 cross-reference: same blood supply, three different mechanisms.** Adult NoF AVN (traumatic), Perthes (idiopathic), SUFE (mechanical) all hit the same retrograde medial/lateral circumflex artery supply to the femoral head. Age determines whether you replace, contain, or fix in situ. See also: **L1 — NoF blood supply and AVN**.

### Card 10d — Slipped Upper Femoral Epiphysis (SUFE)

📎 Figure L2.17 — SUFE: slipped right femoral epiphysis (frog-leg view).
📎 Figure L2.18 — SUFE post-op: single percutaneous screw across physis.

### Q: SUFE mechanism in one sentence.

- The proximal femoral **metaphysis slips relative to the epiphysis** (epiphysis stays in the acetabulum).
- Effectively a **Salter-Harris I-equivalent** through the proximal femoral physis without trauma.
- "Ice cream slipping off the cone."

### Q: SUFE classic stem.

- **Obese adolescent male, 12–13 years**, during the pubertal growth spurt.
- Limp, externally rotated leg.
- **Hip/groin/KNEE pain** — knee pain is the classic referred-symptom trap.
- Often history of vague pain over weeks.

### Q: Why is the imaging order AP pelvis + frog-leg lateral?

- AP can be subtle — slip is mostly posterior.
- **Frog-leg lateral** shows the slip clearly.
- **Klein's line** drawn along superior femoral neck on AP should intersect the epiphysis; in SUFE it doesn't.

### Q: Why is SUFE fixed in situ rather than reduced?

- **Aggressive reduction risks AVN** (disrupts the same retrograde blood supply as Perthes/NoF).
- **Single percutaneous screw across physis** stabilises and prevents further slip.
- Goal is to prevent progression, not to restore anatomy.

⚠️ **Trap:** an adolescent with knee pain and no knee findings → image the hip. Examining only the knee misses SUFE. The same trap repeats for any hip pathology in this age group.

## Module 11 — Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: Three structural questions that predict almost any paediatric MSK answer.

- Is the **physis** at risk?
- Is the **joint surface** involved?
- Is the **blood supply** at risk?
- Same three questions as adult fractures, with physis added.

### Q: Age stratification — same hip, different diagnosis.

- **4–8 + acute + fever** → septic arthritis
- **4–8 + acute + recent URTI + well** → transient synovitis
- **4–8 + chronic limp + no fever** → Perthes
- **12–13 + obese male + knee pain** → SUFE
- **>65 + low-energy fall + shortened/externally rotated leg** → NoF (L1)

🧠 **Reasoning pattern (transferable):** in any limp/hip-pain stem, **read the age first**. The differential collapses to 1–2 entities once you have the age.

## Interleaved SBA block

### Q: SBA 1. A 5-year-old boy refuses to walk for 24 hours. Snotty nose for a week. Afebrile. Right hip held in slight flexion and external rotation. WBC 9,500. ESR 28. Most appropriate next step?

- **Answer: hip ultrasound + aspiration of any effusion.**
- Why: Kocher 1–2/4 (refuses to walk +/- borderline ESR) → ~20–40% chance of septic arthritis. Recent URTI fits transient synovitis but doesn't exclude SA. **Aspirate before discharge.**
- Wrong-answer logic: discharge with "transient synovitis" is premature without aspiration; empirical antibiotics commit to treating an unproven infection; MRI is too slow.

### Q: SBA 2. A 7-year-old girl falls onto an outstretched arm. Distal radius X-ray: line begins at the joint surface, crosses the physis, exits through the metaphysis with a small metaphyseal fragment. Salter-Harris type and dominant concern?

- **Answer: Salter-Harris IV — concern is BOTH growth arrest AND post-traumatic arthritis.**
- Why: line crosses epiphysis (joint) + physis + metaphysis = type IV by definition. Intra-articular AND physeal.
- Wrong-answer logic: Type III exits through epiphysis only (no metaphyseal extension); Type II is metaphyseal only (doesn't enter joint); Type V is pure crush.

### Q: SBA 3. A 13-year-old boy, BMI 32, presents with 3 weeks of left knee pain and a limp. Knee examination unremarkable. Best next step?

- **Answer: AP pelvis + frog-leg lateral X-rays of both hips.**
- Why: classic SUFE — knee pain referred from hip is the diagnostic trap. Bilateral imaging because SUFE is bilateral in ~25%.
- Wrong-answer logic: knee MRI misses the diagnosis; reassurance is dangerous (further slip → AVN); referring to physiotherapy without imaging delays definitive treatment.

### Q: SBA 4. A 4-month-old female infant, breech delivery, baby check shows limited right hip abduction. Most appropriate imaging?

- **Answer: hip ultrasound.**
- Why: <4 months → femoral head is cartilaginous → US sees cartilage. Female + breech = high DDH risk, plus exam finding (limited abduction is the key sign).
- Wrong-answer logic: X-ray would show only ossified pelvis and miss the cartilaginous head; MRI is excessive; observation alone delays Pavlik harness, which is most effective <6 months.

### Q: SBA 5. A 5-year-old boy with a 6-month history of intermittent right-sided limping presents to clinic. He is otherwise well. X-ray pelvis shows a flattened, sclerotic, fragmented right femoral epiphysis. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Answer: Perthes disease.**
- Why: 4–8yo with chronic limp + no systemic illness + epiphyseal fragmentation on X-ray. Idiopathic AVN of proximal femoral epiphysis.
- Wrong-answer logic: septic arthritis would be acute and unwell; SUFE is 12–13 with metaphyseal slip, not epiphyseal fragmentation; transient synovitis has normal X-ray.

### Q: SBA 6. A 2-year-old has an autosomal dominant skeletal disorder with disproportionate short stature: humerus shorter than forearm, femur shorter than tibia, normal trunk and intelligence. Which gene and which physeal zone?

- **Answer: FGFR3, proliferative zone.**
- Why: classic achondroplasia. AD G380 FGFR3 mutation inhibits chondrocyte proliferation in the proliferative zone of the physis → defective endochondral ossification → rhizomelic short stature.
- Wrong-answer logic: COL1A1 = OI; FBN1 = Marfan; reserve zone is for stem cell potential; hypertrophy zone houses larger maturing chondrocytes (not the FGFR3 target).
