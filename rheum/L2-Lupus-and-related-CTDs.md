## The bundle spine — and the L1 → L2 link

📎 Figure L2.1 — Master classification tree, lupus branch circled (4th appearance — same diagram as L1.2).

### Q: Where does SLE sit in the master arthritis classification?

- Inflammatory → autoimmune → **lupus and related disorders** (the third autoimmune branch alongside RA and seronegatives).
- Joint involvement is **non-erosive** — different from RA (erosive pannus).
- Disease is **multi-organ**, not joint-dominant — joints are one of many sites.

### Q: What are the four autoimmune connective tissue disorders?

📎 Figure L2.2 — The four CTDs.

- **Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)** — main focus.
- **Sjögren's syndrome** — autoimmune exocrinopathy.
- **Inflammatory muscle disease** — polymyositis, dermatomyositis.
- **Systemic sclerosis (scleroderma)** — diffuse and limited cutaneous.

📘 *"Connective tissue disorder"* is a slight misnomer — internal organs are often involved. **Overlap syndromes** can occur, especially in children.

## Cross-cutting principles for autoimmune CTDs

### Q: Three features that recur across all four CTDs — and how they differ from RA?

- **Arthralgia ± non-erosive arthritis** — joint deformities can occur from soft-tissue/ligament damage but X-rays remain normal. (Contrast: RA = erosive.)
- **Serum autoantibodies** are characteristic — useful diagnostically, sometimes track activity, sometimes directly pathogenic.
- **Raynaud's phenomenon** is common across SLE, scleroderma, Sjögren's.

### Q: Walk through Raynaud's phenomenon — the triphasic colour change.

📎 Figure L2.4 — Raynaud's phenomenon (vasospasm, blanching).

- Trigger: cold exposure (most often).
- **WHITE** — vasospasm of digital arterioles → blanching.
- **BLUE** — cyanosis as static venous blood deoxygenates.
- **RED** — reactive hyperaemia when blood flow returns.
- Severe Raynaud's in CTDs → tissue ischaemia, ulcers, even necrosis (digit can fall off).

⚠️ **Trap:** primary Raynaud's (no underlying CTD) is bothersome but not dangerous. CTD-related Raynaud's can be limb-threatening — search the stem for other CTD features (rash, mouth ulcers, pleurisy, sclerodactyly).

## SLE — what it is and who gets it

### Q: One-sentence definition of SLE.

An autoimmune disease with disturbance of **both innate and adaptive immunity**, producing **antinuclear antibodies** that form **immune complexes**, which deposit in tissues and cause **multi-site inflammation** — particularly joints, skin and kidneys.

### Q: SLE epidemiology — four numbers worth knowing.

- Onset typically **15–45 years**.
- **F:M ~9:1** (much stronger sex bias than RA's 2:1).
- Prevalence and severity vary by ancestry: **African > Asian > White European**.
- Manifestations highly variable → diagnostic difficulties (patients scatter to derm / hep / resp / etc. before someone joins the dots).

### Q: Why are lupus manifestations so much more varied than RA's?

- **Organ-specific autoimmunity** (e.g. Hashimoto's thyroiditis → just thyroid; T1DM → just islets).
- **Mid-spectrum** = RA (mainly synovium + a few extra-articular features).
- **Systemic autoimmunity** = lupus — every cell with a nucleus is potentially vulnerable, because the targets are **nuclear antigens** common to all cells.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern:** any organ a stem mentions (kidney, brain, heart, skin, blood) can be the lupus presentation. Don't anchor on the joint when working a multisystem stem.

## SLE — clinical features (atlas of organs)

📎 Figure L2.3 — Malar rash sparing the nasolabial fold.
📎 Figure L2.5 — Jaccoud's arthropathy: swan neck deformity without bony erosion.

### Q: Skin and mucosa — four features?

- **Malar (butterfly) rash** — erythema across cheeks and nose bridge that **spares the nasolabial folds**.
- Photosensitive rash on sun-exposed areas.
- Mouth ulcers.
- Hair loss.

### Q: Vascular and MSK manifestations of SLE?

- **Raynaud's phenomenon** (vascular).
- Arthralgia and sometimes arthritis — **non-erosive** (no bony damage on X-ray).
- Can produce **swan neck deformity** with normal X-ray = **Jaccoud's arthropathy** (soft tissue/ligament damage only).

### Q: Internal organ involvement in SLE?

- **Serositis** — pericarditis (chest pain worse lying flat, better leaning forward), pleuritis, less commonly peritonitis.
- **Renal — glomerulonephritis ("lupus nephritis")** — can be silent until late.
- **Cerebral lupus** — psychosis, motor/sensory deficits, seizures.
- **Myocarditis** — much more serious than pericarditis, can be life-threatening.

### Q: Haematological and other manifestations?

- **Autoimmune thrombocytopenia** (low platelets).
- **Haemolytic anaemia** (antibody-mediated red cell destruction).
- **Lymphopenia** — very common, often the first abnormality.
- Lymphadenopathy and fever in absence of infection (mimics infection or lymphoma → diagnostic confusion).

⚠️ **Trap:** a lupus patient with fever + lymphadenopathy + low lymphocytes can look infective. Always exclude infection first — lupus patients are often immunosuppressed and **really do** get infections.

## SLE — autoantibodies (the diagnostic engine)

📎 Figure L2.6 — ANA immunofluorescence patterns (homogenous, speckled, nucleolar, fine speckled).

### Q: ANA — what it does and doesn't tell you?

- Hallmark of SLE — found in **all SLE patients**.
- **Negative ANA effectively rules out SLE** → high sensitivity, good rule-out test.
- **NOT specific** — also positive in other autoimmune disease, infection, even healthy people.
- Lab reports **strength** (max dilution at which still detectable; e.g. 1:320 stronger than 1:80) and **pattern** (suggests autoantigen).

### Q: The four SLE-associated autoantibodies you must know.

| Antibody | Specificity | Tracks activity? |
|---|---|---|
| **Anti-dsDNA** | **Specific for SLE** | **Yes — correlates with activity** |
| **Anti-Smith (Sm)** | Specific for SLE | **No** |
| **Anti-phospholipid** | Not specific | (Tracks thrombosis risk) |
| Anti-Ro / Anti-La | Also in Sjögren's | No |

⭐ **Master discriminator:** *Anti-dsDNA is the only one that's both specific AND tracks disease activity.* Anti-Sm is specific but does NOT correlate with activity.

### Q: What clinical syndromes arise from anti-Ro / anti-La antibodies?

- **Secondary Sjögren's syndrome** in lupus patients.
- **Neonatal lupus syndrome** if mother passes them transplacentally:
  - Transient rash in the neonate (resolves once maternal antibody is cleared).
  - **Permanent congenital heart block** — needs antenatal fetal heart-rate monitoring.

### Q: Antiphospholipid antibodies — three things to remember.

📎 Figure L2.4 (also referenced for Raynaud's) — visualise vascular consequences.

- Directed at phospholipids on cell membranes.
- **Two clinical consequences:**
  - **Thrombosis** — arterial (stroke, MI) and venous (DVT, PE).
  - **Pregnancy loss** (recurrent miscarriage).
- Lab assays: **anticardiolipin** Abs and **anti-β₂-glycoprotein 1** Abs.

### Q: When do you call it "antiphospholipid syndrome"?

- Persistent APL antibodies (positive on **two occasions ≥12 weeks apart**) **PLUS** a clinical event (thrombosis or recurrent pregnancy loss).
- Can be **primary** (without SLE) or **secondary** (with SLE).

### Q: Where do RA's autoantibodies sit on the unified rheumatology table?

| Disease | Key autoantibodies |
|---|---|
| **RA** | Rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP |
| **SLE** | ANA, anti-dsDNA, APL, anti-Sm (+ Ro/La/RNP) |
| **OA / ReA / Gout / AS** | **None** |

📘 *Vasculitis = ANCA — won't be tested* (lecturer-flagged).

## SLE — the central mechanism

📎 Figure L2.7 — Waste disposal hypothesis cascade.

```
Cell undergoes apoptosis
        │
        ▼
Nuclear antigens translocate to membrane surface
        │
        │ Impaired clearance of apoptotic cells (lupus-specific defect)
        ▼
Enhanced presentation of nuclear antigens to immune cells
        │
        ▼
B-cell autoimmunity → ANTINUCLEAR ANTIBODIES
        │
        ▼
Antibody + nuclear antigen → IMMUNE COMPLEXES
        │
        │ Deposit in tissues, activate complement, engage Fc receptors
        ▼
Multi-site tissue inflammation and damage
```

### Q: Why does the immune system make antibodies against nuclear antigens at all — they should be hidden inside the cell?

- This is the puzzle the **waste disposal hypothesis** solves.
- During **apoptosis**, nuclear antigens translocate from nucleus → cell surface.
- In SLE, **clearance of apoptotic cells is impaired** → nuclear antigens hang around → enhanced presentation to immune cells → B-cell autoimmunity.

### Q: Three immunopathogenic mechanisms in SLE — innate vs adaptive?

- **Innate:** overactivity of **type-1 interferon pathway** + complement abnormalities.
- **Adaptive:** **autoreactive B and T cells**.
- The "interferon gene signature" in blood (genes activated downstream of IFN receptor) is high in active disease — drove development of **anifrolumab** (anti-IFN-receptor antibody, FDA-approved abroad, not UK).

## SLE — investigations

### Q: Why is the ESR–CRP relationship in SLE different from RA?

- **High ESR** in active disease — but **CRP usually normal**.
- A **high CRP in a lupus patient suggests infection**.
- Exception: an **active inflammatory arthritis** or **serositis** in the lupus itself can drive CRP up.

⭐ **Examiner's voice:** this discordance is a classic SLE clue in MCQ stems.

### Q: Renal investigations in SLE — what to do and why?

- **Urine dip** — bedside, every visit. First sign of lupus nephritis is **leak of protein into urine**.
- **uPCR** (urine protein:creatinine ratio) — quantify proteinuria.
- **Creatinine + albumin** — but creatinine often normal until late, so don't wait for it.
- **Kidney biopsy** if persistent proteinuria — definitive test.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern:** *protein leaks before creatinine rises.* Catch lupus nephritis at the proteinuria stage — by the time creatinine has moved you may already have permanent damage.

### Q: Immunological tests beyond ANA?

- Anti-dsDNA — confirm + track activity.
- Anti-Sm, Ro, La if ANA pattern suggests them.
- **Complement C3 and C4** — consumed by immune-complex deposition → **low C3/C4 = active SLE**.
- Antiphospholipid antibodies (anticardiolipin, anti-β₂-GP1).

### Q: How do you measure SLE disease activity over time?

📎 Figure L2.8 — Complement consumption pattern (immune complexes ↑, C3/C4 ↓, anti-dsDNA ↑).

- Active flare: ↑ anti-dsDNA, ↓ C3/C4.
- Real-patient charts show this pattern repeating with each flare and resolving with treatment.
- Combine with clinical features and end-organ markers (uPCR, platelets, haemoglobin).

### Q: How is imaging used in SLE?

- Symptom-directed only — there's no routine SLE imaging.
- Examples: **CT chest** (pulmonary involvement), **echocardiogram or cardiac MRI** (cardiac involvement), **CTPA** if PE suspected from APS, **MRI brain** for neuropsychiatric lupus.

## SLE — management

```
EVERY SLE patient: HYDROXYCHLOROQUINE (backbone)
        │
        ├──► Mild disease: HCQ alone may suffice
        │
        ├──► Acute flare:  short course steroids — taper as soon as safe
        │
        ├──► More serious disease:
        │       Add immunomodulator (mycophenolate, methotrexate, azathioprine)
        │
        ├──► Renal disease (lupus nephritis):
        │       Mycophenolate ± rituximab
        │
        ├──► Persistently active:
        │       B-cell therapy — rituximab (anti-CD20) or belimumab (anti-BAFF)
        │
        ├──► Severe / life-threatening (e.g. myocarditis):
        │       IV steroids + IV cyclophosphamide ± rituximab
        │
        └──► SLE + APS (clot + persistent APL+):
                ANTICOAGULATE with warfarin
```

### Q: General principle of SLE treatment?

- Aim **remission or low disease activity** + prevent flares.
- Walk the tightrope: **control disease vs avoid iatrogenic harm** (especially steroids).
- Use immunomodulators promptly to **expedite steroid taper**.

### Q: Which drug is recommended for ALL SLE patients?

- **Hydroxychloroquine** — antimalarial, probably acts via interfering with TLR signalling.
- Backbone of SLE treatment regardless of severity.
- **Pregnancy-safe** (one of the few).

### Q: How do rituximab and belimumab differ?

- **Rituximab** = anti-CD20 → **kills B cells**.
- **Belimumab** = anti-BAFF (B-cell activating factor / BLyS) → **starves B cells of survival signal**, doesn't deplete.
- Belimumab is gentler on infection risk; rituximab is more aggressive.
- Both used for persistently active disease; mycophenolate ± rituximab is typical for renal lupus.

### Q: When do you anticoagulate in SLE?

- Persistent APL antibodies **PLUS** a clinical event = **antiphospholipid syndrome** → **warfarin**.
- APL antibodies alone (no event) → don't anticoagulate; consider aspirin in some cases.

### Q: Why is avascular necrosis a particular pitfall in lupus?

📎 Figure L2.9 — Femoral head AVN MRI showing wedge-shaped infarct.

- Steroid side effect made worse by lupus itself.
- Especially elevated in patients with **antiphospholipid antibodies** (microvascular impairment to femoral head).
- Can lead to femoral head collapse → joint replacement.

🧠 **L1 → L2 link:** the steroid SE list from RA still applies, but in lupus think *infection, osteoporosis, AVN* — and screen carefully if APL antibodies are present.

## SLE & pregnancy

### Q: Four specific pregnancy considerations in SLE.

- **APL antibodies** → miscarriage. Mitigate with **aspirin** ± **heparin (LMWH)**.
- Pregnancy ↑ haemodynamic demand → **worsens existing renal dysfunction**.
- **Ro antibodies** → cross placenta → **fetal heart block** (need enhanced fetal HR monitoring).
- Pre-pregnancy planning + remission first → best outcomes.

### Q: Which lupus drugs are teratogenic — and which are pregnancy-safe?

| Status | Drug |
|---|---|
| **Teratogenic — stop pre-conception** | Mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide, **methotrexate**, **warfarin** |
| **Safe in pregnancy** | Hydroxychloroquine, azathioprine, low-molecular-weight heparin |

⚠️ **Trap:** an SLE patient on warfarin for APS who plans pregnancy must be switched to **LMWH** before conception. Methotrexate and MMF must also be stopped.

## Other autoimmune CTDs (de-emphasised — for awareness)

📘 Lecturer explicitly said: *"Don't worry too much about other connective tissue diseases — for awareness for later clinical years."* The toggles below are **lighter** than the SLE section by design.

📎 Figure L2.10 — Sjögren's: lymphocytic infiltrate on salivary gland biopsy + Schirmer's test strip.

### Q: Sjögren's syndrome — definition + classic findings?

- **Autoimmune exocrinopathy** — lymphocytic infiltration of **lacrimal + salivary glands**.
- **Dry eyes (xerophthalmia) + dry mouth (xerostomia)** + parotid enlargement.
- Autoantibodies: **Ro+, La+**; RF often + but **CCP −** (helps distinguish from RA).
- **Schirmer's test:** filter paper under lower lid 5 min — abnormal if **<5 mm** wetting.
- "Secondary Sjögren's" if it occurs in another CTD (e.g. SLE).

📎 Figure L2.11 — Dermatomyositis: heliotrope eyelid rash + Gottron's papules on knuckles.

### Q: Inflammatory muscle disease — polymyositis vs dermatomyositis?

- Both = autoimmune muscle inflammation → **proximal muscle weakness** (± pain).
- **Polymyositis:** muscle only — **CD8 T cell** infiltrate on biopsy.
- **Dermatomyositis:** muscle + skin — **CD4 T cells + B cells** on biopsy.
- DM skin features: **heliotrope** (lilac) **rash** on eyelids/malar/nasolabial folds + **Gottron's papules** on knuckles.
- Tests: **↑ creatine kinase**, EMG abnormal, muscle biopsy definitive.
- **Dermatomyositis is associated with malignancy in 10–15%** — screen.

📎 Figure L2.12 — Scleroderma sclerodactyly + digital ischaemia.

### Q: Systemic sclerosis — limited vs diffuse?

- Both = thickened skin (dermal fibrosis) + prominent **Raynaud's** + cutaneous calcinosis + telangiectasia.
- **Limited:** skin below elbow/knee + face. **Anti-centromere Abs.** Pulmonary hypertension. Long Raynaud's history.
- **Diffuse:** skin proximal to elbow/knee. **Anti-Scl-70 Abs.** Pulmonary fibrosis, renal involvement (thrombotic microangiopathy). Short Raynaud's history.
- **CREST** = limited subtype mnemonic: **C**alcinosis, **R**aynaud's, **E**sophageal dysmotility, **S**clerodactyly, **T**elangiectasia.

## Reusable reasoning patterns

### Q: How do I work a multisystem stem that might be lupus?

🧠 **The lupus filter:**
1. Young woman (15–45, F:M 9:1), often non-White ancestry → demographic match.
2. Multiple organ systems involved — skin + joint + serositis + cytopenias + renal.
3. ESR up, CRP normal? Lupus flag (vs RA where both rise).
4. Order **ANA first** (if negative, SLE essentially excluded).
5. If ANA+, narrow with **anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, complement C3/C4**.
6. Don't forget **urine dip** every visit — lupus nephritis is silent until proteinuria appears.

### Q: How do I tell antibody-driven autoimmunity apart by what each antibody does?

🧠 **The four antibody questions:**
- *Sensitive enough to rule out?* (e.g. **ANA negative → not SLE**.)
- *Specific enough to rule in?* (e.g. **anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, anti-CCP**.)
- *Does it track disease activity?* (e.g. **only anti-dsDNA does** — RF, CCP and anti-Sm don't.)
- *Does it predict a specific complication?* (e.g. **Ro → fetal heart block**, **APL → thrombosis/miscarriage**, **CCP → erosive RA**.)

## Cross-references

🧠 **Cross-ref to L1:** *Why does RA cause bony erosions but SLE doesn't?*
RA's synovitis builds an invasive **pannus** that activates osteoclasts and releases MMPs → bone destruction visible on X-ray. SLE arthritis is driven by **immune-complex deposition** without persistent pannus, so any deformity (e.g. swan neck) is **soft-tissue/ligament-only** (Jaccoud's) and X-rays are **normal**. *See also: L1 — RA central mechanism.*

🧠 **Cross-ref to L1:** *RF/CCP in RA vs ANA/dsDNA in SLE — same job, different rules?*
RF and CCP help diagnose RA, but **don't track activity** — once positive, always positive. **Anti-dsDNA** is unique: specific for SLE AND tracks activity — fall it tracks remission, rise tracks flare. *See also: L1 — Investigations in RA.*

🧠 **Cross-ref to L1:** *Hydroxychloroquine — supporting role in RA, lead role in SLE.*
In RA: combination DMARD partner alongside methotrexate. In SLE: backbone monotherapy in mild disease, recommended for **all** patients. Same drug, different position in the ladder. *See also: L1 — DMARD ladder.*

🧠 **Cross-ref to L1:** *Steroid side effects — same list, different priorities.*
The L1 anatomical map of steroid SEs still applies. In lupus the priorities shift to **infection**, **osteoporosis**, and especially **avascular necrosis** (compounded by APL). *See also: L1 — Steroid side effects.*

## Interleaved SBAs — within-lecture and discrimination across L1

### Q: SBA 1 — A 24-year-old woman of African ancestry presents with 2 months of fatigue, joint aches, mouth ulcers and a rash across her cheeks that spares the nasolabial folds. Bloods: WCC 3.0 (low), platelets 95 (low), ESR 70, CRP 4. Most appropriate first-line antibody test?
A. Rheumatoid factor
B. Anti-CCP antibodies
C. Antinuclear antibodies (ANA)
D. Anti-dsDNA antibodies
E. Anti-Sm antibodies

**Answer: C — ANA.**
- Stem matches SLE: young woman, non-White ancestry, malar rash sparing nasolabial folds, mouth ulcers, cytopenias, ESR-CRP discordance.
- **ANA is the rule-out test** — done first because if negative, SLE essentially excluded.
- If ANA positive, then proceed to D (anti-dsDNA) and E (anti-Sm).
- A and B point to RA, which is symmetrical small-joint poly without skin/serositis features.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 30-year-old woman with known SLE develops sudden left-leg swelling. Doppler confirms DVT. She had a miscarriage 18 months ago. Anticardiolipin antibodies positive on two occasions 12 weeks apart. Most appropriate ongoing treatment?
A. Aspirin alone
B. Warfarin (anticoagulation)
C. Hydroxychloroquine alone
D. Rituximab
E. Mycophenolate mofetil

**Answer: B — Warfarin.**
- Persistent APL antibodies + clinical events (DVT and miscarriage) = **antiphospholipid syndrome**.
- Treatment: **anticoagulation with warfarin**.
- Aspirin (A) is for APL antibody positivity without thrombotic events / for reducing miscarriage risk in pregnancy.
- C, D, E target SLE itself, not the thrombotic risk.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 28-year-old woman with SLE has been stable on hydroxychloroquine and low-dose prednisolone. Her urine dip now shows 3+ protein, uPCR markedly raised, but creatinine is at her baseline. Most appropriate next investigation?
A. Repeat urine dip in 6 weeks
B. Renal ultrasound
C. Kidney biopsy
D. CT abdomen
E. Anti-dsDNA antibody titre only

**Answer: C — Kidney biopsy.**
- Persistent proteinuria in known SLE = **lupus nephritis until proven otherwise**.
- Definitive test = **kidney biopsy**.
- Creatinine remains normal until late — proteinuria is the early signal.
- E may be informative as an adjunct but biopsy is needed to grade nephritis and guide treatment.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 32-year-old woman has been newly diagnosed with SLE. She and her partner are planning to start a family in the next year. Which of her medications must be reviewed for teratogenicity before conception?
A. Hydroxychloroquine
B. Azathioprine
C. Methotrexate
D. Low-molecular-weight heparin
E. Aspirin

**Answer: C — Methotrexate.**
- **Teratogenic drugs: methotrexate, mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide, warfarin** — must be stopped pre-conception.
- A, B, D = pregnancy-safe.
- E (aspirin) is actually used to mitigate APL-related miscarriage.

### Q: SBA 5 — DISCRIMINATION — A 60-year-old woman has had progressive joint pains for 10 years. Examination shows symmetrical swan-neck deformities and reduced grip. X-rays show no bony erosions. Inflammation markers: CRP normal, ESR 60. What pattern fits this best?
A. Active rheumatoid arthritis
B. Osteoarthritis
C. Psoriatic arthritis
D. Jaccoud's arthropathy in SLE
E. Reactive arthritis

**Answer: D — Jaccoud's arthropathy in SLE.**
- Swan-neck deformity + **NO X-ray erosions** + ESR up but CRP normal = lupus pattern.
- Active RA (A) would produce **erosions** on X-ray after 10 years.
- OA (B) gives DIPJ + CMCJ involvement, not swan-neck deformities, no inflammation marker pattern.
- PsA (C) erosive at IPJs.
- ReA (E) is acute and post-infectious.

### Q: SBA 6 — DISCRIMINATION — Which autoantibody has BOTH high specificity for SLE AND correlates with disease activity over time?
A. ANA
B. Rheumatoid factor
C. Anti-dsDNA
D. Anti-Sm
E. Anti-CCP

**Answer: C — Anti-dsDNA.**
- ANA (A): sensitive but not specific.
- Anti-Sm (D): specific but **does NOT track activity**.
- RF (B) and anti-CCP (E): RA antibodies, neither tracks RA activity well either.
- **Anti-dsDNA is the unique antibody** that's both specific AND activity-tracking.

### Q: SBA 7 — DISCRIMINATION — A 35-year-old woman develops sudden positional chest pain (worse lying flat, relieved by leaning forward), Raynaud's phenomenon, mouth ulcers and fatigue. ANA strongly positive. Which complication should be excluded urgently?
A. Pulmonary fibrosis
B. Pericarditis
C. Lupus nephritis
D. Cerebral lupus
E. Avascular necrosis of hip

**Answer: B — Pericarditis.**
- Positional chest pain (worse lying flat, relieved by leaning forward) = **classic pericarditis pattern**.
- SLE causes **serositis** (pericarditis, pleuritis, peritonitis) — this matches.
- An echocardiogram is the next step.
- Other lupus complications are real but don't fit the chest-pain pattern.

### Q: SBA 8 — DISCRIMINATION — A 26-year-old woman with new-onset arthritis has ANA positive, anti-dsDNA negative, anti-Ro positive. She is planning pregnancy. Which fetal complication needs specific antenatal monitoring?
A. Neural tube defects
B. Congenital heart block
C. Cleft palate
D. Limb reduction defects
E. Intrauterine growth restriction

**Answer: B — Congenital heart block.**
- **Anti-Ro antibodies cross the placenta** → can cause **permanent fetal heart block** + transient neonatal lupus rash.
- Antenatal fetal heart-rate monitoring required.
- A, C, D are typical drug teratogenicity concerns (e.g. methotrexate).
- E is more general / multifactorial.

### Q: SBA 9 — DISCRIMINATION — Which finding most strongly supports SLE rather than RA in a young woman with symmetrical polyarthritis?
A. ESR raised and CRP raised
B. Anti-CCP positive
C. ESR raised but CRP normal
D. Erosions on hand X-ray
E. RF positive at high titre

**Answer: C — ESR raised but CRP normal.**
- Classic lupus discordance — high ESR with normal CRP.
- A, B, D, E all favour RA.
- ⭐ Lecturer-flagged distinguisher.

### Q: SBA 10 — A 29-year-old woman with SLE on long-term moderate-dose prednisolone presents with insidious onset right hip pain and limited internal rotation. X-ray initially normal. MRI shows a wedge-shaped infarct in the right femoral head. Diagnosis?
A. Septic arthritis of the hip
B. Lupus arthritis of the hip
C. Avascular necrosis of femoral head
D. Stress fracture
E. Osteoarthritis

**Answer: C — Avascular necrosis.**
- Long-term steroids + lupus + APL antibodies = high AVN risk.
- Wedge-shaped infarct in femoral head on MRI = pathognomonic.
- Worth specifically asking about APL antibody status in this patient.
