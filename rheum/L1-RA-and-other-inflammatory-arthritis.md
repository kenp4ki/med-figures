### Q: What 8 history & exam features discriminate any arthritis stem?

- Speed of onset / duration
- Worse or better with movement?
- Prolonged morning / inactivity stiffness?
- Number of joints (mono / oligo / poly)
- Size of joints (small / large)
- Spinal involvement?
- Pattern / symmetry?
- Signs of inflammation (red, warm, swollen)

### Q: How do you separate the four big arthritis categories on a single vignette?

| | OA | Immune-mediated | Crystal | Septic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Onset** | Slow (years) | Subacute (wks–mths) | Rapid | Rapid |
| **Synovial fluid** | Sterile, no cells | Sterile, cells | Sterile, cells, **crystals** | Cells, **bacteria** |
| **CRP / WCC** | Both normal | CRP ↑, WCC normal | CRP ↑/↑↑, WCC normal | CRP ↑↑, **WCC ↑** |

⭐ **Lecturer-flagged exam frame:** any joint vignette → walk this table mentally before answering.

### Q: Why is "acute hot swollen joint" treated as septic arthritis until proven otherwise?

- Missing it destroys cartilage, growth plates and joint within days.
- **Single best investigation: joint aspiration** → Gram stain + culture.
- Mx: **joint washout (lavage) + IV antibiotics**.
- Lecturer quote: *"If you don't put a needle in it, you'll put your foot in it."*

## Rheumatoid arthritis — what it is

### Q: One-sentence definition of RA?

A chronic autoimmune disease whose primary site of pathology is the **synovium**, leading to **synovitis** and (untreated) joint damage.

### Q: Where is synovium found — and what does this explain?

📎 Figure L1.3 — Three sites of synovium.

- **Synovial (diarthrodial) joints** → joint synovitis (e.g. PIPJ swelling).
- **Tenosynovium** around tendons → tenosynovitis (extensor tenosynovitis on dorsum of hand can cause incomplete finger extension from extensor tendon damage).
- **Bursae** (fluid-filled sacs) → bursitis (e.g. olecranon).
- **Pattern:** RA can present with swelling that doesn't sit over a joint — think tendon sheath or bursa.

### Q: Demographics of RA — three numbers worth knowing.

- Prevalence ~**1%**.
- F:M ~**2:1**.
- Age of onset usually **30–50s**.

### Q: Four hallmark features of established RA?

- Chronic **symmetrical polyarthritis** of small + large joints.
- Pain, swelling, **early-morning / inactivity stiffness**.
- Joint damage → **erosions** on radiographs (only late).
- Systemic disease + autoantibodies in blood.

## RA aetiology — genes × environment

### Q: How do twin studies tell us RA isn't purely genetic?

- Monozygotic concordance ~**15%**, dizygotic ~**4%**.
- MZ > DZ → genetic component is real.
- MZ ≪ 100% → environment matters too.

### Q: Two environmental triggers and the molecular reason they both matter?

- **Smoking** (lung) and ***Porphyromonas gingivalis*** (gum disease).
- Both cause **citrullination** of proteins → generate the antigen for **anti-CCP / ACPA** antibodies.
- Lecturer-flagged: the *"poor oral health"* link to RA was recognised long before the molecular mechanism was understood.

### Q: Strongest genetic risk factor for RA?

- **HLA-DR** (specifically HLA-DRβ chain amino acids 70–74 — the "shared epitope").
- **Pattern:** smoking × shared epitope = **synergistic** (multiplicative) risk, not additive.
- ~100 other loci identified by GWAS → polygenic; cumulative burden matters more than any single variant.

### Q: Why does the HLA-class signal predict whether autoantibodies appear?

📎 Figure L1.4 — HLA class 1 vs class 2 antigen presentation.

- **HLA class 1** (A, B, C — all cells) → presents to **CD8 T cells**.
- **HLA class 2** (HLA-D — APCs only: dendritic, macrophage, B cells) → presents to **CD4 T helpers**, who help **B cells** make antibody.
- AS = HLA-**B27** (class 1) → CD8-driven, **no autoantibodies**.
- RA = HLA-**DR4** (class 2) → CD4 + B cell, **autoantibodies positive (RF, CCP)**.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern (reusable):** *Class 1 association → think cytotoxic T cells, no antibodies. Class 2 association → think CD4/B cells, autoantibodies likely.* Apply this across all rheumatology HLA stems.

## RA — pattern of joint involvement

📎 Figure L1.5 — Skeleton joint distribution map (red = very common, orange = common, yellow = uncommon).

### Q: What's the textbook RA joint pattern?

- **Symmetrical polyarthritis**.
- Both small and large joints, but **almost always small joints involved** — particularly hands and feet.
- Commonest: **MCPJ, PIPJ, wrist, knee, ankle, MTPJ**.
- A single swollen knee or knee + wrist alone is **not** RA — that's mono/oligoarthritis.

### Q: How do you tell an RA hand from an OA hand at a glance?

📎 Figure L1.6 — RA vs OA hand patterns.

| | RA | OA |
|---|---|---|
| **Affected joints** | MCPJ, PIPJ, wrist | DIPJ, PIPJ, thumb CMCJ |
| **Spared** | DIPJ usually spared | **MCPJ spared** |
| **Stiffness** | Prolonged morning/inactivity | Worse with **activity** |

⚠️ **Trap:** "DIPJ involvement" in the stem points away from RA → think OA or **PsA**.

## RA — the central mechanism

📎 Figure L1.8 — Pannus formation (synovitis → bone erosion → pannus → cartilage degradation → joint space narrowing).

```
Healthy synovium (1–3 cell layer)
        │
        │  immune-mediated inflammation
        ▼
Synovitis: B cells, T cells, plasma cells, mast cells, activated macrophages
        │
        │  neovascularisation + lymphangiogenesis
        ▼
PANNUS — invasive proliferated synovial mass (acts almost like a tumour locally)
        │
        ├──► osteoclast activation     →  bone erosions, periarticular osteopenia
        ├──► matrix metalloproteases   →  cartilage loss, joint-space narrowing
        └──► further cytokine release  →  amplifies inflammation
```

### Q: What is pannus and why does it matter?

- **Pannus** = proliferated mass of inflamed synovium that invades joint tissue.
- Built from neovascularisation, lymphangiogenesis, and inflammatory cell influx.
- Mechanism for the three end-organ injuries in RA: **erosions, cartilage loss, periarticular osteopenia**.

### Q: Three cellular/molecular drivers of RA — and the drug that targets each?

📎 Figure L1.9 — Pleiotropic actions of TNF-α.

- **Autoreactive B cells** → **rituximab** (anti-CD20).
- **Autoreactive T cells** → **abatacept** (CTLA-4 fusion, blocks T-cell co-stimulation).
- **Cytokines:** **TNF-α** (dominant), IL-6, (IL-1) → **anti-TNF, anti-IL6R**.

### Q: Why does TNF-α get top billing in RA?

- Recruits inflammatory cells, drives angiogenesis/lymphangiogenesis → builds pannus.
- Activates **osteoclasts** → bone erosions.
- Triggers **matrix metalloproteases** → cartilage loss.
- Amplifies further cytokine release → cytokine imbalance entrenched.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern:** the rheumatoid drug ladder mirrors the cellular/molecular drivers. If a stem asks "which drug for this mechanism?", map the mechanism onto the cell/cytokine, then read off the drug.

## RA — extra-articular features

### Q: What systemic features of RA might appear in a stem?

- **Systemic:** fatigue (very common), low-grade fever, weight loss.
- **Lung:** nodules, **interstitial lung disease/fibrosis**, pleuritis.
- **Eye:** episcleritis.
- **Vasculitis** → digital ischaemia.
- Peripheral neuropathies.
- Nodules (~30% of patients — see below).

### Q: What is Felty's syndrome?

- Triad: **splenomegaly + leukopenia + RA**.
- Rare in the modern biologic era.
- Worth recognising in MCQ stems pairing RA with cytopenias and big spleen.

### Q: Where do you look for rheumatoid nodules — and what do they confirm?

📎 Figure L1.7 — Rheumatoid nodules (ulnar border of forearm + hand PIPJs).

- **Ulnar border of forearm**, just proximal to the elbow — the classic site.
- Also extensor surfaces, back of fingers (around PIPJ).
- Histology: central **fibrinoid necrosis** ringed by histiocytes + connective tissue.
- Present in ~**30%** of RA patients.
- ⭐ **High-yield association:** invariably linked to **rheumatoid factor positivity** and to more severe disease + extra-articular manifestations.

## RA — investigations

### Q: Bloods you'd order and what each tells you?

- **ESR ↑, CRP ↑** — non-specific inflammation.
- **Normocytic anaemia, ↑ platelets** — chronic-inflammation pattern (not always present).
- **Rheumatoid factor (RF):** antibody that binds IgG. Positive in many other conditions and ~5% healthy → **not specific**.
- **Anti-CCP (ACPA):** **most specific** for RA, associates with **more aggressive, erosive** disease.

⭐ **Single best test in an SBA:** anti-CCP antibodies if the question is "which blood test for RA?".

### Q: What does the "preclinical autoimmunity" data tell us about ACPA?

📎 Figure L1.10 — ACPA sensitivity in stored blood samples vs years before symptoms.

- ACPA positive a median **4.5 years before first clinical symptoms**.
- Concept: **genetic predisposition → preclinical autoimmunity (asymptomatic) → tissue inflammation/injury (symptoms)**.
- Implication: by the time we see them in clinic, they're already deep in the journey — **treat early**.

### Q: Imaging in RA — what each test shows and when to use it?

📎 Figure L1.11 — Established RA hand X-ray (erosions, periarticular osteopenia).
📎 Figure L1.12 — Ultrasound MCPJ: gray-scale synovial thickening + power Doppler signal.

- **X-ray:** soft-tissue swelling, periarticular osteopenia, **erosions** — but only late. Aim of modern Mx is to **prevent X-rays from ever looking like this**.
- **Ultrasound (best test for early disease):** synovial thickening, **Doppler signal** (= active blood flow = active synovitis), can pick up erosions before X-ray.
- **MRI:** equivalent to US, but expensive and slow.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern:** if the stem hinges on detecting early/active synovitis → **ultrasound**. If it's about chronic damage already done → X-ray.

## RA — management

```
Diagnosis confirmed (clinical + RF/CCP + US/X-ray)
        │
        ▼
FIRST LINE:  combination DMARDs
             Methotrexate + hydroxychloroquine ± sulfasalazine
             PLUS short course of oral steroids OR IM methylprednisolone
        │
        │  If disease still active (DAS28 not suppressed)
        ▼
SECOND LINE: BIOLOGICAL therapy
             Anti-TNF first (infliximab / adalimumab / etanercept …)
             — or anti-IL6R, rituximab, abatacept
```

### Q: Treatment goal in RA — and the equation that drives urgency?

- **Goal:** prevent joint damage.
- **Joint destruction = inflammation × time** → every week of uncontrolled disease adds permanent damage.
- Requires **early recognition, prompt referral, aggressive pharmacological treatment**.

### Q: First-line drug regimen in UK RA practice?

- **Combination DMARDs:** methotrexate + hydroxychloroquine ± sulfasalazine.
- **PLUS** short course oral prednisolone OR IM methylprednisolone to settle inflammation.
- DMARDs = disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (immunomodulators that halt/slow disease).

### Q: Where do NSAIDs fit in modern RA management?

- Historically central, now **largely peripheral**.
- Provide partial symptom relief but **do NOT prevent disease progression**.
- Unfavourable long-term SE profile (peptic ulcer, renal, cardiovascular).
- Don't pick "ibuprofen" as the answer for disease control.

### Q: What is "treat-to-target" and what is DAS28?

- **Concept:** suppress disease activity to a numeric target → escalate treatment if not met.
- **DAS28** = composite score of: tender joint count + swollen joint count + patient VAS + ESR (or CRP), measured across 28 joints.
- If DAS28 not suppressed → escalate (e.g. add anti-TNF biologic).

### Q: Two main families of biological therapy in RA?

📎 Figure L1.14 — Abatacept blocks T-cell co-stimulation.

- **Cytokine-targeted:**
  - Anti-TNF: **antibodies** (infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab, certolizumab) + **fusion protein** (etanercept).
  - Anti-IL-6 receptor: tocilizumab, sarilumab.
- **Lymphocyte-targeted:**
  - **Rituximab** = anti-CD20 → depletes peripheral B cells (2 IV infusions, 2 wks apart, repopulate ~6–9 months). SE: infusion reactions, infection, **hypogammaglobulinaemia**.
  - **Abatacept** = CTLA-4-Fc fusion → blocks T-cell co-stimulation **signal 2** (CD80/86 ↔ CD28). T cells need 2 signals to activate; abatacept blocks the second.

### Q: Why are steroids "many and bad" — yet still useful?

📎 Figure L1.13 — Steroid SE anatomical map.

- Bind cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptor → translocate to nucleus → bind DNA response elements → broad anti-inflammatory transcriptional effects.
- **Useful acutely** to bridge while DMARDs kick in.
- **Avoid long-term** — Cushing's syndrome features:

| System | Side effect |
|---|---|
| Metabolic / vascular | T2DM, ↑BP, weight gain, central obesity |
| Bone / muscle | **Osteoporosis**, myopathy |
| Skin / eye / mood | Skin thinning, cataracts, mood disturbance |
| Immune | ↑ infection risk |

⚠️ **Trap:** in a patient with chronic inflammation already thinning their bones, steroid-driven osteoporosis is a particularly bad combination.

🧠 **L1 → L2 link:** lupus borrows the same steroid SE profile, with an extra emphasis on **avascular necrosis of the femoral head** — risk especially elevated when antiphospholipid antibodies are present. *See also: L2 — Avascular necrosis as a steroid (and lupus-specific) complication.*

## Seronegative inflammatory arthritis — atlas

### Q: What does "seronegative" mean — and which conditions belong here?

- **Seronegative** = no RF, no CCP in serum.
- Still **immune-mediated** but B cells less central.
- Family: **psoriatic arthritis, reactive arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis** (covered in Back Pain lecture), and IBD-associated arthritis (**not tested in exam — lecturer-flagged**).

### Q: Psoriatic arthritis — five things to know.

📎 Figure L1.15 — Psoriasis: extensor plaque, nail pitting, onycholysis.
📎 Figure L1.16 — PsA: asymmetrical IPJ erosions, MCPJs spared, dactylitis.

- ~10% of psoriasis patients also get joint inflammation.
- **Dominant pathway: IL-17 / IL-23** (different from RA).
- **Asymmetrical** arthritis classically affecting **IPJs** (including DIPJ) — **MCPJs not affected** (unlike RA).
- **Enthesitis** (inflammation of tendon insertions) → **dactylitis ("sausage finger")**.
- Skin severity does **not** correlate with joint severity — examine carefully (scalp, umbilicus, nails). **Nail pitting / onycholysis** may be the only manifestation.

⚠️ **Trap:** PsA can mimic RA (symmetrical small-joint pattern) — look for the skin/nail clue or DIPJ involvement to make the call.

### Q: Reactive arthritis — what defines it?

- **Sterile** inflammation in joints **following** infection elsewhere (NOT infection in the joint).
- Classic precipitants:
  - **Urogenital:** *Chlamydia trachomatis*.
  - **GI:** *Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter*.
- Symptoms typically **1–4 weeks after** the infection (which may have been mild and forgotten).
- Common in young adults with **HLA-B27**.
- Extra-articular: enthesitis, skin and eye inflammation.
- May be **first manifestation of HIV or hepatitis C** → consider those tests.

### Q: How do you separate reactive arthritis from septic arthritis on a vignette?

| | Septic | Reactive |
|---|---|---|
| **Synovial fluid culture** | Positive | **Sterile** |
| **Joint pattern** | Monoarthritis | Asymmetrical large-joint **oligo**arthritis |
| **Antibiotics** | Yes — IV | No (for joint); maybe for underlying STI |
| **Joint lavage** | Yes | No |

⭐ **Tutorial pattern (L3a Case 3):** afebrile + normal WCC + modestly raised CRP + history of food poisoning 3 weeks ago + asymmetrical large-joint oligo-arthritis = **reactive**, not septic.

### Q: Where does ankylosing spondylitis sit in this family?

- Chronic spinal + sacroiliac joint inflammation → spinal fusion + deformity.
- Site of inflammation = **enthesis** (insertion of tendon/ligament into bone), not synovium.
- **No autoantibodies** (seronegative).
- HLA-**B27** strongly associated.
- Detail covered in the Back Pain lecture.

🧠 **Reasoning pattern across seronegatives:** *enthesitis-dominant, asymmetrical, HLA-B27-linked, no autoantibodies.* If a stem has these features, you're in the seronegative family — then look for skin (PsA), preceding infection (ReA), or spine (AS) to pick the specific one.

## Reusable reasoning patterns

### Q: How should I work any arthritis vignette?

🧠 **The walk-the-tree method:**
1. Inflamed or not? (red, hot, swollen, prolonged morning stiffness → inflammatory)
2. If inflammatory: speed of onset → rapid (septic/crystal) vs subacute (autoimmune).
3. Number + pattern of joints → mono/oligo (septic, crystal, ReA) vs symmetric poly small-joint (RA, lupus) vs asymmetric (PsA, ReA).
4. Autoantibody status → seropositive (RA) vs seronegative.
5. Extra-articular clues → skin (psoriasis/lupus), eye (uveitis/episcleritis), GI/GU (ReA precipitant).
6. Map onto the right branch of L1.2.

### Q: When the stem asks "next investigation", which test wins for which scenario?

- Acute hot mono-arthritis → **joint aspiration** (Gram + culture).
- Suspected RA, want to detect early synovitis → **ultrasound hands**.
- Suspected RA, want to confirm diagnosis on bloods → **anti-CCP**.
- Inflammatory back pain in young patient, suspect AS → **MRI spine + SI joints** (avoids irradiation; X-ray would only show late changes).
- Suspect PsA but no obvious psoriasis → examine **scalp, umbilicus, nails** before any test.

## Cross-references

🧠 **Cross-ref to L2:** *Why is RA arthritis erosive but lupus arthritis is not?*
RA's synovitis builds an invasive **pannus** that releases MMPs and activates osteoclasts — leading to bony erosions on X-ray. Lupus arthritis is driven by immune complex deposition without sustained pannus, so the joint deformities (e.g. swan neck) are due to **soft tissue / ligament damage only** (Jaccoud's arthropathy) and the X-ray is normal. *See also: L2 — General principles of autoimmune CTDs.*

🧠 **Cross-ref to L2:** *Why is the ESR–CRP relationship different between RA and SLE?*
In RA both rise in active disease (concordant). In SLE you classically see **high ESR but normal CRP** — when CRP is high in a lupus patient, suspect **infection** (or serositis / arthritis). *See also: L2 — SLE investigations.*

🧠 **Cross-ref to L2:** *Methotrexate is RA's mainstay — but when must it be stopped?*
MTX is **teratogenic** — must be stopped before conception. Hydroxychloroquine and azathioprine are pregnancy-safe alternatives. *See also: L2 — SLE & pregnancy drug considerations.*

🧠 **Cross-ref to L2:** *Rituximab in RA vs lupus — same drug, different position in the ladder.*
In RA: second-line biologic if anti-TNF fails. In SLE: used for **renal disease** and **persistently active** disease. Same mechanism (anti-CD20 B-cell depletion), same dosing logic. *See also: L2 — SLE management ladder.*

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 32-year-old woman has a 3-month history of symmetrical pain and stiffness in MCPJs, PIPJs and wrists, worse in the mornings and lasting 90 minutes. Examination shows synovitis at the MCPJs and wrists. Which single blood test is most specific for the likely diagnosis?
A. ESR
B. Rheumatoid factor
C. Anti-CCP antibodies
D. ANA
E. Anti-dsDNA

**Answer: C — Anti-CCP antibodies.**
- Stem is textbook RA: symmetrical polyarthritis, MCPJ + PIPJ + wrist, prolonged morning stiffness, age + sex match.
- ESR (A) shows inflammation but not specific.
- RF (B) supports but is positive in many other conditions and in healthy people.
- ANA (D) and anti-dsDNA (E) point to lupus, not RA.
- **CCP** is most specific for RA and predicts more aggressive/erosive disease.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 28-year-old man presents with a hot, painful, swollen left knee 24 hours after returning from a hiking trip. Temperature 38.4°C, WCC 15. Which single next investigation is most important?
A. Plain X-ray of knee
B. MRI of knee
C. Joint aspiration with Gram stain and culture
D. ESR and CRP
E. Anti-CCP antibodies

**Answer: C — Joint aspiration with Gram stain and culture.**
- Acute hot swollen joint = **septic arthritis until proven otherwise**.
- Lecturer-flagged single best answer.
- Imaging (A, B) doesn't change immediate management and delays diagnosis.
- D would just confirm inflammation.
- E is irrelevant at this acuity.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 42-year-old woman with established seropositive RA has been on methotrexate + hydroxychloroquine for 12 months but DAS28 remains elevated, with persistently swollen MCPJs. What is the most appropriate next step?
A. Add long-term oral prednisolone
B. Add ibuprofen
C. Switch to sulfasalazine monotherapy
D. Add an anti-TNF-α biologic
E. Refer for joint replacement

**Answer: D — Add an anti-TNF biologic.**
- Failure of first-line combination DMARDs with active disease = escalate to **second-line biologic**, anti-TNF first.
- Long-term steroid (A) avoided due to SE profile.
- NSAIDs (B) don't modify disease.
- Stepping down to monotherapy (C) is the wrong direction.
- Surgery (E) is rarely needed with modern medical Mx.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 35-year-old man develops asymmetrical pain and swelling of the L knee and L wrist 3 weeks after an episode of bloody diarrhoea. He is afebrile, WCC normal, CRP 35. Synovial aspirate: inflammatory cells but no organisms. Most likely diagnosis?
A. Rheumatoid arthritis
B. Septic arthritis
C. Reactive arthritis
D. Gout
E. Psoriatic arthritis

**Answer: C — Reactive arthritis.**
- Classic timing (1–4 weeks after GI infection — *Salmonella/Shigella/Campylobacter* territory).
- Asymmetrical large-joint oligoarthritis pattern.
- Sterile synovial fluid + afebrile + normal WCC = NOT septic.
- RA (A) would be symmetrical small-joint poly.
- Gout (D) would show crystals; not the right age/sex demographic alone.
- PsA (E) needs psoriasis or nail changes.

### Q: SBA 5 — A 45-year-old man with longstanding plaque psoriasis develops asymmetrical arthritis affecting his right DIPJs and a swollen second toe (whole digit). RF and anti-CCP negative. Most likely diagnosis?
A. Rheumatoid arthritis
B. Reactive arthritis
C. Osteoarthritis
D. Psoriatic arthritis
E. Gout

**Answer: D — Psoriatic arthritis.**
- Psoriasis history + **DIPJ** involvement + **dactylitis** ("sausage toe", from enthesitis) + seronegative = textbook PsA.
- RA (A): symmetrical, MCPJ + PIPJ, RF/CCP positive.
- ReA (B): preceding GU/GI infection, no skin disease.
- OA (C): chronic, no inflammatory pattern, single sausage toe atypical.
- Gout (E): would expect crystal-confirming aspirate; can affect first MTPJ.

### Q: SBA 6 — Which of the following is the strongest genetic risk factor for rheumatoid arthritis?
A. HLA-B27
B. HLA-DR (shared epitope)
C. PTPN22
D. IL-6 receptor variants
E. Rheumatoid factor positivity

**Answer: B — HLA-DR shared epitope.**
- Class 2 HLA association → CD4 + B-cell driven → fits autoantibody-positive RA.
- HLA-B27 (A) is class 1 → AS, not RA.
- PTPN22 (C) and IL-6R (D) are real but modest-effect loci.
- RF (E) is a serological marker, not a genetic risk factor.

### Q: SBA 7 — A 50-year-old woman with active RA on methotrexate is being considered for biologic therapy. Her clinician chooses a drug that depletes B cells. Which drug?
A. Infliximab
B. Tocilizumab
C. Rituximab
D. Abatacept
E. Etanercept

**Answer: C — Rituximab.**
- Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody → peripheral B-cell depletion (2 IV infusions, 2 wks apart, repopulate 6–9 months).
- Infliximab (A) and etanercept (E) = anti-TNF.
- Tocilizumab (B) = anti-IL6R.
- Abatacept (D) = blocks T-cell co-stimulation (signal 2).

### Q: SBA 8 — Which feature on examination most strongly suggests RA over OA in a 60-year-old woman with painful hands?
A. Heberden's nodes at the DIPJs
B. Synovial swelling at the MCPJs and wrists
C. Pain worse with activity, relieved by rest
D. Squaring of the thumb base
E. Joint crepitus

**Answer: B — Synovial swelling at MCPJs and wrists.**
- MCPJ involvement + synovitis = RA territory; MCPJs are spared in OA.
- Heberden's nodes (A) at DIPJs and squaring of the thumb CMCJ (D) = OA pattern.
- Activity-related pain (C) and crepitus (E) = OA.
