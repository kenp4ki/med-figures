# Lecture 1 — Diagnosis

## The unifying idea

### Q: What is the single chain of reasoning that generates every dementia diagnosis?

- **Symptom → Region → Pathology → Dementia**
- Plus: rule out **mimics** (delirium, depression, reversible causes) before committing
- Every investigation answers one of three questions: *reversible?*, *which region?*, *which pathology?*

### Q: 🟦 Reusable reasoning pattern — the mimic ladder

- Work top-down before labelling anyone with dementia
- 1. Delirium (acute + fluctuating + precipitant)
- 2. Depression ("I don't know" answers, biological features)
- 3. Reversible medical (TFTs, B12, HIV, syphilis, Wilson's)
- 4. Rapidly progressing dementia (weeks-to-months → CJD, limbic encephalitis)
- 5. Only now — which neurodegenerative dementia

## The clinical workflow

```
Referral → History → Examination → Investigations → Diagnosis → Management
              ↑          ↑              ↑              ↑           ↓
              └──────────┴──────────────┴──────────────┴───────────┘
                     iterative over 6 months – 2 years
```

### Q: What is the definition of dementia?

- **Severe loss of memory and other cognitive abilities leading to impaired daily function, regardless of the underlying cause**
- It is a **syndrome**, not a specific disease
- 🟨 The "regardless of cause" phrase is the examinable bit — distinguishes dementia-the-syndrome from any single aetiology

### Q: Why is every Alzheimer's diagnosis in life labelled "probable"?

- Definitive diagnosis requires **post-mortem histology**
- In life we infer from pattern + imaging + biomarkers → highest confidence = "probable"
- Criteria are evolving with in-vivo biomarkers but "probable" remains the exam answer

### Q: Why is the workflow iterative, not a single visit?

- Dementias decline slowly and subtly
- Snapshot diagnosis is unreliable — need to see successive change
- Typical confident diagnosis requires **6 months to 2 years** of follow-up

## History (the most important box)

### Q: Why does the slide put "+ collateral" in red on the interview checklist?

- Patients with dementia **lose insight** — they genuinely don't perceive deficits
- Relying on the patient's account alone misses the diagnosis
- Family/partner describes actual functional decline over time
- 🟨 Lecturer's ranking: **collateral > patient > exam > neuropsych > scans**

### Q: Recall the four domains of the history checklist

- **Cognitive core** — memory, language, numerical skills, executive skills
- **Perceptual/spatial** — visuospatial, neglect, visual perception, route finding
- **Behavioural/social** — personality, conduct, sexual behaviour, eating, mood, motivation, anxiety, delusions, hallucinations
- **Functional** — activities of daily living
- Plus **chronology** of each

### Q: Why are numerical skills examined in dementia assessment?

- Failure of numerical skills → inability to manage finances
- 🟥 Patients become vulnerable to **financial exploitation** — may need lasting power of attorney
- Unpaid bills / unexplained transfers in a stem = dementia red flag

### Q: What is the head-turning sign?

- Patient turns head to look at companion for answers to direct questions
- Mechanism: repeated self-failure → loss of confidence → external verification-seeking
- Bedside sign of **mild cognitive impairment / early AD**
- Not diagnostic on its own but highly examinable (see Case 1)

### Q: Distinguish the three Ds — delirium vs dementia vs depression

| Feature | Delirium | Depression | Dementia |
|---|---|---|---|
| Onset | Hours–days (acute) | Weeks–months | Months–years (insidious) |
| Course | Fluctuates | Persistent low mood | Progressive |
| Key clue | Precipitant (UTI, drugs) | Biological features, "I don't know" | Functional decline over time |
| Reversible? | Yes — treat cause | Yes — treat depression | No (but mimics yes) |

### Q: 🟥 Why is depression often called "pseudodementia"?

- Severe depression in elderly mimics AD: apathy, poor concentration, memory complaints
- Clue: relatively **faster onset**, biological depression features, patient says "I don't know" rather than confabulating
- **Reversible on treatment** — SSRI/SNRI or CBT, cognition often returns

### Q: What are the characteristic hallucination types for each dementia?

- **DLB** — visual, people or animals, well-formed, often non-threatening
- **Alcohol-related** — Lilliputian (tiny people/objects)
- AD — delusions (usually of theft), less commonly hallucinations
- FTD — not typical unless late

## Examination

### Q: Name three frontal release signs and what they indicate

- **Pout reflex** — lips purse when upper lip tapped
- **Root reflex** — head turns toward cheek stroke
- **Utilisation behaviour** — compulsively uses objects placed in front
- All are normal in infants, suppressed by mature frontal cortex
- Re-emergence = **frontal lobe dysfunction** (→ FTD)

### Q: What is the Luria three-stage sequence and what does it test?

- Patient copies hand sequence: fist → edge → palm (or similar three-part motor sequence)
- Tests **frontal lobe set-shifting / executive function**
- FTD patients are slow or fail; AD patients usually succeed early on

## Investigations

### Q: Recall the MMSE scoring bands

- **24–30** — no cognitive impairment
- **18–23** — mild cognitive impairment
- **0–17** — severe cognitive impairment
- 🟥 MMSE is **memory-weighted** — FTD can score normal early; a normal MMSE does NOT exclude dementia

📎 Figure L12.7 — MMSE scoring form and thresholds

### Q: How much cognitive benefit do acetylcholinesterase inhibitors provide?

- **ΔMMSE ≈ 1 point over 1–2 years**
- Symptomatic only — no disease modification
- Named example: **donepezil**
- Indicated for AD and DLB; unhelpful (and potentially harmful) in FTD

### Q: What does a dementia blood panel screen for, and why?

- **Routine** — FBC, CRP, U&E, TFTs, glucose, clotting → infection, renal failure, hypothyroidism, diabetes
- **Vitamins** — B12 and folate → reversible cognitive impairment
- **Rarer** — syphilis serology, HIV, caeruloplasmin (Wilson's)
- 🟨 Purpose verbatim: "rule out reversible causes" — always the first layer of workup

### Q: What are the four key structural MRI findings in Alzheimer's?

- **Narrowed gyri** (atrophic ridges)
- **Widened sulci** (grooves expand as tissue shrinks)
- **Dilated ventricles** (ex-vacuo — CSF fills retreated brain space)
- **Bilateral medial temporal / hippocampal atrophy** — hippocampi replaced by CSF (black on T1)
- Lecturer names **CA1 subfield** specifically

📎 Figure L12.8 — sMRI progression: cognitively normal → aMCI → AD
📎 Figure L12.14 — Healthy elderly vs AD patient (hippocampal atrophy visible)

### Q: What tracer is used in amyloid PET and what does it target?

- **¹⁸F-florbetapir** — a radioactive tracer that binds amyloid-β plaques
- Measured as **SUVR** (standardised uptake value ratio)
- SUVR 0.87 → minimal plaque; 1.17 → moderate; 1.68 → heavy
- 🟨 Most clinically useful when **negative** — rules AD out

📎 Figure L12.9 — ¹⁸F-Florbetapir PET vs amyloid post-mortem (Clark 2011)

### Q: What are the CSF biomarker patterns in Alzheimer's, and why?

- **CSF β-amyloid (1–42) LOW** — amyloid is trapped in brain plaques, less reaches CSF
- **CSF tau HIGH** — dying neurons release tau into CSF
- 🟦 Mechanism memory aid: *"Amyloid goes down because it's stuck. Tau goes up because it's leaked."*

📎 Figure L12.18 — CSF biomarkers in AD: Aβ low, tau high

### Q: What is the biomarker sequence in Alzheimer's disease?

- **Amyloid → Tau → Brain structure → Cognition**
- 🟨 Amyloid is the **earliest** detectable biomarker, often abnormal years before symptoms
- **Cognition lags last** — by the time patient is symptomatic, pathology has been accumulating for a decade

📎 Figure L12.16 — Biomarker sequence in AD (Jack 2010)

### Q: What is the A/T/N framework?

- Modern diagnostic classification for AD (transcript-only, not on slides)
- **A**myloid + **T**au + **N**eurodegeneration
- Single biomarker not enough — want all three to commit to AD diagnosis

## Management

### Q: What are the five management strategies in dementia?

- **Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors** (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) — AD and DLB
- **Watch and wait** — because diagnosis needs serial assessment
- **Treat behavioural/psychological symptoms** — antidepressants, cautious antipsychotics
- **OT / social services** — home adaptations, carer assessment, LPA
- **Specialist therapies** — speech therapy (FTD), cognitive stimulation therapy

# Lecture 2 — Cases

## Territory map — four dementias in parallel

### Q: What is the master pairing of protein-region-dementia?

| Dementia | Protein | Region first hit | Lead symptom |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AD** | Amyloid-β + tau | Medial temporal lobe | Short-term memory loss |
| **DLB** | α-synuclein (Lewy body) | Substantia nigra + cortex | Fluctuation + visual hallucinations + parkinsonism |
| **FTD** | Tau or TDP-43 | Frontal + anterior temporal | Personality/language change |
| **VaD** | None — ischaemia | Wherever infarcts landed | Stepwise decline + vascular risk |

### Q: 🟦 Reusable reasoning pattern — region-to-dementia mapping

- **Memory + getting lost on familiar routes** → hippocampus → AD
- **Hallucinations + fluctuation + parkinsonism** → substantia nigra + cortex → DLB
- **Personality/behaviour OR language change** → frontal/anterior temporal → FTD
- **Stepwise + vascular risks + focal signs** → anywhere vascular → VaD
- Young + visuospatial-predominant → posterior cortex → posterior cortical atrophy (AD variant)

### Q: 🟨 Why does the lecturer repeat "mixed pathology is normal"?

- Most real-world dementia, especially in patients over 80, is mixed
- Commonest combination = **AD + VaD**
- Clinical picture rarely fits one clean template
- Treatment targets the dominant component (usually AD → AChEI) plus vascular risk management

📎 Figure L12.6 — Mixed pathology in an Alzheimer brain on MRI

## Case card — Alzheimer's disease

### Q: What is the typical AD case stem (Case 1)?

- 70y♂, wife brings him to clinic, patient says he's fine
- Family reports: repetitive questioning, medication errors, lost driving to son's house, watching same TV twice, irritable
- **Head-turning sign** on examination
- MMSE shows episodic memory deficits for recently learned material
- MRI: bilateral medial temporal atrophy, dilated ventricles

📎 Figure L12.10 — AD patient coronal T1 MRI

### Q: What is episodic memory and why is it affected first in AD?

- Memory for **particular episodes in life** (what you had for breakfast, where you went yesterday)
- Depends on **medial temporal lobe structures** — hippocampus + entorhinal cortex
- AD pathology (tau tangles) begins here → episodic memory fails first
- Old memories (consolidated) often preserved well into the disease

### Q: What is the Thal/Braak pattern of AD pathology spread?

- **Amyloid (Thal I–V)**: neocortex → allocortex → striatum/thalamus → brainstem → cerebellum
- **Tau (Braak I–VI)**: transentorhinal → limbic (hippocampus) → isocortex
- Tau starts near hippocampus → spreads outward → explains why memory fails first and executive/language later

📎 Figure L12.15 — Thal amyloid and Braak tau staging

### Q: What is the AD treatment ladder?

- **Mild–moderate** — AChEI (donepezil first-line; rivastigmine, galantamine alternatives)
- **Moderate–severe** — add **memantine** (NMDA antagonist)
- Plus — OT, social services, lasting power of attorney, driving assessment
- 🟨 Expected benefit: ~1 MMSE point over 1–2 years; no disease modification

## Case card — Dementia with Lewy bodies

### Q: What is the typical DLB case stem (Case 2)?

- 78y♂ with son, "disorientated some days but fine on others"
- Memory **not** the lead complaint
- MMSE 28/30 but errors on **attention** (serial 7s, DLROW)
- On leaving clinic: daily visual hallucinations of children/people, non-threatening
- Follow-up: **shuffling gait, reduced facial expression** (parkinsonism)
- Hallucinations improved on **rivastigmine**

### Q: What are the core clinical features of DLB?

- **Fluctuating cognition** (waxes and wanes over hours–days)
- **Visual hallucinations** — people or animals, well-formed, often non-threatening
- **Parkinsonism** (shuffling gait, masked facies, bradykinesia)
- **REM sleep behaviour disorder** — patient acts out dreams (lashing out, shouting)
- **High falls risk** — combination of parkinsonism + fluctuation

### Q: 🟨 What is the one-year rule for DLB vs Parkinson's disease dementia?

- **Cognitive impairment before or within 1 year of parkinsonism → DLB**
- Parkinsonism first, dementia 5+ years later → Parkinson's disease dementia
- Same α-synuclein pathology, different label based on **timing**

### Q: Describe the α-synuclein aggregation cascade

```
α-synuclein monomer → oligomer → fibril
                                   ↓
                         + neurofilaments
                         + ubiquitin
                         + αβ-crystallin
                                   ↓
                              Lewy body
```

- Found in cortical neurons and substantia nigra
- Substantia nigra involvement → presynaptic dopamine loss → parkinsonism + DaTscan abnormality

### Q: What are the imaging discriminators between AD and DLB?

| Imaging | AD | DLB |
|---|---|---|
| MRI hippocampi | Bilateral atrophy, CSF replacement | **Relatively preserved** |
| DaTscan (¹²³I-FP-CIT SPECT) | Bright commas (caudate + putamen intact) | **Dot-like**, tails faded |
| Specificity of DaTscan for DLB | — | ~85% |

📎 Figure L12.20 — AD vs DLB: MRI and ¹²³I-FP-CIT SPECT

### Q: Why does a DaTscan discriminate DLB from AD?

- DaTscan tracer binds presynaptic **dopamine transporters** in striatum
- DLB: α-synuclein kills substantia nigra neurons → presynaptic dopamine loss → no tracer binding → dots
- AD: substantia nigra spared → intact dopaminergic projections → normal comma pattern
- 🟦 Test works because it's specific to the *mechanism* differentiating the two diseases

### Q: 🟥 What drug class must be avoided in DLB and why?

- **Typical antipsychotics** (especially haloperidol) — essentially contraindicated
- DLB patients have severe **neuroleptic sensitivity** from dopaminergic denervation
- Further dopamine blockade → life-threatening rigidity, autonomic instability, worsened parkinsonism
- If antipsychotic truly needed → atypical (quetiapine) at lowest dose

### Q: Why does rivastigmine help DLB hallucinations?

- DLB has **profound cholinergic deficit** — often worse than AD
- Boosting ACh with AChEI helps cognition AND reduces visual hallucinations
- Avoids the neuroleptic sensitivity risk of antipsychotics
- Case 2 demonstrates this clinical response

## Case card — Frontotemporal dementia

### Q: What is the typical FTD case stem (Case 3)?

- 72y♂, initial MMSE high → dementia initially dismissed
- Later: **speech errors** ("Where's my blue chatter?" for sweater), agrammatical
- Behavioural: rude, abruptly leaves meals, obsessive about bread-and-butter pudding, hygiene decline, still driving
- Exam: paraphasic errors, neologisms, anomia, **positive pout + root reflexes**, utilisation behaviour
- MRI: extensive temporal + frontal opercula volume loss, worse on the right

### Q: 🟥 Why is a normal MMSE a trap for FTD?

- MMSE is **memory-weighted**; FTD leaves memory relatively spared early
- Patient can score 27+ while profoundly affected behaviourally/linguistically
- If stem describes personality change in a 50s–60s patient with normal MMSE → think FTD
- Use **ACE-III** — more sensitive to FTD and specifically differentiates AD from FTD

### Q: Recall the three FTD subtypes

| Variant | Fails first | Stem cue |
|---|---|---|
| **Behavioural (bvFTD)** | Social conduct, inhibition | Rude, disinhibited, stereotyped behaviour, hygiene decline |
| **Progressive non-fluent aphasia (PNFA)** | Speech production | Agrammatical, halting, paraphasic errors, neologisms, anomia |
| **Semantic dementia** | Word meaning, object recognition | Fluent but empty speech, points to hippo for "bird" |

### Q: What is the FTD MRI signature?

- **Frontal + anterior temporal lobe atrophy**, typically **asymmetric**
- **Perisylvian volume loss** — widens the sylvian fissure around language areas
- Case 3 MRI was worse on the right
- Broca's (frontal side of sylvian fissure) + Wernicke's (temporal side) both sit here

📎 Figure L12.21 — FTD on MRI: perisylvian volume loss

### Q: What is the examinable FTD gene?

- **C9orf72** — pathological hexanucleotide repeat expansion
- Commonest genetic cause of familial FTD (and familial ALS — overlap syndrome)
- **Autosomal dominant**
- ~25% of FTD cases are familial
- Family history of "brain disease in 60s/70s" → consider genetic counselling + testing

### Q: What is the FTD treatment strategy?

- **No disease-specific pharmacotherapy** — AChEIs don't help and may worsen behaviour
- Treat behaviour symptomatically — SSRIs for compulsive behaviours, cautious antipsychotics for aggression
- Speech therapy for language variants
- Early OT + social services + LPA — capacity erodes early
- Driving assessment (Case 3 was still driving, which is unsafe)

## Case card — Vascular dementia

### Q: What is the vascular dementia signature?

- **Stepwise decline** — sudden drops followed by plateaus (not a smooth slope)
- **Vascular risk factors** — HTN, AF, DM, smoking, prior stroke/TIA
- **Focal neurological signs** — hemiparesis, extensor plantars, dysarthria
- MRI: multiple infarcts + white matter ischaemic change
- 🟨 **"Stepwise" in a stem = VaD** — near-deterministic trigger word

### Q: What is the treatment approach for vascular dementia?

- **No specific cognitive medication** — cornerstone is vascular risk management
- BP control, statin, antiplatelet (or anticoagulation if AF), glycaemic control
- Lifestyle: smoking cessation, exercise, diet
- AChEIs sometimes used if mixed AD/VaD picture
- Focus: **prevent the next event**

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: 🟦 Reusable reasoning pattern — choosing the next best investigation

```
Step 1: Mimics excluded? → No: BLOODS
Step 2: Region identified? → No: sMRI
Step 3: Pathology confirmed?
  Suspect AD → amyloid PET or CSF
  Suspect DLB → DaTscan
  Suspect FTD → MRI usually sufficient; genetic testing if familial
  Suspect VaD → MRI usually sufficient
```

### Q: What is the preserved-hippocampus paradox?

- Students default to "look for atrophy" when told "dementia MRI"
- Sometimes the diagnosis is in what's **NOT atrophied**
- Preserved hippocampi in a demented patient → **DLB clue**, not a reassuring finding
- Reinforces: always read the imaging against the clinical pattern

### Q: What are the young-onset dementia considerations?

- Cut-off = **under 65**
- AD still commonest but proportion of FTD, VaD, alcohol-related and familial AD is much higher
- **FTD is the commonest young-onset dementia** (not AD)
- Consider genetic testing: APP/presenilin (fAD), C9orf72 (FTD/ALS)

📎 Figure L12.1 — YOD vs LOD aetiology proportions

### Q: What is posterior cortical atrophy (PCA)?

- Rare **AD variant** — same pathology (amyloid + tau) but concentrated posteriorly
- Visuospatial deficits predominate; memory often preserved
- Presents with: RTAs (backing into objects), misjudging steps, difficulty with pentagon copying
- Transcript-only; not on slides
- 🟥 Don't be reassured by normal memory + normal MMSE in a patient with visuospatial symptoms

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — 72F, husband-reported repetitive questioning, got lost on a 20-year-familiar route, MMSE 22/30. Patient denies problems. Most important diagnostic information?

- **B — Collateral history from husband**
- Loss of insight is part of the disease — patient's account unreliable
- MMSE gives severity, not the change-over-time that diagnoses dementia
- MRI confirms pattern but requires a clinical pattern to interpret
- 🟦 The lecturer's explicit ranking: collateral > patient > exam > neuropsych > scans

### Q: SBA 2 — 68M, 3 weeks of worsening confusion, worse in evenings, dysuria, positive urinalysis. Most likely diagnosis?

- **C — Delirium secondary to UTI**
- Onset too fast for any neurodegenerative dementia
- Fluctuation + infective precipitant = delirium
- Treat the UTI first; reassess cognition once resolved
- 🟥 Never commit to dementia during an active delirium

### Q: SBA 3 — 74F, 9 months memory decline, bradycardia, dry skin, brittle hair. Most appropriate first investigation?

- **C — Thyroid function tests**
- Stem hands you hypothyroidism — a reversible cause of cognitive decline
- Must exclude reversible causes before dementia workup
- MRI, amyloid PET, LP are all wrong answers — they skip Job 1

### Q: SBA 4 — 71M, 2 years progressive memory loss, bilateral hippocampal atrophy on MRI. Strongest in-vivo confirmation of amyloid pathology?

- **B — Reduced CSF β-amyloid (1–42)**
- Amyloid trapped in plaques → less reaches CSF → LOW
- High CSF amyloid = controls pattern, not AD
- High CSF tau supports AD but reflects neurodegeneration, not amyloid
- Normal amyloid PET would rule AD OUT, not in

### Q: SBA 5 — 58M, 18 months of rudeness, obsessive pudding eating, hygiene decline, intact memory, MMSE 27/30, father died of "brain disease". Most likely diagnosis?

- **C — Behavioural variant frontotemporal dementia**
- Young age + disinhibition + stereotyped behaviour + normal MMSE = bvFTD
- Family history suggests autosomal dominant (possibly C9orf72)
- Not AD — wrong age and wrong symptom profile
- 🟨 Don't default to AD just because it's commonest

### Q: SBA 6 — 76M, good days and bad days, sees non-existent children in garden, tremor, masked facies, MMSE 24/30. Most specific confirmatory investigation?

- **B — DaTscan (¹²³I-FP-CIT SPECT)**
- Complete DLB picture: fluctuation + hallucinations + parkinsonism
- DaTscan shows reduced striatal dopamine transporter uptake (dots, not commas)
- ~85% specificity — the discriminating test when AD and DLB are both on the differential

### Q: SBA 7 — 82F, HTN + AF + prior TIA, 3 years decline "not gradual — every few months a step down", extensor plantar. Most likely diagnosis?

- **C — Vascular dementia**
- **Stepwise decline** + vascular risk factors + focal UMN sign = VaD
- AD = smooth slope, usually no focal signs
- DLB needs hallucinations/parkinsonism; none described
- 🟨 "Stepwise" is a near-deterministic trigger word for VaD

### Q: SBA 8 — 70M probable AD based on history + MRI. Which biomarker pattern most supports AD?

- **CSF β-amyloid LOW + CSF tau HIGH**
- Amyloid sequestered in plaques (low CSF)
- Tau leaked from dying neurons (high CSF)
- Counter-intuitive direction — mechanism-anchor it in memory

### Q: SBA 9 — 79M with DLB, distressing visual hallucinations. Most appropriate first-line pharmacological approach?

- **B — Rivastigmine (AChEI)**
- Profound cholinergic deficit in DLB → topping up ACh helps cognition AND hallucinations
- 🟥 Haloperidol contraindicated (neuroleptic sensitivity)
- Quetiapine only if AChEI fails and symptoms severe
- Benzodiazepines worsen confusion and fall risk

### Q: SBA 10 — 78F newly admitted with 2 days confusion, fever, suprapubic tenderness, positive urinalysis. Next step?

- **C — Treat the UTI and reassess cognition once resolved**
- Acute + precipitant = delirium, not dementia
- Dementia workup is premature and risks anchoring
- Treat the reversible cause first; many patients' cognition returns towards baseline

### Q: SBA 11 — 73F with AD on donepezil, MMSE drops from 20 to 17 over 3 months with new confusion, lethargy, weight loss. Next step?

- **C — Investigate for delirium / reversible causes**
- AD declines over years, not months — this change is too fast
- Bloods, urine dip, CXR, medication review
- Treat any reversible cause first; don't change dementia medication mid-delirium

### Q: SBA 12 — 76M with 3 years of fluctuation + garden hallucinations + tremor + masked facies, MRI shows mild cortical atrophy with preserved hippocampi. Most specific confirmatory test?

- **C — ¹²³I-FP-CIT SPECT (DaTscan)**
- Full DLB triad + preserved hippocampi
- Amyloid PET and CSF target AD, not α-synuclein
- ACE-III is a cognitive screen, not a pathology test

### Q: SBA 13 — 68M with 2 years of sexual disinhibition, sweet food cravings, inappropriate laughter, asymmetric frontal/temporal atrophy, father died similar in 70s. Best next step for the family?

- **B — Refer patient for genetic counselling and testing**
- ~25% of FTD is familial; **C9orf72** most common gene
- Family history strongly suggests hereditary FTD
- Imaging asymptomatic relatives doesn't diagnose; genetic testing does — with proper counselling
- AChEIs don't help FTD

### Q: SBA 14 — 67F, 6 months slower thinking after husband died, poor sleep, 4kg weight loss, MMSE 25/30, answers "I don't know" to many questions. Most appropriate initial approach?

- **Treat depression (SSRI/SNRI ± CBT), reassess cognition in 2–3 months**
- Bereavement trigger + biological features + "I don't know" pattern = pseudodementia
- Depression is top of the reversible-mimics list
- Committing to AD label here would miss a reversible cause

### Q: SBA 15 — 64M with 3 weeks rapid decline + myoclonus + DWI cortical ribboning on MRI. What category?

- **Rapidly progressing dementia — Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease suspected**
- Weeks-to-months timescale excludes neurodegenerative dementias
- Myoclonus + cortical DWI ribbon = classic CJD
- Differential includes autoimmune/limbic encephalitis, paraneoplastic, metabolic — some treatable
- 🟥 Timescale of weeks → NOT a memory clinic referral; urgent workup needed
