## The unifying intuition

Adrenal excess = one hormone is being made in surplus. Ask *which hormone*, and the clinical picture, investigation, and treatment all follow.

Treatment is always one of three: **block the receptor, inhibit the enzyme, or excise the tumour.**

> ⭐ **High-yield:** L2's three syndromes share an organ but nothing else — Cushing's (cortex / cortisol), Conn's (cortex / aldo), phaeo (medulla / catecholamines). Treat as ATLAS — three identical-template cards, then cross-cutting patterns.

## CARD 1 — Cushing's syndrome

### Q: What's the difference between Cushing's "syndrome" vs "disease"?

- **Syndrome** = any cause of cortisol excess (umbrella term)
- **Disease** = specifically pituitary-dependent (Harvey Cushing, 1936)
- If exam stem says "disease" → assume pituitary adenoma
- If "syndrome" → cause not yet identified

> ⚠️ **Trap:** This terminology is repeatedly tested. "Cushing's disease" never means adrenal or ectopic.

### Q: Causes of Cushing's syndrome — ranked by frequency?

- **Exogenous steroids** — commonest today (iatrogenic, not a tumour)
- **Pituitary-dependent Cushing's disease** — commonest endogenous
- **Ectopic ACTH** — typically small cell lung cancer
- **Adrenal adenoma** — autonomous, ACTH suppressed

### Q: Clinical features of cortisol excess — derive from physiology

- Catabolic on protein → thin skin, bruising, **proximal myopathy**, poor wound healing
- Protein → fat conversion (centripetal) → moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity, thin limbs
- Skin stretches over fattening torso → **red striae**
- Mineralocorticoid effect at high cortisol → HTN + hypokalaemia
- Insulin antagonism → diabetes
- Bone catabolism → osteoporosis

📎 Figure L2.2 — Netter Cushing's body illustration

### Q: Three-test diagnostic workup for Cushing's — what does each test answer?

1. **24h urinary free cortisol** — is total cortisol output raised?
2. **Diurnal blood cortisol** (9am + midnight) — is the rhythm lost?
3. **LDDST** (0.5 mg dex 6-hourly × 48h) — is the axis autonomous?

📎 Figure L2.3 — Diurnal cortisol normal vs Cushing's (flat curve)

### Q: Why must imaging wait until biochemistry confirms Cushing's?

- ~1 in 10 of the general population has an incidental pituitary adenoma
- Scanning unconfirmed cases → chasing innocent tumours → unnecessary surgery
- **Confirm biochemically first**, then image

### Q: What does LDDST suppression failure tell you, and what does it NOT tell you?

- **Failure to suppress confirms Cushing's** (axis is autonomous)
- Does **NOT** distinguish pituitary vs ectopic vs adrenal — all three fail to suppress
- Source identification needs ACTH level + HDDST + imaging, AFTER biochem confirms

### Q: Classic LDDST exam numbers?

- Basal 9am cortisol = **800 nM**
- End-of-LDDST cortisol = **680 nM** (not suppressed)
- Normal would suppress to near zero

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern — suppression tests:** Suppression tests fail when the gland is **autonomous**. LDDST for Cushing's; OGTT for acromegaly; calcium infusion for medullary thyroid Ca. Mirror image of stimulation tests in L1. *See also: L1 — Synacthen.*

## Pharmacology of Cushing's — enzyme inhibitors

📎 Figure L2.5 — Metyrapone block diagram

### Q: Metyrapone — mechanism and target enzyme?

- Blocks **11β-hydroxylase**
- Synthesis arrests at 11-deoxycortisol
- 11-deoxycortisol has NO negative feedback → drug effect not blunted
- Same enzyme whose deficiency causes 11-OH CAH

### Q: Metyrapone — uses?

- Bridge therapy before pituitary surgery
- Improves protein synthesis pre-op → better wound healing
- Post-radiotherapy control (radiotherapy is slow to work)
- **Target mean serum cortisol 150–300 nmol/L**

### Q: Metyrapone side effects — and why are they predictable?

- **Hypertension** (11-DOC accumulates, mineralocorticoid-like)
- **Hirsutism** (androgen pathway shunt)
- Side effects = iatrogenic 11-OH CAH phenotype

📎 Figure L2.6 — Metyrapone side-effect pathway

> 🧠 **Cross-link to L1:** If you know 11-OH CAH, metyrapone side effects come free. *See also: L1 — 11-OH deficiency.*

### Q: Ketoconazole — mechanism, use, key safety point?

- Originally an antifungal (inhibits fungal P450)
- At higher doses: blocks **17α-hydroxylase** (mainly), inhibiting cortisol
- Withdrawn 2013 as antifungal due to fatal hepatotoxicity
- Off-label for Cushing's; orally active
- **Monitor LFTs weekly, clinically and biochemically**

📎 Figure L2.7 — Ketoconazole block diagram

### Q: Osilodrostat — what is it?

- Newer agent, UK named-patient basis
- Blocks **11β + 17α hydroxylase**
- Effectively combines metyrapone + ketoconazole sites
- Use case: Cushing's not controlled by other agents

## Surgical treatment of Cushing's

### Q: Match cause to surgery

- **Pituitary disease** → transsphenoidal hypophysectomy (curative if successful)
- **Failed pituitary surgery** → bilateral adrenalectomy (creates iatrogenic Addison's)
- **Adrenal adenoma** → unilateral adrenalectomy (other adrenal wakes up)
- **Ectopic ACTH** → treat the underlying tumour

> 🧠 **Cross-link to L1:** Post-bilateral adrenalectomy patients need lifelong **pred 3 mg OD + fludrocortisone 50–100 μg** — the Addison's regimen. *See also: L1 — Replacement therapy.*

## CARD 2 — Conn's syndrome

### Q: What is Conn's syndrome?

- Benign adrenal cortical tumour of zona glomerulosa
- Autonomous **aldosterone** secretion
- Clinical: HTN + hypokalaemia
- Jerome Conn, ~1950

📎 Figure L2.9 — Aldosterone summary diagram

### Q: How do you diagnose Conn's biochemically?

- **Aldosterone HIGH**
- **Plasma renin SUPPRESSED** (the discriminator)
- Aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR) elevated
- High ARR rules in **primary** hyperaldosteronism (Conn's) and rules out **secondary** (where renin would also be high)

### Q: Why is renin suppressed in Conn's?

- Autonomous aldo → Na retention + volume expansion
- Kidney senses overfilling → stops releasing renin
- Secondary hyperaldo (renal artery stenosis, CHF, cirrhosis): kidney correctly senses LOW effective volume → renin stays HIGH
- Renin direction is the diagnostic key

### Q: Spironolactone — mechanism and clinical uses?

- Pro-drug → metabolised to **canrenone** (active form)
- Canrenone = competitive **mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist**
- Blocks Na resorption + K excretion (K-sparing diuretic)
- Used in: Conn's, heart failure, resistant HTN, ascites

### Q: Spironolactone side effects — and the receptor mechanism?

- **Menstrual irregularities** — agonism at progesterone receptor
- **Gynaecomastia** — antagonism at androgen receptor
- Spironolactone is non-selective at steroid receptors

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Gynaecomastia in a middle-aged man on antihypertensive → think spironolactone.

### Q: Eplerenone — what's different about it?

- Selective MR antagonist
- Similar affinity at MR vs spironolactone
- Much less binding to androgen + progesterone receptors
- Better tolerated → less gynaecomastia, less menstrual disturbance
- *Note: Meeran personally claims clinical equivalence; SPC and exam expectation favour eplerenone for tolerability*

## CARD 3 — Phaeochromocytoma

### Q: What is phaeochromocytoma?

- Tumour of the **adrenal medulla** (NOT cortex)
- Secretes **catecholamines** (adrenaline + noradrenaline)
- Episodic release → episodic catastrophic clinical events
- Medical emergency

📎 Figure L2.10 — Catecholamine synthesis (Netter)

### Q: Clinical features of phaeo?

- **Hypertension in young people** — always think phaeo
- **Episodic severe HTN** (peaks to 300 mmHg systolic)
- Often precipitated by abdominal palpation (pressing the tumour)
- Triad: headache (55%), palpitations (38%), sweating (27%)
- Pallor, dyspnoea, weight loss, anxiety, fear-like attacks

📎 Figure L2.11 — Phaeo distribution + BP traces

### Q: Phaeo — the 10/10/10 rule (lecturer-flagged for exams)?

- **10%** extra-adrenal (sympathetic chain, organ of Zuckerkandl, bladder wall)
- **10%** malignant
- **10%** bilateral (higher in children)
- ~2% multiple

### Q: Inherited associations of phaeo?

- Tumour-suppressor gene mutations in families
- VHL (von Hippel-Lindau)
- NF1 (neurofibromatosis type 1)
- MEN2 (RET)
- SDH mutations (succinate dehydrogenase complex)
- → Families are screened before crisis

### Q: Phaeo complications — what kills patients?

- Severe HTN → MI or stroke
- High adrenaline → ventricular fibrillation → sudden death
- Anaesthesia can precipitate crisis if unprepared

### Q: Phaeo management — drug sequence?

1. **Alpha blockade FIRST** (e.g. phenoxybenzamine)
2. IV fluids if needed (volume contracted from chronic catecholamines)
3. **Beta blockade SECOND** (to prevent reflex tachycardia)
4. Surgical excision once stable

> ⚠️ **Trap (always tested):** Beta-blocker first → unopposed alpha vasoconstriction → hypertensive crisis → death. **Alpha before beta is non-negotiable.**

## Cross-cutting pattern — all three cause HTN, but differently

| Syndrome | HTN mechanism | Discriminating biochem |
|---|---|---|
| **Cushing's** | cortisol mineralocorticoid effect at high levels | ↑ UFC, fail LDDST |
| **Conn's** | aldosterone at MR | ↑ aldo, ↓ renin |
| **Phaeo** | catecholamines at vascular α | ↑ urinary metanephrines |

> 🧠 **Cross-link to L1 + within L2 — Shared outcome:** HTN + low K converges from Cushing's, Conn's, 11-OH CAH, 17-OH CAH, and metyrapone side effect. Use age, sex steroid signs, cortisol, and renin to discriminate. *See also: L1 — 11-OH deficiency, 17-OH deficiency.*

## SBAs — interleaved discrimination

### Q: SBA 1. 42-yr-old, weight gain, HTN, K low, thin skin, striae. UFC raised. LDDST: cortisol 720 nM (not suppressed). ACTH mildly elevated. MRI: 7 mm pituitary lesion. Treatment?

**A. Transsphenoidal hypophysectomy**

- Pituitary-dependent Cushing's disease with visible microadenoma → curative surgery first-line
- Wrong: bilateral adrenalectomy (only if pituitary surgery fails); spironolactone (wrong target); metyrapone alone (bridge, not definitive)

### Q: SBA 2. 29-yr-old, episodic HTN to 215/120 on abdominal palpation. Urinary metanephrines elevated. First drug pre-op?

**A. Phenoxybenzamine (alpha-blocker)**

- Alpha-blockade always first in phaeo
- Wrong: propranolol or labetalol (β-component would cause unopposed α-mediated crisis)

### Q: SBA 3. 35-yr-old, BP 178/112 on 3 drugs, K 3.0. Aldo high, renin suppressed. Best add-on?

**A. Spironolactone**

- Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's pattern)
- MR antagonist directly addresses mechanism
- Wrong: amlodipine (generic HTN, doesn't fix mechanism); doxazosin (phaeo prep, not Conn's)

### Q: SBA 4. 35-yr-old woman with Conn's intolerant of spironolactone (breast tenderness, menstrual change). Alternative?

**A. Eplerenone**

- Selective MR antagonist with much less androgen + progesterone receptor binding
- Same target, fewer steroid receptor side effects

### Q: SBA 5. 55-yr-old on metyrapone pre-op for Cushing's develops BP 172/98, K 3.0, hirsutism. Best step?

**A. Reduce metyrapone dose**

- Predictable iatrogenic 11-OH-like picture (11-DOC ↑, androgen overflow)
- Address the cause; spironolactone could mask but not solve

### Q: SBA 6. 42-yr-old, Cushingoid, raised UFC, fails LDDST. ACTH undetectable. Likely cause?

**A. Adrenal adenoma**

- Failed LDDST = Cushing's confirmed
- ACTH undetectable → cortisol production is independent of pituitary → adrenal source
- Wrong: pituitary or ectopic (ACTH would be high or normal)

## Cross-references back to L1

> 🧠 **Q: How does the steroid pathway from L1 explain every drug in L2?**
>
> Metyrapone blocks 11β; ketoconazole blocks 17α; osilodrostat blocks both. Same enzymes whose deficiencies cause CAH in L1. Pharmacology = iatrogenic CAH at chosen sites. *See also: L1 — Steroid pathway.*

> 🧠 **Q: Pigmentation in Cushing's — when present?**
>
> Only in **ACTH-driven** Cushing's (pituitary or ectopic) — high ACTH co-secretes MSH from POMC. **Absent** in adrenal Cushing's (ACTH suppressed) and exogenous (no endogenous ACTH). Same POMC mechanism as Addison's pigmentation in reverse. *See also: L1 — POMC/MSH.*

> 🧠 **Q: Why is the LDDST direction the mirror of the Synacthen test?**
>
> Synacthen tests if a damaged gland can be **stimulated** (Addison's fails). LDDST tests if an autonomous gland will **suppress** to feedback (Cushing's fails). Two halves of the same logic across the bundle. *See also: L1 — Synacthen test.*
