## The unifying intuition

Adrenal failure = the gland can't make what it's supposed to make. Clinical picture follows directly from which hormone is missing and what that hormone normally does.

> ⭐ **High-yield:** Draw the steroid pathway once. Every condition in this lecture is a position on it. To predict the clinical picture, ask: *what's missing downstream of the block?* and *what overflows into the open branches?*

## Steroid pathway (cascade spine)

📎 Figure L1.1 — Steroid synthesis pathway with enzyme numbers

```
                 cholesterol
                      |
                 pregnenolone
                      |
                 progesterone ─(17)→ 17-OH prog ─→ sex steroids
                      |                  |              |
                    (21)                (21)            ↓
                      ↓                  ↓         androgens (testo)
              11-deoxycort.       11-deoxycortisol      |
                      |                  |              ↓
                    (11)                (11)        oestrogens
                      ↓                  ↓
              corticosterone         cortisol
                      |
                    (18)
                      ↓
                  aldosterone
```

Two regulatory drivers sit on top:
- **Angiotensin II** drives 3β-HSD → 21 → 11 → 18 → **aldosterone** (left branch)
- **ACTH** drives 3β-HSD → 21 → 11 → **17** → **cortisol** (middle branch)

📎 Figure L1.2 — Angiotensin II → aldosterone pathway
📎 Figure L1.3 — ACTH → cortisol pathway

### Q: Which two enzyme numbers are explicitly flagged as exam-critical?

- **21** and **17**
- 21-hydroxylase deficiency is the commonest CAH
- 17-hydroxylase deficiency is rarer but classically tested via the HTN + low K + undermasculinised pattern

### Q: Which regulatory hormone drives which adrenal branch?

- **Angiotensin II** → aldosterone (zona glomerulosa)
- **ACTH** → cortisol (zona fasciculata)
- ACTH spills over into adrenal androgens (zona reticularis) when chronically high

### Q: What is the cortisol diurnal rhythm (Debono & Ross 2009)?

- **Nadir ~55 nM at 0018h** (asleep)
- **Peak ~428 nM at 0832h**
- Rise begins ~5am, before waking
- Replacement therapy must mimic this: dose AM, not PM

## Addison's disease

### Q: Commonest cause of Addison's — UK vs worldwide?

- **UK:** autoimmune destruction of cortex (~55%)
- **Worldwide:** TB of adrenals (~40%)
- Other minor: metastasis, adrenoleukodystrophy
- Vitiligo coexists in autoimmune cases (shared autoimmunity)

### Q: Why do Addison's patients have hyperpigmentation?

- Cortisol low → no negative feedback → **POMC** synthesis up
- POMC cleaves into **ACTH + MSH** + endorphins
- High **MSH** drives melanocytes → tan in sun-unexposed areas (buccal mucosa, palmar creases, scars)

📎 Figure L1.5 — POMC → ACTH/MSH/endorphins

> ⭐ **Classic exam Q (lecturer-flagged):** "Why do these people have such a good tan?" Answer = POMC → ACTH + MSH co-secretion.

### Q: Adrenal crisis — list the features that kill them

- Severe hypotension
- Hyponatraemia (salt loss)
- Hyperkalaemia (no aldo-driven K excretion)
- Hypoglycaemia (no glucocorticoid)
- Vomiting, diarrhoea, fever, syncope, convulsions

### Q: Other clinical features of Addison's (Netter)?

- Hyperpigmentation: mucous membranes, skin creases, scars, friction areas, nipples
- Vitiligo patches
- Weight loss, anorexia, weakness
- Postural hypotension
- GI: vomiting, diarrhoea

## Diagnostic workup — the three-step ladder

### Q: Step-by-step diagnostic flow for suspected Addison's?

1. **Clinical suspicion** — low Na + high K + unexplained hypotension, ± pigmentation
2. **9am cortisol + ACTH** — expect cortisol low, ACTH high (primary)
3. **Short Synacthen test** — 250 μg IM, measure cortisol at 0 and 30 min

### Q: Classic Addison's lab numbers — memorise these

- 9am cortisol = **100 nM** (normal range 270–900)
- Post-Synacthen cortisol = **150 nM** (normal rise to >600)
- Failure to rise above 600 confirms primary adrenal failure

### Q: Why use Synacthen rather than relying on 9am cortisol alone?

- Early Addison's: residual gland still produces some baseline cortisol
- Synacthen delivers a maximal stimulus the failed gland can't meet
- Distinguishes "borderline low" from "destroyed gland"

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern — stimulation tests:** Stimulation tests **fail when the gland is broken**. Synacthen for adrenals; insulin tolerance for pituitary; GnRH for gonadotrophs. Pattern transfers to every endocrine axis.

## Replacement therapy

### Q: How do you replace aldosterone, and why fludrocortisone not aldosterone itself?

- **Fludrocortisone 50–100 μg OD**
- Aldosterone t½ = 20 min — useless orally
- Fluorine substitution blocks metabolism → t½ 3.5h, effects last 18h
- Binds MR + GR; MR effect dominates at this dose

📎 Figure L1.7 — Fludrocortisone structure (fluorine highlighted)

### Q: Cortisol replacement options — HC vs prednisolone?

- **Hydrocortisone 10 + 5 + 2.5 mg TDS** — traditional, but short t½ creates harmful evening peaks
- **Prednisolone 3 mg OD** — Meeran's preferred; smoother PK, mimics circadian curve
- Equivalence: **3 mg pred ≈ 20 mg HC** (ratio 6–8:1)
- 8h post-dose pred level target **15–25 mcg/L**

📎 Figure L1.8 — Hydrocortisone PK curve (with harmful late peaks)
📎 Figure L1.10 — Prednisolone vs cortisol structure

> ⚠️ **Conflict flag (lecturer vs textbook):** Standard pharmacology tables list pred as 4× cortisol potency (5 mg ≡ 20 mg HC). Meeran says **7× and 3 mg ≡ 20 mg HC** based on his own data (Williams/Choudhury/Tan/Meeran 2016 JALM; Choudhury/Lightman/Meeran 2019). On slide 91 he marks the textbook table with explicit X corrections.

📎 Figure L1.17 — Steroid equivalence table with X corrections

### Q: Why does over-replacement matter?

- Choudhury/Lightman/Meeran 2019: SMR > 2 in adrenal insufficiency
- Excess steroid exposure drives CV mortality, osteoporosis, weight gain
- Why pred OD with monitoring (target 15–25 mcg/L at 8h) is safer than HC TDS over-dosed

## CAH — territory map

The three CAH subtypes share one logic: **block in the cortisol pathway → ACTH drive up → adrenals enlarge ("hyperplasia") → upstream precursors back up and overflow into still-open branches**.

What differs is which branches are still open.

| Subtype | Block | Open branches | Clinical signature | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **21-OH def** | both 21 arrows | sex steroid branch only | salt-losing crisis + virilisation | **Major** |
| **11-OH def** | both 11 arrows | sex steroid + 11-DOC accumulates (acts like aldo) | HTN + low K + virilisation | Minor |
| **17-OH def** | 17 arrow | aldo branch (full output) | HTN + low K + sex steroid deficiency | Minor |

## 21-hydroxylase deficiency — the examinable one

📎 Figure L1.13 — 21-OH deficiency on pathway

### Q: Complete 21-OH deficiency — what's missing, what's in excess, when does it kill?

- **Missing:** aldosterone + cortisol (both)
- **Excess:** sex steroids + testosterone (only open branch)
- **Survival without treatment:** <24h post-birth
- In utero: rescued by maternal cortisol via placenta
- Birth: salt-losing Addisonian crisis, hypoglycaemia, hyperkalaemia

### Q: Why are boys with complete 21-OH deficiency more often missed than girls?

- Girls are virilised in utero by adrenal testosterone → ambiguous genitalia at birth → kept in hospital
- Boys' genitalia look normal (already male) → discharged → collapse on day 2

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Don't assume "newborn salt-losing crisis = girl with ambiguous genitalia." Boys with 21-OH deficiency have NO genital ambiguity and are missed until they crash.

### Q: Partial 21-OH deficiency — typical presentation?

- Some residual cortisol + aldo → no neonatal crisis
- Persistent ACTH drive → ongoing testosterone overflow for years
- **Girls:** hirsutism, virilisation, infertility (adrenal testo suppresses LH/FSH)
- **Boys:** precocious adrenarche / pseudo-puberty
- Can present at any age

📎 Figure L1.14 — Netter partial 21-OH deficiency virilisation

### Q: Treatment of CAH — and why it works dual-purpose?

- **Pred 3 mg OD** (or HC 10+5+2.5) + **fludrocortisone 50–100 μg**
- Replaces the missing hormones
- Suppresses ACTH drive → testosterone overflow falls → virilisation regresses
- Started early enough, can reverse partial virilisation

## 11-OH and 17-OH deficiency — pattern recognition

### Q: 11-OH deficiency — why HTN + low K instead of salt-losing crisis?

- Block at 11 means cortisol AND aldo can't be made
- BUT **11-deoxycorticosterone accumulates upstream** of the block
- 11-DOC binds MR with **aldo-like activity** → salt retention, K loss, HTN
- Sex steroid overflow still happens → virilisation

📎 Figure L1.15 — 11-OH deficiency pathway

> ⭐ **High-yield pattern:** child with **HTN + low K + virilisation** in adrenal context = **11-OH deficiency**. Rare, but a classic discriminator stem.

### Q: 17-OH deficiency — why feminisation in 46,XY?

- 17 enzyme is needed for both cortisol AND sex steroids
- Block 17 → no sex steroids → 46,XY undermasculinise (female-appearing external genitalia)
- Aldo branch fully intact AND overdriven by ACTH → **HTN + low K**
- Failure of puberty in either sex
- Cortisol still low → low glucose

📎 Figure L1.16 — 17-OH deficiency pathway

> ⭐ **High-yield pattern:** phenotypic girl at 18 who never menstruated, with HTN and low K, karyotype 46,XY = **17-OH deficiency**.

## SBAs — interleaved discrimination

### Q: SBA 1. Newborn girl, ambiguous genitalia. Day 2: vomiting, lethargy, Na 122, K 6.8, glucose 2.4. Diagnosis?

**A. 21-OH deficiency (complete)**

- Salt-losing crisis (low Na, high K, low glucose) + ambiguous genitalia in a girl = textbook complete 21-OH
- Wrong: Cushing's (excess, not deficiency); 17-OH (would feminise 46,XY, normal 46,XX); 11-OH (HTN, not hypotension); Conn's (adult, not neonate)

### Q: SBA 2. 12-yr-old boy, BP 160/105, K 3.1, undermasculinised, 46,XY. Which enzyme is deficient?

**A. 17-hydroxylase**

- HTN + low K + undermasculinised 46,XY = 17-OH (cortisol AND sex steroid synthesis blocked; aldo + 11-DOC excess)
- Wrong: 21-OH (virilises, doesn't undermasculinise); 11-OH (would virilise too, not undermasculinise)

### Q: SBA 3. 16-yr-old girl, hirsutism, BP 145/95, K 3.3, raised 17-OH progesterone, raised 11-DOC. Diagnosis?

**A. 11-OH deficiency (partial)**

- Hirsutism + HTN + low K + raised 11-DOC = 11-OH partial. Upstream 17-OH prog also rises.
- Wrong: 21-OH partial gives hirsutism but **without HTN** (aldo deficient, not in excess)

### Q: SBA 4. 32-yr-old on pred 3 mg OD for Addison's. 8h post-dose pred level 32 mcg/L. What now?

**A. Decrease the dose**

- Target 8h level 15–25 mcg/L. She's over-replaced.
- Excess steroid → CV mortality, bone loss (Choudhury/Lightman/Meeran 2019, SMR >2)

### Q: SBA 5. Suspected Addison's. 9am cortisol 95 nM, ACTH high. Confirmatory test?

**A. Short Synacthen test**

- Stimulation test fails when gland is broken
- Wrong: LDDST is for Cushing's (suppression test, opposite logic)

## Cross-references to L2

> 🧠 **Q: Why do metyrapone side effects look like 11-OH CAH?**
>
> Metyrapone blocks 11β-hydroxylase pharmacologically — same lesion as 11-OH deficiency, just iatrogenic. So 11-DOC accumulates → HTN + low K, and androgen overflow → hirsutism. If you understand 11-OH CAH, metyrapone side effects come for free. *See also: L2 — Pharmacology / Metyrapone.*

> 🧠 **Q: How is the Addison's diagnostic logic mirrored in L2 Cushing's?**
>
> Addison's = **stimulation** test (Synacthen) fails when gland is destroyed. Cushing's = **suppression** test (LDDST) fails when gland is autonomous. Same logic, opposite direction. *See also: L2 — LDDST.*

> 🧠 **Q: Adrenal failure post-bilateral adrenalectomy — what's the regimen?**
>
> Same as Addison's: **pred 3 mg OD + fludrocortisone 50–100 μg**. Bilateral adrenalectomy creates iatrogenic primary adrenal insufficiency. *See also: L2 — Treatment of Cushing's.*
