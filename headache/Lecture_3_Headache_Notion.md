## Section 1 — Gatekeeper: primary vs secondary

### Q: Central principle for diagnosing any headache?

History is the diagnosis. Every question sorts into: does this sound like the brain generating pain on its own (primary) or reporting something else is wrong (secondary)?

### Q: Primary vs secondary — definitions?

- Primary = the headache is the disease (~90%).
- Secondary = headache is a symptom of something else (bleed, infection, tumour, raised ICP, drug overuse).

### Q: Is a primary headache diagnosis permanent?

No — it is a working diagnosis. A migraineur can still develop a subarachnoid haemorrhage. If the pattern changes, re-evaluate.

### Q: Are serious secondary causes common?

No. Most secondary headaches are benign.

🟥 When a stem gives a chronic featureless headache, the answer is usually **primary**. Don't reach for the scary diagnosis just because it's listed.

### Q: Draw the clinical approach to any headache.

```
History + Exam

```

### Q: What are the four red-flag categories and what does each warn of?

| Red flag | Trigger | Classic worry |
|---|---|---|
| **Age** | New/different headache >50 yrs | GCA, tumour |
| **Onset** | Peaks in <1 min (thunderclap) | **Subarachnoid haemorrhage** |
| **Systemic** | Fever, neck stiffness, rash, weight loss | Meningitis, malignancy |
| **Neurological** | Confusion, reduced GCS, focal signs, papilloedema | Stroke, tumour, raised ICP |

🟥 Slide gives **4 categories**, not SNOOP. Age cut-off is **>50** (not "<5 or >65" — that's a student-note error).

### Q: Why is a thunderclap headache so feared?

Brain's own pain builds gradually. Peak within 1 minute = something ruptured or occluded. Most classically subarachnoid haemorrhage; also cervical artery dissection, RCVS, pituitary apoplexy.

### Q: What is medication-overuse headache?

A patient with underlying headache (usually migraine) who takes analgesics frequently — >10–15 days/month of paracetamol, NSAIDs, triptans, or especially codeine/opiates — develops a new daily headache driven by the medication itself.

### Q: How is medication-overuse headache treated?

Withdraw the offending drug. Expect the headache to worsen before it improves.

🟥 Opiates and mixed analgesics are banned in all headache treatment — they are the worst offenders for driving MOH.

### Q: 🧠 Reusable method for any headache stem

```
1. Red flags?  Age>50 new / thunderclap /
              fever+neck / focal neuro or papilloedema
              Yes → secondary workup
              No  → continue

2. Pain        Where? Quality? Severity?

3. Attack      Duration? Frequency? Bouts?

4. Companions  Autonomic ipsilateral → cluster
              N/V + photo/phono     → migraine
              Nothing               → tension

5. Behaviour   Lies still → migraine
              Paces      → cluster
              Carries on → tension

6. Answer + justify why the other two are excluded.
```

## Section 2 — Migraine

📎 Figure L3.8.1 — Migraine central-hypothesis pathophysiology

### Q: What type of disorder is migraine?

A neurovascular disorder — both neurological (CSD, central sensitisation) and vascular (CGRP-mediated vasodilation) factors contribute.

### Q: What kind of event is a migraine, conceptually?

A 4-phase wave passing through the brain over hours-to-days — not a pain episode. Lecturer: *"Migraines are not headaches. Migraine is a journey."*

### Q: Draw the migraine pathophysiology cascade.

```
Trigger (disrupted routine, sleep, food, hormones)
              │
              ▼
Brainstem/hypothalamic activation
(+ cortical hyperexcitability: ↑Ca²⁺, ↑Glu, ↓Mg²⁺)
              │
              ▼
CORTICAL SPREADING DEPRESSION (CSD)
              │
      ┌───────┴───────┐
      ▼               ▼
    AURA         Activates TGVS
 (5–30 min)            │
                       ▼
                Releases CGRP
                       │
              ┌────────┴────────┐
              ▼                 ▼
      Vasodilation +       Central
      neurogenic           sensitisation
      inflammation              │
              │                 ▼
              │         Photo/phonophobia,
              │         osmophobia, motion
              │         sensitivity
              │                 │
              └─→ HEADACHE PAIN ←┘
                        │
                        ▼
                    Recovery
```

### Q: What does each cascade step do, and what targets it?

| Step | Effect | Targeted by |
|---|---|---|
| Hypothalamic trigger | Tips a sensitive brain into the cycle | Lifestyle (sleep, food, routine) |
| CSD | Slow occipital wave | Produces aura |
| TGVS activation | Trigeminal fibres on meningeal vessels fire | Oxygen (in cluster) |
| CGRP release | Vasodilation + neurogenic inflammation | **Triptans** block release; **CGRP antibodies** neutralise |
| Central sensitisation | Pain threshold drops | Explains photo/phonophobia, movement aggravation |

⭐ **CGRP is the peptide to know** — released centrally and peripherally, the link between mechanism and every acute drug. CGRP monoclonal antibodies are the first drug class designed for migraine.

### Q: What are the four phases of migraine and their durations?

📎 Figure L3.8.4 — Migraine phases timeline

| Phase | Duration | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Premonitory | Up to 48 h before | Yawning, polyuria, mood change, neck pain, concentration difficulty, cravings |
| Aura | 5–30 min (<60) | Visual (zigzag, C's, scintillating), sensory numbness/paraesthesia, rarely weakness/speech |
| Headache | 4–72 h | Unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, aggravated by activity, +N/V, +photo/phonophobia |
| Recovery | Up to 48 h | "Hungover", food intolerance, mood disturbed |

### Q: Is aura always present in migraine?

No. Migraine **without** aura is more common. Aura is not a diagnostic prerequisite.

### Q: Diagnostic criteria for a migraine headache?

Need several of:
- Unilateral
- Pulsating
- Moderate–severe
- Aggravated by routine activity
- Lasts 4–72 h

PLUS ≥1 of nausea/vomiting OR photo/phonophobia. ± aura.

### Q: What does migraine visual aura look like?

📎 Figure L3.8.2 — Visual aura historical description
📎 Figure L3.8.3 — Two aura morphologies

- Starts small and central, enlarges outward
- Zigzag / fortification / criss-cross edges
- Scintillating (shimmering)
- Evolves gradually over 5–30 min
- Always <60 min (ICHD-3 cap)

🟥 **Sudden-onset visual disturbance is NOT aura** — that's vascular (TIA/stroke). Migraine aura builds. Timeframe protects you.

### Q: Migraine management — three tracks?

```
          Migraine management
                 │
    ┌────────────┼────────────┐
    │            │            │
 Lifestyle    Acute       Preventative
 (first       (hard &      (low & slow,
  line)        fast)        ≥5 days/month)
```

### Q: Why is lifestyle first-line for migraine?

It directly targets the hypothalamic trigger step. Regular sleep, food, exercise, mindfulness. Lecturer: *"Migraines love routine."*

### Q: Acute ("hard and fast") drugs for migraine?

- Paracetamol — simple analgesic
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen 600–800 mg, aspirin 900 mg) — **high dose and soluble** (speed matters)
- Prokinetics (metoclopramide) — treat nausea + speed gastric emptying so oral drugs absorb
- **Triptans** (sumatriptan) — 5-HT₁B/1D/1F agonists → block CGRP release

### Q: Why take acute migraine treatment within 15 minutes of onset?

⭐ Once central sensitisation has set in, drugs stop working. Early = aborts the journey; late = chases it.

### Q: Preventative migraine treatment — threshold and lead-in?

- Threshold: ≥5 headache days/month
- Strategy: "low and slow" — titrate up
- Takes ~4–6 weeks to work — counsel the patient
- Bridge the lead-in with a greater occipital nerve block (~2 weeks of cover)

### Q: Which preventative classes should you know?

Classes — not individual drugs (slide says "you don't need to learn these").

- **β-blockers** (propranolol) — common first-line
- TCAs / SNRIs (amitriptyline, venlafaxine) — also helps sleep, mood
- Anticonvulsants (topiramate, valproate) — avoid valproate in childbearing-age women
- CCBs (verapamil, flunarizine) — verapamil's starring role is cluster
- ACEi / ARBs (candesartan) — found incidentally in BP trials
- CGRP monoclonal antibodies — first class designed for migraine; main side effect constipation
- Botulinum toxin A — chronic migraine only

## Section 3 — Tension-type headache

### Q: Single history question that discriminates tension-type from migraine?

"Do you carry on with what you were doing, or do you stop?"

- Carries on → tension-type
- Stops, lies down → migraine
- Paces → cluster

### Q: Tension-type — laterality, quality, severity?

- Bilateral
- Pressing, tightening, non-pulsating — "head in a vice"
- Mild–moderate, not disabling

### Q: Tension-type — duration, aggravation, associated features?

- ~30 min to hours
- NOT aggravated by movement (red-underlined on slide)
- Featureless: no N/V, no photo/phonophobia, no autonomics
- Patient carries on with normal activity

### Q: Tension-type treatment ladder?

1. Reassurance
2. Aspirin or paracetamol for attacks
3. Preventatives rarely needed

🟥 No triptans for tension-type. Triptans target the CGRP/TGVS pathway — not the driver here.

## Section 4 — Cluster headache

### Q: Three features that together define cluster?

1. Severe unilateral periorbital pain (V1 territory)
2. Ipsilateral autonomic features (≥1)
3. Bouts — daily for weeks/months, then remission

Lecturer: "Worst pain in humanity."

### Q: Why does cluster cause ipsilateral autonomic features?

The pain pathway hijacks facial parasympathetic outflow on the same side:

- Lacrimation, conjunctival injection — parasympathetic to lacrimal gland, ocular vessels
- Nasal congestion, rhinorrhoea — parasympathetic to nasal mucosa
- Miosis + ptosis — sympathetic *under*activity → partial Horner's-like picture

### Q: Cluster — laterality, location, quality?

- Unilateral, never bilateral; side-locked within a bout (can switch between bouts)
- Periorbital / behind one eye (V1)
- Very severe

### Q: Cluster — attack duration and frequency pattern?

- 15–180 min per attack (average ~30 min)
- 1–3/day (up to 8), daily for 2–3 months, then remission
- Often nocturnal (wakes at e.g. 3am); circadian → hypothalamic involvement

### Q: Cluster — full autonomic list (≥1 ipsilateral required)?

- Conjunctival injection / lacrimation
- Nasal congestion / rhinorrhoea
- Eyelid oedema
- Forehead / facial sweating
- Miosis and/or ptosis

### Q: Cluster — behaviour during attack, and MRI finding?

- Restless, pacing, agitated (opposite of migraine)
- MRI normal (no brain lesion)

### Q: Acute cluster treatment?

- Sumatriptan — nasal or subcutaneous (not oral)
- High-flow oxygen ~12–15 L/min via face mask

🟥 Route matters. Oral triptan = correct drug, wrong route — attack peaks too fast for oral absorption.

### Q: How does oxygen work in cluster?

Inhibits neuronal activation in the trigeminocervical complex.

🟦 Same anatomy explains why greater occipital nerve block also works — C2/occipital nerve feeds the same complex.

### Q: NICE banned list for cluster acute treatment?

Do NOT offer: paracetamol, NSAIDs, oral triptans, ergots, opioids. No evidence of benefit.

Correct regimen: nasal or SC triptan + high-flow oxygen.

### Q: Cluster prophylaxis?

- Verapamil (CCB) — first-line
- Greater occipital nerve block — bridge while verapamil takes effect

### Q: Why must you get an ECG before starting verapamil in cluster?

🟥 Verapamil prolongs the PR interval and risks AV block, especially at the high doses used in cluster. Re-ECG with each dose increase.

## Section 5 — Cross-cutting comparison

📎 Figure L3.8.5 — Pain distribution in the three primary headaches

### Q: Pain character — migraine vs tension vs cluster?

| Feature | Migraine | Tension | Cluster |
|---|---|---|---|
| Laterality | Unilateral (spreads) | Bilateral | Unilateral, never bilateral |
| Quality | Pulsating / throbbing | Pressing, tightening | Severe boring periorbital |
| Severity | Moderate–severe | Mild–moderate | Very severe |

### Q: Attack pattern — migraine vs tension vs cluster?

| Feature | Migraine | Tension | Cluster |
|---|---|---|---|
| Duration | 4–72 h | 30 min – hours | 15 min – 3 h |
| Frequency | 1–2 / month | Variable | 1–3 / day, 2–3 mo bouts |
| Worse on activity? | Yes | No | No, but restless |

### Q: Associated symptoms — which headache?

- N/V + photo/phonophobia (± aura) → migraine
- None → tension
- Ipsilateral autonomics (tears, runny nose, ptosis) → cluster

### Q: Patient behaviour during attack — which headache?

- Lies still in dark room → migraine
- Carries on → tension
- Paces, agitated → cluster

### Q: 🧠 Duration ladder — which headache, given duration only?

- 15 min – 3 h, multiple daily → cluster
- 4–72 h, ~1–2/month → migraine
- 30 min – hours, variable → tension
- Seconds, shock-like, trigger-induced → trigeminal neuralgia (distractor)
- <1 min to peak, single event → thunderclap → SAH

### Q: Stem mentions restlessness + ipsilateral lacrimation — diagnosis?

Cluster headache. No other primary headache combines autonomic features with agitated behaviour.

### Q: Migraineur + new daily headache + frequent codeine use — diagnosis?

Medication-overuse headache. Withdraw the codeine.

## Section 6 — Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 45-year-old woman has a 3-year history of bilateral headaches, tight-band quality, mild, 1–2 h each, continues working through them. No nausea, no visual changes. Most likely?

A. Chronic migraine
B. **Tension-type headache**
C. Medication-overuse headache
D. Cluster headache
E. Trigeminal neuralgia

**Answer: B.** Bilateral + pressing + mild + featureless + carries on.
- A: migraine is unilateral, pulsating, disabling.
- C: no analgesic overuse mentioned.
- D: cluster is unilateral, severe, with autonomics.
- E: seconds-long, shock-like, trigger-induced.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 28-year-old woman has right-sided headaches once a month lasting 24 h. Preceded by a 20-min shimmering zigzag that gradually enlarges. During the headache she has nausea and lies still in a dark room. Most likely?

A. Migraine without aura
B. **Migraine with aura**
C. Occipital lobe epilepsy
D. Transient ischaemic attack
E. Cluster headache

**Answer: B.** Unilateral pulsating 24 h + N/V + photophobia + gradually-evolving scintillating zigzag.
- A: has aura.
- C: occipital seizures last seconds-minutes.
- D: sudden onset, older patient.
- E: short attacks with autonomics.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 40-year-old man has severe left-sided headaches around the left eye, 1 h each, 2–3/day for 3 weeks. Left eye waters and left nostril runs. Can't stay still. Similar bout 2 years ago resolved after 6 weeks. Most likely?

A. Migraine with aura
B. Trigeminal neuralgia
C. **Cluster headache**
D. Giant cell arteritis
E. Sinusitis

**Answer: C.** Periorbital short-duration multi-daily + ipsilateral autonomics + restless + bouts with remission.
- A: no autonomics or multiple daily attacks in migraine.
- B: seconds, shock-like.
- D: >50 years, continuous pain, jaw claudication.
- E: continuous facial pain, bilateral discharge.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 68-year-old woman: new continuous right-sided throbbing headache for 10 days, jaw pain on chewing, scalp tenderness. ESR 90. Most appropriate immediate step?

A. Sumatriptan
B. **High-dose oral prednisolone**
C. MRI brain
D. Amitriptyline
E. Verapamil

**Answer: B.** Red flags: age >50 new-onset + systemic + markedly raised ESR = giant cell arteritis. Start steroids immediately; temporal artery biopsy follows. Risk of permanent blindness (AION) within days.
- A, D, E: primary-headache drugs.
- C: GCA is vessel inflammation, not a lesion on MRI.

### Q: SBA 5 — A 35-year-old man: "worst headache of my life", started 90 min ago while straining, reached maximum intensity within a minute. Neck stiffness, photophobia. GCS 15. First investigation?

A. MRI brain
B. Lumbar puncture
C. **Non-contrast CT head**
D. CT angiogram
E. Blood cultures

**Answer: C.** Thunderclap + meningism = SAH until proven otherwise. Non-contrast CT within 6 h is near 100% sensitive. LP follows if CT negative.
- A: MRI slower, less sensitive for acute blood.
- B: LP before imaging risks coning.
- D: CTA identifies aneurysm after bleed confirmed.
- E: thunderclap points to SAH, not meningitis.

### Q: SBA 6 — A 22-year-old woman with migraines 2–3 days/month on ibuprofen has developed a daily dull headache over 6 months. Taking codeine-containing analgesics almost daily. Most likely?

A. Chronic migraine
B. New daily persistent headache
C. **Medication-overuse headache**
D. Tension-type headache
E. Idiopathic intracranial hypertension

**Answer: C.** Migraineur + frequent opiate-containing analgesic use + new daily background headache.
- A: chronic migraine is ≥15 migrainous days/month.
- B: NDPH has no drug driver.
- D: not typically daily constant.
- E: needs papilloedema + visual symptoms.

### Q: SBA 7 — A 50-year-old man in a cluster bout. Correct statement about oxygen?

A. 2 L/min nasal cannula for 20 min
B. Only if triptan fails
C. **High-flow oxygen inhibits neuronal activation in the trigeminocervical complex**
D. Contraindicated in smokers
E. Ventilator only

**Answer: C.** High-flow (~12–15 L/min) via non-rebreather at attack onset.
- A: flow too low.
- B: first-line, not backup.
- D: not contraindicated in smokers.
- E: standard face mask.

### Q: SBA 8 — A 30-year-old woman with migraine with aura gets 6 headache days/month. First-line preventative?

A. Topiramate
B. **Propranolol**
C. Daily sumatriptan
D. Codeine
E. Verapamil

**Answer: B.** ≥5 days/month meets threshold; β-blocker is standard first-line.
- A: valid but avoided in young women without contraception.
- C: triptans are acute only — daily use = MOH + cardiovascular risk.
- D: opiates banned.
- E: verapamil's starring role is cluster.

### Q: SBA 9 — A 27-year-old woman asks what distinguishes her condition from cluster headache. Which feature points most reliably to migraine?

A. Severe pain intensity
B. Unilateral location
C. **Attack duration 8 hours with N/V and photophobia, lies in a dark room**
D. Lacrimation during the headache
E. Multiple attacks per day

**Answer: C.** Long duration + N/V + photo/phonophobia + lying still = migraine signature.
- A: cluster is usually more severe.
- B: both can be unilateral.
- D: lacrimation is a cluster autonomic feature.
- E: cluster is 1–3/day; migraine 1–2/month.

### Q: SBA 10 — A 55-year-old man starting verapamil for cluster. Most important pre-treatment investigation?

A. Echocardiogram
B. **ECG**
C. Exercise stress test
D. CT coronary angiogram
E. Troponin

**Answer: B.** Verapamil prolongs PR and risks AV block at the high cluster doses. ECG before first dose and each dose increase.

### Q: SBA 11 — A 25-year-old with 10-year migraine history presents with his usual headache but also sudden right-arm weakness and word-finding difficulty for 45 min. Next action?

A. Subcutaneous sumatriptan
B. Reassure — likely migraine aura
C. **Urgent stroke pathway — CT head**
D. High-flow oxygen
E. Oral ibuprofen

**Answer: C.** Sudden-onset focal motor weakness + dysphasia = stroke until proven otherwise. Migraine aura builds gradually; motor aura is rare and a red flag.

### Q: SBA 12 — A 17-year-old boy, 6-month history, "banging" headaches, 6 in past 2 months lasting 2–4 h, photophobia, nausea, can't go to school. Paracetamol + ibuprofen give partial relief. Diagnosis + next step?

A. Tension-type; reassurance
B. **Episodic migraine without aura; high-dose soluble NSAID + antiemetic within 15 min, escalate to triptan if fails**
C. Cluster; SC sumatriptan + oxygen
D. Medication-overuse; withdraw analgesics
E. Secondary headache; urgent MRI

**Answer: B.** Tutorial Case 1. Episodic (<15 days/month) migraine. Hard-and-fast acute within 15 min; antiemetic doubles as a prokinetic.
- A: not featureless, disabling + associated features.
- C: no autonomics, duration too long.
- D: not enough analgesic overuse.
- E: no red flags.

### Q: SBA 13 — Which statement about migraine aura is TRUE?

A. Aura is always present in migraine
B. Aura lasts <5 minutes
C. **Aura evolves gradually over minutes, usually <60 minutes total**
D. Sudden-onset visual loss is typical aura
E. Aura is caused by vasospasm alone

**Answer: C.** Aura is CSD and evolves gradually (5–30 min typically, always <60 min). Sudden = TIA/stroke. Migraine without aura is more common.

### Q: SBA 14 — Which is NOT a red flag for secondary headache on the lecture slide?

A. New-onset headache in a 62-year-old
B. Headache reaching maximum intensity in 30 seconds
C. Headache with fever and neck stiffness
D. Papilloedema
E. **Headache triggered by stress in a 25-year-old**

**Answer: E.** Stress-triggered headache in a young adult fits primary (tension or migraine). Slide's 4 categories: age >50 new-onset, thunderclap, systemic, neurological (including papilloedema).

### Q: SBA 15 — A patient with cluster headache is prescribed acute treatment. Which is explicitly banned by NICE?

A. Subcutaneous sumatriptan
B. Nasal sumatriptan
C. High-flow oxygen
D. **Oral codeine**
E. Home (ambulatory) oxygen

**Answer: D.** NICE excludes paracetamol, NSAIDs, oral triptans, ergots, and opioids for cluster — no evidence of benefit. Correct regimen: nasal/SC triptan + oxygen.
