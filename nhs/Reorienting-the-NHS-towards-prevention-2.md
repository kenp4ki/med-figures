## The mechanism spine — policy chain (5YFV → LTP → Darzi → 10YHP)

**Single unifying intuition:** every NHS reform document since 2014 has tried to bend the system in the same three directions — **out of hospital, towards prevention, with better data**. Each iteration narrows further. The 10YHP's three strategic shifts are the cleanest statement of that direction.

```
2014  ─►  Five Year Forward View    "Three gaps"
2019  ─►  NHS Long Term Plan        "Six themes"   (overshadowed by COVID)
2024  ─►  Darzi Review              "Critical condition, vital signs strong"
2025  ─►  10-Year Health Plan       "Three radical shifts"
          (Fit for the Future)         ↓
                              hospital → community
                              analogue → digital
                              sickness → prevention
```

### Q: Name the three "gaps" identified by the Five Year Forward View (2014).

- **Health and wellbeing gap** — preventable illness (obesity, smoking, alcohol) outpacing clinical care
- **Care and quality gap** — fragmentation between hospital, primary, community settings
- **Funding and efficiency gap** — projected demand outstripping funding without new investment + efficiency

### Q: What was the NHS Long Term Plan, and why is its detail de-emphasised for exams?

- 2019 plan looking 10 years ahead — six themes (new models, disease programmes, prevention, workforce, digital, finance)
- *"I don't need you to learn all six"* — Pinder
- Examinable detail: it was **overshadowed by COVID-19** and never fully implemented

### Q: Who chaired the 2024 NHS review, and what was the headline conclusion?

- **Lord Ara Darzi** — Imperial, surgeon at St Mary's, ex-health-minister under Blair
- Headline: **"NHS in critical condition, but its vital signs are strong"**
- Six conclusions; the headline quote is the most likely recall item

### Q: What is the name and publication date of the current NHS strategy?

- **10-Year Health Plan: Fit for the Future**
- Published **July 2025**
- Acronym: **10YHP**

⚠️ **Trap:** Don't confuse "three gaps" (5YFV 2014) with "three shifts" (10YHP 2025). Gaps describe the **problem**; shifts describe the **solution**. Different documents, different framings.

## Three radical shifts — highest-yield concept in L2

⭐ **Lecturer-flagged.** *"Those three things really important to remember, guys."* Retested in practice question.

### Q: What are the three strategic shifts in the 10-Year Health Plan?

- **Hospital to community**
- **Analogue to digital**
- **Sickness to prevention**

### Q: What's the case FOR the hospital-to-community shift?

- **Patient experience** — older patients prefer "pretty good care 20 min from home" over "best care 6 hours away"
- **Cost** — community care avoids hospital cost drivers (advanced imaging, diagnostic cascades, hospital-acquired infections, incidentalomas)

### Q: What are the two main barriers to delivering the hospital-to-community shift?

- **Clinician risk aversion** — community medicine means high-risk decisions in low-resource settings; few want to take that on
- **Money hasn't followed patients** — bed closures haven't been matched by community reinvestment

### Q: Why does Pinder say analogue-to-digital is uncontroversial in principle but a "complete disaster" in practice?

- No one disagrees the NHS should be digital
- IT infrastructure is a mess — even staff struggle to log in
- Realistic short-term wins: rebuilding **legacy software** for cross-system communication, not replacing doctors with AI
- **Digital scribes** flagged as the technology with most transformative near-term potential

### Q: What's the implementation bottleneck for "sickness to prevention"?

- **Not knowledge** — vaccinations, screening, behaviour change are well understood
- **Implementation and trust** — text-message health alerts now compete with phishing scams, so people ignore them
- Prevention requires **public trust** that has been eroded

### Q: How does the Donabedian curve from L1 connect to the three shifts of the 10YHP?

- L1: acute care reaches diminishing and eventually negative returns at high spending
- L2: hospital→community and sickness→prevention **reallocate from overprovision (acute) to underprovision (community, prevention)**
- The curve is the economic justification; the shifts are the policy operationalisation

## Five enablers — what has to change underneath

### Q: What are the five enablers in the 10-Year Health Plan? (you don't need word-perfect labels — recognise them)

- **Operating models** — ICBs become strategic commissioners; NHS England + DHSC merge
- **Transparency** — provider league tables; outcome and waiting time comparison via NHS App
- **Workforce** — AI as clinical assistant; training overhauled
- **Innovation and technology** — five priority technologies
- **Finance** — value-based payment replacing activity-based

### Q: Name the five priority technologies of the 10YHP.

- **Data**
- **AI**
- **Genomics**
- **Wearables**
- **Robotics**

### Q: What headline numbers attach to each enabler?

- **Operating models**: **50%** headcount cut at NHS England + ICBs
- **Workforce**: international recruitment **<10% of new hires by 2035**
- **Innovation**: **£600m** Health Data Research Service (with Wellcome)
- **Finance**: **2% productivity gain/year** for 3 years; surplus by 2030

### Q: Why does Pinder describe finance as "the elephant in the room"?

- Workforce shortage was the bottleneck — but workforce is itself a money problem
- The "peace dividend" since the 1980s is ending; defence spend is rising
- Money the NHS thought it might receive is no longer secure

## Delivery tools — ICB pivot and PHM (cross-references L1)

### Q: What is "strategic commissioning" in the context of the ICB pivot?

- ICBs' **old job**: operational — running NHS services in their footprint
- ICBs' **new job**: **strategic commissioning** — using data to plan long-term population health, redesign care pathways, allocate resources, evaluate impact
- All while losing **50% of their staff**

### Q: What are the four stages of the Model ICB cycle (ICB Blueprint, May 2025)?

- **1. Understanding local context** — assess current and future population needs; identify underserved communities
- **2. Developing long-term population health strategy** — pathway redesign, value-based
- **3. Delivering through payer functions and resource allocation** — oversight, assurance
- **4. Evaluating impact** — usage, feedback, outcome measurement

### Q: Define Population Health Management (PHM).

- A **conceptual approach** that uses data to identify and stratify groups within a population
- Stratification is by **health needs and risk levels**
- Aim: **predict and mitigate health risks** before high-cost episodes occur

### Q: What are the four key components of PHM?

- **Data integration**
- **Risk stratification**
- **Proactive outreach**
- **Personalised care planning**

### Q: How does PHM differ from epidemiology?

- Epidemiology = surveillance and analysis
- PHM = also **operational delivery and system-level accountability**, not only surveillance
- PHM is held responsible for outcomes, not just for measuring them

⚠️ **Trap:** The slide explicitly says PHM has **"little evidence that it actually decreases cost."** Pinder calls it "Emperor's new clothes" — know the term and the components, but reject SBA distractors that say PHM has been shown to reduce per capita cost.

### Q: What does the PHM maturity matrix capture, and how detailed do I need to know it?

- 5-level scale: Reactive → Siloed → Coordinated → Integrated → **Predictive**
- Across 4 enablers (integration & delivery, data & systems, strategy & leadership, culture & readiness)
- *"You don't need to memorise the slide"* — Pinder
- Recognise: most NHS systems sit at Levels 1–2; goal is Level 5 (predictive, AI-driven)

## Horizon-scanning — atlas bolt-on

### Q: Climate-driven extreme events — what are the key UK health numbers and the most vulnerable group?

- Heat deaths projected: **1,602 (2007–18)** → **10,889 by 2050s** without adaptation
- **Older adults** are the greatest risk factor for both heat and cold mortality
- UKHSA **Weather-Health Alerting system** launched **2023**

### Q: Why does Pinder argue temperature (not vector-borne disease) will be the dominant climate-health threat in the UK?

- UK housing stock is poorly insulated for both hot summers and cold winters
- NHS hospital estate can't manage internal temperature
- Care/nursing homes are particularly vulnerable
- Direct temperature effects > vector-borne disease in the UK context

📎 Figure PVB-L2.1 — UKHSA Health Effects of Climate Change (2023)

### Q: Distinguish climate-pandemic adaptation from mitigation.

- **Adaptation** — responding **after** climate-driven impact (outbreak detection, pharmaceuticals)
- **Mitigation** — preventing the impact arising **upstream** (addressing spillover risk, emissions)
- Most pandemic frameworks heavily favour adaptation; mitigation is under-developed

### Q: Why is climate change expected to amplify pandemic risk?

- Encroachment on natural habitats + climate change → new zoonotic spillovers
- Once-in-100-year pandemic risk now estimated as 1-in-30 to 1-in-10
- 76.5% of climate-pandemic research focuses on **vector-borne** disease; non-vector zoonoses (Ebola, Hendra, Nipah, SARS-CoV-2) are under-studied

### Q: What share of UK and English public-sector emissions does the NHS produce?

- **~4%** of total UK carbon footprint
- **~40%** of all public-sector emissions in England

### Q: What are the NHS net-zero targets?

- **Direct emissions** → net zero **2040**, with interim **80% reduction between 2028–2032**
- **Carbon Footprint Plus** (supply chain + commuting) → net zero **2045**
- **Inhalers + anaesthetic gases** make up **20% of direct emissions** and have already been **cut by 33%**

### Q: Why is the two-tier system Pinder's most-emphasised horizon risk?

- It's **preventable** through policy choices (unlike geopolitical or climate risk)
- Once started, it's a **self-reinforcing cycle**: private growth → staff drift → NHS decline → more private uptake
- End state: NHS no longer functions as a universal service — undermines the L1 founding principles

### Q: What's the current UK rule on private/NHS care, and how does it differ from Singapore?

- UK: **must opt out** of NHS to use private — no top-up within an episode
- Singapore: tiered upgrades **within** publicly-funded system (premium ward etc.)
- UK rule protects against the two-tier dynamic; pressure on it is the concerning trend

### Q: What single statistic captures the rapid growth of for-profit involvement in NHS-funded care?

- **Cataract surgery: for-profit providers grew from 1% to 46% of NHS-funded cases between 2016–2022**

## Reusable reasoning pattern

🧠 **Direction-of-travel filter for NHS policy SBAs.** Every NHS reform since 2014 pulls in the same direction: **out of hospital, towards prevention, with better data**. When a stem describes a policy or intervention, ask three questions:

1. **Which way does it move the system?** — Toward community/prevention/digital (with the direction) or toward acute/curative/analogue (against)?
2. **Which level decides?** — Trust (provider) → ICB (commissioner) → NHS England (national coordination) → DHSC (political).
3. **Does an L1 framework underpin the answer?** — Donabedian's curve justifies reallocation; population health (level + distribution) defines the outcome; ICB architecture defines the actor; resilience explains the long-game investment case.

If the stem describes a policy moving the wrong way (e.g. concentrating care in fewer hospitals, ignoring inequalities), that's the wrong answer in a 10YHP-aligned question. If it describes a policy in the right direction but with weak evidence (e.g. "PHM has been proven to reduce costs"), the trap is the over-claim, not the direction.
