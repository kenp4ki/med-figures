## The idea — three founding principles

### Q: What are the three founding principles of the NHS?

- **Universal** — available to all
- **Free at the point of delivery**
- **Provision based on clinical need, not the ability to pay**

### Q: What three things do you actually pay for in the NHS?

- **Dentistry**
- **Optical** (glasses)
- **Prescriptions**

Carve-outs added after 1948 because they were judged too expensive to keep free.

### Q: Distinguish "the NHS in England" from "NHS England".

- **NHS in England** = the whole system, ~6,500–8,000 organisations sharing the blue logo
- **NHS England** = the corporate HQ that runs the system
- NHS England is being **abolished and absorbed into DHSC by October 2027** — the system itself is not affected

### Q: How many NHSs exist across the UK, and what does Northern Ireland call it?

- **Four NHSs**: England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland
- Northern Ireland calls it **Health and Social Care**, not NHS

⚠️ **Trap:** Slide and Take-Aways feature 1948 prominently, but Pinder said directly *"I'm not going to test you on [1948] in the exam."* Know the date but don't bank on it being a stem answer — the **principles** are the recall target.

## Who delivers the idea — structural atlas

### Q: Who is politically accountable for the NHS, and where do they sit?

- **Rt Hon Wes Streeting MP** — Secretary of State for Health and Social Care
- He sits in **DHSC** (Department of Health and Social Care)
- Accountable to Parliament for NHS spend

### Q: Who runs NHS England day-to-day, and what's happening to the organisation?

- **Sir Jim Mackey** — Chief Executive of NHS England
- NHS England is **scheduled to be abolished by October 2027** and absorbed into DHSC
- Some functions (e.g. Workforce Training and Education, IMT National) will land elsewhere in DHSC

### Q: What is an ICS, and what's the population scale it's designed to serve?

- **ICS = Integrated Care System** — the geographic unit of NHS planning
- **~1.5–2.5 million people** per ICS (target ~2 million)
- That scale is needed to plan primary, secondary, and specialist services together

### Q: What's the difference between an ICB and an ICP? (highest-yield single fact in this section)

- **ICB (Integrated Care Board)** = the **NHS-only** part of the ICS — makes NHS commissioning decisions (new hospital? GP system?)
- **ICP (Integrated Care Partnership)** = ICB + **local government** + **VCSE**
- VCSE = Voluntary, Community and Social Enterprise sector (charities)

⭐ **Lecturer quote:** *"The ICB for Board is the NHS part of the system. That's the key thing for you to take away from this."* If a stem says "the NHS body that commissions care for ~2 million people," it's the ICB.

### Q: What is a Foundation Trust, and how does it differ from a regular NHS Trust?

- A **subtype of Trust with extra financial freedoms**
- Can **retain surpluses** (regular Trust must return them to centre)
- Can **raise money on financial markets**
- Has a **higher private-practice cap**

### Q: What does Mid Staffs illustrate about Foundation Trust status?

- Trust cut staffing and safety to chase Foundation Trust financial criteria
- Patients drinking water from flower vases; many died
- Cautionary tale: financial incentives without safety oversight are dangerous

### Q: In NW London, which trust delivers mental health care?

- **West London NHS Trust**
- Last year's students didn't recognise this — classic SBA distractor
- **Acute trusts** (Imperial, Chelsea & Westminster) deal with physical health and have an ED; mental health trusts often combine MH with community services

## Why the idea is hard to deliver — fragmentation and system cost

### Q: Name the three fragmentation/integration dimensions in the NHS.

- **Vertical** — primary ↔ secondary care
- **Horizontal** — health ↔ social care
- **Triple / Third** — physical ↔ mental health

### Q: Match each fragmentation dimension to its concrete clinical example.

| Dimension | Clinical example | Integration target |
|---|---|---|
| Vertical | F1 in hospital pushing tasks back to GP | Primary + secondary as one system |
| Horizontal | "Exit block" — patient ready for discharge but no social care bed | NHS + local councils coordinated |
| Triple/Third | Mental health trusts run separately from acute trusts | Physical + mental + voluntary integrated |

### Q: What are the per-night costs of acute, intermediate, and social care beds?

- **Acute hospital bed**: £410/night (NHS pays)
- **Intermediate care bed**: £205/night ("Who pays?" — disputed boundary)
- **Social care bed**: £180/night (local authority pays)

⚠️ **Trap:** The intermediate bed is exactly the budget boundary where NHS and local-authority funding fight over responsibility. That's why patients pile up in expensive acute beds — the cheaper bed is technically available but no-one wants to pay.

### Q: Why is "exit block" a horizontal-fragmentation problem, not a clinical one?

- Patient is **medically fit for discharge** — clinically nothing more to do
- But **social care** (local-authority budget, means-tested) has no capacity to take them
- Patient stays in a £410 acute bed used as a £180 social-care bed
- The boundary that fails is **NHS ↔ council**, not doctor ↔ patient

### Q: How is social care different from NHS care in terms of who pays?

- **NHS care** — free at the point of delivery, not means-tested
- **Social care** — means-tested (you pay if you have assets), commissioned by **local government**
- Social care was **not nationalised in 1948** — it stayed with councils

## What we measure — population health (highest-yield concept)

⭐ **Lecturer-flagged.** Repeated three times, retested in practice question, named in TILO 2.

### Q: What are the two dimensions of population health?

- The **amount of health** in a population (the level)
- The **distribution of health** across that population (variation / inequalities)

Improving population health = **raise the line AND flatten it**.

### Q: A new policy raises mean life expectancy by 6 months but the gap between most-deprived and least-deprived widens. Has population health improved?

- **Partially.** The level improved; the distribution worsened
- By the two-dimensional definition, you've succeeded on one axis and failed on the other
- Chasing only the average can mask widening inequality

### Q: What four levers does the slide give for improving population health?

- Preventing disease
- Protecting from threats
- Promoting self-care and community resilience
- Improving health services

**Note:** only one of four ("improving health services") is healthcare itself. Three are upstream — sets up the diminishing-returns argument and L2's "sickness to prevention."

## Frameworks linking cost, quality, outcome — Triple and Quadruple Aim

### Q: Name the three corners of the IHI Triple Aim (Berwick 2008).

- **Population health**
- **Experience of care** (patient experience)
- **Per capita cost**

The three are interdependent — you can't drive one up by trashing the others.

### Q: What does the Quadruple Aim (Bodenheimer 2014) add to the Triple Aim?

- **Provider satisfaction** / workforce wellbeing

### Q: Why was workforce/provider satisfaction added as a fourth aim?

- For ~20 years the bottleneck on NHS performance was **money**
- For the last 5–6 years it has shifted — even with money, the NHS doesn't have the **staff**
- Workforce became a structural input, not a "nice to have"

### Q: Why does the Triple Aim measure "per capita cost" rather than total cost?

- Per capita cost reflects efficiency for each person served
- Forces the comparison against **opportunity cost** — what else could that pound have bought (education, defence, science)?

## The economic problem — diminishing returns

### Q: What does the Donabedian curve plot, and what shape does the health-benefit curve take?

- **x-axis**: healthcare resources
- **y-axis**: health benefit AND total cost (overlaid)
- **Health benefit** (cyan): inverted U — rises, plateaus, then **falls**
- **Total cost** (black): exponential rise

### Q: Name the three zones along the x-axis of the Donabedian curve.

- **Underprovision** (left) — adding resources gives big health gains
- **Optimum** — value sweet spot, where marginal benefit = marginal cost
- **Overprovision** (right) — additional resources cost more than they help, eventually causing harm

⚠️ **Easy-to-miss exam detail:** The health-benefit curve eventually goes **down**, not just plateaus. Overprovision actively **causes harm** (overdiagnosis, overtreatment, hospital-acquired infections, incidentalomas). SBA distractors will say "plateau" — wrong, it descends.

### Q: How does the Donabedian curve justify shifting NHS investment from acute care to prevention?

- If acute care is in the **overprovision zone**, marginal £ yields little or negative health
- Prevention is likely in the **underprovision zone** where marginal £ yields large health
- Reallocation maximises total population health

## Resilience — the "peacetime" principle

### Q: Define resilience.

- From Latin *resilire* = to bounce back
- The depth (physical, mental, structural, relational) to **absorb a shock and recover**
- Approximated as: net balance of risk factors and protective factors

### Q: At what three levels can resilience be analysed?

- **Individual** — depths of physical/mental health
- **Community** — cope with major events (pandemic, flood)
- **Health system** — absorb shocks without collapse

### Q: What is the "peacetime" principle for health-system resilience?

- Resilient systems must be **grown in peacetime** — sustained investment when there's no visible crisis
- You can't build resilience during the disaster; it must already be there
- Political problem: peacetime investment looks wasteful until the crisis arrives

## EPRR — emergency preparedness skeleton

```
EPRR cycle (closed loop)

   RISK MANAGEMENT half (before)
   Preparedness → Prediction & early warning
                                        ↓
   Mitigation & prevention ← Protection ← (pre-disaster)
                                        ↓
                                    DISASTER
                                        ↓
   CRISIS MANAGEMENT half (after)
   Impact assessment → Response → Recovery → Reconstruction
                                        ↓
   (back to Preparedness for next cycle)
```

### Q: What does EPRR stand for?

- **Emergency Preparedness, Resilience and Response**

⭐ **Lecturer:** *"I need you to know what EPRR stands for"* — the acronym expansion is examinable.

### Q: Name the three coordinating bodies for NHS emergency response.

- **Cabinet Office** — runs **COBRA** (Cabinet Office Briefing Room A) for national civil contingencies
- **NHS England** — coordinates NHS response
- **UKHSA** (UK Health Security Agency) — health protection (succeeded Public Health England in 2021)

### Q: Define the three types of NHS incident.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Business Continuity** | Disrupts normal service delivery below acceptable predefined levels | IT outage; staff sickness surge; ransomware attack on diagnostic system |
| **Critical** | Localised loss of ability to deliver critical services, or risk of harm to patients/staff | RAAC ceiling collapse; serious infection control breach; loss of power |
| **Major** | Serious consequences requiring special arrangements by ≥1 emergency responder agency | Mass casualty (transport disaster); pandemic surge; chemical exposure; terror attack |

### Q: A trust's IT system goes down for 8 hours overnight. What incident type?

- **Business Continuity Incident**
- Disrupts normal service delivery below acceptable predefined levels
- Doesn't cause loss of critical services or require multi-agency response

### Q: A ward ceiling collapses (RAAC) and the ward is closed. What incident type?

- **Critical Incident**
- Localised loss of ability to deliver critical services
- Internal to the trust, doesn't require multi-agency response

### Q: How many NHS incident response levels exist, and what defines Level 4?

- **Four levels** (1 → 4)
- **Level 4 = national incident** requiring NHS England national command and control
- The most recent Level 4 was **COVID-19**

⭐ **Lecturer-flagged:** *"I don't need you to learn the definitions of the different levels beyond the fact there are four levels and the top level is national."* Don't memorise Levels 1–3 wording — know the escalation pattern (organisation → ICS → region → national).

### Q: Match the three EPRR event typologies to their lead times.

| Event type | Lead time | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Sudden Impact** | Little or no warning | Transport accident; utility failure; terror attack |
| **Rising Tide** | Days to months — preparation possible | Pandemic; flooding; foot and mouth |
| **Foreseeable Event** | Clearly predicted in advance | Planned protests; large public gatherings |

### Q: What COVID lesson does Pinder draw about NHS surge response?

- The NHS is **good at surging** an acute response
- The NHS is **poor at sustaining the surge** over months or years
- This is why peacetime resilience matters — chronic capacity, not just acute mobilisation

## Reusable reasoning pattern

🧠 **NHS-architecture triangulation.** When a stem describes an NHS body acting at population scale, ask three questions in this order:

1. **What level am I at?** — Single organisation (Trust) → ICS sub-population → ICS-wide (~2M people, ICB or ICP) → NHS region → national (NHS England / DHSC) → political (Parliament).
2. **NHS-only or partnership?** — If decision involves only NHS commissioning, it's the **ICB**. If it involves local government and VCSE alongside the NHS, it's the **ICP**. Single letter, completely different scope.
3. **What's the function?** — Commissioning (planning, paying for services) vs providing (Trusts) vs accountability (DHSC) vs incident coordination (NHS England + Cabinet Office + UKHSA).

This triangulation collapses most NHS-architecture SBAs to one or two answer options.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: Three things to remember about ICBs that connect L1 to L2.

- ICB = **NHS-only** commissioner part of an ICS (~2M people)
- ICB will become a **strategic commissioner** (L2's 10YHP)
- ICB simultaneously faces **50% headcount cut** (L2's enabler reform)

### Q: How does the Donabedian curve set up L2's "sickness to prevention" shift?

- L1: acute care faces diminishing and eventually negative returns at high spending
- L2: the 10YHP shift "sickness → prevention" reallocates from the overprovision zone (acute) to the underprovision zone (prevention)
- The curve is the **economic justification** for the policy direction

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A NW London resident's care is commissioned by an organisation responsible for population-level NHS planning across about 2 million people. Which body? (A) NHS England (B) ICP (C) ICB (D) DHSC (E) The Trust

- **C — ICB.**
- ICB = NHS-only commissioner at population scale (~1.5–2.5M)
- ICP includes local government and VCSE — broader partnership, not commissioning decisions
- NHS England operates nationally; DHSC is the government department; Trusts provide care

### Q: SBA 2 — A 92-year-old has been medically fit for discharge for 8 days but is still in an acute bed. Which fragmentation dimension explains this?

- **Horizontal fragmentation**
- NHS (acute, free) ↔ social care (means-tested, council budget)
- Patient ready but no social care bed available — "exit block"

### Q: SBA 3 — A government adviser argues NHS spending should shift to early-years education and air quality. Which concept best supports this?

- **Donabedian's law of diminishing returns**
- Acute healthcare in the overprovision zone yields negative marginal benefit
- Triple Aim describes what to optimise but doesn't directly justify reallocation
- Vertical integration is about primary–secondary care

### Q: SBA 4 — Best captures the two dimensions of population health: (A) Mortality and morbidity (B) Amount of health and its distribution (C) Acute and chronic disease (D) Physical and mental (E) Triple and Quadruple Aim

- **B — amount of health and its distribution across the population**
- The most-repeated single fact in L1
- Other options are real distinctions but not the population-health framework

### Q: SBA 5 — A regional NHS England office is coordinating multiple trusts and ICBs for a multi-borough flooding event. NHS England Incident Management Team (National) has not been activated. Most likely level?

- **Level 3**
- Multiple organisations within an NHS England region; coordinated by NHS England with the ICB
- Level 4 would require national IMT activation (ruled out)
- Level 1 = single org; Level 2 = within one ICS

### Q: SBA 6 — A novel respiratory pathogen identified overseas with rising case numbers. Which event typology?

- **Rising Tide**
- Lead time of days to months allowing some preparation
- Distinct from Sudden Impact (no warning) and Foreseeable (predicted specific event)

### Q: SBA 7 — Which best describes the difference between Triple and Quadruple Aim?

- Quadruple adds **provider satisfaction / workforce wellbeing**
- Other three (population health, experience of care, per capita cost) are identical

### Q: SBA 8 — A trust achieves Foundation Trust status by aggressive cost-cutting that compromises patient safety. Which case is the canonical warning?

- **Mid Staffs**
- Chase for FT financial criteria led to staffing/safety cuts
- Multiple avoidable deaths
- Cited as the main lesson against unchecked financial incentives

### Q: SBA 9 — Three NHS-funded items that are NOT free at the point of delivery?

- **Dentistry, optical, prescriptions**
- Carve-outs added since 1948 because too expensive to keep free

### Q: SBA 10 — A ransomware attack knocks out the diagnostic IT system across a region for 6 months. Most appropriate incident classification?

- **Business Continuity Incident** (probably escalating to Critical depending on services lost)
- Disrupts normal service delivery below acceptable predefined levels
- Real example cited: South East London diagnostic system, ~6 months
- Not Major Incident — doesn't require multi-agency emergency-responder arrangements

### Q: SBA 11 — Which body coordinates national emergencies through the COBRA committee?

- **Cabinet Office**
- COBRA = **Cabinet Office Briefing Room (A)**
- NHS England coordinates NHS-side response within that

### Q: SBA 12 — Which best captures the "peacetime principle" for resilient health systems?

- Resilient systems must be **grown when no crisis is visible**
- Surge capacity and depth must already exist before they're needed
- COVID lesson: NHS could surge but couldn't sustain — peacetime under-investment caught up
