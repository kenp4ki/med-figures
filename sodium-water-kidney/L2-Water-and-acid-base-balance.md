## Section 1 — Foundations: osmolarity and body fluid distribution

🧠 **The one idea that unifies all of L2 (water half).** Water moves passively, so the kidney must (1) **build a hyperosmotic medullary interstitium** then (2) **decide whether water can cross the CD apical membrane** to reach it. AQP2 inserted = concentrate. AQP2 absent = dilute.

### Q: Define osmolarity and calculate it for 100 mmol/L glucose vs 100 mmol/L NaCl.

> **Osmolarity = concentration × number of dissociated particles** (mOsm/L).
> - 100 mmol/L glucose → **100 mOsm/L** (glucose doesn't dissociate)
> - 100 mmol/L NaCl → **200 mOsm/L** (Na⁺ + Cl⁻)

### Q: What's an "ineffective osmole" and what's the canonical example?

> An osmole that **crosses cell membranes freely** → equilibrates instantly → can't pull water across. Doesn't drive thirst or ADH.
>
> **Canonical example: urea.** Direct test in Tutorial Q2.

### Q: Body fluid distribution in a 70 kg adult — proportions and membranes?

> - Total body water ≈ **60% body weight**
> - **2/3 ICF, 1/3 ECF**
> - Of ECF: **3/4 extravascular, 1/4 intravascular (plasma)**
> - **Capillary wall** separates plasma from interstitial; **cell membrane** separates ECF from ICF
>
> 📎 Figure L2.2.

### Q: When you drink water orally, which compartment does it enter first?

> **ECF first** (gut → portal → plasma → interstitial), then equilibrates into ICF over minutes via osmosis.
>
> **Pattern:** acute oral water load drops *plasma* osmolarity before anything else. Tutorial Q3.

🧠 **See also:** L1 — ECF osmolarity and volume. L1 says total body Na sets ECF volume; L2 says total body water tunes osmolarity at constant Na.

## Section 2 — Building the gradient: countercurrent multiplication

```
Cortex      ─── 300 mOsm/L  (filtrate enters here)
Outer medulla ─── 600  
Inner medulla ─── 1200       ← deep tip = max concentration
```

⭐ The lecturer flags this slide explicitly. Walk it carefully.

### Q: Why is the loop of Henle called "counter-current"?

> Fluid moves in **opposite directions** in the descending and ascending limbs, which lie next to each other in the medulla. Allows osmotic equilibration between them.

### Q: What's the "single effect" the loop of Henle multiplies?

> **TAL actively pumps NaCl out** of the lumen into the interstitium, but is **water-impermeable**. So the lumen leaves dilute and the interstitium concentrates — that one event is the "single effect" that gets repeated.

### Q: Walk through how the medullary gradient is built (countercurrent multiplication).

> 1. Filtrate enters loop at 300 mOsm/L (isoosmotic)
> 2. Thin descending limb is **water-permeable, NaCl-impermeable** → water leaves down osmotic gradient → lumen concentrates
> 3. TAL is **NaCl-permeable (NKCC), water-impermeable** → NaCl pumped out → interstitium concentrates further, lumen leaves at ~100 mOsm/L
> 4. New filtrate cycles in; gradient builds further with each iteration
> 5. Steady state: cortex 300, deep medulla **1200**
>
> 📎 Figure L2.5.

### Q: Why is the TAL called the "diluting segment"?

> Active NaCl pumping out + no water permeability = lumen leaves **dilute** (~100 mOsm/L) regardless of plasma osmolarity.

🧠 **Builds-on:** L1 — Loop diuretics. Furosemide blocks NKCC at TAL → no active salt pumping → no gradient → urine cannot be concentrated even with maximal ADH. See also: L1 — Loop diuretics (furosemide). Tutorial Q7 hinges on this.

### Q: Where in the nephron does urine reach its highest osmolarity in a dehydrated patient?

> **Tip of the loop of Henle** (~1200 mOsm/L). The gradient is built whether dehydrated or not — the final urine *exits* matching the deep medullary tip in antidiuresis. Tutorial Q1.

## Section 3 — Urea recycling

⭐ **Two functions.** (1) Adds to the medullary osmotic gradient (urea contributes about half of the 1200 at the deep tip). (2) Lets you excrete urea in less water.

```
Collecting duct (high ADH) → UT-A1/UT-A3 export urea → medulla (~600 mOsm/L)
Thin descending limb (UT-A2) ← urea re-enters
Vasa recta (UT-B1) ← recycles to keep the reservoir
```

### Q: What two solutes build the medullary osmotic gradient and roughly how much does each contribute at the inner medullary tip?

> - **NaCl** from TAL active pumping: ~600 mOsm/L
> - **Urea** from collecting duct recycling: ~600 mOsm/L
> - Total ~**1200 mOsm/L**

### Q: Which urea transporters are upregulated by vasopressin (ADH), and which segment hosts them?

> **UT-A1 and UT-A3** in the **inner medullary collecting duct**. Together they let urea exit into the deep medullary interstitium when ADH is high.

### Q: Why must NH₄⁺ generated in the PCT be excreted, not reabsorbed?

> If reabsorbed, it's broken down in the liver to NH₃ + H⁺. That H⁺ would consume the new HCO₃⁻ the kidney just made → net zero. **The whole point would be defeated** (lecturer's own words).

### Q: A patient has a UT-B1 mutation. What happens to maximum urinary concentrating ability?

> UT-B1 is the **vasa recta urea transporter** that recycles urea back to keep the medullary reservoir. Mutation → impaired recycling → smaller urea contribution to gradient → **lower maximum urine osmolarity**. Tutorial Q4 distractor.

## Section 4 — Using the gradient: ADH/AVP

### Q: ADH — type, length, source, storage?

> - **Peptide hormone, 9 amino acids**
> - Made in **hypothalamus** (supraoptic + paraventricular nuclei)
> - Stored and released from **posterior pituitary**

### Q: ADH stimulators?

> - **↑plasma osmolarity** (osmoreceptors — sensitive, ~1% changes)
> - **Hypovolaemia / ↓BP** (baroreceptors — coarser, 5–10% needed)
> - **Nausea, angiotensin II, nicotine**

### Q: ADH inhibitors?

> - **↓plasma osmolarity**
> - **Hypervolaemia / ↑BP**
> - **Ethanol, ANP**

⚠️ **Trap.** Osmoreceptors are sensitive (1%); baroreceptors are coarse (5–10%). In **major haemorrhage**, ADH stays high even when osmolarity is low — baroreceptor signal overrides.

### Q: Step-by-step mechanism of ADH on the principal cell?

> 1. ADH binds **V2 receptor** (basolateral, Gs-coupled)
> 2. Adenylate cyclase → **cAMP** → PKA
> 3. PKA phosphorylates AQP2-bearing vesicles
> 4. Vesicles fuse with **apical membrane** → **AQP2 inserted**
> 5. Water crosses apical (AQP2) → exits basolateral (AQP3/AQP4) → blood
>
> 📎 Figure L2.8.

### Q: Compare aldosterone vs ADH on the principal cell — receptor, speed, effector, net effect.

> - **Aldosterone:** intracellular MR → transcription → ENaC + Na/K ATPase. Slow (hours). Net: ↑Na, ↑K secretion, ↑H secretion
> - **ADH:** V2 (Gs) → cAMP → PKA → AQP2 trafficking. Fast (minutes). Net: ↑water reabsorption
>
> Same cell, two independent machines.

🧠 **Builds-on:** L1 — Aldosterone in the cortical collecting duct. Both hormones converge on the same cell type but act on different transporters.

### Q: Diuresis (low/zero ADH) — final urine osmolarity and why?

> ~**50 mOsm/L (dilute)**. AQP2 absent from CD apical membrane → water trapped in lumen despite the medullary gradient. 📎 Figure L2.9.

### Q: Antidiuresis (high ADH) — final urine osmolarity and ceiling?

> Up to ~**1200 mOsm/L**. Cannot exceed the medullary tip because CD lumen equilibrates with surrounding interstitium. ADH also boosts NKCC, NCC, ENaC distally to drive Na out aggressively. 📎 Figure L2.10.

## Section 5 — ADH-related disorders

⭐ The diagnostic triad spine for Tutorial Q4 and a classic exam comparison.

| Disorder | ADH | Urine | Plasma Na | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Central DI (AVP deficiency) | ↓ | Dilute, polyuria | ↑ (water loss) | **Desmopressin** |
| SIADH | ↑ inappropriate | **Concentrated** | **↓ (hyponatraemia)** | **Tolvaptan / conivaptan** |
| Nephrogenic DI | normal/↑ | Dilute, polyuria | ↑ | **Thiazide + NSAIDs** |

📎 Figure L2.11.

### Q: Why does SIADH cause hyponatraemia?

> ADH driving water reabsorption when not needed → **water retained, Na unchanged** → dilution → ↓plasma Na. Patient is *euvolaemic* on exam — water spreads everywhere, doesn't pool.

### Q: How do thiazides paradoxically help nephrogenic DI?

> Thiazides cause mild Na+water loss → activates RAAS slightly → ↑proximal Na+water reabsorption → less fluid reaches the diluting segments → smaller urine volume. NSAIDs add by reducing prostaglandin antagonism of ADH. Mechanism not fully understood — examined as "thiazide paradox".

### Q: Distinguishing central from nephrogenic DI clinically?

> **Desmopressin (synthetic ADH) trial.** Central DI **responds** (urine concentrates). Nephrogenic DI **doesn't respond** (V2 or AQP2 broken). Both look identical at baseline.

⚠️ **Trap.** "Polyuria + polydipsia + dilute urine + ↑plasma Na" could be either DI. Don't pick central just because it's commoner — use the desmopressin test logic.

## Section 6 — Acid-base: the kidney's three jobs

🧠 **The one idea (acid-base half).** Diet + metabolism produce 50–100 mEq/day of acid. ECF holds only ~350 mEq HCO₃⁻ — **buffer would deplete in 4–7 days** if not replenished. So the kidney must (1) **reabsorb filtered HCO₃⁻**, (2) **secrete H⁺**, (3) **make new HCO₃⁻**.

### Q: Henderson-Hasselbalch — clinical form?

> **[H⁺] = 24 × P_CO₂ / [HCO₃⁻]**
>
> ↑P_CO₂ or ↓HCO₃⁻ → ↑[H⁺] → acidosis. Mirror for alkalosis.

### Q: Which parameter signals respiratory vs metabolic disorder?

> - Disturbance in **P_CO₂** → **respiratory** (lungs)
> - Disturbance in **[HCO₃⁻]** → **metabolic** (kidneys/metabolism)

### Q: % of filtered HCO₃⁻ reabsorbed at each segment?

> - **PCT 80%**
> - **TAL 10%**
> - **DCT 6%**
> - **CD 4%**
> - Total ≈ 100%; excretion ≈ 0

## Section 7 — Reabsorbing HCO₃⁻

### Q: Step-by-step reabsorption of filtered HCO₃⁻ in PCT?

> 1. CO₂ diffuses into the cell
> 2. **Carbonic anhydrase** catalyses CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
> 3. H⁺ exits to lumen via **NHE3 (Na/H antiporter)** + V-ATPase
> 4. HCO₃⁻ exits to blood via **NBC1 (Na/HCO₃ symporter, 1 Na : 3 HCO₃)**
> 5. Lumenal H⁺ + filtered HCO₃⁻ → CO₂ + H₂O (CA in lumen) → CO₂ diffuses back in
>
> 📎 Figure L2.13.

### Q: What's the trick the kidney uses to "reabsorb" HCO₃⁻?

> Doesn't directly transport the filtered HCO₃⁻ molecule. **Secretes an H⁺**, neutralises the lumenal HCO₃⁻ to CO₂, regenerates HCO₃⁻ inside the cell, ships *that* HCO₃⁻ to blood. Same accounting, different molecule.

### Q: α- vs β-intercalated cell — polarity and role?

> - **α-intercalated cell** = **acid handler.** Apical H⁺-ATPase + H/K-ATPase pump H out. Basolateral Cl/HCO₃ antiporter ships HCO₃⁻ to blood. Used in **acidosis**.
> - **β-intercalated cell** = base handler. Mirror polarity. Used in **alkalosis**.

### Q: Why does CA inhibitor cause both diuresis and metabolic acidosis?

> Block intracellular CA → no H⁺ to drive Na/H exchange → ↓Na reabsorption (diuresis) AND ↓HCO₃⁻ reclaimed (urinary HCO₃⁻ wasted → metabolic acidosis). Net urine becomes alkaline.

🧠 **See also:** L1 — Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors. L1 frames CA inhibitors as diuretics; L2 frames them as acid-base modifiers. Same drug, two angles.

## Section 8 — Making new HCO₃⁻

```
PCT:       Glutamine ─► 2 NH₄⁺ + 2 HCO₃⁻
                          │           │
                          ▼           ▼
                       URINE       BLOOD
                       (must)      (new)

DCT/CD:    Cellular H⁺ ─► HPO₄²⁻ ─► H₂PO₄⁻ (urine)
                          new HCO₃⁻ ─► BLOOD
```

### Q: Glutamine pathway — what enters, what's made, what's the net gain?

> - **Glutamine** enters PCT cell
> - Metabolised to **2 NH₄⁺ + 2 HCO₃⁻**
> - **2 HCO₃⁻** to blood (new buffer)
> - **2 NH₄⁺** must be excreted in urine (not reabsorbed — see next Q)
>
> 📎 Figure L2.14.

### Q: Phosphate buffer (titratable acid) pathway in DCT/CD?

> - α-intercalated cell pumps H⁺ into lumen (H-ATPase)
> - Lumenal HPO₄²⁻ buffers it → **H₂PO₄⁻ excreted** (titratable acid)
> - Each H secreted leaves a **new HCO₃⁻ in the cell** → blood

### Q: How does the kidney upregulate net acid excretion in chronic acidosis?

> Increases **glutamine metabolism** → more NH₄⁺ excreted (the amount of urinary NH₄⁺ is the kidney's measure of new HCO₃⁻ generated). Also ↑α-intercalated cell H⁺ pumping.

## Section 9 — The four primary acid-base disorders

⭐ The spine of every acid-base SBA. Memorise direction of change.

| Disorder | [HCO₃⁻] | P_CO₂ | pH | Compensation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Metabolic acidosis | ↓ | normal | ↓ | ↑ventilation; ↑renal HCO₃⁻ reabs/production |
| Metabolic alkalosis | ↑ | normal | ↑ | ↓ventilation; ↑renal HCO₃⁻ excretion |
| Respiratory acidosis | normal | ↑ | ↓ | Acute: cellular buffer. Chronic: ↑renal HCO₃⁻ |
| Respiratory alkalosis | normal | ↓ | ↑ | Acute: cellular buffer. Chronic: ↓renal HCO₃⁻ |

### Q: Reusable case method for any acid-base ABG?

> 1. Look at **pH** — acidosis (<7.4) or alkalosis (>7.4)?
> 2. Find the parameter moving the **same direction as pH disturbance** (↓pH + ↑P_CO₂ = respiratory acidosis; ↓pH + ↓HCO₃⁻ = metabolic acidosis)
> 3. Check if the **other parameter compensated** in the right direction
> 4. If both parameters worsen pH in the same direction → **mixed disorder**

### Q: Normal ABG values (memorise)?

> - **pH 7.4**
> - **[HCO₃⁻] 24 mEq/L**
> - **P_CO₂ 40 mmHg**

🧠 **Pattern.** In a single primary disorder with appropriate compensation, the two parameters move in **opposite directions on the pH scale** (one drives, one compensates). Same direction = mixed.

## SBAs — interleaved across sections

### Q: SBA1. Athlete drinks 2 L distilled water after a marathon. Which body fluid change is correct?

> - A. ↓ECF volume
> - B. **↓plasma osmolarity ✅**
> - C. ↓ICF osmolarity (only)
> - D. ↓interstitial fluid volume
>
> **B.** Water enters ECF first → dilutes plasma → ↓plasma osmolarity. ECF volume *rises* (A wrong). ICF osmolarity falls *equally* with ECF over minutes (not selectively, C wrong). D wrong direction. Tutorial Q3.

### Q: SBA2. Plasma solute that has NO effect on ADH production?

> - A. Glucose
> - B. **Urea ✅**
> - C. Salt
> - D. Ethanol
>
> **B.** Urea is an **ineffective osmole** — crosses cell membranes freely → no osmotic gradient on osmoreceptors → no ADH stimulus. Salt and glucose are effective osmoles. Ethanol *inhibits* ADH actively (different mechanism, not "no effect").

### Q: SBA3. Highest urine osmolarity among 4 patients: V2 LoF mutation; ADH deficiency; UT-B1 mutation; hepatic cirrhosis?

> - A. V2 LoF — nephrogenic DI → dilute
> - B. ADH deficiency — central DI → dilute
> - C. UT-B1 — impaired urea recycling → can't max-concentrate
> - D. **Hepatic cirrhosis ✅**
>
> **D.** Cirrhosis → effective hypovolaemia → max ADH + max aldosterone → max urinary concentration. The other three all *impair* concentration. Tutorial Q4.

### Q: SBA4. Patient with central DI given desmopressin. Immediate cellular event?

> - A. ↑MR binding
> - B. **↑V2 → cAMP → AQP2 insertion ✅**
> - C. ↑ENaC expression
> - D. ↑NKCC in TAL
>
> **B.** Desmopressin is V2-selective. A is aldosterone. C is aldosterone. D is a slow secondary ADH effect, not the immediate event.

### Q: SBA5. 60-year-old smoker: Na 122, urine osm 600, normal renal function, euvolaemic. Diagnosis?

> - A. Central DI
> - B. Nephrogenic DI
> - C. **SIADH ✅**
> - D. Primary polydipsia
>
> **C.** Hyponatraemia + **concentrated** urine + euvolaemic = SIADH. Both DIs give *dilute* urine. Primary polydipsia gives dilute urine + low Na from over-drinking.

### Q: SBA6. Trekker in Himalayas: pH 7.6, HCO₃⁻ 16, P_CO₂ 25. Diagnosis?

> - A. Metabolic acidosis
> - B. Metabolic alkalosis
> - C. Respiratory acidosis
> - D. **Respiratory alkalosis ✅**
>
> **D.** pH ↑ = alkalosis. P_CO₂ low = respiratory cause. Altitude → hyperventilation → blow off CO₂ → respiratory alkalosis. ↓HCO₃⁻ is *renal compensation*, not the primary disorder. Tutorial Q8.

### Q: SBA7. Patient: pH 7.1, HCO₃⁻ 16, P_CO₂ 30. What's the compensatory mechanism?

> - A. Renal — ↓HCO₃⁻ reabsorption
> - B. Renal — ↓H⁺ excretion
> - C. Respiratory — hypoventilation
> - D. **Respiratory — hyperventilation ✅**
>
> **D.** Primary = metabolic acidosis (↓HCO₃⁻). Compensation = blow off CO₂ to raise pH = hyperventilation (P_CO₂ now 30, below normal 40). A and B worsen acidosis. C worsens it. Tutorial Q9.

### Q: SBA8. Asthmatic with diarrhoea: pH 7.1, HCO₃⁻ 16, P_CO₂ 45. Diagnosis?

> - A. Metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation
> - B. Respiratory acidosis with metabolic compensation
> - C. Mixed alkalosis
> - D. **Mixed acidosis ✅**
>
> **D.** Both HCO₃⁻ low (metabolic) AND P_CO₂ high (respiratory) → both pulling pH down → mixed. Diarrhoea (HCO₃⁻ loss) + asthma exacerbation (CO₂ retention). Tutorial Q10.

⚠️ **Trap.** A chronic asthmatic may have a baseline elevated HCO₃⁻ (chronic respiratory acidosis compensation). Acute diarrhoea drops the HCO₃⁻ sharply, unmasking both insults. Always check for two simultaneous disorders.

## Reusable cross-lecture pattern

🧠 **The principal cell hosts two hormones running two missions.** Aldosterone (L1) controls Na/K/H. ADH (L2) controls water. They're independently regulated but commonly co-activated (volume contraction triggers both). When solving a renal-physiology stem, ask: *which lever is broken — Na (aldosterone arm)? Water (ADH arm)? Or the medullary gradient (TAL/urea)?*
