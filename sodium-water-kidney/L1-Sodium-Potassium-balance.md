## Section 1 — Foundations

🧠 **The one idea that makes all of L1 derivable.** Sodium controls *volume*, not *concentration*. Plasma osmolarity is held tight at 285–295; whenever Na arrives, water follows to preserve it. So total body Na sets ECF volume, which sets blood pressure. Every regulator (RAAS, ANP, sympathetic, every diuretic) is a lever on "how much Na the kidney keeps".

```
Na load ─────► ECF volume ─────► Blood pressure
   ▲                                    │
   └────── kidney retains/excretes ◄────┘
```

### Q: Normal plasma osmolarity range?

> **285–295 mOsmol/L.** Tight homeostatic set point. Urine osmolarity, by contrast, swings 50–1200.

### Q: Approximate ECF [Na⁺] and [K⁺]? Why does the difference matter?

> - Na⁺ ≈ **140 mmol/L**
> - K⁺ ≈ **4 mmol/L**
> - **35-fold difference** → Na drives ECF volume; K can't (even huge K swings are osmotically trivial vs Na). Reason behind Tutorial Q5.

### Q: % of filtered Na⁺ reabsorbed at each segment?

> - **PCT 67%**
> - **TAL 25%**
> - **DCT 5%**
> - **CD 3%**
> - Excreted **<1%**
>
> 📎 Figure L1.5 — Na⁺ handling in the nephron.

### Q: What does dietary Na load do to body weight, and why?

> ↑Na intake → transient positive Na balance → obligatory water retention → **~1 kg body weight gain** → new steady state when excretion catches up. Mirror image on returning to lower intake. 📎 Figure L1.2.

### Q: Why does ↑Na intake raise BP rather than raise plasma osmolarity?

> Osmolarity *would* rise but can't — thirst + ADH instantly pull water in to dilute. Water comes with the Na. **Volume rises, osmolarity stays constant, BP rises.**

## Section 2 — How the kidney decides how much Na to keep

⭐ **The four-lever map.** Three retention levers + one excretion lever. Memorise this and slide 18 collapses to a single image.

| Lever | Direction | Sites of action |
|---|---|---|
| Sympathetic (β₁) | Retain | AA constriction (↓GFR) + PCT |
| Angiotensin II | Retain | PCT, JGA, DCT, CT |
| Aldosterone | Retain | DCT, CT only |
| ANP | Excrete | ↑GFR, ↓Na in PCT, JGA, DCT, CT |

📎 Figure L1.9 — Master Na excretion control diagram.

### Q: Walk through tubuloglomerular feedback (macula densa) step by step.

> 1. **High tubular Na** at DCT
> 2. Macula densa cells take up Na/Cl via **NKCC (triple transporter)**
> 3. Release **adenosine**
> 4. Adenosine acts on **extraglomerular mesangial cells**
> 5. ↓Renin + afferent arteriolar SMC contraction
> 6. ↓Perfusion pressure → **↓GFR**
>
> 📎 Figure L1.8 — JGA microanatomy.

### Q: Why does the kidney use *tubular Na at the DCT* as its sensing variable?

> **Pattern:** Na that escaped the upstream segments and reached the DCT is a proxy for whole-body Na status. Too much getting that far = body has plenty → pull GFR back via TGF.

### Q: RAAS — what makes Ang II, what does Ang II do, what makes aldosterone, what does aldosterone do?

> - **Renin** (from JG cells, ↓BP / ↓tubular Na / sympathetic) cleaves **angiotensinogen** (liver) → **Ang I** → **ACE** (lung) → **Ang II**
> - Ang II: vasoconstriction (raises BP), drives **aldosterone** from zona glomerulosa, ↑Na PCT
> - Aldosterone: ↑Na reabsorption + **↑K⁺ secretion + ↑H⁺ secretion** in DCT/CT

### Q: Aldosterone — class, source, two main release stimuli, speed of action?

> - **Steroid hormone**
> - **Adrenal cortex, zona glomerulosa**
> - Released by **Ang II** and **↓BP via baroreceptors**
> - **Slow** (hours) — works via MR → transcription. 📎 Figure L1.11.

### Q: How does aldosterone act on the principal cell?

> 1. Diffuses through membrane
> 2. Binds **mineralocorticoid receptor (MR)** in cytoplasm
> 3. MR translocates to nucleus → drives transcription
> 4. Upregulates **ENaC** (apical Na entry) + **Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase** (basolateral)
> 5. Net: Na in, K out, lumen-negative voltage drives K⁺ secretion + H⁺ secretion
>
> 📎 Figure L1.14.

⭐ **Aldosterone excess → hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis.** The single line linking L1 to L2's acid-base section. Aldosterone secretes Na's two unwanted partners (K and H).

🧠 **Builds-on:** L2 — α-intercalated cell uses the H⁺ pump that aldosterone upregulates. See also: L2 — Reabsorption of bicarbonate ion.

### Q: ANP — source, trigger, four actions, signalling pathway?

> - Made in **atria** (BNP in ventricles)
> - Released by **atrial stretch** (↑volume / ↑BP)
> - Actions: vasodilation; ↓Na PCT + CD; **↓renin + ↓aldosterone**; ↑GFR
> - Receptor with built-in **guanylyl cyclase** → cGMP → PKG (not Gs/cAMP)
>
> 📎 Figure L1.16.

🧠 **Pattern.** ANP is the *anti-aldosterone* — undoes everything aldosterone does. Different speed (ANP minutes, aldosterone hours), different signalling (cGMP vs transcription).

### Q: Why is ANP's signalling pathway distinguishing in exam stems?

> Most peptide hormones use **Gs/cAMP**. ANP uses **cGMP** via a receptor with intrinsic guanylyl cyclase. Stem trap: "peptide hormone, must be cAMP" → wrong for ANP.

## Section 3 — Pressure sensing and the volume-stress diagrams

⭐ **The pressure-sensor map.** Two sides, three responses.

| Side | Locations | What they signal when firing falls |
|---|---|---|
| Low-pressure (volume) | Atria, RV, pulmonary vasculature | ↑sympathetic + ↑ADH |
| High-pressure (BP) | Carotid sinus, aortic arch | ↑sympathetic + ↑ADH (via brainstem) |
| High-pressure (renal) | Juxtaglomerular apparatus | **↑renin** (direct, fast) |

### Q: What turns on / off when blood volume falls (volume contraction)?

> - **On:** sympathetic, renin → Ang II → aldosterone, ADH
> - **Off:** ANP/BNP
> - Net: ↓Na excretion, ↓water excretion, vasoconstriction → ↑BP
>
> 📎 Figure L1.17b.

### Q: What turns on / off when blood volume rises (volume expansion)?

> - **On:** ANP/BNP from atrial stretch
> - **Off:** sympathetic, renin → Ang II → aldosterone, ADH
> - Net: ↑Na excretion, ↑water excretion → ↓BP
>
> 📎 Figure L1.17a.

🧠 **See also:** L2 — Body fluid distribution. The ECF volume on this page is what physiology textbooks define on L2's slide 5.

## Section 4 — Diuretics

⭐ **The diuretic-segment map.** Each class targets one transporter. Memorise the segment, and the rest follows.

| Class | Site | Transporter | Distal Na | K⁺ effect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CA inhibitors | PCT | Carbonic anhydrase → ↓Na/H exchange | ↑ | ↑ wasted |
| Loop (furosemide) | TAL | NKCC | ↑↑↑ | ↑↑ wasted |
| Thiazide | Early DCT | NCC | ↑↑ | ↑ wasted |
| K-sparing (spironolactone) | DCT/CCD | MR (or ENaC) | ↑ | ↓ spared |

📎 Figure L1.19 — Diuretic site map across the nephron.

### Q: Why do non-K-sparing diuretics cause hypokalaemia?

> Block upstream Na reabsorption → **↑Na delivery to CD** → ↑tubular flow rate → bends **primary cilium** on principal cells → activates **PDK1** → ↑Ca²⁺ → opens apical K⁺ channels → ↑K⁺ secretion → hypokalaemia. 📎 Figure L1.23.

### Q: Why does furosemide (and not thiazide) impair urine concentrating ability?

> Furosemide blocks **NKCC in TAL** — the active salt-pumping step that **builds the medullary gradient**. No gradient → ADH has nothing to pull water against. Thiazide acts in DCT, *after* the gradient is built. Tutorial Q7 hinges on this.

🧠 **See also:** L2 — Countercurrent multiplication.

### Q: Why are thiazides hypocalciuric (and loop diuretics hypercalciuric)?

> Thiazide blocks NCC → cell becomes Cl-depleted, hyperpolarises → drives basolateral Ca²⁺ extrusion → **↑Ca²⁺ reabsorption**. Useful in osteoporosis + recurrent Ca stones. Loop diuretics do the opposite (lose Ca with NaCl in TAL).

### Q: Why is spironolactone K-sparing whereas amiloride is also K-sparing — same mechanism?

> Both prevent ENaC-driven Na reabsorption in the principal cell → no lumen-negative voltage → no driving force for K⁺ secretion. **Spironolactone** blocks the MR upstream; **amiloride** blocks ENaC directly. Liddle's syndrome responds to amiloride (ENaC gain-of-function) but **not** spironolactone (because aldosterone isn't driving the channel — the channel is broken on its own).

## Section 5 — Disorders of Na regulation

### Q: Hypoaldosteronism — what's altered, what compensates, clinical features?

> - **Defect:** ↓Na reabsorption in distal nephron → ↑urinary Na loss → ↓ECF volume
> - **Compensation:** ↑renin, ↑Ang II, ↑ADH
> - **Features:** dizziness, low BP, salt craving, palpitations

### Q: Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's) — what's altered, what compensates, clinical features?

> - **Defect:** ↑Na reabsorption distally → ↑ECF volume → **HTN**
> - **Compensation:** ↓renin, ↓Ang II, ↓ADH; ↑ANP/BNP
> - **Features:** hypertension, **muscle weakness (from hypokalaemia)**, polyuria, thirst

### Q: Liddle's syndrome — what's the defect and what's the biochemical fingerprint?

> - **Inherited gain-of-function ENaC mutation** — channel constitutively active
> - **HTN + hypokalaemic alkalosis**, just like Conn's
> - **But** renin AND aldosterone both **suppressed** (volume expansion feedback)
> - Treat with **amiloride** (direct ENaC blocker), NOT spironolactone

⚠️ **Trap.** "Refractory HTN + hypokalaemia + low renin + low aldosterone" → Liddle's, not Conn's. Conn's would have ↑aldosterone with ↓renin.

### Q: Gitelman's syndrome — defect, biochemistry, mimic?

> - **Loss-of-function NCC mutation** (DCT)
> - **Mimics chronic thiazide use:** hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, hypocalciuria, metabolic alkalosis, **normal-low BP**
> - Distinguishes from Liddle's by BP and from Bartter's (NKCC mutation → mimics loop diuretic)

## Section 6 — Potassium balance

🧠 **The one idea for K⁺.** K matters because of **excitable membranes** (nerve, muscle, heart), not volume. Two lines of defence: **immediate** (cells take it up via insulin) and **slow** (kidneys excrete via principal cells).

### Q: Plasma vs intracellular [K⁺], and clinical effect of swings either way?

> - Plasma 3–5 mmol/L; intracellular ~150 mmol/L
> - **High K⁺:** depolarises membranes → arrhythmias
> - **Low K⁺:** hyperpolarises → arrhythmias including **asystole**

### Q: What drives cellular K⁺ uptake immediately after a meal? Clinical relevance?

> - **Insulin** (primary — activates Na/H exchanger → cell more negative → drives K in via Na/K ATPase)
> - **Aldosterone**, **adrenaline** also push K into cells
> - Clinical: **insulin + glucose IV** treats acute hyperkalaemia
>
> 📎 Slide 46 cell diagram (not separately cropped).

### Q: K⁺ handling along the nephron in depletion vs normal/high intake?

> - **PCT 67% reabsorbed; TAL 20% reabsorbed** — fixed
> - Distal nephron is variable:
>   - Depletion: small distal *reabsorption*; final excretion ~1%
>   - Normal/high intake: large distal *secretion*; final excretion 15–80%
>
> 📎 Figure L1.22.

### Q: Four stimulators of K⁺ secretion?

> 1. **↑plasma [K⁺]**
> 2. **↑aldosterone**
> 3. **↑tubular flow rate** (via primary cilium → PDK1 → Ca²⁺ → apical K channel)
> 4. **↑plasma pH** (alkalosis)

⭐ Tutorial Q5 (low plasma K can't shift water) and Q6 (diuretics → ↑K secretion via flow) both test this list.

### Q: Hypokalaemia — common causes (lecturer flagged ~20% of inpatients)?

> - Inadequate intake (processed food)
> - **Diuretics** (the flow mechanism)
> - **Vomiting** (gastric loss + aldosterone activation)
> - Diarrhoea
> - **Gitelman's** syndrome (NCC mutation)

### Q: Hyperkalaemia — common causes?

> - **K-sparing diuretics**, **ACE inhibitors** (block aldosterone)
> - Elderly
> - **Severe diabetes** (insulin deficiency → K stays out of cells)
> - **Kidney disease**

🧠 **See also:** L2 — Acid-base imbalance. Hyperkalaemia and acidosis often co-exist (acidosis drives K out of cells; aldosterone deficiency causes both).

## Section 7 — Reusable reasoning patterns

### Q: A patient has hypertension + hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis. What three diagnoses must I distinguish, and how?

> Look at **renin and aldosterone**:
> - Both **high** → secondary hyperaldosteronism (renovascular HTN)
> - Aldosterone **high**, renin **low** → primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's)
> - Aldosterone **low**, renin **low** → **Liddle's** (or apparent mineralocorticoid excess)

### Q: A patient is on a diuretic and develops hypokalaemia. What's the unifying mechanism?

> **↑Distal flow → ↑K⁺ secretion** via primary cilium → PDK1 → apical K channel. Same mechanism whether it's loop, thiazide, or CA inhibitor. K-sparing escapes by blocking the principal cell directly.

### Q: A patient on furosemide can't concentrate urine despite max ADH. Why?

> NKCC blockade in TAL **destroys the medullary gradient**. AQP2 inserts but no osmotic driving force exists. Tutorial Q7. See L2 — Countercurrent multiplication.

## SBAs — interleaved across sections

### Q: SBA1. A 55-year-old man has primary aldosteronism. Most likely plasma changes?

> - A. ↑Na, ↑K, ↑pH
> - B. ↑Na, ↓K, ↑pH ✅
> - C. ↓Na, ↑K, ↓pH
> - D. ↑Na, ↓K, ↓pH
>
> **B.** Aldosterone retains Na (mild), secretes **K** (hypokalaemia) and **H⁺** (alkalosis). A reverses K. C reverses everything. D wrong on pH — aldosterone secretes acid → pH rises.

### Q: SBA2. A volume-overloaded patient has high ANP. Compared to baseline, ANP would:

> - A. ↑renin, ↑Na reabsorption
> - B. ↓renin, ↑Na reabsorption
> - C. ↑renin, ↓Na reabsorption
> - D. ↓renin, ↓Na reabsorption ✅
>
> **D.** ANP suppresses renin AND inhibits Na reabsorption (PCT, CD). Anti-RAAS hormone.

### Q: SBA3. A 28-year-old man: refractory HTN + hypokalaemia + low renin + low aldosterone. Diagnosis?

> - A. Conn's syndrome
> - B. Renovascular HTN
> - C. **Liddle's syndrome ✅**
> - D. Phaeochromocytoma
>
> **C.** Conn's would have ↑aldosterone. Renovascular would have ↑renin AND ↑aldosterone. Phaeo doesn't typically cause hypokalaemia. Liddle's is the only one with both suppressed.

### Q: SBA4. A 70-year-old with CKD starts ramipril. Two weeks later K⁺ is 6.2. Mechanism?

> - A. Primary cilium activation in CCD
> - B. **↓aldosterone from blocked Ang II → ↓K⁺ secretion ✅**
> - C. ↑NKCC in TAL
> - D. ↑Na reabsorption in PCT
>
> **B.** ACE inhibitors → ↓Ang II → ↓aldosterone → ↓K secretion (less Na/K ATPase, less ENaC). A would *increase* K secretion. C is loop-diuretic mechanism. D doesn't apply.

### Q: SBA5. A patient with primary hyperaldosteronism has urinary K⁺ excretion 80% of filtered. Which combination most directly drives this?

> - A. **↑aldosterone, ↑distal flow, ↑plasma pH ✅**
> - B. ↓aldosterone, ↓distal flow, ↓plasma pH
> - C. ↑aldosterone, ↓distal flow, ↑plasma pH
> - D. ↓aldosterone, ↑distal flow, ↑plasma pH
>
> **A.** All four K-secretion stimulators are present in Conn's: aldosterone (definition), increased distal flow (more Na delivered by pressure natriuresis), alkalosis (aldosterone secretes H), and elevated plasma K (response variable). Multifactorial.

⚠️ **Trap.** Hyperaldosteronism's K wasting isn't "just aldosterone" — alkalosis and ↑flow add to it. That's why Conn's gives such severe hypokalaemia.
