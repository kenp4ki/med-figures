## The unifying intuition

The endothelium is an active tissue maintaining an **anti-inflammatory, anti-thrombotic, anti-proliferative balance**. CV risk factors (chronic) or cytokine storms (acute) flip the balance to **pro-inflammatory, pro-thrombotic, pro-angiogenic** — and that flip is the proximate cause of atherosclerosis, COVID vascular disease, and a long tail of other conditions.

## Section 1 — Endothelium as an organ

### Q: Why does the endothelium have to be one cell thick?

- Blood must flow smoothly inside vessels
- A bumpy/multi-layered lining would disturb flow → damaging (this principle returns in atherosclerosis at branch points)
- **Contact inhibition** at cell–cell junctions stops ECs proliferating once they touch — keeps the monolayer monolayered

### Q: What is the surface area and weight of the human endothelium?

- Surface area **>1000 m²**
- Weight **>100 g**
- ECs are flat: **1–2 μm thick × 10–20 μm wide**
- **98% of all ECs live in the microvasculature** — "blood vessels" really means "capillaries"

### Q: Define endothelium vs epithelium etymologically.

- *Endo-* (inside) + *-thelium* (covering layer) → lines inside-the-body surfaces (vessels, heart chambers)
- *Epi-* (outside/upon) + *-thelium* → lines outside-facing surfaces (skin, gut lumen)
- The **endocardium = endothelium of the heart chambers and valves**

### Q: Name the three layers of a blood vessel wall, inside out.

- **Tunica intima** — endothelium + basement membrane + thin connective tissue
- **Tunica media** — smooth muscle cells (between internal + external elastic membranes)
- **Tunica adventitia** — connective tissue, nerves, **vasa vasorum** (vessels supplying the vessel wall)

📎 Figure L1.2 — Blood vessel wall layers.

### Q: Which vessel types lack a three-layer wall, and what do they have instead?

- **Capillaries and venules** — too small for three layers
- They have: **EC + basement membrane + pericytes** (mural support cells)

📎 Figure L1.3 — Capillary structure.

> ⚠️ **Trap:** if a stem asks which vessel type lacks smooth muscle, the answer is capillary (and often post-capillary venule). Don't pick a small artery.

### Q: Why flag vasa vasorum now?

- They reappear in advanced atherosclerosis as the source of pathological angiogenesis invading the plaque
- Plaque rupture risk rises when these new microvessels bleed into the plaque

## Section 2 — The six functions and the homeostatic balance

📎 Figure L1.4 — Endothelial homeostatic balance.

### Q: Name the six functions the endothelium regulates.

- Vascular tone
- Permeability
- Haemostasis & thrombosis
- Inflammation
- Angiogenesis
- Tissue homeostasis & regeneration

### Q: For each function, what's the resting state and the activated state?

| Function | Resting (anti-) | Activated (pro-) |
|---|---|---|
| Vascular tone | NO, prostacyclin (vasodilator) | ACE/Ang II, TXA₂, endothelin (vasoconstrictor) |
| Permeability | Tight junctions | Leakage |
| Haemostasis | Prostacyclin, thrombomodulin, antithrombin, tPA, heparan | vWF, TXA₂, tissue factor, PAI-1 |
| Inflammation | Resists leukocytes | Selectins, ICAMs, VCAM expressed |

(Angiogenesis — quiescent vs growth-factor-secreting; tissue homeostasis — angiocrine signals normal vs disrupted.)

> ⭐ **High-yield framework:** memorise the six functions and the structure (each has a pro/anti pair). When you see a new molecule, slot it into "which function, pro or anti?" — you don't need to memorise every molecule.

### Q: Why does the homeostatic balance matter clinically?

- Healthy endothelium = balance maintained
- Acute injury = balance briefly flipped pro-thrombotic to seal injury, then flipped back
- **Disease = balance stuck flipped** — chronic in atherosclerosis, acute in severe COVID

## Section 3 — Endothelial heterogeneity (organotypic vasculature)

📎 Figure L1.5 — Organotypic vasculature across tissues.

### Q: Why is the blood–brain barrier endothelium ultra-tight?

- Brain sits inside a rigid skull with no room for oedema
- Tight EC junctions keep plasma proteins and immune cells out of brain parenchyma
- Loss of BBB integrity → cerebral oedema → herniation risk

### Q: Why are liver sinusoid ECs deliberately leaky (fenestrated)?

- Liver is a filtration organ — must let plasma + particles cross easily
- Fenestrations enable rapid hepatocyte exposure to incoming portal blood

### Q: What does "organotypic vasculature" mean?

- ECs in different organs differ structurally, functionally, and in gene expression
- Same EC lineage; the **tissue microenvironment shapes the phenotype**
- Conversely, EC phenotype shapes the tissue (angiocrine — see next section)

> 🧠 **Pattern:** when an exam asks why a vascular problem is worse in one organ, think about its organotypic phenotype. BBB breakdown is devastating because of the skull. Liver fenestrations are normal physiology, not pathology.

## Section 4 — Angiocrine factors — "blood vessels are not just pipes"

📎 Figure L1.6 — Cardiomyocyte (red) + capillary (green) close-apposition.

### Q: Define angiocrine factors.

- Factors **produced by endothelial cells** that maintain tissue homeostasis and regeneration
- Tissue-specific (heart ECs make different angiocrines from kidney ECs)
- Bidirectional — tissue microenvironment feeds back to shape EC phenotype

### Q: Why is the heart's EC story particularly emphasised?

- ECs are the most abundant cell type in the heart by count (capillaries densely woven through myocardium)
- Capillaries sit directly against cardiomyocytes; signal via autocrine + paracrine + microvesicles + transcription factors
- **Cardiomyocyte health depends on adjacent EC health**

### Q: What is endothelial resilience?

- Ability of ECs to maintain or return to homeostasis under stressors (CV risk factors)
- Intrinsic molecular mechanisms actively oppose damaging effects of smoking/HTN/glucose/oxLDL/inflammation
- NHLBI strategic priority because reinforcing it could treat many diseases at once

## Section 5 — Sprouting angiogenesis (cascade)

📎 Figure L1.8 — Sprouting angiogenesis cascade.

### Pathway skeleton

```
HYPOXIA
  ↓
(1) Angiogenic factor production (VEGF)
(2) Release into tissue
(3) EC receptor binding → intracellular signalling
(4) EC activation + basement membrane degradation
(5) EC proliferation
(6) Directional migration (up the hypoxia gradient)
(7) ECM remodelling
(8) Tube formation
(9) Loop formation + differentiation
(10) Vascular stabilisation (pericyte recruitment)
```

### Q: What is the single trigger of angiogenesis, and what is the main signal it releases?

- Trigger = **hypoxia**
- Main signal = **VEGF** (vascular endothelial growth factor)
- Hypoxic tissue calls for new vessels; nearby ECs respond

### Q: What's the four-point compressed version of the cascade you actually need?

- (1) Hypoxia is the trigger
- (2) VEGF is the main signal
- (3) Sprouting is from existing vessels (not de novo)
- (4) Cells temporarily lose contact inhibition + degrade basement membrane to migrate

### Q: Angiogenesis vs vasculogenesis — what's the difference?

- **Angiogenesis** = new vessels from existing vessels (the main adult process)
- **Vasculogenesis** = vessel formation from progenitor cells de novo (mainly embryonic)

### Q: Name three physiological and three pathological contexts for angiogenesis.

- Physiology: development, menstrual cycle, wound healing
- Pathology: **cancer, atherosclerosis**, retinopathies (also chronic inflammation, ischaemic disease, vascular malformations)
- "Different angiogenic microenvironments" — pathway dominance varies by context (why some anti-angiogenic drugs are tolerated)

### Q: What is the cancer angiogenic switch?

- Small tumour survives by diffusion alone (pre-angiogenic)
- Tumour core becomes hypoxic → VEGF release
- Tumour recruits its own blood supply → can grow without limit + metastasise
- **Anti-VEGF antibodies (e.g. bevacizumab) target this switch** [Extra context — classically examined]

## Section 6 — Bleeding and thrombosis

📎 Figure L1.10 — Bleeding vs haemostasis vs thrombosis (three-panel).
📎 Figure L1.11 — Vessel injury sequence and clotting cascade.

### Q: Define haemostasis, bleeding, thrombosis.

- **Haemostasis** (*haem-* blood + *-stasis* stopping) = normal clotting in response to vessel injury
- **Bleeding** = insufficient haemostasis
- **Thrombosis** = excessive or spontaneous clotting in an intact vessel

### Q: Three stages of normal haemostasis.

- **Vasoconstriction** — injured vessel narrows, slows flow
- **Platelet plug (primary haemostasis)** — platelets adhere to exposed collagen via VWF, activate, aggregate
- **Clot formation (secondary haemostasis)** — coagulation cascade → thrombin → fibrin weaves through plug

### Q: What is the lecturer's "partnership" framing?

- Control of bleeding = **partnership between blood and blood vessels**
- Bleeding can be wrong because of the blood (platelets, factors) OR because the vessel wall is wrong
- VWD's GI bleeding is the worked example — see toggles below

> 🧠 **Pattern:** any bleeding case where standard replacement therapy fails should prompt you to ask — is the vessel wall itself abnormal? (Angiodysplasia, hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia, Ehlers–Danlos.)

## Section 7 — Von Willebrand Factor and Von Willebrand Disease

📎 Figure L1.12 — VWF platelet tethering and aggregation.

### Q: Name the two haemostatic jobs of VWF.

- Mediates **platelet adhesion** to subendothelial collagen via platelet receptor **GPIb-IX-V**
- Mediates **platelet aggregation** indirectly (after activation, GPIIb/IIIa binds fibrinogen)
- Bonus job: **stabilises circulating Factor VIII** (low VWF → low FVIII → APTT prolonged)

### Q: What is VWF's angiocrine job?

- Endothelial VWF controls vessel formation + integrity
- Acts via VEGFR2 and Angiopoietin-2 signalling
- VWF-deficient ECs make **excessive, leaky, malformed vessels**
- Predicts: VWD patients can bleed both from failed platelet plugs AND from malformed vessels

### Q: One-liner definition of VWD.

- **Most common hereditary bleeding disorder**
- Decrease or dysfunction of VWF
- Mainly **mucosal bleeding** (epistaxis, menorrhagia, gum bleeding, bruising)
- Most forms mild

### Q: How is most VWD treated?

- **VWF concentrate** (replacement)
- **DDAVP** (desmopressin) — triggers EC release of stored VWF; works in mild–moderate type 1
- Effective in **most cases**

📎 Figure L1.13 — Angiodysplasia in small bowel and colon.

### Q: Why does GI bleeding from angiodysplasia in VWD not respond to VWF replacement?

- The bleeding source is **structurally malformed gut vessels**, not just failed haemostasis
- Replacement fixes platelet adhesion but the vessel wall stays abnormal
- Treatment must target both: circulating VWF (replacement) AND endothelial angiogenic pathways

> ⚠️ **Trap:** VWD's APTT is mildly prolonged (because VWF carries Factor VIII). PT is normal. Stems contrasting VWD with haemophilia hinge on this.

> 🧠 **See also:** L2 — vegetation composition uses the same platelet–fibrin machinery.

## Section 8 — Master diagram: resting vs activated endothelium

📎 Figure L1.14 — Resting vs activated endothelium with CV risk-factor inputs.

### Q: List the CV risk factors that chronically activate endothelium.

- Mechanical stress / high blood pressure
- Smoking
- Inflammation (any chronic state)
- **Oxidised LDL (oxLDL)**
- High glucose (diabetes)
- Viruses

### Q: What does an activated endothelium do downstream?

- Pro-thrombotic surface
- Increased permeability
- Cellular senescence (premature ageing)
- Leukocyte recruitment
- All converge on **atherosclerosis**

### Q: Why is endothelial activation an *early* event in plaque formation?

- The endothelium is the first thing exposed to risk factors in flowing blood
- Activation precedes any visible plaque
- **Prevention is possible if you catch activation before lesions form**

## Section 9 — Atherosclerosis: response to injury (3 stages)

📎 Figure L1.15 — Ross response-to-injury model.

### Q: Stage 1 — what does "endothelial dysfunction" mean morphologically?

- Three early changes:
- Increased **permeability**
- Increased **leukocyte adhesion**
- Increased **leukocyte migration** into vessel wall

### Q: What forms in stage 2 (fatty streak)?

- Smooth muscle migrates from media into intima
- Monocytes enter, differentiate into macrophages
- Macrophages ingest oxLDL → **foam cells**
- T cells activate; platelets adhere

> ⭐ Fatty streaks are found in **19-year-olds** — they are early, not late.

### Q: What features define stage 3 (advanced/complicated lesion)?

- Macrophage accumulation
- **Necrotic core** (dead foam cells + extracellular lipid)
- **Fibrous cap** overlies the core
- **Pathological angiogenesis** invades from vasa vasorum
- This is the rupture-prone plaque

## Section 10 — Atherosclerosis atlas: five EC dysfunction mechanisms

### Territory map

- **Mechanism 1 — Leukocyte recruitment**: right process in the wrong place (large artery)
- **Mechanism 2 — Permeability**: junctions loosen → LDL leaks in → oxidises → foam cells
- **Mechanism 3 — Shear stress**: disturbed flow at branches → loss of NO → activated phenotype
- **Mechanism 4 — Angiogenesis**: Janus paradox — bad in plaques, potentially good post-MI
- **Mechanism 5 — EndoMT**: ECs lose identity → fibroblast-like, pro-fibrotic

### Mechanism 1 — Leukocyte recruitment

📎 Figure L1.16 — Leukocyte adhesion cascade (Ley 2007).

### Q: Name the steps of the leukocyte adhesion cascade in order.

- Capture → rolling → slow rolling → arrest (activation)
- Adhesion strengthening / spreading
- Intravascular crawling
- **Paracellular** or **transcellular** transmigration

### Q: Which molecules mediate which step?

- **Selectins** (E-, P-, L-) — capture + rolling (weak, reversible)
- **Chemokines + integrin activation** — arrest signal
- **ICAM-1, VCAM-1** + leukocyte integrins (LFA-1, VLA-4) — firm arrest
- **VE-cadherin, PECAM-1, JAM, CD99** — paracellular transmigration

### Q: Where does physiological leukocyte transmigration normally happen?

- **Post-capillary venules** — thin walls (EC + basement membrane + few pericytes)
- Leukocytes chew through basement membrane → enter tissue → do their job

### Q: Why does atherosclerosis represent "the right process in the wrong place"?

- Activation occurs at a **large artery** (not a post-capillary venule)
- Monocytes adhere + transmigrate into subendothelial space
- Tunica media is too thick to cross — leukocytes are stuck
- They become macrophages → ingest oxLDL → **foam cells**

### Mechanism 2 — Permeability

📎 Figure L1.18 — Vascular permeability and lipoprotein trapping.

### Q: Walk the permeability sequence in atherosclerosis.

- Activated EC → junctions loosen → permeability ↑
- LDL leaks into subendothelial space
- Trapped LDL is **oxidised → oxLDL**
- oxLDL further activates EC (positive feedback) + is preferentially eaten by macrophages
- Lipid-laden macrophages = **foam cells**

> ⚠️ **Trap:** it's not normal LDL that does the damage — it's LDL trapped long enough to oxidise. High plasma LDL just feeds the trap.

### Mechanism 3 — Shear stress

📎 Figure L1.19 — Laminar vs disturbed flow.
📎 Figure L1.20 — Shear-stress effects on EC phenotype.

### Q: Compare laminar vs disturbed flow effects on the endothelium.

| Feature | Laminar / high shear | Disturbed / low shear |
|---|---|---|
| Phenotype | Antithrombotic, anti-inflammatory | Pro-thrombotic, pro-inflammatory |
| EC survival | Favoured | Apoptosis ↑ |
| SMC proliferation | Inhibited | Promoted |
| **NO production** | **High** | **Lost** |

### Q: Why do plaques form preferentially at bifurcations and curvatures?

- Branch points have **disturbed, low-shear flow**
- Low shear → eNOS down → loss of NO
- EC switches to pro-thrombotic, pro-inflammatory phenotype
- Plaques form where the flow makes the surface vulnerable

### Q: Why was NO honoured with a Nobel Prize?

- 1998 Nobel Physiology/Medicine — Furchgott, Ignarro, Murad
- NO from EC eNOS → vasodilation + inhibits platelet aggregation + inhibits leukocyte adhesion
- Loss of NO is a core feature of dysfunctional endothelium

### Mechanism 4 — Angiogenesis (Janus paradox)

📎 Figure L1.21 — Janus paradox.

### Q: What is the Janus paradox of angiogenesis in CVD?

- **Bad face** — vasa vasorum sprouts into advanced plaque → leaky microvessels → bleeding into plaque → rupture
- **Good face** — therapeutic angiogenesis post-MI could rescue ischaemic tissue
- Neither side clinically solved (anti-angiogenic in plaque too non-selective; pro-angiogenic post-MI not yet effective)

### Mechanism 5 — Endothelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EndoMT)

📎 Figure L1.22 — EndoMT in cardiovascular and systemic disease.

### Q: Define EndoMT.

- ECs **lose endothelial identity** + acquire mesenchymal (fibroblast-like) phenotype
- Morphology: cobblestone → spiky/elongated
- Gene expression: EC markers down, mesenchymal markers up
- Functionally **pro-fibrotic**

### Q: What triggers EndoMT in adult tissue?

- Oxidative stress
- Inflammation
- Mechanical forces
- Main signalling pathway: **TGF-β** (also hypoxia, inflammation)

### Q: Where does EndoMT contribute to disease?

- Atherosclerosis (vascular calcification)
- Cardiac fibrosis
- Pulmonary arterial hypertension
- Renal, liver, intestinal fibrosis
- Scleroderma, systemic sclerosis

> ⚠️ **Trap:** EndoMT is **not** the same as EMT (epithelial-to-mesenchymal). Same pattern, different starting cell.

### Cross-cutting pattern across the 5 mechanisms

> 🧠 **Pattern:** activated EC phenotype is the upstream cause of all five mechanisms. Different downstream consequences, one upstream switch. Memorise the framework, slot the molecules.

## Section 11 — COVID-19 as endothelial disease

📎 Figure L1.23 — COVID-19 vascular disruption.
📎 Figure L1.24 — Resting vs activated EC monolayer (Libby/Lüscher 2020).

### Q: What is the mechanism by which COVID-19 disrupts endothelium?

- SARS-CoV-2 infects respiratory epithelium (not primarily endothelium)
- → **Cytokine storm** (IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, IP-10, G-CSF, MCP-1/3 etc.)
- → Systemic endothelial activation
- → Procoagulant switch + multi-system disease

### Q: Across which axes does COVID flip the endothelium (Libby/Lüscher 2020)?

- Bleeding/thrombosis: anticoagulant → procoagulant (tissue factor, NETs, PAI-1)
- Inflammation: resists leukocytes → recruits leukocytes
- Permeability: VE-cadherin intact → BM degraded, EC sloughing
- Vascular tone: vasodilator (NO) → vasoconstrictor (thromboxane)
- Redox: antioxidant → ROS, peroxynitrite

### Q: Why is severe COVID called "an endothelial disease"?

- ARDS, capillary leak, thrombosis (PE/DVT), myocarditis, MI, ischaemic stroke, AKI all map to **flipped EC functional axes**
- Same homeostatic balance concept, **acute version**
- Atherosclerosis is the chronic version of the same story

> ⚠️ **Trap:** SARS-CoV-2 doesn't cleanly infect endothelium directly — damage is via the systemic cytokine response. Be careful with stems implying direct viral infection of EC.

## Interleaved SBA block

### Q: SBA — A 68 y/o man with poorly controlled T2DM, hypertension, and 40-pack-year smoking is told the damage from his risk factors is cumulative. His doctor explains one tissue has an intrinsic capacity to resist stressors but it's nearly exhausted. Which property?

- **D — Endothelial resilience.** Defined as ability to maintain/return to homeostasis under stress.
- A (angiogenesis) — a function, not a resistance property
- B (vascular tone) — one of the six functions
- C (contact inhibition) — keeps the monolayer monolayered, not a stress-resistance concept
- E (angiocrine signalling) — how ECs maintain surrounding tissue, not how they resist damage

### Q: SBA — Researcher compares two organs. Organ A's ECs are extremely impermeable with tight junctions; organ B's are deliberately fenestrated. Best A/B pair?

- **B — A = blood-brain barrier; B = liver sinusoids.**
- BBB is the textbook ultra-tight EC layer (no room for oedema in skull)
- Liver sinusoids are textbook fenestrated (filtration organ)

### Q: SBA — 26 y/o woman with heavy menses since menarche, recurrent epistaxis, easy bruising. Mother similar. Platelet count normal. APTT mildly prolonged, PT normal. Diagnosis?

- **B — Von Willebrand disease.** Mucocutaneous pattern, familial, normal platelets, mildly prolonged APTT (low VWF → low Factor VIII → APTT ↑). Most common hereditary bleeding disorder.
- A (Haemophilia A) — deep/joint bleeds, X-linked, mother usually carrier not affected
- C (ITP) — would have low platelets
- D (DIC) — acute, sick patient, low platelets and fibrinogen
- E (Vitamin K deficiency) — would prolong PT first

### Q: SBA — 54 y/o man with mild type 1 VWD has recurrent severe GI bleeding from small bowel angiodysplasia despite VWF concentrate that corrects plasma VWF. Best explanation?

- **B — Angiodysplastic vessels are structurally malformed and bleed independently of circulating VWF.**
- VWF has a haemostatic AND an angiocrine job — replacement only fixes the haemostatic side
- Treatment must target both circulating VWF AND endothelial angiogenic pathways

### Q: SBA — 58 y/o smoker with T2DM and HTN has a 70% LAD plaque at the LM bifurcation. Mechanism explaining preferential plaque location?

- **C — Disturbed flow at bifurcations causes low shear stress, loss of NO, and a pro-inflammatory EC phenotype.**
- A (vasa vasorum) — consequence of advanced plaque, not explanation of location
- B (LDL concentration) — equally distributed in plasma; local permeability matters
- D (pericytes) — capillary feature
- E (smooth muscle) — doesn't explain branch-point predilection

### Q: SBA — Patient hospitalised with severe COVID-19 develops PE, AKI, and myocarditis simultaneously. Best unifying mechanism?

- **C — Systemic endothelial activation driven by cytokine storm, flipping EC across multiple functional axes.**
- A (direct viral infection of all parenchyma) — SARS-CoV-2 doesn't cleanly infect all these directly
- B (molecular mimicry) — that's RHD, not COVID
- D (DIC) — downstream of EC activation, not the unifying cause
- E (hypoxia alone) — doesn't explain PE, AKI, myocarditis together

### Q: SBA — Cardiologist describes a pathway where ECs lose cobblestone shape, become spiky/elongated, downregulate EC markers, upregulate fibroblast genes, driven by TGF-β. Process?

- **B — EndoMT (endothelial-to-mesenchymal transition).**
- A (angiogenic sprouting) — ECs keep their identity
- C (senescence) — ageing, not identity change
- D (transendothelial migration) — leukocytes crossing, not EC transformation
- E (foam cell formation) — macrophages, not ECs
