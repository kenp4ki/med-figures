## The unifying intuition

The endocardium is endothelium. Damaged or turbulent valve endothelium + a circulating particle (bacteria in IE; immune attack in RF) → deposition or scarring. Acute version = vegetation in IE. Chronic version = fibrosis/stenosis in RHD. Both end the same way: damaged valve → pressure/volume backs up → heart failure.

> 🧠 **Bridges from L1:** every L1 rule applies here with one substitution — instead of lipid + leukocytes deposited on coronary endothelium, you get bacteria deposited on valvular endothelium. See L1 — Master diagram: resting vs activated endothelium.

## Section 1 — Anatomy refresher

📎 Figure L2.1 — Normal heart chambers and valves.

### Q: Trace blood through the four chambers + four valves.

- SVC/IVC → RA → **tricuspid** → RV → **pulmonary valve** → pulmonary arteries → lungs
- Pulmonary veins → LA → **mitral** → LV → **aortic valve** → aorta → body

### Q: AV valves vs semilunar valves — what's the distinction?

- **AV (atrioventricular) valves** = tricuspid (right) + mitral (left)
- **Semilunar valves** = aortic + pulmonary, named for half-moon-shaped cusps
- AV valves anchor to ventricular papillary muscles via chordae tendineae

### Q: What is the endocardium?

- The **endothelium of the heart's inner surface** (chambers + valves)
- Same tissue type as vessel endothelium — therefore subject to the same damaged-surface-plus-flow-plus-particle pathology
- IE = infection of the endocardium

## Section 2 — IE: Definition and vegetation formation

📎 Figure L2.2 — Vegetation formation (4-step).

### Q: One-line definition of infective endocarditis.

- **Infection of the endocardium**, usually affecting heart valves
- Etymology: *endo-* (inner) + *card-* (heart) + *-itis* (inflammation)
- Result of bacteria entering bloodstream + sticking to a damaged valve surface

### Q: What is a vegetation made of?

- **Bacteria** (or fungi)
- **Platelets**
- **Fibrin**
- **Immune cells** (neutrophils, macrophages — trapped inside the mass)
- A vegetation is essentially an **infected thrombus**

### Q: Walk the four-step formation sequence.

- (1) Damaged endothelium + microthrombi (pre-existing valve damage or turbulent flow)
- (2) Bacterial adherence (requires bacteraemia — bacteria can't easily stick to intact endothelium)
- (3) Bacterial proliferation + neutrophil/macrophage infiltration
- (4) Vegetation formation (mass grows, disrupts valve, can embolise)

> 🧠 **Pattern:** the platelet–fibrin machinery is borrowed from normal haemostasis. Bacteria hitch a ride on a scaffold the body builds for repair. See L1 — Three stages of haemostasis.

## Section 3 — IE: Which valves and which patients

### Q: General population — order of valve involvement in IE.

- **Aortic > mitral > right-sided** valves
- Left side carries higher pressure → more turbulence on damaged valves
- Aortic + mitral more often pre-damaged by age, calcification, prior RHD

### Q: Why do IVDUs preferentially get tricuspid IE?

- Injected bacteria enter peripheral vein → SVC/IVC → RA → **tricuspid valve first**
- Tricuspid is the first endothelial surface they encounter
- Skin flora (Staph aureus, coagulase-negative staph) and unsterile needles supply the bacteria

> ⚠️ **Trap:** "IVDU → always right-sided" is too strong. Right-sided is *most common* in IVDUs but they can get left-sided IE too, especially with repeated exposure.

### Q: Risk factors for IE — three categories.

- **Damaged valve**: RHD, age-related calcification, congenital defects, prosthetic valves
- **Route of bacteraemia**: IV drug use, dental procedures, GI/GU procedures, catheters, devices
- **Host factors**: immunosuppression, diabetes, HIV, chronic illness

### Q: How does IE epidemiology differ between developing and high-income countries?

- Developing countries — RHD as the dominant predisposing condition
- High-income — degenerative valve disease in elderly; **>50% of cases are >50 y/o**
- Prosthetic valves and intravascular devices contribute disproportionately in high-income settings

### Q: Most common pathogens in IE.

- **Streptococci** (especially viridans group) — 20–40% of cases; dental/oral source, subacute course
- **Staphylococcus aureus** — IVDUs, prosthetic valves, healthcare-associated; aggressive/acute course
- Enterococci — older adults with GI/GU source
- Coagulase-negative staph — early prosthetic valve IE
- Culture-negative IE — Coxiella burnetii, Bartonella, Tropheryma whipplei (need serology/PCR)

## Section 4 — IE: Clinical presentation

📎 Figure L2.3 — Heart failure / cardiac decompensation signs.

### Q: Three layers of IE clinical presentation.

- **Layer 1 — Systemic infection**: fever, malaise, sweats, weight loss, anaemia, ↑ CRP/ESR
- **Layer 2 — Cardiac**: new heart murmur, regurgitation, abscess, conduction block, heart failure
- **Layer 3 — Peripheral phenomena**: vascular/embolic + immunological stigmata

### Q: Vascular/embolic peripheral stigmata of IE.

- **Stroke** (cerebral embolus)
- **Janeway lesions** — painless red-brown macules on palms/soles
- **Splinter haemorrhages** — linear streaks under nails
- **Conjunctival haemorrhages**
- Splenic / renal infarcts

### Q: Immunological peripheral stigmata of IE.

- **Osler's nodes** — tender, painful red nodules on fingertips/toes
- **Roth spots** — retinal haemorrhages with pale centres (fundoscopy)
- Glomerulonephritis (immune-complex deposition)

> ⚠️ **Trap — Osler vs Janeway:** **O**sler's = **O**uch (painful, finger pads, immunological). **Ja**neway = painless, palms/soles, embolic.

### Q: Define cardiac decompensation.

- **Inability of the heart to maintain adequate circulation**
- The bridge concept into heart failure — when valve damage is severe enough that cardiac output can't meet demand

### Q: New heart block in a patient with IE — significance?

- Suggests **aortic root abscess** eroding into AV node / conduction system
- **Surgical emergency** — usually requires urgent operative intervention

## Section 5 — IE: Investigations

### Q: First three investigations in suspected IE.

- **Blood cultures** — minimum 3 sets from different sites, before antibiotics; identifies organism
- **TTE (transthoracic echocardiogram)** — first-line imaging for vegetation/abscess/regurgitation
- FBC + CRP + urine dipstick — supports systemic infection picture; haematuria suggests immune-complex glomerulonephritis

### Q: TTE vs TOE — which has higher sensitivity and why?

- **TOE has higher sensitivity** than TTE
- Oesophagus sits directly behind LA — no ribcage/lung in between → closer to valves, higher-frequency probe
- TOE picks up vegetations as small as 1–2 mm, abscesses, prosthetic valve involvement

> ⭐ **High-yield decision rule:** if TTE is normal but suspicion for IE remains high, **escalate to TOE**. Also use TOE for prosthetic valves and to assess complications.

### Q: Modified Duke criteria — major criteria.

- **Positive blood cultures** typical for IE (e.g. viridans strep, S. aureus, enterococci in specified patterns; or persistently positive cultures)
- **Evidence of endocardial involvement** — vegetation, abscess, new valve regurgitation, or new dehiscence of prosthetic valve

### Q: Modified Duke criteria — minor criteria.

- Predisposition (cardiac condition, IVDU)
- Fever >38°C
- Vascular phenomena (Janeway, emboli, mycotic aneurysm)
- Immunological phenomena (Osler's, Roth, GN, RF positive)
- Microbiological evidence not meeting major

### Q: Diagnostic threshold for definite IE.

- **2 major** OR
- **1 major + 3 minor** OR
- **5 minor**

## Section 6 — IE: Management and prevention

### Q: Three pillars of IE management.

- **Prolonged IV antibiotics** (typically 4–6 weeks; tailored to organism); vegetations have poor drug penetration
- **Surgery** for selected indications (HF from valve destruction, uncontrolled infection, high embolic risk, abscess, prosthetic valve IE)
- **Multidisciplinary team** — ID + cardiology + cardiac surgery

### Q: Antibiotic prophylaxis for dental procedures — current UK position?

- **No longer routinely recommended** for most patients (UK, US, France)
- Restricted to high-risk groups: prosthetic valves, prior IE, certain congenital lesions [Extra context]
- Don't pick "give all patients with a murmur antibiotics before dental work" in an SBA

### Q: Mortality of IE with best modern care?

- **~25%** mortality even with optimal treatment
- Highlights why surgery + prolonged IV abx + MDT input are the standard

## Section 7 — Activity 1 worked case

### Q: SBA — 21 y/o IVDU male, PUO, T 39°C, HR 115, BP 90/60, **early diastolic murmur at LSE loudest sitting forward and end expiration**, distal leg cellulitis with penetrating ulcer. Diagnosis?

- **Infective endocarditis with aortic regurgitation**, likely Staph aureus
- Early diastolic murmur at LSE = AR (occurs when aortic valve should be closed); sitting forward brings root to chest wall
- Cellulitis = portal of skin-flora entry
- Note: IVDUs *most often* have right-sided IE, but here the AR murmur points to aortic involvement; multi-valve disease possible

### Q: Next 5 management steps for this patient.

- 3 sets of blood cultures from different sites
- Empirical IV antibiotics covering Staph aureus (after cultures drawn)
- TTE → if non-diagnostic and suspicion persists, TOE
- ID + cardiology + cardiac surgery referral
- Monitor for HF / decompensation; consider urgent surgery if valve being destroyed

## Section 8 — Rheumatic fever vs Rheumatic heart disease

📎 Figure L2.8 — RHD/ARF primer infographic.

### Q: Pin down RF vs RHD — the core distinction.

| | Rheumatic fever (RF) | Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) |
|---|---|---|
| Course | Acute, **temporary** inflammatory illness | Chronic, **permanent** valve damage |
| Cause | Group A streptococcal pharyngitis | Sequela of one or more prior RF episodes |
| Tissue | Connective tissue (heart, joints, CNS, skin) | Heart valves specifically |
| Time from GAS | 2–4 weeks | **>7 years, up to 30 years** later |

### Q: What is molecular mimicry?

- GAS surface antigens (M protein, group A carbohydrate) molecularly resemble human tissue antigens
- Anti-GAS antibodies + T cells cross-react with **heart valves, joints, basal ganglia, skin**
- Result: cross-reactive immune attack on host tissue → carditis, arthritis, chorea, rashes

### Q: What are Aschoff bodies?

- Granulomatous lesions in myocardium and valves
- **Histological hallmark of rheumatic carditis**
- Reflect the cross-reactive immune attack on valve connective tissue

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Aschoff bodies (RHD histology) ≠ vegetations (IE). Different mechanisms, different microscopic appearances.

## Section 9 — Jones criteria

📎 Figure L2.6 — 12-lead ECG with PR interval marked.

### Q: Prerequisite for diagnosing acute rheumatic fever.

- **Evidence of preceding GAS infection**
- Methods: positive throat culture, rapid antigen test, OR rising **anti-streptolysin O (ASO)** / anti-DNase B titres

### Q: Five major Jones criteria.

- **Carditis** (clinical or echo evidence)
- **Arthritis** (migratory, large joints)
- **Chorea** (Sydenham's — basal ganglia involvement)
- **Erythema marginatum** (pink rings on trunk, pale centres)
- **Subcutaneous nodules**

### Q: Four minor Jones criteria.

- Polyarthralgia
- Fever >38.5°C
- Elevated acute phase reactants (CRP or ESR)
- Prolonged PR interval on ECG

### Q: Diagnostic threshold for acute rheumatic fever.

- **2 major** OR
- **1 major + 2 minor** OR
- 3 minor (slide says this — most modern guidelines require ≥1 major; flag if it appears in a stem)
- Plus the prerequisite evidence of preceding GAS infection

> ⚠️ **Trap — carditis vs prolonged PR:** carditis is a **major** criterion (echo regurgitation / pericarditis / myocarditis). Prolonged PR is only a **minor** criterion. Don't double-count.

## Section 10 — Mitral stenosis from RHD

📎 Figure L2.5 — Normal vs rheumatic mitral valve.

### Q: Commonest valve dysfunction caused by RHD.

- **Mitral stenosis** (slide 15 Q2 answer)
- Acute RF can also cause aortic regurgitation (~20%, then later stenosis), tricuspid regurgitation (~10%)
- Pulmonary valve rarely affected

### Q: Why does regurgitation come first, then stenosis?

- Acute episode → leaflets inflamed, oedematous → fail to coapt → **regurgitation**
- Repeated episodes / chronic remodelling → scarring, fibrosis, commissural fusion, calcification → leaflets stiff → **stenosis**

### Q: Pathognomonic features of rheumatic valves on echo.

- Leaflet or chordal **thickening**
- Leaflet **prolapse**
- Excessive leaflet tip motion during systole

### Q: Walk the upstream consequences of mitral stenosis ("follow the blood backwards").

- Narrowed mitral valve → LA can't empty → **LA pressure ↑ → LA dilates**
- → Pulmonary venous + capillary pressure ↑ → **pulmonary oedema** (SOB, orthopnoea, PND, haemoptysis)
- → Sustained pulmonary hypertension → **RV hypertrophy → RV failure → cor pulmonale**
- → Systemic venous congestion → raised JVP, peripheral oedema, hepatomegaly, ascites

### Q: Symptoms of mitral stenosis.

- **Haemoptysis** (rupture of dilated bronchial veins under high pressure)
- Fatigue (low forward output)
- SOB, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea
- Palpitations (AF)

### Q: Signs of mitral stenosis.

- **Malar flush** (cheekbone redness — low cardiac output + vasoconstriction)
- **Hoarse voice** — left recurrent laryngeal nerve compressed by dilated LA (Ortner's syndrome)
- **Loud S2** (loud P2 from pulmonary HTN), **RV heave**
- **Mid-diastolic rumbling murmur**, low-pitched, loudest at apex, with pre-systolic accentuation (lost when AF develops)

### Q: Why does AF develop in chronic MS, and what is the consequence?

- Chronic high LA pressure → **LA dilation** → atrial myocardium remodels → AF
- Stagnant blood in dilated LA appendage → **thrombus** → embolic stroke risk

### Q: Anticoagulation for AF in mitral stenosis — agent of choice?

- **Warfarin (vitamin K antagonist)** — this is **valvular AF**
- DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban) are NOT recommended in valvular AF
- Trials show DOACs are inferior or harmful in this setting

> ⚠️ **Trap:** non-valvular AF → DOAC first-line. Valvular AF (significant MS or mechanical valve) → warfarin. Stems hinge on this distinction.

## Section 11 — MS management

📎 Figure L2.7 — ESC/EACTS 2022 mitral stenosis algorithm.

### Q: Three pillars of MS management.

- **Definitive valve treatment**: percutaneous mitral commissurotomy (PMC) — preferred when feasible; surgical commissurotomy; valve replacement
- **Manage complications**: diuretics for HF, rate control + warfarin for AF
- **Prevent further damage**: long-term penicillin prophylaxis if RF recurrence risk

### Q: When to delay surgery in MS.

- **With annular calcification** — every option carries higher complication risk
- **Delay surgery until severely symptomatic** — procedural risk may exceed benefit until disease is severe
- Without annular calcification — all options open

### Q: Penicillin prophylaxis duration after ARF.

- **Typically 10 years after the last ARF episode**
- Sometimes longer for established RHD
- Aim: prevent recurrent GAS infection → prevent further valve damage

### Q: Why is severe RHD a major issue in pregnancy?

- Pregnancy increases plasma volume + cardiac output by 30–50%
- A stenosed mitral valve cannot accommodate the increased flow → decompensation, pulmonary oedema
- **RHD is a leading cause of obstetric mortality in low-income countries**
- Ideal: cardiac surgery before conception in women with severe RHD

## Section 12 — Activity 2 worked case

### Q: SBA — 64 y/o female, peripheral oedema, SOB worse lying flat, **flushed face, hoarse voice, low-pitched decrescendo-crescendo rumbling diastolic murmur loudest at apex**. Diagnosis?

- **Mitral stenosis, almost certainly rheumatic**
- Apical low-pitched diastolic rumble = MS
- Malar flush + hoarse voice (Ortner's) = chronic severe MS with LA dilation
- Peripheral oedema + orthopnoea = decompensated HF (left + right congestion)

### Q: Next investigations and management.

- **TTE** — assess MV area, gradient, LA size, pulmonary pressures
- **ECG** — look for AF (very likely), P-mitrale (broad bifid P from LA enlargement)
- **CXR** — LA enlargement, pulmonary congestion
- Treat: diuretics for congestion, rate control + warfarin if AF, plan PMC vs surgery

## Cross-lecture interleaved SBA block

### Q: SBA — 70 y/o smoker with T2DM and HTN has crushing chest pain and STEMI on ECG. Best mechanism at the coronary endothelium?

- **B — Long-standing endothelial activation at a coronary branch point led to plaque formation; rupture exposed thrombogenic core, triggering platelet-rich thrombus.**
- A (molecular mimicry) — that's RHD, attacks valves not coronaries
- C (vegetation embolisation) — wrong source/destination geometry
- D (EndoMT-driven fibrosis) — not the acute MI mechanism
- E (vasculitis) — risk-factor profile and presentation point to atherosclerosis

### Q: SBA — 28 y/o woman with severe rheumatic MS develops sudden left hemiparesis and dysphasia. Pulse irregularly irregular at 110. She stopped warfarin. Best mechanism?

- **C — Embolism of LA appendage thrombus formed in dilated, fibrillating LA.**
- A (septic embolus) — would need vegetation/IE
- B (carotid plaque) — unlikely at 28 with no risk factors
- D (retrograde coronary) — emboli don't travel against flow
- E (vasculitic stroke) — molecular mimicry produces carditis at acute RF, not strokes decades later

### Q: SBA — 32 y/o IVDU with fever, dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain. CXR shows multiple bilateral cavitating lung lesions. Echo shows a vegetation. Most likely valve?

- **D — Tricuspid valve.** Injected bacteria → peripheral vein → SVC/IVC → RA → tricuspid first.
- Vegetation fragments → RV → pulmonary artery → bilateral septic pulmonary emboli (cavitating lesions on CXR)
- A/B/E — left-sided valves are general-population predilection, not IVDU
- C — pulmonary valve rarely involved

### Q: SBA — 19 y/o with 4-day fever, sore throat, swollen right knee (left knee yesterday), soft systolic apical murmur, faint pink trunk rash with pale centres. Most useful test for the most likely diagnosis?

- **C — Anti-streptolysin O (ASO) titre.** This is acute rheumatic fever — migratory polyarthritis + carditis + erythema marginatum + recent pharyngitis.
- A (angiography) — for ischaemic disease
- B (blood cultures) — IE investigation; ARF is immune-mediated, not bacterial endocardial infection
- D (TOE) — would show regurgitation but not establish ARF
- E (LP) — no meningitic features

### Q: SBA — 64 y/o with SOB worse lying flat, leg oedema, raised JVP, S3 gallop, low-pitched mid-diastolic apical murmur, AF on ECG. Best management description?

- **B — Decompensated HF secondary to mitral stenosis. Manage AF with rate control + warfarin (valvular AF), diuretics for congestion, plan definitive valve treatment.**
- A (ACS) — no chest pain or ischaemic ECG
- C (PE) — no risk factors mentioned; clinical picture fits valvular HF
- D (sepsis from IE) — no fever or infective features
- E (tension pneumothorax) — no respiratory failure / asymmetry

### Q: SBA — 14 y/o from low-income country, 3 weeks post sore throat, migratory knee/elbow polyarthritis, T 39°C, new pansystolic apical murmur, prolonged PR, ASO ↑. Diagnosis?

- **B — Acute rheumatic fever, 2 major + 2 minor.**
- Major: arthritis + carditis (new pansystolic apical murmur = mitral regurgitation, the early valvular lesion of acute rheumatic carditis)
- Minor: fever >38.5°C + prolonged PR
- + evidence of preceding GAS (ASO)
- E (IE) — wrong age/risk profile; new MR in acute RF fits carditis better than vegetation in 3 weeks

### Q: SBA — 58 y/o man with mechanical mitral valve, fevers 8 weeks post-op, blood cultures grow coagulase-negative staph, echo shows partial prosthetic dehiscence. Single key management element beyond IV abx?

- **B — Surgical re-do of valve replacement.**
- Indications for surgery in IE include prosthetic valve involvement + dehiscence + biofilm on foreign material (antibiotics alone rarely cure)
- A (early oral switch) — IE needs prolonged IV
- C (thrombolysis) — no role in IE; risks dislodging septic emboli
- D (immune modulation) — not standard
- E (dental prophylaxis) — prevention, not treatment
