### Q: What cell types give rise to the GI cancers taught in this lecture?

- Glandular epithelium → **adenocarcinoma** (colorectal, pancreatic, gastric, oesophageal)
- Enteroendocrine cells → **neuroendocrine tumours (NETs)** — anywhere in the gut
- Hepatocytes → **HCC**; bile duct epithelium → **cholangiocarcinoma**

### Q: Define primary, secondary / metastasis, and the singular vs plural spelling.

- **Primary** — arising directly in an organ (e.g. colorectal primary starts in colon)
- **Secondary / metastasis** — spread from the primary via blood or lymph
- Singular: metastasis; plural: metastases — 🟥 commonly misspelled; the lecturer specifically flagged this

## Cross-cutting patterns

🟦 **First-pass liver.** Bowel (SMV) and pancreas (splenic vein) drain via portal vein → liver. Escaped tumour cells filter out there first. That's why CRC's commonest met site is liver, and why NETs only cause carcinoid syndrome when there are liver mets or a bronchial primary — otherwise the liver metabolises the hormones before they reach the systemic circulation.

🟦 **Tumour markers = monitoring, not diagnosis.** CEA, CA19-9, chromogranin A all rise in many benign conditions (inflammation, jaundice, pancreatitis) and fail to rise in many patients with the cancer. Useful only when the baseline is high at diagnosis — watch it fall after surgery, rise again = recurrence.

🟦 **Precursor-lesion surveillance works only when precursors are visible.** Colorectal adenomas are endoscopically visible → FIT → colonoscopy → polypectomy prevents cancer. Pancreatic PanINs are <5 mm and invisible on imaging → surveillance doesn't work → PDA presents late.

🟦 **Surgery is the only curative option, but most patients miss the window.** Across HCC, gallbladder CA, cholangiocarcinoma and PDA, only ~5–30% are resectable. Everything else is palliative.

🟦 **Staging CT CAP is universal.** Any GI cancer found anywhere → staging CT of chest, abdomen, pelvis before treatment.

## Card 1 — Colorectal cancer

### Q: Colorectal cancer — three epidemiology facts.

- **Most common GI cancer in Western societies**
- Third commonest cause of cancer death in men and women
- Lifetime risk **1 in 10 men, 1 in 14 women**; >90% of cases in patients >50 years

### Q: What are the three forms of colorectal cancer?

- **Sporadic** — no family history, older patient, isolated lesion
- **Familial** — family history; higher risk if index case <50 years and relative is 1st-degree
- **Hereditary syndrome** — family history, younger onset, specific gene defect (FAP, HNPCC/Lynch, Peutz-Jeghers)

### Q: FAP vs HNPCC — the key difference?

- **FAP** — APC mutation → thousands of polyps → near-100% cancer risk → prophylactic total colectomy (often in 20s)
- **HNPCC (Lynch)** — mismatch-repair mutation (MLH1/MSH2/MSH6/PMS2) → fewer polyps, any can transform; also causes endometrial, ovarian, gastric cancer
- Both autosomal dominant

### Pathogenesis — adenoma–carcinoma sequence

📎 Figure L1.3 — Adenoma–carcinoma sequence

```
Normal epithelium
    │ APC mutation
    ▼
Hyperproliferative epithelium / aberrant cryptic foci
    │ COX-2 overexpression
    ▼
Small adenoma
    │ K-ras mutation
    ▼
Large adenoma
    │ p53 mutation + loss of 18q
    ▼
Colon carcinoma
```

### Q: What mutation initiates the adenoma–carcinoma sequence?

- **APC** — drives normal epithelium → hyperproliferative/aberrant cryptic foci
- FAP patients inherit one defective APC allele → sequence is primed from birth

### Q: Why does this cascade matter clinically?

- 🟨 It takes years → **surveillance works**: remove polyps before they become invasive carcinoma
- Same "lottery-in-reverse" concept reappears in pancreas (PanIN) — but PanINs are invisible, so surveillance fails there

### Q: Colorectal cancer — main risk factors.

- **Past history**: previous CRC, adenoma, **ulcerative colitis**, prior radiotherapy
- **Family history**: 1st-degree relative <55 years; FAP / HNPCC / Peutz-Jeghers
- **Lifestyle**: smoking, obesity, low socioeconomic status

### Presentation depends on location

### Q: Where do colorectal cancers sit anatomically?

- **⅔ in descending colon and rectum**
- **½ in sigmoid colon and rectum** — within reach of flexible sigmoidoscopy
- Explains why initial investigation often starts with sigmoidoscopy

### Q: Right-sided (caecal) cancer — how does it present?

- **Iron deficiency anaemia** (most common) — stool has long way to travel → no fresh blood
- Change of bowel habit (often diarrhoea)
- Distal ileum obstruction (late)
- Palpable abdominal mass (late, indicates advanced disease)

### Q: Left-sided / sigmoid cancer — how does it present?

- **PR bleeding with mucus** — fresh blood (cancer is closer to anus)
- Thin stool (late — lumen is narrower on the left)
- Bowel obstruction (late)

### Q: Rectal cancer — how does it present?

- **PR bleeding, mucus**
- **Tenesmus** — urge to defecate with nothing to pass; tumour sits where faeces "should be" and triggers the urge
- Anal / perineal / sacral pain (late → local infiltration)

🟦 **Blood colour rule.** Bright red PR bleed = haemorrhoids or rectal tumour (close to anus). Altered/dark blood = source is higher in the tree. The further blood travels through the gut, the more it changes colour.

### Q: Signs of metastatic colorectal cancer on examination?

- Hepatomegaly → liver mets (commonest site, via first-pass drainage)
- Monophonic wheeze → endobronchial lung met
- Bone pain → bone met
- **Sister Mary Joseph nodule** — periumbilical deposit from peritoneal spread 📎 Figure L1.4

### Q: DRE — what's the clinical rule?

- Most rectal tumours sit **<12 cm from the dentate line** — within reach of the examining finger
- 🟥 Lecturer's warning: many clinicians document "PR normal" without examining properly. Do it properly or don't do it.

### Investigations

### Q: What is FIT and why does it matter?

- **Faecal Immunochemical Test** — detects minute amounts of occult blood in stool using anti-human-Hb antibodies
- 🟨 "The most important test now" — NHS bowel cancer screening posts kits to people aged 55–60
- Positive FIT → colonoscopy

### Q: Why is CEA NOT diagnostic?

- **Carcinoembryonic antigen** — many CRCs don't produce it; many non-cancer conditions elevate it
- Useful only if baseline is known high at diagnosis → watch it fall after surgery → rise later = recurrence
- 🟥 A normal CEA does not exclude CRC

### Q: Colonoscopy vs CT colonography — which, when?

- **Colonoscopy**: visualises lesions <5 mm; allows biopsy and polypectomy; requires bowel prep and sedation
- **CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy)**: visualises lesions >5 mm; no sedation; better for frail patients
- If CT colonography finds a lesion → still needs colonoscopy to biopsy

📎 Figure L1.5 — Colonoscopy in situ and polyp appearances
📎 Figure L1.6 — CT colonography reconstruction

### Q: When is MRI pelvis used in colorectal cancer?

- **Rectal cancer only** — depth of invasion, mesorectal nodes, **CRM (circumferential resection margin)** threatened?
- Determines preoperative chemoradiotherapy vs straight-to-surgery

### Q: What's the universal staging imaging for any GI cancer?

- **CT chest / abdomen / pelvis (CT CAP)** before treatment
- Looks for distant disease that would change the treatment plan

### Management

### Q: What operation for a colon cancer — and why does site matter?

📎 Figure L1.7 — Colonic arterial supply; L1.8 — right hemicolectomy; L1.9 — left hemicolectomy; L1.10 — rectal operations

- Resection is dictated by **blood supply**, not tumour size alone
- Right-sided: **right hemicolectomy** (ileocolic + right colic + middle colic arteries)
- Left-sided: **left hemicolectomy** (left colic + sigmoid arteries)
- Rectal: anterior resection (if feasible) or abdominoperineal resection (APR) for very low tumours

### Q: Why does obstructing left-sided cancer often need Hartmann's, but right-sided doesn't?

- **Blood supply is poorer on the left** → anastomosis more likely to leak (~10%)
- **Hartmann's**: resect tumour, bring proximal end up as LIF colostomy, close distal rectal stump; reverse ~6 months later if fit
- Right-sided: safer to resect and primary-anastomose immediately

### Q: When is a palliative colonic stent used?

- Holds the obstructing cancer open — bridges an acute obstruction without operating
- Reserved for: frail patients unfit for surgery, or as a bridge to elective resection

🟥 **Trap.** In a fit patient with obstructing left-sided cancer, "stent and wait" is NOT the textbook first-line answer. Check the stem for age / ECOG / comorbidities before picking stent.

### Q: Synchronous vs metachronous liver metastases — definitions?

- **Synchronous** (15–20%) — liver mets present at time of primary diagnosis
- **Metachronous** (25%) — develop later after primary treatment
- Both potentially curable — 5-year survival 25–50% after complete metastasectomy

### Q: CRC + fit patient + resectable synchronous liver mets — staged plan?

- **Resect primary → neoadjuvant chemotherapy → liver resection** (the tutorial Case 2 answer)
- Not palliative chemo alone; not simultaneous everything
- 🟨 Lecturer's message: "Very aggressive — it's not the end of the world"

### Q: What do neoadjuvant, adjuvant, and palliative chemotherapy each do?

- **Neoadjuvant** — before surgery, to shrink / downstage
- **Adjuvant** — after surgery, to mop up occult disease (given if nodes positive)
- **Palliative** — cancer is inoperable; chemo extends life and controls symptoms

## Card 2 — Liver cancers (sub-atlas)

🟦 **Structural analogy (lecturer).** Liver = tree. Branches = bile ducts. Leaves = hepatocytes.
- Cancer of leaves → **HCC**
- Cancer of branches → **cholangiocarcinoma**
- Plus two neighbours: gallbladder CA + CRC liver mets

### Template comparison — four liver cancers

| Cancer | Median survival (no Rx) | 5-yr after resection | % resectable |
|---|---|---|---|
| HCC | 4–6 months | >30% | 5–15% |
| Gallbladder CA | 5–8 months | Stage II 64 / III 44 / IV 8% | <15% |
| Cholangiocarcinoma | <6 months | 20–40% | 20–30% |
| CRC liver mets | <1 year | 25–50% | 25% |

### HCC

### Q: HCC aetiology — what precedes it?

- **70–90% have underlying cirrhosis** — hepatitis B, hepatitis C, alcoholic cirrhosis, increasingly **NAFLD/fatty liver**
- Aflatoxin (rare in UK)
- Mechanism: recurrent inflammation → repair → accumulated mutations

### Q: Why is liver transplant (OLTx) a particularly good option in HCC?

- Resecting only the tumour leaves the diseased cirrhotic liver behind → ongoing new-cancer risk
- **Transplant replaces tumour AND the field of risk**
- Alcoholic patients must demonstrate abstinence before listing

### Q: HCC — non-surgical options?

- **TACE** (trans-arterial chemoembolisation) — catheter-delivered chemo + embolisation
- **RFA** (radiofrequency ablation) — heat-destroys small lesions
- Systemic chemotherapy largely ineffective (RR <20%)

📎 Figure L1.13 — HCC: cirrhotic liver on CT and planned resection

### Gallbladder cancer

### Q: Gallbladder cancer — risk associations.

- Aetiology officially unknown, but associated with:
- **Gallstones** — chronic irritation → fibrosis → dysplasia
- **Porcelain gallbladder** — calcified wall; "ring of calcium" on X-ray
- Chronic typhoid infection (historical)

🟨 **Incidental gallbladder cancer on histology.** Found after routine cholecystectomy for stones. If early (stage II), **radical re-operation** (resect segments IVb/V of liver + regional lymphadenectomy) transforms prognosis — **~64% 5-yr survival vs ~20% without**. Always read the histology report after a cholecystectomy.

📎 Figure L1.14 — Radical gallbladder cancer resection

### Cholangiocarcinoma

### Q: Cholangiocarcinoma — key risk factors.

- **PSC (primary sclerosing cholangitis) + UC** — ~1.5% per year cancer risk after PSC diagnosis
- **Liver fluke** (*Clonorchis sinensis*) — Southeast Asia
- **Choledochal cyst** — ~20% lifetime cancer risk → prophylactic excision recommended

### Q: Where does cholangiocarcinoma usually arise?

- Most commonly at the **hilum** — hepatic duct bifurcation = **Klatskin tumour**
- Can occur anywhere along biliary tree; distal tumours blur with pancreatic head cancer
- 🟦 The more proximal the tumour, the more liver must be sacrificed to resect it

📎 Figure L1.15 — Hilar cholangiocarcinoma resection (right trisectionectomy)

### CRC liver metastases

### Q: Why is CRC the commonest cancer needing liver surgery?

- CRC is commonest GI cancer; 15–20% synchronous liver mets + 25% metachronous
- First-pass liver principle: escaped colonic cells land in liver
- **25% resectable** → 5-yr survival 25–50% after metastasectomy

### Q: What has modern chemotherapy done for inoperable mCRC?

- Best supportive care alone: 4–6 months
- Modern combination chemotherapy (FOLFOX/FOLFIRI + biologics such as bevacizumab): **~20–25 months median OS**
- Lecturer: "Different disease now" — some patients live >3 years

## Card 3 — Pancreatic cancer (PDA)

### Q: What is pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma and how lethal is it?

- **Commonest form of pancreatic cancer** (~80%) — adenocarcinoma of pancreatic ducts
- **80–85% present late** — overall median survival <6 months; 5-yr survival 0.4–5%
- **15–20% resectable** — even then, virtually all dead within 7 years of surgery

### Q: Why is PDA incidence almost equal to its mortality?

- UK 2015: 9,921 new cases, 9,263 deaths — almost everyone diagnosed dies of it
- **4th commonest cause of cancer death** despite being 10th in incidence
- Predicted to become 2nd commonest US cancer death by 2030

📎 Figure L1.16 — US 2008 data: pancreas 10th incidence, 4th mortality

### Q: PDA — strongest risk factors.

- **Chronic pancreatitis → 18-fold ↑ risk**
- **Cigarette smoking → 25–30% of PDAs**
- **Type 2 diabetes → RR 1.8** — 🟨 new-onset DM in an older adult can be an early sign of PDA
- Family history: 7–10%; 1/2/3 affected 1st-degree relatives → **2 / 6 / 30-fold** RR

### Q: Which inherited syndrome carries the highest PDA lifetime risk?

- **Hereditary pancreatitis** (PRSS1/SPINK1/CFTR) — **40% lifetime risk**
- Peutz-Jeghers (STK11): 11–36%
- Familial atypical multiple mole melanoma (CDKN2A): 10–17%
- HNPCC 3.7%, FAP 4.5% — real but smaller

### Pathogenesis — PanIN cascade

📎 Figure L1.17 — PanIN histology; L1.18 — PanIN progression cascade

```
Normal duct → PanIN-1 → PanIN-2 → PanIN-3 → invasive PDA
              KRAS       CDKN2A    TP53, SMAD4,
              ERBB2                 BRCA2
```

### Q: What is a PanIN and why does it matter?

- **Pancreatic Intraepithelial Neoplasia** — microscopic (<5 mm) precursor lesion in pancreatic duct
- Same "lottery-in-reverse" cascade as colorectal adenomas
- 🟥 **Cannot be seen on imaging** → surveillance can't remove them pre-emptively (unlike colonic polyps)

### Q: Head-of-pancreas cancer — classic presentation triad.

- **Painless obstructive jaundice** (>90% — tumour compresses/invades CBD)
- **Weight loss** (anorexia, exocrine malabsorption, new-onset diabetes)
- **Pain in 70%** — epigastric, radiates to back in 25%
- 🟨 **Courvoisier's sign** — palpable non-tender gallbladder + painless jaundice → malignant obstruction

### Q: Why does Courvoisier's law work?

- 🟦 A palpable, non-tender gallbladder in a jaundiced patient is **unlikely to be due to gallstones**
- Logic: gallstones cause chronic inflammation → fibrotic, non-distensible gallbladder
- Malignant distal CBD obstruction in a previously healthy GB → GB distends like a balloon
- Two-thirds of PDAs arise in the head → this sign picks them up

### Q: Body/tail pancreatic cancer — why so late?

- **Doesn't obstruct anything** — no jaundice, no early warning
- **Back pain + marked weight loss in 60%** — often the only features
- Usually unresectable at diagnosis

### Q: What does back pain in pancreatic cancer imply?

- 🟥 Usually indicates **posterior capsule invasion → irresectability**
- Distinct from the 25% with nonspecific epigastric → back radiation early on

### Investigations

### Q: CA19-9 — rules for interpretation.

- **Falsely elevated** in pancreatitis, hepatic dysfunction, obstructive jaundice
- 🟥 CA19-9 of 1000 in a jaundiced patient is meaningless until jaundice is relieved
- >200 U/mL → 90% sensitivity; thousands → high specificity
- Same rule as CEA — monitoring, not diagnostic

### Q: First-line imaging for suspected PDA?

- **Dual-phase contrast CT** — predicts resectability in 80–90%
- Looks for: contiguous organ invasion, vascular invasion (coeliac axis, SMA), distant mets
- US can be an initial screen (dilated bile ducts, liver mets); CT is definitive

📎 Figure L1.19 — Pancreatic cancer on contrast CT (head vs body)

### Q: What does ERCP offer in pancreatic cancer?

- **Diagnostic + therapeutic**
- Diagnostic: confirms **"double-duct sign"** (dilated CBD + pancreatic duct); brushings for cytology
- Therapeutic: biliary stenting to relieve obstructive jaundice before surgery/chemo

📎 Figure L1.20 — ERCP image

### Q: When do you use EUS vs PET vs staging laparoscopy?

- **EUS** — highly sensitive for small tumours; assesses vascular invasion; FNA biopsy
- **PET** — occult distant metastases (doesn't see the primary well)
- **Staging laparoscopy** — picks up peritoneal / small liver mets missed on CT

📎 Figure L1.21 — PET/CT for occult pancreatic metastases

### Q: Head-of-pancreas cancer — what operation?

- **Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy)** — remove head of pancreas + duodenum + distal stomach + gallbladder + distal CBD
- Reconstruction: pancreaticojejunostomy + hepaticojejunostomy + gastrojejunostomy

📎 Figure L1.22 — Whipple procedure

### Q: Body/tail pancreatic cancer — what operation?

- **Distal pancreatectomy ± splenectomy**
- Splenectomy usually included because splenic vessels run along the tail

📎 Figure L1.23 — Distal pancreatectomy

## Card 4 — Neuroendocrine tumours (NETs)

### Q: What are neuroendocrine tumours?

- Tumours of **enteroendocrine cells** from the gastroenteropancreatic (GEP) tract, or bronchopulmonary system
- Diverse but grouped because they share a secretory-cell origin
- **75% sporadic; 25% genetic syndrome-associated**

### Q: Which genetic syndrome is most associated with GEP-NETs?

- **MEN1 (Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia type 1)**
- Triad: **parathyroid + pancreatic + pituitary tumours** ("3 Ps")
- Autosomal dominant

### Q: Why are most NETs asymptomatic?

- ~40% secrete hormones; of those, **<10% produce symptoms**
- 🟦 **First-pass liver**: secretions drain via portal vein → liver metabolises them before systemic circulation
- Symptoms only when: (a) liver is bypassed (bronchial NET), or (b) liver is overwhelmed (liver mets)

### Q: What is carcinoid syndrome?

- Clinical syndrome from unmetabolised serotonin + tachykinins reaching systemic circulation
- **Vasodilatation** (flushing) + **bronchoconstriction** + **↑ intestinal motility** (diarrhoea) + **endocardial fibrosis** → **TR (tricuspid regurgitation) and PR (pulmonary regurgitation)**
- Only with liver mets or a lung/bronchial primary

📎 Figure L1.24 — Carcinoid syndrome facial flushing

### Functional NETs by site

| NET | Cell | Syndrome | Clinical features |
|---|---|---|---|
| Insulinoma | β | Hypoglycaemia | Whipple's triad; often mislabelled epilepsy |
| Glucagonoma | α | DM + skin | Necrolytic migratory erythema |
| Gastrinoma | G | Zollinger-Ellison | Recurrent peptic ulcers; small-bowel perforation |
| VIPoma | VIP | Verner-Morrison | Profuse watery diarrhoea |

### Q: Somatostatinoma — what clinical triad?

- **Gallstones + diabetes + steatorrhoea**
- Cell type D; less common than the others

### Q: Midgut vs hindgut NETs — how do they behave?

- **Midgut** — most non-functioning, but 40% develop carcinoid syndrome; small-intestine NETs metastasise to liver 45% of the time
- **Hindgut** — usually non-functioning; rectal NETs rarely metastasise (only 6%)

📎 Figure L1.26 — Midgut NET (CT + gross)
📎 Figure L1.28 — GEP-NET primary site + liver met frequency

### Q: NETs biochemical workup — key tests.

- **Chromogranin A** — secretory product of NETs (monitoring, not diagnostic)
- **24-hr urinary 5-HIAA** — serotonin metabolite; diagnostic for carcinoid
- Fasting gut hormones: insulin, gastrin, somatostatin, PPY
- MEN1 screen: calcium, PTH, prolactin, GH

### Q: NETs imaging — why arterial-phase CT matters.

- 🟨 NETs are **very vascular** — invisible on standard portal-venous-phase CT
- **Arterial-phase CT** lights them up
- **⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT** (somatostatin receptor scintigraphy) is the most sensitive test

📎 Figure L1.25 — Insulinoma: CT arterial vs ⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT

### Q: GEP-NET grading — three grades.

- **G1**: <2 mitoses/10 HPF; Ki-67 ≤2% — well-differentiated, indolent
- **G2**: 2–20 mitoses/10 HPF; Ki-67 3–20%
- **G3**: >20 mitoses/10 HPF; Ki-67 >20% — poorly-differentiated **neuroendocrine carcinoma**
- 🟨 Nomenclature shift: G3 = "carcinoma"; G1–2 = "tumour"

### Q: Why does Ki-67 matter?

- **Proliferation index** — predicts behaviour and drives treatment intensity
- Low Ki-67 (G1) → can watch small tumours
- High Ki-67 (G3) → treat as aggressive cancer
- Translates directly to survival

📎 Figure L1.27 — pNET survival by grade (Kaplan-Meier)

### Q: NETs — treatment principles.

- **R0 resection** ideal; cytoreductive (R1/R2) still helps
- Liver-directed: OLTx, RFA, microwave ablation, embolisation (TAE/TACE), SIRT (⁹⁰Y microspheres)
- **Peptide receptor radionuclide therapy**: ⁹⁰Y-DOTA, ¹⁷⁷Lu-DOTA
- Medical: **octreotide, lanreotide** (somatostatin analogues); mTOR inhibitors; α-interferon

## Tutorial cases — applied reasoning

### Case 1 — 76-year-old man, retrosternal dysphagia, weight loss

🟦 **Dysphagia decision logic:**
- Food painful to swallow → **upper** dysphagia
- Food easy to swallow but stuck seconds later → **lower** dysphagia (this case)
- Solids > liquids → **mechanical / tumour**
- Liquids > solids → **achalasia**
- Both equally hard → **neurological**
- Solids > liquids + weight loss + older patient → malignant until proven otherwise → **2-week-wait OGD**

📎 Figure L1.29 — CXR: right lower lobe aspiration pneumonia
📎 Figure L1.30 — OGD + histology: lower-oesophageal adenocarcinoma

### Q: Case 1 — investigation bundle (be organised).

- **Bedside**: ECG (rule out post-prandial angina)
- **Blood tests**: FBC (IDA from chronic bleed?), U&E (dehydration?)
- **Imaging**: CXR (aspiration pneumonia in this patient)
- **Special / invasive**: **urgent OGD** via 2-week-wait → biopsy confirmed adenocarcinoma

### Q: Case 1 — MDT staging bundle for resectable upper-GI cancer?

- **All of the above**: staging CT CAP + PET + staging laparoscopy
- Laparoscopy picks up peritoneal disease missed on cross-sectional imaging

📎 Figure L1.31 — Staging PET/CT: oesophageal T3N1M0
📎 Figure L1.32 — Oesophagectomy schematic

### Case 2 — 68-year-old woman, microcytic anaemia on routine bloods

🟦 **Iron-deficiency workup logic:**
- Low Hb + low MCV = **microcytic anaemia**
- Microcytic causes: iron deficiency, anaemia of chronic disease, thalassaemia, sideroblastic
- In an older patient, IDA = **colorectal cancer until proven otherwise**

### Q: Anaemia by MCV — mnemonics.

- **Microcytic (<80)**: Iron deficiency, Chronic disease, Thalassaemia, Sideroblastic
- **Normocytic (80–96) — ABCDE**: Aplastic, Bleeding, Chronic disease, Destruction (haemolysis), Endocrine
- **Macrocytic (>96) — FAT RBC**: Foetus, Alcohol, Thyroid, Reticulocytosis, B₁₂/folate, Cirrhosis

### Q: GI causes of iron-deficiency anaemia in order of frequency?

- 1. Aspirin / NSAID use
- 2. **Colonic adenocarcinoma**
- 3. **Gastric carcinoma**
- 4. Benign gastric ulcer
- Then: angiodysplasia, coeliac disease, gastrectomy, *H. pylori*

### Q: Case 2 — staging CT shows T3N0M1 (synchronous liver mets). Correct management?

- **Resect primary colon → neoadjuvant chemo → liver resection**
- 🟥 Trap: "palliative chemo only" or "resect everything simultaneously" are not the plan for a fit patient with resectable mets
- 5-yr survival after complete metastasectomy: 25–50%

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — 62-year-old with iron-deficiency anaemia and weight loss. OGD normal. Next step?

- **Colonoscopy** (via 2-week-wait)
- Right-sided colon cancer classically presents with IDA (stool too far to show fresh blood)
- If both OGD and colonoscopy are normal → small-bowel imaging (capsule endoscopy)

### Q: SBA 2 — 55-year-old with painless jaundice and palpable gallbladder. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Carcinoma of the head of pancreas**
- Courvoisier's law: palpable GB + painless jaundice → malignant distal CBD obstruction, not stones
- Next step: dual-phase contrast CT

### Q: SBA 3 — 68-year-old with PSC and UC, now weight loss and jaundice. Very high CA19-9. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Cholangiocarcinoma** (Klatskin / hilar most likely)
- PSC background is the key clue — ~1.5%/year cancer risk
- 🟥 Don't leap to pancreatic cancer just because CA19-9 is raised — biliary cancers elevate it too

### Q: SBA 4 — 45-year-old with episodic flushing, diarrhoea and wheeze. Best diagnostic test?

- **24-hr urinary 5-HIAA** (serotonin metabolite)
- Classic carcinoid syndrome → implies liver mets or a lung/bronchial primary
- Follow up with CT + ⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT

### Q: SBA 5 — Patient with recurrent duodenal ulcers refractory to PPI. What to investigate?

- **Fasting gastrin** — suspect **gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison)**
- Think MEN1 if there are also parathyroid / pituitary features
- PPI-refractory ulcer alone isn't diagnostic — need documented hypergastrinaemia

### Q: SBA 6 — Fit 70-year-old with CRC and two resectable liver mets. Best plan?

- **Resect primary → neoadjuvant chemo → liver resection**
- 5-yr survival after staged combined approach: 25–50%
- 🟥 "Palliative chemo only" is wrong for a potentially curable patient

### Q: SBA 7 — Incidental 1 cm rectal NET, Ki-67 1%. Best initial approach?

- **Local resection and surveillance** (watch-and-follow acceptable for small low-grade rectal NETs)
- Rectal NETs rarely metastasise (6%); low Ki-67 = indolent
- Aggressive treatment unnecessary for G1 small lesions

### Q: SBA 8 — Elective cholecystectomy histology reports T2 gallbladder adenocarcinoma. Next step?

- **Refer for radical re-operation** — extended cholecystectomy (segments IVb/V of liver + regional lymphadenectomy)
- 🟨 Transforms 5-yr survival from ~20% to ~64%
- Single most important take-home on gallbladder cancer

### Q: SBA 9 — 32-year-old post prophylactic colectomy for FAP. What else does he need?

- **Upper GI endoscopy surveillance** — FAP causes duodenal adenomas → periampullary cancer risk
- Also: desmoid surveillance, thyroid examination
- FAP is not just a colon problem

### Q: SBA 10 — Cirrhotic patient, 2 cm HCC on surveillance US. Drinks 40 units/week. Best Rx?

- **Liver transplant** — but only after documented abstinence
- Resection leaves cirrhotic liver → ongoing cancer risk
- RFA / TACE can bridge to transplant

### Q: SBA 11 — 48-year-old with new-onset T2DM and unexplained 5 kg weight loss. Think of…?

- 🟨 **Pancreatic cancer** — new-onset DM in an older adult can be an early sign (RR ~1.8)
- Imaging: CT abdomen with contrast
- The lecturer flagged this as an early pickup opportunity

### Q: SBA 12 — Oesophageal adenocarcinoma T3N1M0, ECOG 0. Best plan?

- **Neoadjuvant chemoradiation followed by surgery (oesophagectomy)**
- T3 with node positivity benefits from downstaging
- ECOG 0 means fitness permits surgery

### Q: SBA 13 — What single caveat applies to CEA, CA19-9, and chromogranin A?

- 🟦 **None are diagnostic** — all rise in benign conditions, and fail to rise in some cancer patients
- Useful only when baseline is known high → watch response and recurrence
- Same rule across colorectal, pancreatic and NET cancers

### Q: SBA 14 — Suspected small-bowel NET, invisible on standard portal-venous-phase CT. Next imaging?

- **Arterial-phase CT** or **⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT**
- NETs are highly vascular → miss them on portal-venous phase
- PET/CT also picks up metastases

### Q: SBA 15 — Cholangiocarcinoma arose in a known choledochal cyst. What was the lifetime cancer risk of that cyst?

- **~20% lifetime risk**
- 🟨 Choledochal cysts are prophylactically excised once diagnosed
- Lecturer's anecdote: patient who refused surgery, developed cancer, died

### Q: SBA 16 — Rectal bleeding + tenesmus + sacral pain in a 65-year-old. Most likely site?

- **Rectal carcinoma** — all three features point to the rectum
- Tenesmus from a tumour where faeces "should be"; sacral pain from late local infiltration
- Investigation: urgent DRE → flexible sigmoidoscopy → MRI pelvis to assess CRM

### Q: SBA 17 — Incidental 3 cm pancreatic head tumour on CT arterial phase, very vascular. Likely diagnosis?

- **Pancreatic neuroendocrine tumour (pNET)** — often insulinoma or non-functioning
- 🟦 PDA is typically hypovascular; vascular lesion on arterial phase = think NET
- Confirm with EUS + FNA; ⁶⁸Ga-DOTATATE PET/CT for staging

### Q: SBA 18 — 55-year-old with UC for 15 years, now new weight loss and colonic stricture. Think of…?

- **Colorectal cancer complicating UC** — UC is a recognised CRC risk factor
- Long-standing UC needs surveillance colonoscopy
- Next step: colonoscopy with biopsy
