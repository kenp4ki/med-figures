## Section 1 — Multisystemic disorders

### Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)

![SLE — three forms of cutaneous lupus: acute (malar, top), subacute (annular polycyclic, middle), discoid/chronic (scarring + dyspigmentation, bottom)](figures/L1_03_cle_three_forms.jpg)
*Figure L1.3 — Three forms of cutaneous lupus.*

![Tutorial Case 2 stem — classic SLE malar rash sparing nasolabial folds](../Tutorial/figures/T_03_case2_sle_malar.jpg)
*Figure T.3 — Tutorial Case 2: 24F, 3-month fatigue + arthralgia + photosensitive malar rash + MCP/wrist synovitis = SLE.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.01 (Case 1 stem) — 38F with weeks of rash on face and forearms, fever, arthritis, fatigue, no medications. Pancytopenia, proteinuria, ANA 1:640, anti-dsDNA+. Diagnosis?

- **SLE.**
- Multisystem autoimmune disease — skin, joints, kidneys, blood, immunology all hit.
- ANA + anti-dsDNA + low complement = the immunological triad.
- **Don't skip urinalysis** — SBA-1 directly tested this.

### Q: What is SLE — central mechanism?

- Chronic, multisystem **autoimmune** disease.
- **Autoantibodies against nuclear (self) antigens** form **immune complexes** → tissue deposition → **complement activation** → inflammation → organ damage.
- Drivers: genetic, environmental, immunoregulatory, hormonal, epigenetic.
- See Figure L1.02 for the pathogenesis cartoon.

### Q: Three forms of cutaneous lupus — and which systemic risk pairs with each?

- **Acute CLE** — most often associated with SLE. **Malar rash** (sparing nasolabial folds).
- **Subacute CLE (SCLE)** — annular plaques with raised borders + **central clearing** → polycyclic patterns. Upper chest/upper back.
- **Chronic CLE → discoid LE (DLE)** — **scarring** plaques, **follicular plugging**, dyspigmentation. Scalp + face.
- **Pattern:** ~10% of DLE-only patients develop systemic disease.

### Q: SLE — non-malar skin manifestations to recognise?

- Photodistributed erythematous rash (sun-exposed areas) — Figure L1.04.
- Cutaneous vasculitis — palpable purpura on legs — Figure L1.05.
- **Chilblains** on toes (cold-response, Raynaud-like) — Figure L1.04.
- **Livedo reticularis** — net-like rash on lower legs — Figure L1.05.
- Non-scarring alopecia.

### Q: SLE — diagnostic criteria (group them so you remember)?

- **Mucocutaneous:** acute/chronic CLE, oral ulcers, alopecia, synovitis.
- **Other organ:** serositis, renal, neurological, haemolytic anaemia.
- **Haematological:** thrombocytopenia, leukopenia.
- **Immunological:** ANA, **anti-dsDNA**, anti-Sm, antiphospholipid, **low complement**, direct Coombs'.
- See Figure T.4 for the full criteria table from the tutorial.

### Q: SLE — pathophysiology MCQ. Which mechanism is correct (tutorial Q2)?

- **Correct:** autoantibody-mediated immune complex deposition with complement activation.
- ✗ CD8+ T-cell-mediated keratinocyte destruction → **lichen planus**.
- ✗ Hyperproliferation of keratinocytes with abnormal differentiation → **psoriasis**.
- ✗ IgE-mediated type I hypersensitivity → **urticaria**.
- ✗ T-cell-mediated granuloma in multiple organs → **sarcoidosis**.
- **Reusable pattern:** match mechanism class → disease.

### Q: SLE — how do you investigate the multisystem picture?

- **Bloods:** FBC, U&E, ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm, complement (C3/C4), antiphospholipid Ab, direct Coombs'.
- **Urinalysis** — for proteinuria/RBCs (lupus nephritis is silent).
- Organ-targeted: BP, CXR, ECG/echo, MRI/CT if neurological, kidney biopsy if renal.

⭐ **Don't drop urinalysis from the SLE workup.** It is the screening test for lupus nephritis. SBAQ-1 stem (Figure L1.34) tests this directly: correct answer = urinalysis.

### Dermatomyositis (DM)

![DM named cutaneous signs — Gottron's papules, ragged cuticles, shawl sign, heliotrope rash, photosensitive cheek erythema](figures/L1_07_dm_named_signs.jpg)
*Figure L1.07 — DM named cutaneous signs.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.06 (Case 2 stem) — 50F with new rash on face, chest, upper back, dorsal hands; weakness, weight loss, fatigue. ALT and CK both raised. Diagnosis?

- **Dermatomyositis.**
- Photodistributed pink-**violet** rash + proximal muscle weakness.
- ↑CK from muscle. ↑ALT can be from muscle, **not always liver**.
- **Adult-onset DM = paraneoplastic until proven otherwise.**

### Q: DM — what is it, and which two body systems define it?

- Autoimmune connective tissue disease.
- **Skin + muscle** = the core dyad.
- "Proximal" weakness = shoulders + hips → patient can't climb stairs, can't comb hair.

### Q: Named cutaneous signs of DM (recognise these)?

- **Heliotrope rash** — periocular violet erythema (heliotrope = a violet flower).
- **Gottron's papules** — violaceous flat papules over MCP / PIP / DIP joints.
- **Shawl sign** — V-shaped violet erythema across upper back/neck.
- **Ragged cuticles** — irregular periungual cuticles, sometimes with infarcts.
- Photosensitive cheek erythema.

### Q: DM — non-cutaneous manifestations linked to autoantibody status?

- **Myopathy** — proximal muscle weakness.
- **Interstitial lung disease** (anti-MDA5 — also digital ulcers/ischaemia).
- **Malignancy in adults** — especially **ovarian, lung, pancreatic**.
- Dysphagia, arthritis.

### Q: DM — what's the principle behind the autoantibody-phenotype map (Figure L1.08)?

- Autoantibody profile predicts the clinical pattern (muscle / lung / cancer / skin variants).
- **Concept** is examinable; **specific antibody names are not** (lecturer flagged this).
- One worth recognising: **anti-MDA5 → ILD + digital ulcers/ischaemia**.

### Q: DM — investigations?

- Bloods: ANA, **CK** (raised), **LFTs (ALT often raised)**.
- Skin biopsy.
- **EMG** (electrical activity of muscle).
- Muscle MRI.
- **CT chest/abdomen/pelvis** to screen for malignancy.

⚠️ **Trap: raised ALT in DM is from damaged muscle, not liver.**

### IgA vasculitis

![Vasculitis classification by vessel size](figures/L1_11_vasculitis_tree.jpg)
*Figure L1.11 — Vasculitis classification: large (Takayasu, GCA), medium (PAN, Kawasaki), small (ANCA-associated, immune-complex incl. IgA), variable (Behçet's, Cogan's).*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.09 (Case 3 stem) — 3-year-old male with recent sore throat, abdominal pain, lower-leg rash with palpable purpura, ankle oedema. Urinalysis 50–100 RBCs. Diagnosis + biopsy finding?

- **IgA vasculitis** (formerly Henoch-Schönlein purpura).
- Skin biopsy direct immunofluorescence: **perivascular IgA deposition** (Figure L1.10).
- Often post-infectious (post-streptococcal URTI).
- **Renal involvement determines prognosis** — always check BP + urinalysis.

### Q: IgA vasculitis — the tetrad?

- **Abdominal pain.**
- **Palpable purpura** (you can feel the bumpiness — bleeding around vessels).
- **Arthralgia / arthritis.**
- **Renal involvement** — IgA-associated glomerulonephritis, raised BP.

### Q: Vasculitis — match cutaneous morphology to vessel size (Figure L1.12)?

- **Small vessel** → macular or palpable **purpura**.
- **Medium vessel** → **digital necrosis**, **retiform purpura with ulcers** (cornered, not round), subcutaneous nodules along vessels.
- **Pattern:** the deeper the vessel, the bigger and more sculpted the lesion.

---

### Q: 🧠 How do photo-distributed rash + multisystem features narrow your autoimmune CTD differential?

- Both **SLE** and **DM** present with photo-distributed rash + systemic symptoms.
- SLE: pink-red rash + immune-complex deposition + multi-organ.
- DM: pink-**violet** rash + proximal muscle weakness + ↑CK/ALT + paraneoplastic risk.
- Hue (red vs violet) and proximal weakness are the highest-yield discriminators.

## Section 2 — Severe cutaneous adverse drug reactions (SCARs)

### DRESS — Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms

![DRESS — widespread morbilliform / erythrodermic eruption with internal organ involvement](figures/L1_14_dress_dark_skin.jpg)
*Figure L1.14 — DRESS rash.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.13 (Case 4 stem) — 58M with multiple myeloma. Rash, fever, tachycardia, raised eosinophils, deteriorating renal function, deranged LFTs. Recently treated co-amoxiclav + tazocin. Diagnosis?

- **DRESS** — Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms.
- Rash + systemic upset + haematological + solid-organ disturbance.
- Antibiotics (esp. β-lactams) are common triggers.
- **Liver = the killer organ.**

### Q: DRESS — RegiSCAR diagnostic criteria (the components)?

- **Fever ≥38.5°C.**
- **Lymphadenopathy ≥2 sites, >1 cm.**
- **Circulating atypical lymphocytes.**
- **Peripheral hypereosinophilia >0.7 × 10⁹.**
- Internal organ involvement (liver / kidneys / cardiac).
- Negative ANA + hep/mycoplasma/chlamydia (ruling out mimics).

### Q: DRESS — skin involvement criteria?

- **>50% body surface area** affected.
- At least 2 of: oedema, infiltration, purpura, scaling.
- Cutaneous eruption suggestive of DRESS.
- Biopsy suggestive of DRESS.
- Late-stage shows desquamation (Figure L1.15) + multiple morphologies (Figures L1.16–17).

### Q: DRESS — internal organ involvement and which one kills?

- **Liver (hepatitis) — associated with majority of deaths.**
- Kidneys (interstitial nephritis).
- Heart (myocarditis).
- Brain.
- Thyroid (thyroiditis).
- Lungs (interstitial pneumonitis).

⭐ **Liver failure = the cause of most DRESS deaths.** Withdraw the drug + start steroids before fulminant hepatitis.

### Q: DRESS — drug latency + common triggers?

- Onset **2–6 weeks after drug exposure**.
- Triggers: **allopurinol** (gout), **sulfonamides**, **anti-epileptics** (carbamazepine, phenytoin, lamotrigine), **antibiotics** (vancomycin, amoxicillin, minocycline, piperacillin-tazobactam), **ibuprofen**.

### Q: DRESS — treatment + mortality?

- **Withdraw culprit drug.**
- **Corticosteroids first-line** — may need months (sometimes years to taper).
- **Mortality 5–10%.**

### Q: 🧠 How do you tell DRESS from SJS/TEN at the bedside?

- DRESS = **systemic** (fever, lymphadenopathy, eosinophilia, organ derangement) + morbilliform rash. **No skin detachment.**
- SJS/TEN = **skin sloughing** + mucosal involvement + Nikolsky positive. **No prominent lymphadenopathy.**
- DRESS comes 2–6 weeks after drug; SJS/TEN within 3 weeks.

### SJS / TEN — Stevens-Johnson Syndrome / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis

![SJS/TEN — atypical targetoid macules + blisters merging into sheets of skin detachment with mucosal involvement](figures/L1_19_sjsten_clinical_bsa.jpg)
*Figure L1.19 — SJS/TEN clinical photo with BSA cut-off table.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.18 (Case 5 stem) — 27M with epilepsy, started new antiepileptic. Flu-like illness → sore eyes → oral ulceration → extensive painful rash with skin loss. Diagnosis?

- **SJS/TEN.**
- **Derm emergency** — full-thickness epidermal necrosis + sheet detachment.
- Aetiology: cell-mediated cytotoxic reaction; **drugs >80%**, may be started up to 3 weeks before rash.
- AED triggers: phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine.

### Q: SJS vs SJS/TEN overlap vs TEN — BSA cut-offs?

- **SJS: <10% BSA detachment.**
- **SJS/TEN overlap: 10–30%.**
- **TEN: >30%.**
- Late-stage TEN shown in Figure L1.22 (back + lip haemorrhagic crusting).

### Q: SJS/TEN — clinical course and key signs?

- **Prodromal flu-like symptoms** + sore eyes + oral ulceration.
- Then abrupt rash on **trunk > face/limbs**.
- Atypical targetoid macules + blisters → merge into sheets of detachment.
- Mucosal involvement: oral, ocular, urogenital.

### Q: SJS/TEN — drug culprits (Figure L1.20)?

- **Antibiotics:** β-lactams, sulphonamides.
- **Allopurinol.**
- **Anti-epileptics:** phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine.
- **NSAIDs.**

### Q: SCORTEN — what is it and what does ≥5 mean (Figure L1.21)?

- 7-point score (1pt each): **age >40, HR >120, neoplasia, detachment >10%, urea >10, bicarb <20, glucose >14**.
- Mortality: 0–1 → 3% · 2 → 12% · 3 → 35% · 4 → 58% · **≥5 → 90%**.

### Q: SJS/TEN — DDx of "extensive skin loss"?

- **Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS)** — exfoliative-toxin-mediated, no mucosal involvement, children, blanching erythema.
- Thermal burns.
- Cutaneous graft-versus-host disease.

⚠️ **Trap: lymphadenopathy is NOT a characteristic of SJS/TEN.** Lymphadenopathy belongs to **DRESS** criteria. SBA-2 (Figure L1.35) tests this.

### Q: TEN — supportive management essentials?

- **Withdraw culprit drug.**
- **Burns-unit / barrier nursing**, wound care/dressings.
- Fluid + electrolyte balance, nutrition (NG tube), thermoregulation, pressure-relieving mattress.
- Emollients to support remaining skin barrier.
- Multi-system care: ocular (lubricant + topical steroid), oral (mouthwash, benzydamine, topical steroid), urogenital (topical steroid).

### Q: TEN — medical therapies and complications?

- **Therapies:** IV immunoglobulin, ciclosporin, TNF-α inhibitors (e.g. etanercept), G-CSF, LMWH, systemic antibiotics, analgesia.
- **Acute complications:** sepsis, multi-organ failure, AKI, respiratory failure, DIC.
- **Long-term:** ocular (blindness, ectropion, symblepharon), strictures (oral/oesophageal/genital), nail dystrophy, pigment changes, PTSD.

### Q: 🧠 SJS/TEN vs SSSS — how to distinguish?

- SJS/TEN — drug cause, **mucosal involvement**, full-thickness epidermal necrosis.
- SSSS — *S. aureus* exfoliative toxins targeting **desmoglein I**, **no mucosal involvement**, children, splits superficial epidermis only.
- See also: L2 — "Impetigo + exfoliative toxins"

### AGEP — Acute Generalised Exanthematous Pustulosis

![AGEP — widespread red skin studded with pinhead-sized non-follicular sterile pustules](figures/L1_27_agep_close_up.jpg)
*Figure L1.27 — AGEP close-up: pinhead non-follicular sterile pustules on erythematous background.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.26 (Case 7 stem) — 68F with acute rash 2 days after starting amoxicillin. Fever 38.8°C, widespread erythema, numerous small non-follicular pustules, facial oedema, no mucosal involvement, neutrophilia. Diagnosis?

- **AGEP** — Acute Generalised Exanthematous Pustulosis.
- **>90% caused by medications, most often β-lactam antibiotics.**
- Onset within ~2 days of drug.
- No mucosal involvement (in contrast to SJS/TEN).

### Q: AGEP — defining clinical pattern?

- Rapid (within ~2 days) **widespread red skin studded with pinhead-sized sterile pustules** (non-follicular).
- Starts **face / armpits / groin** → widespread (Figure L1.28).
- Often facial swelling.
- ~20% have oral lesions; mostly mucosa-spared.
- Patient often **not particularly unwell**; ~10% have systemic involvement.
- Same pattern in dark skin shown in Figure L1.29.

### Q: AGEP — main drug triggers?

- **>90% caused by medications, most often β-lactam antibiotics (penicillins).**
- Other: tetracyclines, sulfonamides, quinolones, oral antifungals (esp. terbinafine), CCBs (diltiazem), hydroxychloroquine, carbamazepine, paracetamol.
- In **children**, viral triggers (EBV, enterovirus, adenovirus, CMV, HBV) are common.

### Q: AGEP — investigations and biopsy finding?

- Diagnosed clinically.
- Bloods: **neutrophilia**, raised inflammatory markers.
- **Skin biopsy: subcorneal pustules filled with neutrophils.**

### Q: AGEP — management?

- **Discontinue culprit medication** (esp. antibiotics).
- Supportive: moisturisers, topical corticosteroids, oral antihistamines, analgesics.
- Resolves spontaneously over **~10 days** — peels off with **collarettes of desquamation** (Figure L1.30).
- Admit if systemically unwell; otherwise can manage at home.

### Erythroderma

![Erythroderma — generalised dusky erythema in dark skin](figures/L1_24_erythroderma_dark_skin.jpg)
*Figure L1.24 — Erythroderma in dark skin.*

![Tutorial Case 1 — 62M with erythrodermic psoriasis, hands](../Tutorial/figures/T_01_case1_erythroderma.jpg)
*Figure T.1 — Tutorial Case 1: 62M, 3-week worsening generalised redness/scaling, hypothermia 35.8°C, tachycardia 105, ankle oedema. Hx: 15-year psoriasis, stopped Enstilar 2 weeks ago = erythrodermic psoriasis.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.23 (Case 6 stem) — 40M with longstanding psoriasis. Recently received oral steroids for chest infection. Psoriasis worsened after steroid course. Diagnosis + mechanism?

- **Erythrodermic psoriasis** from **rebound after systemic steroid withdrawal**.
- Generalised erythema affecting >90% BSA → skin-failure picture.
- Late-stage scaling shown in Figure L1.25.

### Q: Erythroderma — definition and what makes it dangerous?

- **Generalised erythema affecting >90% body surface area.**
- Skin is functionally failing → systemic complications:
  - **Heat loss → hypothermia.**
  - **Fluid + protein loss → electrolyte abnormalities + dehydration + hypoalbuminaemia.**
  - **Red skin → high-output heart failure / tachycardia.**
  - **Risk of sepsis** (secondary bacterial infection).

### Q: Erythroderma — main aetiologies (tutorial Q2)?

- **Psoriasis** (often after withdrawal of systemic steroids — rebound).
- **Atopic eczema.**
- **Drug reactions.**
- **Cutaneous T-cell lymphoma** — Sézary syndrome (the erythrodermic form of CTCL).
- **Idiopathic** in ~30%.
- Other: pityriasis rubra pilaris, contact dermatitis, stasis dermatitis, pemphigus / bullous pemphigoid.

### Q: 📎 Figure T.1 (tutorial Q3) — most appropriate initial management for the 62M with erythrodermic psoriasis?

- **A. Admit to hospital for supportive care and specialist dermatology input ✓**
- ✗ Methotrexate alone, outpatient → patient is hypothermic + tachycardic, needs admission.
- ✗ Oral antibiotics for presumed infection → no clear infection in stem.
- ✗ Discharge with topical steroids → too unwell.
- ✗ Antihistamines + emollients only → undertreatment.

### Q: Erythroderma — initial management?

- **Treat underlying cause** (resume psoriasis treatment, withdraw drug, oral steroids if eczema).
- **Hospitalise if systemically unwell.**
- Restore fluid + electrolyte balance, manage temperature.
- Emollients (skin barrier).
- Mild topical steroids.
- Antibiotics if signs of secondary skin infection.
- Discontinue all unnecessary medications.
- ⭐ **Skin biopsy if uncertain regarding underlying cause** — tutorial slide footnote.

⚠️ **Bilateral red legs ≠ "bilateral cellulitis".** True cellulitis is almost always unilateral. Diffuse bilateral redness should make you think erythroderma or stasis dermatitis.

### Q: 🧠 How do you decide if erythroderma is from CTCL vs another cause?

- Persistent, treatment-refractory erythroderma in an adult → consider **Sézary syndrome** (erythrodermic CTCL).
- Workup: skin biopsy + peripheral blood for atypical T cells (Sézary cells).
- See also: Pruritus DDx below — CTCL is also a cause of itch without rash.

## Section 3 — Pruritus without rash

![Nodular prurigo — firm pigmented nodules from chronic scratching](figures/L1_33_nodular_prurigo.jpg)
*Figure L1.33 — Nodular prurigo: skin reaction to chronic systemic itch.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.31 (Case 8 stem) — 54M with 1 year of pruritus, no rash, worsening. Antihistamines unhelpful. Polycythaemia on FBC. Diagnostic approach?

- **Itch without rash → suggests an internal cause.**
- Polycythaemia is one of the classic systemic itch causes.
- Excoriations from chronic scratching may form (Figure L1.32) → eventually nodular prurigo (Figure L1.33).
- Run the systemic itch panel.

### Q: Pruritus — DDx (the systemic causes)?

- **Lymphoma** (esp. Hodgkin's).
- **Polycythaemia.**
- **Uraemia** (CKD).
- **Cholestasis.**
- **Iron deficiency or iron overload.**
- **HIV / Hepatitis B / C.**
- Cancer; drugs (NB **opiates/opioids**); psychogenic; pruritus of old age.

⭐ **Never forget scabies as a cause of itch.** Even when there's no obvious rash. See L2 — Scabies.

### Q: Pruritus — investigation panel?

- **FBC** (look for anaemia, polycythaemia, lymphocytosis).
- **LDH** (raised in lymphoma).
- **Renal profile** (uraemia).
- **LFTs** (cholestasis).
- **Bone profile, ferritin, thyroid function, HbA1c.**
- **CXR** (rule out lung pathology / TB).
- HIV / hepatitis B / C; immunoglobulins + protein electrophoresis.

### Q: 🧠 When pruritus comes with no rash — what should I always think before diagnosing "psychogenic"?

- Lymphoma / polycythaemia / uraemia / cholestasis / iron / HIV / hepatitis are all reversible-or-treatable causes you cannot afford to miss.
- **Scabies** can mimic any rashless itch.
- Psychogenic is a diagnosis of exclusion only after this workup.

## Section 4 — Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: Pattern — Drug-to-rash latency narrows the SCAR?

| SCAR | Latency | Headline feature |
|---|---|---|
| **AGEP** | ~2 days | Pinhead pustules, β-lactams, well patient |
| **SJS/TEN** | Up to 3 weeks | Mucocutaneous detachment, BSA criterion |
| **DRESS** | 2–6 weeks | Eosinophilia + organ failure (liver kills) |

### Q: Pattern — Photo-distributed rash + multisystem features → which CTDs?

- = think **autoimmune CTD**.
- Hue: pink-red → SLE; pink-**violet** → DM.
- Plus muscle weakness → DM.
- Plus low complement / pancytopenia / proteinuria → SLE.

### Q: Pattern — "Skin failure" complications (any >90% BSA red skin)?

- Peripheral oedema (protein loss).
- Loss of fluid + electrolytes.
- Thermoregulation failure → hypothermia.
- High-output cardiac strain → tachycardia.
- Secondary bacterial infection (esp. *S. aureus* — see L2).
- Sepsis.

### Q: Pattern — "Killer organ" by entity?

- **SLE** — kidney (lupus nephritis, silent → urinalysis mandatory).
- **DM** — paraneoplastic malignancy + interstitial lung disease.
- **IgA vasculitis** — kidney (IgA-associated GN).
- **DRESS** — liver (fulminant hepatitis, majority of deaths).
- **SJS/TEN** — sepsis + multi-organ failure from skin failure.

## Section 5 — Interleaved SBAs

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.34 (SBA-1 stem) — 38F with rash, fever, arthritis, fatigue, no medications. Bloods: pancytopenia, ↑ESR/CRP, ANA 1:640, anti-dsDNA+. Which Ix is indicated?

- **A. Urinalysis ✓**
- ✗ B. Vitamin C → scurvy.
- ✗ C. Lipid panel → eruptive xanthelasma.
- ✗ D. HbA1c → diabetes-related skin signs.
- **Why A:** lupus nephritis is silent — urinalysis screens for proteinuria/RBCs. **Pattern:** in any SLE stem, always check the kidneys.

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.35 (SBA-2 stem) — 23M with epilepsy on a new antiepileptic develops flu-like illness, sore eyes, oral ulceration, then extensive painful rash with skin loss. Which is INCORRECT?

- ✓ A. Complications include blindness.
- ✓ B. Pain is a characteristic feature and diagnostic clue.
- **✗ C. Lymphadenopathy is a characteristic feature ← this is WRONG**
- ✓ D. Extension may occur into the GI tract.
- **Why C is wrong:** Lymphadenopathy ≥2 sites, >1 cm is a **DRESS** criterion, not SJS/TEN.

### Q: 📎 Figure L1.36 (SBA-3 stem) — 58M with multiple myeloma develops rash, fever, eosinophilia, deranged LFTs after co-amoxiclav + tazocin. Which is correct re DRESS?

- ✗ A. Brain not among organs targeted (brain CAN be targeted).
- ✗ B. Erythroderma complications most fatalities (**fulminant liver failure** does).
- ✗ C. Characteristic biopsy features allow diagnostic confirmation (biopsy is supportive; RegiSCAR).
- **D. Fulminant liver failure may occur ✓**
- **Pattern:** in DRESS, the liver is the killer.

### Q: SBA — Which scenario is most likely AGEP rather than SJS/TEN?

- "68F started on amoxicillin 24 hours ago. Widespread erythema studded with **pinhead non-follicular pustules**, facial oedema, **no mucosal involvement**, neutrophilia." → **AGEP.**
- AGEP cues: rapid onset (~2 days), pustules, no mucosa, often well, β-lactam.
- SJS/TEN cues: 1–3 weeks, mucosal involvement, sheet detachment, painful targetoid lesions.

### Q: SBA — A 3-year-old with recent sore throat, abdo pain, palpable purpura on lower legs, ankle oedema, urinalysis 50–100 RBCs. Diagnosis + what must you check?

- **Dx: IgA vasculitis** (formerly HSP).
- **Tetrad:** abdo pain, palpable purpura, arthralgia, **renal involvement**.
- Must check: renal function + BP (IgA-associated GN determines prognosis).
- Skin biopsy DIF would show **perivascular IgA deposition**.

## Reusable reasoning patterns

### Q: 🧠 How to approach a "rash + systemic features" stem (reusable method)?

- **Step 1.** Note any drug history → defines latency window → narrows SCAR.
- **Step 2.** Note photo-distribution → autoimmune CTD direction.
- **Step 3.** Note mucosal involvement → SJS/TEN if yes; DRESS/AGEP/SLE/DM if no.
