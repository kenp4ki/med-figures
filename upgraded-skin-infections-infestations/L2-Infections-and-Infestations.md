## Section 1 — Bacterial infections

### Folliculitis → Furuncle → Carbuncle

![Folliculitis severity ladder — normal follicle → pimple → boil (furuncle) → carbuncle](figures/L2_01_folliculitis_anatomy.jpg)
*Figure L2.01 — Severity ladder of follicular infection.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.02 — describe the lesion shown and name the organism most likely responsible.

- **Folliculitis** — perifollicular erythema with pustules.
- Most cases: ***Staphylococcus aureus***.
- May be infectious or non-infectious.
- Recurrent — Figure L2.03 — think nasal carriage of *S. aureus* (esp. PVL strains).

### Q: Severity ladder of follicular infection — what gets worse as you go deeper?

- **Folliculitis** — perifollicular erythema ± pustule. Surface only.
- **Furuncle (boil)** — deep follicular abscess.
- **Carbuncle** — cluster of boils, multiple connected follicles.
- More likely to lead to cellulitis or septicaemia at the carbuncle end.

### Q: Folliculitis — main organism, key non-infectious form, recurrent twist?

- Most cases: ***Staphylococcus aureus***.
- **Eosinophilic (non-infectious) folliculitis is associated with HIV.**
- Recurrent cases → think nasal carriage of *S. aureus*, particularly **PVL strains** (Panton-Valentine Leukocidin) → painful abscesses, more aggressive course.

### Q: Folliculitis treatment — and how does PVL Staph change management?

- Standard: **flucloxacillin or erythromycin**.
- Incision + drainage for furuncles.
- **PVL Staph protocol:**
  - Consult local microbiology.
  - Antibiotics — often **tetracycline**.
  - **Decolonisation:** chlorhexidine body wash 7 days + nasal mupirocin 5 days.
  - Treat close contacts.

### Cellulitis

![Cellulitis — unilateral, ill-defined, blanching erythema with oedema; outlined to monitor progression](figures/L2_04_cellulitis.jpg)
*Figure L2.04 — Cellulitis.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.04 — describe the lesion and the organism responsible.

- **Cellulitis** — unilateral, tender, ill-defined, blanching erythema + oedema.
- **Most cases: Streptococcus pyogenes + Staphylococcus aureus.**
- The pen line on the skin is to monitor progression.

### Q: Cellulitis — anatomy, organisms, key sign?

- Infection of **lower dermis + subcutaneous tissue**.
- **Tender swelling** with **ill-defined, blanching erythema** + oedema.
- Most cases: ***Streptococcus pyogenes*** + ***Staphylococcus aureus***.
- **Oedema is a predisposing factor** (chronic lymphoedema, venous insufficiency).
- Treatment: systemic antibiotics, e.g. flucloxacillin.

⚠️ **Bilateral cellulitis is rare.** True cellulitis is almost always unilateral. If both legs look red, consider **stasis dermatitis** or erythroderma — see L1.

### Impetigo + Impetiginisation

![Impetigo with classic honey-coloured crusts](figures/L2_05_impetigo_honey.jpg)
*Figure L2.05 — Impetigo: stuck-on honey crusts (perioral, ears, nares).*

![Impetiginisation of eczema — gold crust on dry/eczematous skin](figures/L2_06_impetiginisation_gold.jpg)
*Figure L2.06 — Impetiginisation of atopic dermatitis: thick gold-yellow crusts.*

### Q: 📎 Figures L2.05 vs L2.06 — what is the visual distinction and why does it matter?

- **L2.05 honey crust = primary impetigo** (no underlying skin disease).
- **L2.06 gold crust = impetiginisation of eczema** (bacterial overlay on existing eczema).
- Both *S. aureus*-driven, but management differs: impetigo treats just the bacterial infection; impetiginisation treats the bacteria PLUS the underlying eczema.

### Q: Impetigo — defining sign, organisms, mechanism?

- **Stuck-on, honey-coloured crusts.** Often face (perioral, ears, nares).
- Causes:
  - **Streptococci** (non-bullous), or
  - **Staphylococci** (bullous or non-bullous).
- **Exfoliative toxins A & B** (Staph) target **desmoglein I** → split epidermis → bullous impetigo + **SSSS** (staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome).
- Treatment: topical ± systemic antibiotics (e.g. flucloxacillin).

### Q: Impetiginisation — what is it and what's the visual cue?

- Secondary *S. aureus* infection on top of an existing skin disease (classically **atopic dermatitis**).
- **Visual cue: gold crust** (versus honey crust of primary impetigo).
- Treat with antibiotics + treat the underlying eczema.

### Q: 🧠 Honey crust vs gold crust — same organism family, different problem?

- **Honey crust = primary impetigo.** No underlying skin disease. Treat the impetigo.
- **Gold crust = impetiginisation.** Bacterial overlay on existing eczema/dermatitis. Treat the bacterial infection AND the underlying skin disease.
- Both involve *S. aureus*. The flip is what was already there.

### Lyme disease (Borreliosis)

![Erythema migrans — annular erythema >20 cm at site of Ixodes tick bite](figures/L2_07_erythema_migrans.jpg)
*Figure L2.07 — Erythema migrans of Lyme disease.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.07 — describe the lesion, name the vector and organism.

- **Erythema migrans** — annular erythema >20 cm with central clearing.
- Vector: ***Ixodes* tick.**
- Organism: ***Borrelia burgdorferi***.
- Only present in ~75% of Lyme cases — **clinical suspicion alone is grounds for treatment** even without EM.

### Q: Lyme disease — primary cutaneous sign?

- **Primary lesion: Erythema migrans** (only ~75% of cases).
  - Erythematous papule at bite site.
  - Progresses to **annular erythema >20 cm**.
- Secondary lesions (Figure L2.08) are similar but smaller.

### Q: Lyme disease — disseminated / late features?

- 1–30 days after infection: fever, headache.
- Multiple secondary lesions — similar to but smaller than primary.
- **Neuroborreliosis:** facial palsy / other CN palsies, aseptic meningitis, polyradiculitis.
- **Arthritis** — large joints, **knee** most affected.
- Carditis.

### Q: Lyme disease — diagnostic gotcha?

- **Serology: not sensitive enough.**
- **Histopathology: non-specific.**
- **High index of suspicion is required** — clinical suspicion alone is grounds for treatment.

### Q: Lyme disease — treatment?

- **Doxycycline.**
- Amoxicillin.
- Cefuroxime.

⭐ **Don't wait for serology.** If the stem says "tick bite + erythema migrans + camping/forest exposure," start treatment.

### Syphilis — the Great Mimicker

![Primary syphilis chancre — painless ulcer with firm indurated border](figures/L2_09_syphilis_chancre.jpg)
*Figure L2.09 — Primary syphilis chancres on the genitalia: painless, firm, indurated.*

![Secondary syphilis — papulosquamous lesions on trunk and soles with collarette of scale](figures/L2_11_secondary_syphilis_morph.jpg)
*Figure L2.11 — Secondary syphilis morphology: pink papules, pityriasis rosea-like, sole lesions with collarette of scale.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.09 — describe the lesion, name the organism, and the painful/painless distinction?

- **Primary syphilis chancre.**
- ***Treponema pallidum*** (a spirochete).
- **Painless** ulcer with **firm indurated border** — pain status is a high-yield clue (vs painful HSV vesicles, vs painful SJS/TEN detachment).

### Q: Primary syphilis — clinical features?

- **Chancre:** painless ulcer with firm indurated border.
- **Painless** regional lymphadenopathy ~1 week after chancre.
- Chancre appears within **10–90 days of exposure**.

### Q: Secondary syphilis — when, and which named features?

- Begins **~50 days** after the chancre.
- Systemic: malaise, fever, headache, pruritus, anorexia, iritis.
- **Skin/mucosa:**
  - **Rash (88–100%)** — pink papules, often trunk; sole/palm lesions with **collarette of scale** (Figure L2.10).
  - **'Moth-eaten' alopecia.**
  - **Mucous patches** in mouth.
  - **Condylomata lata** (broad warty plaques in vulval/anal area — Figure L2.12).
  - Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, residual chancre.

### Q: Tertiary syphilis — features (Figure L2.13)?

- **Gumma** — nodular/plaque skin lesions extending peripherally with central scarring/atrophy.
- Mucosal lesions destroying nasal cartilage.
- **Cardiovascular disease** (aortitis, aneurysm).
- **Neurosyphilis** — general paresis, tabes dorsalis.

### Q: Syphilis — diagnosis + treatment?

- Diagnosis: **clinical findings + serology** (Figure L2.14 shows dark-field + histology + immunostain). Strong index of suspicion needed in 2°.
- Treatment: **IM benzylpenicillin or oral tetracycline (doxycycline).**

⚠️ **Condylomata lata of syphilis can be misdiagnosed as condylomata acuminata (HPV warts).** They're not warts — they're 2° syphilis.

### Q: 🧠 Painless vs painful ulcer — quick differential?

- **Painless** ulcer with indurated border → think **syphilis chancre**.
- **Painful** mucocutaneous detachment → think **SJS/TEN** (drug emergency, see L1).
- **Painful grouped vesicles** → think **HSV** or VZV (dermatomal).

---

### Q: 🧠 How does S. aureus cause secondary skin infection across multiple lectures?

- *S. aureus* is the dominant overlay organism whenever skin barrier is broken: impetiginisation of eczema, kerion superinfection, secondary infection in **erythroderma** (L1) and eczema herpeticum.
- Recognise: gold crust, fluctuance, fever, increased exudate.
- See also: L1 — "Erythroderma — initial management" and "TEN — supportive management"

## Section 2 — Viral infections

### Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)

![HSV — painful grouped vesicles on erythematous base; whitlow on finger](figures/L2_15_hsv_genital_whitlow.jpg)
*Figure L2.15 — HSV genital lesions + herpetic whitlow.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.15 — describe the lesion morphology and the latency biology.

- **Painful grouped vesicles on erythematous base** → ulceration / erosions with **scalloped border**.
- HSV-1: orolabial favoured (saliva contact). HSV-2: genital favoured (sexual contact).
- After replication, virus travels by **retrograde axonal flow to dorsal root ganglia** → latent for life.
- Reactivation triggers: UV, fever, stress, local tissue damage. Recurrent lesions appear in same site (Figure L2.16).

### Q: HSV — types, transmission, latency biology?

- **HSV-1:** direct contact with contaminated saliva / secretions (orolabial favoured).
- **HSV-2:** sexual contact (genital favoured).
- Replicates at mucocutaneous site → travels by **retrograde axonal flow to dorsal root ganglia** → latent.
- Transmission can occur during **asymptomatic viral shedding**.

### Q: HSV — clinical features of the primary or recurrent eruption?

- Symptoms within **3–7 days** of exposure.
- Preceded by tender lymphadenopathy, malaise, anorexia; ± burning/tingling.
- **Painful grouped vesicles on erythematous base** → ulceration / pustules / **erosions with scalloped border**.
- Crusting + resolution within 2–6 weeks.
- Genital HSV → can be excruciating → **urinary retention**.

### Q: HSV — reactivation triggers and rare systemic complication?

- Triggers: spontaneous, UV light, fever or local tissue damage, stress.
- **Aseptic meningitis** in up to 10% of HSV cases — headache + neck stiffness should be taken seriously.

### Eczema herpeticum (HSV emergency)

![Eczema herpeticum — monomorphic, punched-out erosions on poorly-controlled atopic dermatitis](figures/L2_17_eczema_herpeticum.jpg)
*Figure L2.17 — Eczema herpeticum: dermatological emergency.*

![Tutorial Case 3 — paediatric eczema herpeticum: 6yo boy with atopic dermatitis, fever, clusters of painful blisters with central crusting on eczema-affected skin](../Tutorial/figures/T_05_case3_eczema_herpeticum_paed.jpg)
*Figure T.5 — Tutorial Case 3 (paediatric eczema herpeticum).*

![Adult eczema herpeticum on poorly-controlled atopic dermatitis](../Tutorial/figures/T_06_eczema_herpeticum_adult.jpg)
*Figure T.6 — Adult eczema herpeticum.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.17 — describe the morphology and the management urgency.

- **Eczema herpeticum.**
- **Monomorphic, punched-out erosions** (excoriated vesicles, all same size) on background of **poorly-controlled atopic dermatitis**.
- **Dermatological emergency.**
- **Treatment: IV aciclovir** (don't delay) + treat the underlying eczema.

### Q: 📎 Figure T.5 (Tutorial Case 3 stem) — 6yo boy with atopic dermatitis, 2-day fever, painful blisters on face/neck/arms with central crusting, scattered over eczema-affected skin. Diagnosis + most useful Ix + management?

- **Dx: eczema herpeticum** (HSV-1 disseminating across disrupted eczema barrier).
- **Most useful Ix: viral skin swab for PCR.**
- **Management:** dermatological emergency → admit + **IV aciclovir**; secondary bacterial infection → systemic antibiotics; treat underlying eczema with topical steroids (most start ~48h after antiviral); consult ophthalmology if eyelid involvement.

### Q: Eczema herpeticum — who, what, why does it matter?

- HSV (usually **HSV-1**, less likely HSV-2) disseminating across the disrupted barrier of poorly-controlled **atopic dermatitis**.
- **Visual cue: monomorphic, punched-out erosions** on an eczema background.
- **Dermatological emergency.**
- **Treatment: IV aciclovir** + treat the underlying eczema.

### Q: Eczema herpeticum — diagnostic test?

- **Viral skin swab for PCR.**
- Don't wait for the result before starting aciclovir.

⭐ **"Punched-out + monomorphic + on eczema" = eczema herpeticum.** High-yield emergency in lecture and tutorial.

### Other HSV variants

![Herpetic whitlow — HSV infection of digit; often misdiagnosed as paronychia or dactylitis](figures/L2_18_whitlow_gallery.jpg)
*Figure L2.18 — Herpetic whitlow gallery.*

![Neonatal HSV — vesicles → bullae → erosions on scalp/trunk](figures/L2_19_neonatal_hsv.jpg)
*Figure L2.19 — Neonatal HSV.*

![Severe / chronic HSV in immunocompromise — chronic enlarging ulceration, atypical morphology](figures/L2_20_severe_hsv.jpg)
*Figure L2.20 — Severe / chronic HSV in immunocompromised host.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.18 — name this entity, describe who gets it, and what it's misdiagnosed as.

- **Herpetic whitlow** — HSV (1>2) infection of digits.
- **Often in children.**
- Misdiagnosed as **paronychia** or **dactylitis**.
- Pain + swelling first; vesicles may appear later.

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.19 — name this entity, describe acquisition, and the killer complication.

- **Neonatal HSV.**
- Acquired during **vaginal delivery** — risk **highest when mother acquired HSV near delivery**.
- HSV 1 or 2.
- Onset birth → 2 weeks; usually scalp or trunk; vesicles → bullae → erosions.
- **Encephalitis: mortality >50% untreated, 15% even with treatment, often with neurological sequelae.**
- Requires **IV antivirals**.

### Q: HSV in immunocompromise — atypical presentations (Figure L2.20)?

- Chronic, enlarging ulceration is the most common presentation.
- Multiple sites or disseminated.
- Atypical lesion morphology — verrucous, exophytic, pustular.
- Respiratory or GI tract involvement may occur.

### Q: HSV — diagnosis + treatment summary (Figure L2.21)?

- **Diagnosis:** viral skin swab for PCR.
- **Treatment:** don't delay. **Oral or IV aciclovir** as per BNF, depending on severity.

### Varicella Zoster Virus (Shingles / Herpes Zoster)

![Shingles — dermatomal blistering rash; V1 ophthalmic involvement (top), truncal dermatomal (bottom, doesn't cross midline)](figures/L2_22_vzv_v1_truncal.jpg)
*Figure L2.22 — Shingles: V1 ophthalmic + truncal dermatomal.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.22 (top half) — describe the distribution and the urgent referral required.

- **Shingles in V1 (ophthalmic) division of trigeminal nerve.**
- **Eye complications** when V1 is involved → **ophthalmology referral**.
- Same picture in Figure L2.23.

### Q: VZV — biology and what makes shingles, shingles?

- After primary chickenpox, VZV remains **dormant in dorsal root ganglia**.
- Reactivation → migrates **down a sensory nerve** → **dermatomal blistering painful rash**.
- Anyone who's had chickenpox can develop shingles; more common in weak immunity.
- **Doesn't cross midline** (truncal example in Figure L2.22 bottom half).

### Q: Shingles — treatment essentials and the timing rule?

- **Antivirals (aciclovir):** start within **72 hours of rash onset** to shorten illness and reduce nerve pain (post-herpetic neuralgia).
- **Analgesia** — including neuropathic agents; pain may persist 2–3 years.
- **Conservative:** keep clean and dry, loose clothing, emollients.

### Q: Shingles — major complications (Figure L2.23)?

- **Eye complications** when **ophthalmic division (V1) of the 5th cranial nerve** is involved → ophthalmology referral.
- Multi-dermatomal involvement.
- Deep blisters → scarring.
- **Ramsay Hunt syndrome** — facial nerve palsy from VZV; 50% chance of complete recovery.
- Encephalitis, pneumonitis (rare).

⭐ **72-hour antiviral window for shingles.** Same drug (aciclovir) as for HSV — but the timing rule is what's tested.

### Q: 🧠 HSV vs VZV — how to keep them straight at the bedside?

- **HSV:** grouped vesicles, scalloped border, recurrent at the same site, orolabial/genital, no dermatome.
- **VZV (shingles):** **dermatomal** (doesn't cross midline), painful prodrome, single episode (usually).
- Both treated with aciclovir; both confirmed with **viral skin swab + PCR**.

### Pityriasis versicolor (superficial)

![Pityriasis versicolor — hypopigmented + hyperpigmented macules with fine scale on neck/cheek](figures/L2_25_pityriasis_versicolor.jpg)
*Figure L2.25 — Pityriasis versicolor.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.25 — describe the macules and the organism.

- **Pityriasis versicolor.**
- **Hypopigmented, hyperpigmented, or erythematous macules ± fine scale.**
- ***Malassezia*** spp. (e.g. *M. furfur*) — overgrowth of normal skin yeast.

### Q: Pityriasis versicolor — clinical, organism, treatment?

- **Hypopigmented, hyperpigmented, or erythematous macules ± fine scale** on trunk.
- Begins in adolescence (sebaceous glands active); flares with heat + humidity.
- Treatment: **topical azole (ketoconazole)** + treat scalp (Malassezia lives there too).
- Not contagious.

### Dermatophyte infections + Kerion

![Kerion — boggy, tender, pus-discharging scalp mass from inflammatory dermatophyte infection](figures/L2_26_kerion_tinea_capitis.jpg)
*Figure L2.26 — Tinea capitis with kerion.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.26 — describe the lesion and explain why this is "fungal but looks bacterial".

- **Kerion** — inflammatory fungal infection of scalp.
- **Mimics bacterial folliculitis or scalp abscess** (boggy, tender, pus-discharging).
- Cause: ***Trichophyton tonsurans*** dermatophyte.
- **Frequently secondarily infected with *S. aureus*** → needs combined antifungal + antibacterial.
- Often posterior cervical lymphadenopathy.

### Q: Dermatophytes — what they live on, and the two key Trichophyton species?

- Fungi that **live on keratin** (skin, hair, nails).
- ***Trichophyton rubrum*** — causes the most fungal infections overall.
- ***Trichophyton tonsurans*** — causes the most **tinea capitis** (scalp).

### Q: 🧠 Boggy tender scalp with pustules — bacterial or fungal?

- Looks bacterial; often is fungal (kerion) with secondary *S. aureus*.
- Treat for both: antifungal + antibacterial.

### Candidiasis

![Candidal intertrigo — erythema, oedema, characteristic satellite pustules around main red patch](figures/L2_27_candida_satellite.jpg)
*Figure L2.27 — Candida intertrigo with satellite lesions.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.27 — describe the signature sign and the predisposing factors.

- **Candida intertrigo.**
- Signature: **satellite lesions** (small daughter pustules around the main red patch).
- Predisposing: occlusion, moisture, warmth, **diabetes mellitus**.
- Sites: skin folds + oral mucosa.

### Q: Candidiasis — organism, predisposing factors, treatment?

- ***Candida albicans***.
- **Predisposing:** occlusion, moisture, warm temperature, **diabetes mellitus**.
- Sites: intertriginous (skin folds) or oral mucosa; common cause of vulvovaginitis.
- **Signature sign: satellite lesions.**
- Treatment: topical (localised) or systemic azoles (widespread). Can become systemic in immunocompromise.

### Opportunistic / disseminated fungal infections

![Mucormycosis — black eschar on forearm; rhino-orbital involvement](figures/L2_28_mucormycosis.jpg)
*Figure L2.28 — Mucormycosis.*

### Q: Mucormycosis — minimum you need to know?

- Aggressive, deep fungal infection in immunocompromised hosts.
- Presentation: **oedema → pain → eschar**; rhino-orbital-cerebral form (proptosis, facial pain, CN dysfunction).
- Highest-risk associations: **diabetes mellitus (esp. DKA)**; neutropaenia; steroid/immunosuppressant use; HIV; burns.
- Treatment: **aggressive surgical debridement + amphotericin B**.

📘 **Lecturer flagged the organism taxonomy (*Apophysomyces, Mucor, Rhizopus, Absidia, Rhizomucor*) as not memorisation-worthy.** Recognise the clinical pattern + the highest-risk patient profile.

## Section 4 — Parasitic infestations

### Scabies

![Scabies — pruritic papules at interdigital webs/wrists/genitalia with diagnostic burrow; crusted Norwegian scabies (severe immunocompromised form)](figures/L2_29_scabies_norwegian.jpg)
*Figure L2.29 — Scabies + crusted (Norwegian) scabies.*

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.29 — name the organism and explain the two-cycle treatment rule.

- ***Sarcoptes scabiei*** — female mite burrows into upper epidermis, lays eggs, dies after 1 month.
- **Treatment: topical permethrin ± oral ivermectin, in TWO cycles ~1 week apart.**
- First cycle kills mites; second catches eggs that hatch after the first cycle.
- Right-side image = crusted (Norwegian) scabies — severe immunocompromised form.

### Q: Scabies — organism, lifecycle, key clinical features?

- ***Sarcoptes scabiei*** — female mite burrows into upper epidermis, lays eggs, dies after 1 month.
- **Insidious red/flesh-coloured pruritic papules.**
- Sites: **interdigital webs of digits, volar wrists, axillae, genitalia.**
- Diagnostic burrow — fine white scaling line.

### Q: Crusted (Norwegian) scabies — what is it and when?

- Severe form with **hyperkeratosis** (thick scaly crusts).
- **Often asymptomatic** (no itch).
- Immunocompromised individuals.
- Highly contagious due to vast mite numbers in crusts.

### Q: Scabies — treatment essentials and why two cycles?

- **Topical permethrin** ± oral ivermectin.
- **Two cycles ~1 week apart** — first kills mites, second catches eggs that hatch after the first cycle.
- Other options: benzyl benzoate, malathion.
- Treat all close contacts and bedding/clothing.

⭐ **Two cycles, 1 week apart.** Single-dose treatment fails because the eggs aren't killed by the first round.

### Q: 🧠 Scabies as the must-not-miss cause of itch?

- Even without obvious rash, scabies can present with widespread itch only.
- Look for itch worse at night, family/contact pattern, classic site distribution.
- See also: L1 — "Pruritus DDx" ("never forget scabies as a cause of itch").

## Section 5 — Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: Pattern — When clinical suspicion alone is enough?

- **Lyme disease** — serology not sensitive enough; treat on clinical pattern.
- **Eczema herpeticum** — IV aciclovir without waiting for PCR.
- **Shingles** — aciclovir within 72h regardless of confirmation.

### Q: Pattern — S. aureus as the secondary infection organism?

- Impetiginisation of eczema (gold crust).
- Kerion superinfection.
- Secondary infection of erythroderma (see L1).
- Bullous impetigo + SSSS via exfoliative toxins.
- Folliculitis / furuncle / carbuncle ladder.

### Q: Pattern — Painless vs painful primary lesion?

- **Painless ulcer** with firm border → syphilis chancre.
- **Painful grouped vesicles** with scalloped border → HSV.
- **Painful dermatomal vesicles** → shingles.

### Q: Pattern — First-line anti-infective by organism class?

- Bacterial (Staph/Strep skin) → **Flucloxacillin**.
- Bacterial (Lyme) → **Doxycycline**.
- Bacterial (syphilis) → **IM benzylpenicillin** (or doxycycline if allergic).
- Viral (HSV / VZV) → **Aciclovir** (oral or IV by severity).
- Fungal superficial → **Topical azole** (e.g. ketoconazole).
- Fungal mucormycosis → **Amphotericin B + aggressive debridement**.
- Parasitic scabies → **Topical permethrin × 2 cycles**.

## Section 6 — Interleaved SBAs

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.30 (SBA-1 stem) — Forehead + eyelid: painful vesicular rash in V1 distribution. Which is true?

- ✗ A. Caused by HSV — wrong (it's VZV — dermatomal distribution).
- **B. It may involve many dermatomes ✓** (VZV can do this in immunocompromise).
- ✗ C. Diagnostic confirmation required prior to treatment — wrong (start aciclovir immediately).
- ✗ D. Develops due to exfoliative toxins — wrong (those cause SSSS / bullous impetigo, not shingles).

### Q: 📎 Figure L2.31 (SBA-2 stem) — Erythema migrans on neck of patient who went camping. Which is true regarding Lyme disease?

- ✗ A. Diagnosis requires serology before treatment — wrong (serology not sensitive).
- ✗ B. Characteristic histological features support diagnosis — wrong (histology non-specific).
- **C. Clinical suspicion alone is grounds for treatment ✓**
- ✗ D. Primary lesions are multiple annular papules — wrong (primary is a **solitary** erythematous papule that extends radially).

### Q: SBA — Recurrent painful boils on the back of a contact-sport athlete, fluctuant nodules, no underlying skin disease. Most likely organism + management?

- **Likely:** *S. aureus*, possibly **PVL strain**.
- Management: incision + drainage + antibiotics (often **tetracycline** if PVL); **decolonisation** with chlorhexidine wash 7 days + nasal mupirocin 5 days; treat close contacts.

### Q: SBA — Adolescent with hypopigmented patches on trunk that flare in summer, fine scale on scratch. Diagnosis + treatment?

- **Pityriasis versicolor.**
- Cause: *Malassezia* overgrowth.
- Treatment: **topical ketoconazole**, including **scalp** (where Malassezia also lives).

### Q: SBA — Painless penile ulcer with firm indurated border 4 weeks after unprotected sex. Most likely organism + treatment?

- ***Treponema pallidum*** — primary syphilis.
- Treatment: **IM benzylpenicillin** (or doxycycline if penicillin-allergic).

## Reusable reasoning patterns

### Q: 🧠 How to approach a "rash + infection" stem (reusable method)?

- **Step 1.** Site cue — dermatomal? Mucocutaneous? Intertriginous? Photo-distributed?
- **Step 2.** Lesion cue — vesicles/erosions (viral)? Honey crust (impetigo)? Pustules (folliculitis/AGEP)? Annular spreading (Lyme/tinea)?
- **Step 3.** Patient cue — child / immunocompromised / pregnant / atopic / sexually active.
- **Step 4.** Match the entity, then run its diagnostic test (PCR for HSV, serology for syphilis, clinical for Lyme, swab + culture for bacterial, scrape/Wood's for fungal).
- **Step 5.** Treat-on-suspicion when applicable: Lyme, eczema herpeticum, shingles → treat first, confirm later.
