## Why the brain is so vulnerable

### Q: Why does loss of blood supply cause such rapid brain damage?

- Brain = **2%** of body weight
- Uses **10–20%** of cardiac output
- Uses **20%** of body O₂
- Uses **66%** of liver glucose
- Massive metabolic demand, no reserve

## Arterial supply

### Q: The two pairs of vessels supplying the brain — origin and route?

- **Internal carotid artery** — from common carotid (bifurcates at laryngeal prominence), passes through **carotid canal** *(no branches in neck)*, supplies anterior brain
- **Vertebral artery** — from subclavian, ascends in **transverse foramina** of cervical vertebrae *(bony protection)*, two vertebrals join at foramen magnum to form **basilar artery**, supplies posterior brain

![Figure L2.1 — Neck arteries (carotid, vertebral)](figures/L2_1_neck_arteries.jpg)

### Q: What are the components of the Circle of Willis?

- **Anterior:** anterior communicating, anterior cerebral (×2), internal carotid (×2)
- **Posterior:** posterior communicating (×2), posterior cerebral (×2), basilar, vertebral (×2)
- **Branches off the ring:** middle cerebral (×2 — direct continuation of internal carotid)

![Figure L2.2 — Circle of Willis](figures/L2_2_circle_of_willis.jpg)

### Q: Why is the Circle of Willis clinically important?

- It's an **anastomotic circuit** — blood from any single feeder can theoretically supply all
- Evolutionary advantage; people walk around with one carotid almost completely occluded by atherosclerosis
- Treatment when detected: **endarterectomy** ("pipe cleaning")
- **Trap:** the posterior communicating artery is usually minuscule, so anterior↔posterior compensation is much weaker than diagrams suggest

## Venous drainage

### Q: What is the path of venous blood out of the brain?

- Cerebral veins → **dural venous sinuses** (formed by separation of the two dural layers — there are no veins in the cranial cavity, only sinuses) → **internal jugular vein**

### Q: Name the major dural venous sinuses?

- **Superior sagittal sinus** (top, midline)
- **Inferior sagittal sinus** (lower midline)
- **Straight sinus** (drains the **great cerebral vein of Galen** — the brain's biggest internal venous channel)
- **Confluence of sinuses** (back of skull)
- **Transverse sinus** → **sigmoid sinus** → **jugular foramen** → internal jugular vein

![Figure L2.3 — Dural venous sinuses](figures/L2_3_dural_venous_sinuses.jpg)

## Meningeal anatomy — setup for haemorrhage

### Q: Outer to inner — the layers between skull and brain surface?

- **Skull**
- **Outer periosteal dura** (stuck to skull)
- **Inner meningeal dura** (the two dural layers separate at points to form venous sinuses)
- **Arachnoid mater**
- **Subarachnoid space** (CSF here)
- **Pia mater** (on brain surface, follows every gyrus)

## Intracranial haemorrhage — the 4 types

> ⭐ **High-yield core slide.** Memorise the row pattern: site → mechanism → vessel type → onset.

| Type | Site | Mechanism | Vessel & pressure |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Extradural** | Between skull and dura | Trauma (classic: pterion fracture → middle meningeal artery) | **Arterial, high pressure** |
| **Subdural** | Between dura and arachnoid | Trauma (shearing of bridging veins) | **Venous, lower pressure** |
| **Subarachnoid** | In the subarachnoid space (with CSF) | Ruptured aneurysm at Circle of Willis | Arterial |
| **Intracerebral** | In the brain parenchyma | Spontaneous, hypertensive | Small perforating arteries |

![Figure L2.5 — Four types of intracranial haemorrhage](figures/L2_5_haemorrhage_table.jpg)

### Q: Extradural — typical presentation and mechanism?

- Trauma — classic site is the **pterion** (thin bone, four bones meet, middle meningeal artery underneath)
- **Immediate clinical effects** (arterial, high pressure)
- Surgical emergency — craniotomy, remove bone flap, evacuate, reassemble
- **All-or-nothing**: in time = good prognosis; not in time = catastrophic

![Figure L2.6 — Extradural haemorrhage](figures/L2_8_extradural_photo.jpg)

### Q: Subdural — why is the presentation often delayed?

- Venous bleed (bridging veins between dura and arachnoid)
- **Lower pressure** than arterial — blood accumulates over hours to days before mass effect
- Symptoms can lag the trauma by days
- Ultimately raises ICP → brainstem compression → cardiorespiratory failure if not treated
- Treatment: burr holes / craniotomy to evacuate

![Figure L2.7 — Subdural haemorrhage](figures/L2_6_subdural_photo.jpg)

### Q: Subarachnoid — characteristic cause?

- **Rupture of an aneurysm** — typically a saccular ("berry") aneurysm at a Circle of Willis branch point
- Aneurysms are usually congenital wall weaknesses; many people carry them silently
- Hypertension is a key precipitant for rupture
- Treatment of unruptured aneurysm: surgical **clip** (older) or interventional **platinum coil** (modern)

![Figure L2.8 — Subarachnoid haemorrhage](figures/L2_9_subarachnoid_photo.jpg)

### Q: Intracerebral — typical site and cause?

- Spontaneous, **hypertensive**
- Classic site: **basal ganglia** (small perforating arteries fail under chronic high pressure)
- Counts as intraparenchymal (in the substance of the brain)

![Figure L2.9 — Intracerebral haemorrhage in basal ganglia](figures/L2_7_intracerebral_photo.jpg)

## Stroke — definition and terminology

### Q: Define stroke (CVA).

- **Rapidly developing focal disturbance of brain function**
- **Of presumed vascular origin** (rules out tumours, which present subacutely)
- **Lasting >24 hours**
- 85% thromboembolic, 15% haemorrhagic

### Q: TIA — definition and significance?

- Same definition as stroke, but **resolves within 24 hours** (in practice usually minutes — the "funny turn")
- Critical: a TIA is a **major predictor** of subsequent completed stroke — investigate aggressively

### Q: Define infarction, ischaemia, thrombosis, embolism.

- **Infarction** — degenerative changes in tissue *after* arterial occlusion (the dead-tissue end-state)
- **Cerebral ischaemia** — insufficient blood supply (lack of glucose **and** O₂); permanent damage if not restored quickly
- **Thrombosis** — formation of a clot *in situ*
- **Embolism** — plugging of a small vessel by material carried from a larger one (atherosclerotic debris from internal carotid; thrombi from heart)

### Q: FAST — what is it and what does it do?

- **F**ace asymmetry (drooping on one side)
- **A**rms — can the patient raise both and keep them up?
- **S**peech — slurred? (left-MCA = aphasia)
- **T**ime — call 999 immediately
- Public-health bedside screen for stroke; speed to stroke unit determines prognosis

### Q: Stroke — key public-health statistics?

- 3rd commonest cause of death in the UK
- ~100,000 deaths/year UK
- 50% of survivors permanently disabled
- 70% have an obvious neurological deficit

### Q: Five risk factors for stroke?

- **Age** (largest, non-modifiable)
- **Hypertension**
- **Cardiac disease** (e.g. AF → cardioembolic stroke; valve disease)
- **Smoking**
- **Diabetes mellitus** (also linked to Alzheimer's — sometimes called "type 3 diabetes")

![Figure L2.11 — Perfusion fields, medial view](figures/L2_11_perfusion_fields_medial.jpg)

### Q: ACA territory — which surface, which lobes?

- **Medial surface** of the hemisphere — frontal lobes and back to the parieto-occipital junction
- Hits the **medial homunculus** — i.e. the **leg**

### Q: MCA territory — which surface, which structures?

- **Lateral convexity** — most of the lateral hemisphere
- Includes most of motor and sensory cortex (homunculus arm + face), Broca's area, Wernicke's area
- Branches feed deep structures including the **internal capsule**

### Q: PCA territory — which lobes?

- **Occipital lobe** + inferior temporal lobe
- Calcarine cortex (primary visual)

## Per-artery symptom sets

### Q: Anterior cerebral artery occlusion — three symptom areas?

- **Contralateral paralysis, leg > arm > face** (medial homunculus = leg)
- **Disturbance of intellect, executive function, judgement** — **abulia** (loss of will)
- **Loss of appropriate social behaviour** (frontal lobe)

![Figure L2.12 — ACA anatomy](figures/L2_12_aca_anatomy.jpg)

### Q: Middle cerebral artery occlusion — the "classic stroke" — symptoms?

- Contralateral **hemiplegia, arm > leg** (lateral homunculus = arm/face)
- Contralateral **hemisensory deficits**
- **Hemianopia** (homonymous, contralateral side)
- **Aphasia** if **left-sided lesion** (Broca's + Wernicke's both in MCA territory)

> ⚠️ **Caveat:** MCA branches feed the internal capsule. A *proximal* MCA occlusion can produce **full hemiplegia** (legs included), even though leg motor cortex is in ACA territory — because all descending fibres converge in the internal capsule.

![Figure L2.13 — MCA anatomy](figures/L2_13_mca_anatomy.jpg)

### Q: Posterior cerebral artery occlusion — symptoms?

- **Homonymous hemianopia** (primary visual cortex damage)
- **Visual agnosia** — can see, can describe, but doesn't recognise (visual association cortex)
- **Prosopagnosia** is a specific form (face blindness)

![Figure L2.14 — PCA anatomy](figures/L2_14_pca_anatomy.jpg)

## Pattern recognition — pathology specimens

### Q: Yellow discolouration in the walls of basal vessels — what is this and what does it indicate?

- **Atheroma** — fatty deposits in the vessel wall = **atherosclerosis** ("hardening of the arteries")
- Major source of thromboembolic stroke — debris breaks off and lodges downstream
- One of the first things to look for at post-mortem brain examination

![Figure L2.15 — Atherosclerosis at base of brain](figures/L2_15_atherosclerosis_photo.jpg)

### Q: How do you identify the territory of a completed infarct on a coronal slice?

- **Loss of grey-white differentiation** in the affected region
- **Crumbly texture** in chronic infarcts (compared with sharp grey-white edge in healthy tissue)
- Map the region back to ACA/MCA/PCA territory to identify which artery was occluded
- Large MCA infarct (whole lateral hemisphere) is generally not compatible with life

![Figure L2.16 — Right MCA infarct on coronal slice](figures/L2_16_mca_infarct.jpg)

> ⚠️ **Convention trap:** Pathology specimens are laid out as the body is — so image-left = patient's left. **Imaging (CT/MRI) is the opposite** — image-left = patient's right (viewed from the foot of the bed).

## Clinical application — stroke case histories

> 📘 **How to use:** Two contrasting cases applying the haemorrhage taxonomy and CT interpretation. Case 1 reinforces subdural; Case 2 introduces the cerebellar variant of intraparenchymal haemorrhage (not on the main slide deck).

### Case 1 — Mr CW, 64-year-old man

### Q: Cyclist hits head; bruised left side; gradually worsening headache; **5 days later** develops right arm/leg weakness and hesitant speech; admitted **day 7** drowsy and mute. Likely diagnosis?

- **Subdural haemorrhage**
- Pattern: trauma + delayed presentation (days) + slowly progressive symptoms
- Mechanism: shearing of **bridging veins** in subdural space → slow venous bleed → gradually rising ICP

### Q: Why is Mr CW drowsy and mute, not just weak?

- Rising ICP compresses the **brainstem**
- Compression of the **reticular activating system** → reduced consciousness
- Mass effect from the subdural produces the global signs (drowsiness) on top of the focal hemiplegia

### Q: Right-sided weakness — which side is the haemorrhage?

- **Left** — motor pathway is contralateral
- This is the principle from L1 (primary motor cortex topography)

### Q: What does old subdural blood look like on CT, and what asymmetries do you look for?

- **Old (congealed) blood = dark** on CT
- Look for: midline shift (mild here), **lateral ventricle compression** on the affected side (contrast with a clearly-defined fluid-filled ventricle on the unaffected side), **sulcal effacement** (sulci closed up by the mass effect)
- Bonus: well-defined sulci on the *unaffected* side may suggest underlying brain atrophy in an older patient

### Q: Treatment of subdural haemorrhage?

- Refer to neurosurgery
- **Burr holes** or small skull-flap craniotomy → evacuate clot → reassemble skull
- Good prognosis if early enough

### Case 2 — Mrs IRR, mid-to-late 50s, hypertensive

### Q: At a dinner party, sudden severe headache, falls unconscious; on examination dysarthria, nystagmus, ataxia, broad-based gait. Likely diagnosis?

- **Hypertensive intracerebellar haemorrhage**
- Pattern: hypertensive history + **acute** onset + cerebellar syndrome cluster
- Treatment is largely **medical** (manage BP); surgical evacuation often too risky given proximity to brainstem

### Q: What does acute blood look like on CT?

- **Acute (fresh) blood = white** on CT
- *Opposite* of the dark old blood in Case 1
- "Depends on the window" — appearance varies with imaging-window settings, but the rule of thumb holds

> ⭐ **High-yield contrast:** old blood = dark, acute blood = white on CT. This single distinction unlocks most of the case-history bleed-identification questions.

### Q: Distinguish intracerebral, intracerebellar, intraparenchymal.

- **Intraparenchymal** — anywhere in brain substance (umbrella term)
- **Intracerebral** — specifically in the cerebrum (cortex, deep grey)
- **Intracerebellar** — specifically in the cerebellum
- *Mrs IRR's bleed is intraparenchymal AND intracerebellar (a subtype).*

### Q: Why is antihypertensive compliance such a clinical issue?

- Hypertension itself is **asymptomatic**
- Antihypertensives may have unpleasant side effects
- Patients weigh "no overt benefit" vs "real side effects" → silent non-compliance
- When managing hypertension, verify the patient is *actually taking* the medication — not just prescribed it

## Cross-references to L1

> 🧠 **Q: Why does the homunculus matter for predicting stroke symptoms?**
>
> The motor homunculus runs from medial (legs) over the convexity (arm, hand) to lateral (face). ACA = medial cortex = leg paralysis. MCA = lateral convexity = arm + face. *See also: L1 — Primary motor cortex; L1 — Frontal motor areas.*

> 🧠 **Q: Why can a proximal MCA occlusion cause full hemiplegia even though leg motor cortex is ACA territory?**
>
> Because MCA branches also feed the internal capsule, where all descending motor fibres from the entire hemisphere converge into a small region. *See also: L1 — Internal capsule.*

> 🧠 **Q: Why is aphasia a left-sided MCA stroke symptom?**
>
> Broca's area (frontal) and Wernicke's area (temporal) both sit in MCA territory, and language is left-lateralised in most people. *See also: L1 — Broca's & Wernicke's areas.*

> 🧠 **Q: Why are visual deficits the hallmark of PCA strokes?**
>
> Primary visual cortex and visual association cortex sit on the medial occipital surface — supplied by PCA. Same logic produces homonymous hemianopia (primary lesion) and visual agnosia / prosopagnosia (association lesion). *See also: L1 — Occipital lobe lesion.*

## SBA self-test

### Q: A 70-year-old man develops sudden right-leg weakness and apathy. Speech and right arm are largely spared. Most likely vascular event?

- **Left ACA occlusion**
- Leg > arm pattern → medial homunculus → ACA
- Apathy / abulia fits the frontal-lobe component of ACA territory

### Q: A 65-year-old woman presents with right-sided face droop, right-arm weakness, and inability to produce fluent speech (comprehension intact). Most likely vascular event?

- **Left MCA occlusion**
- Right-sided motor signs → left-hemisphere lesion
- Arm + face > leg pattern fits MCA (lateral homunculus)
- Expressive aphasia → Broca's area in left MCA territory

### Q: A patient struck on the side of the head develops a brief lucid interval, then deteriorates rapidly with a fixed dilated pupil. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Extradural haemorrhage** (classic pterion injury → middle meningeal artery)
- Lucid interval then rapid deterioration is the textbook pattern (high-pressure arterial bleed)
- Surgical emergency — neurosurgical evacuation

### Q: A hypertensive 60-year-old collapses with sudden onset of severe headache and a "thunderclap" pattern. CT shows blood diffusely around the basal cisterns. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Subarachnoid haemorrhage** from a ruptured Circle-of-Willis aneurysm
- Pattern: sudden severe headache + diffuse subarachnoid blood
- Treatment of the aneurysm: clipping or endovascular coiling

### Q: What is the major risk factor revealed by yellow discolouration of the basal cerebral vessels at autopsy?

- **Atherosclerosis** (atheroma in vessel walls)
- Tied to all the modifiable risk factors: hypertension, cardiac disease, smoking, diabetes mellitus
- Source of most thromboembolic strokes via atherosclerotic debris emboli
