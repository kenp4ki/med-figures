## Microscopic cortical structure

### Q: Cortex thickness and overall arrangement?

- ~2 mm thick, covers entire brain surface like a peel
- Organised into **6 layers** (molecular I → multiform VI) and **vertical columns**
- Cell types include small pyramidal, granule (stellate), large pyramidal — *details not examinable*

### Q: What are Brodmann areas, and which numbers actually matter?

- 52 regions defined by cytoarchitecture (cell size, packing density, layers) — Brodmann, 1909
- Many areas correspond to function
- **Areas 1, 2, 3** = primary somatosensory cortex
- **Area 4** = primary motor cortex
- *Other numbers not required*

## Lobes — atlas of territories

![Figure L1.1 — Lobes](figures/L1_1_lobes.jpg)

### Q: Frontal lobe — functions?

- Regulating & initiating motor function (precentral gyrus = M1)
- **Executive function** (planning, decision-making)
- Attention, memory
- Language **production** (Broca's area, dominant hemisphere)

### Q: Parietal lobe — functions?

- **Somatic sensation** (touch, pain, vibration, two-point discrimination, proprioception, temperature)
- Sensory aspects of language
- Spatial orientation and self-perception

### Q: Occipital lobe — functions?

- Processing visual information
- Primary visual cortex + visual association cortex

### Q: Temporal lobe — functions?

- Processing auditory information
- Emotions, memories

### Q: Limbic lobe — what does it include and what does it do?

- Includes **amygdala, hippocampus, mamillary bodies, cingulate gyrus**
- Concerned with **learning, memory, emotion, motivation, reward** (dopamine reward system)
- Visible on medial surface

### Q: Insular cortex — where and what?

- Lies deep within the **lateral fissure** (open the fissure to see it)
- **Visceral sensations** (poorly localised, unlike cutaneous), autonomic control, **interoception**
- Auditory processing, visual–vestibular integration (mismatch → motion sickness)

## Internal structure & fibre tracts

### Q: Grey vs white matter — what's where?

- **Grey matter**: cortical surface + deep nuclei = neuronal cell bodies + glial cells (~85 billion of each)
- **White matter**: deep = myelinated axons in tracts
- **Trap:** *opposite arrangement to spinal cord* (where grey is central, white peripheral)

### Q: How do you tell coronal from axial from sagittal on a brain section?

- **Coronal** = "through the crown" (face-on, both hemispheres visible together)
- **Axial / horizontal / transverse** = top-down (viewed from feet on imaging → L–R swapped)
- **Sagittal** = side view (front and back obvious, no L–R confusion)

### Q: Three types of cortical fibre tract — what each connects?

- **Association fibres** — within the same hemisphere (don't cross midline)
- **Commissural fibres** — between hemispheres, connecting **homologous** structures
- **Projection fibres** — cortex ↔ lower brain (thalamus, brainstem, cord)

> ⭐ **High-yield framing:** know the *type* of tract (association / commissural / projection) AND *named examples* of each. Exam-style: "What type of tract is the arcuate fasciculus?" → association fibre.

![Figure L1.2 — Association fibres](figures/L1_3_association_fibres.jpg)

### Q: Named association fibres — short and long?

- **Short fibres** — adjacent gyri (U-fibres)
- **Superior longitudinal fasciculus** — frontal ↔ occipital
- **Arcuate fasciculus** — frontal ↔ temporal *(connects Broca's & Wernicke's)*
- **Inferior longitudinal fasciculus** — temporal ↔ occipital
- **Uncinate fasciculus** — anterior frontal ↔ temporal

### Q: Named commissural fibres?

- **Corpus callosum** — by far the largest
- **Anterior commissure** — smaller, anterior
- Both connect homologous regions L ↔ R

### Q: Projection fibres — afferent vs efferent and how they converge?

- **Afferent** = towards cortex; **efferent** = away from cortex
- Radiate deep to cortex as the **corona radiata**
- Converge through the **internal capsule**, between **thalamus** and **basal ganglia**

![Figure L1.3 — Projection fibres / corona radiata](figures/L1_4_projection_fibres.jpg)

### Q: Why is a stroke in the internal capsule worse than an equivalent cortical stroke?

- All projection fibres converge here from the entire hemisphere
- Same lesion volume → far more fibres affected
- A small internal-capsule lesion can be life-threatening; a much larger cortical lesion may produce only modest deficit

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern (links to L2):** This is exactly why a proximal middle cerebral artery occlusion can cause full hemiplegia — branches of the MCA feed the internal capsule, hitting all descending motor fibres at once. *See also: L2 — MCA symptoms.*

![Figure L1.4 — Internal capsule (coronal + axial)](figures/L1_5_internal_capsule.jpg)

## Localisation of function — primary vs association cortices

### Q: Three rules that distinguish a primary cortex from an association cortex?

| Feature | Primary cortex | Association cortex |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Function | Predictable | Less predictable |
| Topographic arrangement | Yes | No |
| L–R symmetry | Yes | Weak / absent |

### Q: "What/where" vs "why" — what's the apple analogy for?

- **Primary** cortex tells you the *what* and *where*: "I see a green stalky thing in my hand"
- **Association** cortex tells you the *why* (recognition): "that's an apple"
- Same logic for hearing (sound → recognising music), touch (object → stereognosis), faces

## Cortical motor & sensory areas

### Q: Three motor areas of the frontal lobe and what each does?

- **Primary motor cortex (M1, area 4)** — fine, discrete, precise voluntary movements; sends descending signals
- **Premotor area** — planning **externally cued** movements
- **Supplementary motor area (SMA)** — planning complex **internally cued** movements

![Figure L1.5 — Frontal motor areas (M1, premotor, SMA)](figures/L1_6_frontal_motor_areas.jpg)

### Q: Primary somatosensory cortex (parietal) — modalities and association function?

- **Modalities**: fine touch, vibration, two-point discrimination, proprioception, pain, temperature
- **Areas 1, 2, 3** (postcentral gyrus)
- **Somatosensory association cortex** = interpretation (e.g. recognising an object in the hand = stereognosis, awareness of self/personal space)

### Q: Two key language areas, where they sit, and what connects them?

- **Broca's area** — frontal (inferior frontal gyrus) — language **production**
- **Wernicke's area** — temporal — language **comprehension**
- Connected by the **arcuate fasciculus**
- Lateralised — usually **left** hemisphere, sometimes right

![Figure L1.6 — Broca, Wernicke, arcuate fasciculus](figures/L1_9_broca_wernicke_arcuate.jpg)

## Cortical lesion phenomenology — atlas of syndromes

### Q: Frontal lobe lesion — what changes, and the canonical case?

- **Personality changes** and **inappropriate behaviour** (loss of inhibition, poor decisions)
- Phineas Gage (1848) — iron rod through cheek, exited skull. Survived but *"no longer Gage"*
- **Pattern:** Severe damage at one site can be tolerated; minor damage elsewhere can be catastrophic — depends on location

![Figure L1.7 — Phineas Gage](figures/L1_7_phineas_gage.jpg)

### Q: Parietal lobe lesion — what is contralateral neglect?

- Lack of awareness of self **and** extrapersonal space on the contralateral side
- Right-hemisphere lesion → left neglect (more common)
- Manifestations: draws only half a flower, eats only one half of plate, dresses only one side, shaves only one side
- *Not* simply a visual field defect — can't "fix it by turning the head"; the side genuinely doesn't exist for them

![Figure L1.8 — Hemi-neglect demonstrations](figures/L1_8_hemi_neglect.jpg)

### Q: Temporal lobe lesion — agnosia and the patient HM story?

- **Agnosia** = inability to recognise (objects, faces, etc.)
- Patient HM — bilateral resection of anterior medial temporal lobe (incl. hippocampus) for intractable epilepsy
- Result: **anterograde amnesia** — could not form new memories
- Retrograde memories (pre-surgery) preserved

### Q: Broca's vs Wernicke's aphasia — production vs comprehension?

- **Broca's lesion** → **expressive (non-fluent) aphasia** — poor production of speech, **comprehension intact**
- **Wernicke's lesion** → **receptive (fluent) aphasia** — fluent but nonsensical speech, **poor comprehension**
- Distinction is testable: e.g. can the patient follow a written instruction (Broca's: yes; Wernicke's: no)

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Aphasia is *not* paralysis. The muscles of speech work fine — the wiring of language is what's damaged.

### Q: Occipital lobe lesion — primary vs association?

- **Primary visual cortex** lesion → blindness in the corresponding visual field
- **Visual association** lesion → can see but can't interpret
- Specific syndrome: **prosopagnosia** = inability to recognise familiar faces or learn new ones (face blindness)

> 🧠 **Cross-ref to L2:** All of these lesion syndromes have specific vascular causes — frontal/abulia from ACA, hemiplegia/aphasia from MCA, visual deficits from PCA. *See also: L2 — Per-artery symptom sets.*

## Methods of cortical assessment

### Q: Imaging — PET vs fMRI, what does each measure?

- **PET** (positron emission tomography) — blood **flow** to a region (or glucose use)
- **fMRI** — blood **oxygen** level (BOLD signal) in a region
- Both used to localise activity during tasks (e.g. viewing words, listening, speaking, complex speech)

### Q: Encephalography — EEG vs MEG?

- **EEG** — electrical signals at the scalp (electrodes on head)
- **MEG** — tiny magnetic signals produced by the same neuronal activity (huge expensive machine, sit very still)
- Both give event-related / evoked potentials when paired with a stimulus

### Q: Visual evoked potential — what's it good for?

- Pattern-reversal checkerboard stimulus, recording electrodes over occiput
- Components: P50, **N70, P100**, N145
- **P100 prolonged in optic neuritis** → useful in MS workup (demyelination → slow conduction)

![Figure L1.7 — Visual evoked potentials](figures/L1_10_visual_evoked_potentials.jpg)

### Q: Somatosensory evoked potential — what does the wave train show?

- Stimulus to median nerve at wrist, recordings up the pathway
- **N9** at shoulder → **N11/N13** mid-cervical cord → **P14** thalamic → **N19/P22** somatosensory cortex
- Logic: latency at each level localises where conduction is delayed or absent

![Figure L1.8 — Somatosensory evoked potentials](figures/L1_11_somatosensory_evoked.jpg)

### Q: Brain stimulation — TMS vs tDCS?

- **TMS** (transcranial magnetic stimulation) — electromagnetic induction → fires neurons; assesses *integrity* of circuits; can also temporarily disrupt function (e.g. interrupt counting → maps speech)
- **tDCS** (transcranial direct current) — low DC over scalp; **anode = increase** firing, **cathode = decrease** firing
- Clinical research: epilepsy, migraine, tinnitus, depression

### Q: DTI / tractography — what does it visualise?

- Diffusion of water molecules (water moves preferentially along axons)
- 3D reconstruction of white-matter tracts in vivo
- Used to assess tract damage along its length

## Cortical assessment applied — confirming MS is a CNS disease

> 📘 **How to use:** This is a clinical worked example built on the evoked-potential and TMS methods above. The aim is to localise where conduction is slow — central or peripheral — using a single muscle's responses to two different stimulus sites.

**Pathway skeleton:**

> Peripheral stim (calf nerve) → records **M-wave** (direct motor) + **F-wave** (antidromic motor) → calculates **PMCT**
>
> Cortical stim (TMS) → records **MEP** (whole pathway) → equals **TMCT**
>
> **CMCT = TMCT − PMCT**

### Q: What is MS?

- Autoimmune disorder
- Loss of **myelin** from neurons of the **central nervous system** (brain + spinal cord)
- Common symptoms: blurred vision, fatigue, difficulty walking, paraesthesia, muscle stiffness/spasms

### Q: What is the M-wave and how is it generated?

- Stimulus of appropriate intensity to a peripheral nerve activates motor axons
- Action potentials travel **orthodromically** (cord → muscle) → muscle twitch
- EMG records a **fast direct motor response** ≈ **8 ms** in soleus
- The M-wave is *not* a reflex — direct activation only

![Figure L1.9 — M-wave](figures/L1_T2_m_wave.jpg)

### Q: What is the H-reflex?

- Same stimulus also activates **sensory** axons → travel to spinal cord → synapse onto LMN → motor signal back to muscle
- Truly a **reflex** (sensory → motor)
- Latency ≈ **30 ms** in soleus (longer because it's the round-trip)
- Named after Paul **H**offmann

![Figure L1.10 — M-wave + H-reflex](figures/L1_T3_h_reflex.jpg)

### Q: What is the F-wave, and why is it not a reflex?

- A **large** stimulus activates motor axons to fire **antidromically** (towards cord)
- Action potentials "turn around" in the LMN cell body (~1 ms) → travel back orthodromically to muscle
- Latency ≈ **30 ms** in soleus, just like the H-reflex, **but**: motor → motor only, no sensory limb
- First demonstrated in **F**oot

> ⚠️ **Common trap:** F-wave and H-reflex have similar latencies but completely different mechanisms. **F = antidromic motor, NOT a reflex.**

![Figure L1.11 — M-wave + F-wave](figures/L1_T4_f_wave.jpg)

### Q: Orthodromic vs antidromic — quick reference?

- **Orthodromic** = normal direction in a fibre (sensory: periphery → cord; motor: cord → muscle)
- **Antidromic** = opposite direction (motor going cord-ward — only with high-intensity stimuli)
- The same peripheral stimulus produces M (motor orthodromic) + H (sensory orthodromic + motor orthodromic) + F (motor antidromic + motor orthodromic) depending on intensity

![Figure L1.12 — Orthodromic vs antidromic](figures/L1_T1_pns_pathways.jpg)

### Q: What is a motor evoked potential (MEP)?

- TMS coil over motor cortex → activates upper motor neurons
- Action potentials descend the **whole motor pathway** (UMN + LMN) → muscle twitch
- EMG records the MEP — soleus latency ≈ **35 ms**
- TMCT (total motor conduction time) = MEP latency

![Figure L1.13 — TMS-evoked MEP](figures/L1_T5_tms_mep.jpg)

### Q: How is central motor conduction time (CMCT) calculated?

- **PMCT** = (M latency + F latency − 1) / 2
  - The "−1" accounts for ~1 ms turn-around at the LMN cell body
  - This isolates the cord-to-muscle component of the motor pathway
- **TMCT** = MEP latency
- **CMCT = TMCT − PMCT**

![Figure L1.14 — CMCT formula](figures/L1_T6_cmct_formula.jpg)

### Q: How does this confirm MS is a CNS disease?

- **TMCT delayed** (MEP latency longer than normal) — but problem could be UMN, LMN, or both
- **F-wave normal** → LMN intact → PMCT normal
- Therefore the delay must be in the **central** part of the pathway → **CMCT prolonged**
- MS lesions are demyelinating → slow conduction in central white matter → exactly this pattern

> ⭐ **High-yield logic:** delayed TMCT + normal PMCT = central problem. This is how MS is electrophysiologically confirmed as a CNS disease.

### Q: Indicative normative latencies?

- Hand (FDI): MEP 19.5 ms, PMCT 13.6 ms, **CMCT 5.9 ms**
- Leg (TA): MEP 26.5 ms, PMCT 12.7 ms, **CMCT 13.9 ms**
- *Specific numbers not memorisation-critical; the formula and logic are.*

## Cross-references to L2

> 🧠 **Q: Why does an ACA stroke hit the leg, while an MCA stroke hits the arm and face?**
>
> The motor homunculus runs from the medial surface (legs) over the convexity (arm, hand) down the lateral surface (face). ACA supplies the medial cortex (legs); MCA supplies the lateral convexity (arm, face). Topography of M1 → vascular territory → predictable symptom pattern. *See also: L2 — Anterior cerebral artery; L2 — Middle cerebral artery.*

> 🧠 **Q: Why is aphasia a left-MCA-stroke symptom specifically?**
>
> Broca's and Wernicke's areas sit in the lateral frontal and lateral temporal cortex — both are MCA territory. Language is left-lateralised in most people, so a left MCA occlusion takes out the language network. *See also: L2 — Middle cerebral artery.*

> 🧠 **Q: Why are visual deficits the hallmark of PCA strokes?**
>
> The primary visual cortex and visual association cortex sit on the medial occipital surface — supplied by the posterior cerebral artery. Same logic produces both homonymous hemianopia (primary lesion) and visual agnosia / prosopagnosia (association lesion). *See also: L2 — Posterior cerebral artery.*

## SBA self-test

### Q: A 56-year-old man has a stroke. He cannot understand spoken or written language but produces fluent (though nonsensical) speech. Which area is most likely affected?

- **Wernicke's area** (left posterior superior temporal gyrus)
- This is **receptive (fluent) aphasia**
- Trap: don't pick Broca's — that gives the *opposite* pattern (poor production, intact comprehension)

### Q: A patient with a right parietal stroke draws only the right half of a flower and ignores food on the left of the plate. Diagnosis?

- **Contralateral neglect** (left-sided neglect from right parietal lesion)
- Not a visual field defect — the patient genuinely doesn't acknowledge that side of the world
- Right hemisphere is the more common side of the lesion

### Q: An MS patient has a normal F-wave latency in soleus but a prolonged MEP latency. What does this tell you?

- TMCT delayed but PMCT normal → **CMCT prolonged**
- Lower motor neuron and peripheral nerve are intact
- Demyelination is **central** — consistent with MS

### Q: Which fibre type is the corpus callosum?

- **Commissural** — connects homologous structures in left and right hemispheres
- *Largest* commissural tract; anterior commissure is the other named example
