## Oesophageal anatomy & physiology

### Q: Length of the oesophagus and how distance is measured at endoscopy?

- **~25 cm** total length, **measured from the central incisors**.
- UOS at **~15 cm** (end of pharynx, level of **C5**).
- OG junction at **~40 cm**, where it pierces the diaphragm at **T10**.
- An endoscopy report saying *"lesion at 30 cm"* means 30 cm from incisors.

### Q: Three anatomical parts of the oesophagus?

- **Cervical** (upper)
- **Thoracic** (longest; behind trachea, then related to the aorta)
- **Abdominal** (shortest; ~4 cm below diaphragm)

### Q: Four anti-reflux factors of the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS)?

1. **Intra-abdominal oesophagus (~4 cm)** — positive abdominal pressure helps close it.
2. **Crura of the diaphragm** (right + left) — muscular sling around the tube.
3. **Phreno-oesophageal ligament** — anchors oesophagus, prevents it being pulled into the thorax.
4. **Angle of His** — acute angle at OG junction acts as a functional valve when stomach fills.

⭐ This 4-factor set is examinable as a list. Loss of any contributes to GORD or hiatus hernia.

### Q: Four phases of swallowing?

1. **Oral** — chew and form bolus; UOS and LOS both **closed**.
2. **Pharyngeal** — pharynx contracts; UOS opens reflexively; LOS opens early.
3. **Oesophageal** — UOS closes; sequential peristalsis pushes bolus down.
4. **LOS phase** — LOS closes once bolus has passed.

🧠 Swallowing is **not gravity-driven** — coordinated peristalsis is what propels the bolus, which is why you can swallow upside-down. This is why a *functional* (motility) disorder gives dysphagia even to liquids.

### Q: Key manometric pressures in the oesophagus?

- Normal **peristaltic wave: ~40 mmHg**.
- **Resting LOS: ~20 mmHg.**
- During swallow, **LOS drops to <5 mmHg** to match stomach pressure (only then can bolus pass).
- LOS relaxation is mediated by **non-cholinergic, non-adrenergic (NCNA) inhibitory neurones** of the **myenteric plexus**.

### Q: Key dysphagia terms (define each)?

- **Dysphagia** — difficulty swallowing.
- **Odynophagia** — *painful* swallowing (oesophageal infection/inflammation, or pharyngeal e.g. tonsillitis).
- **Regurgitation** — return of contents from above an obstruction.
- **Reflux** — passive return of gastric fluid into the oesophagus.

⭐ **Solids only** = mechanical (red flag for cancer). **Solids + liquids together** = functional (motility). Progressive dysphagia + weight loss = malignancy until proven otherwise.

## Motility disorders — atlas

Three patterns. Same template applied: mechanism → presentation → investigation → management.

### Q: Achalasia — mechanism in one sentence?

**Loss of NCNA inhibitory neurones in the myenteric plexus of the LOS** → LOS fails to relax → food and fluid accumulate in the dilated oesophagus.

### Q: Achalasia — primary vs secondary causes?

- **Primary** — idiopathic. Proposed: viral/environmental trigger ± genetic predisposition → inflammatory infiltrate destroys myenteric ganglia. Some cases autoimmune.
- **Secondary** — mimics achalasia but has an identifiable cause: **Chagas disease** (parasitic), **amyloidosis**, malignant infiltration.

### Q: Achalasia — clinical features and natural history?

- **Insidious** onset, often >1 year before presentation.
- Progressive **dysphagia to both solids and liquids** (functional pattern).
- **Regurgitation, weight loss, halitosis.**
- Stagnant food → **oesophagitis, aspiration pneumonia.**

⭐ **28-fold increased risk of oesophageal cancer** — long-standing achalasia is a premalignant state due to chronic stasis and inflammation.

### Q: Achalasia — investigations and key signs?

- **Barium swallow** — classic **"bird's beak"** appearance (smooth tapering at LOS, dilated proximal oesophagus).
- **Manometry** — high resting LOS pressure, **failure of LOS relaxation on swallowing**, weak/absent peristalsis (<40 mmHg).
- **OGD** — exclude a malignant pseudo-achalasia.

### Q: Achalasia — treatment options and the post-treatment trade-off?

- **Endoscopic pneumatic dilatation** (first-line now) — balloon forcibly tears LOS muscle. **70–80% initial response**; often relapses → repeat dilatations needed.
- **Heller's myotomy** (laparoscopic) — longitudinal cut through LOS muscle onto stomach, **mucosa preserved**.
- **Fundoplication** (e.g. Dor) usually added to myotomy.

⚠️ Cutting the sphincter destroys it → **post-myotomy GORD**. That's why a partial fundoplication is added: recreate a low-pressure valve.

### Q: Risks of laparoscopic Heller's myotomy + fundoplication?

- **Perforation** (mucosal breach during myotomy).
- **Vagus nerve division** — uncommon (~10–16% reported in older series).
- **Splenic injury** (1–5%).

🧠 **Splenic injury happens via tearing the short gastric vessels** when mobilising the fundus — *not* via the splenic artery behind the stomach. The fundus is anatomically close to the splenic hilum.

### Q: Scleroderma of the oesophagus — what happens?

- Smooth muscle **atrophy** → weak peristalsis, **no propagation** of bolus.
- LOS becomes **hypotonic** → severe reflux disease.
- Investigation: **OGD first to exclude organic obstruction/cancer.**
- Treatment: prokinetics (e.g. cisapride). Irreversible once peristalsis is lost.

### Q: Diffuse oesophageal spasm — what's the mechanism and the classic image?

- Muscles contract but **not in coordination**.
- Hypertrophy of circular muscle fibres → **"corkscrew oesophagus"** on barium / endoscopy.
- Treatment: pneumatic dilatation; results unpredictable.

### Q: Comparison — achalasia vs scleroderma vs diffuse oesophageal spasm?

| Feature | Achalasia | Scleroderma | Diffuse spasm |
|---|---|---|---|
| Defect | LOS won't relax | Muscle atrophy, weak peristalsis | Discoordinated contractions |
| LOS tone | ↑↑ | ↓ (hypotonic) | Variable |
| Reflux risk | Low (LOS closed) | **High** | Moderate |
| Classic sign | Bird's beak | Aperistaltic dilated oesophagus | Corkscrew oesophagus |

## Oesophageal perforation

### Q: Three anatomical sites of oesophageal narrowing (= sites of perforation)?

1. **Pharyngeal constriction** (just below UOS).
2. **Aortobronchial constriction** (where aorta + L main bronchus indent the thoracic oesophagus).
3. **Diaphragmatic hiatus** (at OG junction).

### Q: Causes of oesophageal perforation, ranked by frequency?

- **Iatrogenic = most common** — diagnostic or therapeutic OGD (~3 in 10,000; higher with dilatation, sclerotherapy, achalasia treatment). Risk ↑ with diverticula (e.g. Zenker's).
- **Spontaneous = Boerhaave's syndrome (~15%).**
- **Foreign body** — batteries, blades, dishwasher tablets, caustic burns.
- **Trauma** — penetrating in the **neck**; blunt RTAs in the thorax (knives can't reach thoracic oesophagus easily).
- **Malignancy, intra-operative.**

### Q: Boerhaave's syndrome — classic stem and the anatomical detail you need?

- **Forceful vomiting against a closed glottis** → sudden rise in intraluminal pressure → full-thickness tear.
- Classic: **alcoholic / post-binge**, severe chest + epigastric pain after retching.

⭐ **Site = lower oesophagus, posterolateral, LEFT.** Examiner-tested anatomical detail.

### Q: Clinical features of oesophageal perforation?

- **Pain** (chest/epigastric/back).
- **Fever** (especially after 24 h).
- **Dysphagia.**
- **Surgical emphysema** — air tracks from mediastinum to subcutaneous tissues. Palpate the neck → **crepitations under the skin**.

### Q: First-line investigation in suspected oesophageal perforation?

- **CT chest + abdomen with oral contrast** — modern first-line.
- Look for: **pneumomediastinum**, contrast leak outside the lumen, pleural effusion.
- Alternatives: contrast (gastrografin) swallow, OGD.

⚠️ Don't rely on a normal CXR. Pneumomediastinum can be subtle. CT with **oral** contrast is the test that confirms the leak.

### Q: Initial and definitive management of oesophageal perforation?

- **Surgical emergency.** NBM, IV fluids, **antibiotics + antifungals**, bloods, ICU.
- **Refer to a tertiary centre.**
- **Stable patient → conservative management.**
- **Unstable → surgical repair in two layers** (mucosa + muscle).

## GORD & hiatus hernia

### Q: Reflux is normal — so what makes it become GORD?

- Transient LOS relaxation occurs in everyone (especially after meals).
- GORD = failure of the **3 protective mechanisms** beyond the LOS:
  - **Volume clearance** — secondary peristalsis sweeps refluxate back.
  - **pH clearance** — alkaline saliva neutralises acid (extra saliva on reflux is the buffer response).
  - **Epithelial barrier** — mucosa resists acid.

### Q: Factors that ↑ vs ↓ LOS pressure?

| Effect on LOS | Increases pressure (anti-reflux) | Decreases pressure (pro-reflux) |
|---|---|---|
| Drugs / agonists | **α-agonists** | **β-agonists**, dopamine |
| Diet / lifestyle | Protein-rich foods | **Smoking**, fatty foods, alcohol |
| Hormonal / luminal | Alkaline pH | Gastric juice (acid), some hormones |

### Q: Sliding vs paraoesophageal hiatus hernia — the differentiating point?

- **Sliding** — **OG junction** migrates **above** the diaphragm into the chest. Stomach follows.
- **Paraoesophageal (rolling)** — **OG junction stays in normal position**; **fundus herniates** alongside through the hiatus.

⚠️ **Paraoesophageal carries higher complication risk** — strangulation and gastric volvulus. Sliding mostly causes reflux.

### Q: Investigations and treatment for GORD?

- **OGD** — assess oesophagitis, exclude cancer.
- **24-hour pH monitoring** with patient-kept symptom diary — correlate symptoms with low oesophageal pH.
- Treatment: lifestyle → **PPI** → **laparoscopic fundoplication** for refractory cases.

## Stomach

### Q: Parts and three core functions of the stomach?

- **Parts:** fundus, body, antrum, pylorus.
- **Functions:**
  - **Reservoir** for food.
  - **Digestion** — acid + pepsin break down protein.
  - **Endocrine** — secretes **gastrin**.

### Q: Three types of gastritis?

| Type | Cause | Key feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Erosive haemorrhagic** | NSAIDs, alcohol, stress, ischaemia | Acute, may bleed → acute ulcer |
| **Chronic active (Type B)** | **H. pylori** | Most common chronic gastritis |
| **Chronic atrophic** | Autoimmune (anti-parietal-cell Ab) | ↓ acid + ↓ intrinsic factor |

### Q: Why does autoimmune atrophic gastritis cause anaemia?

- Antibodies destroy **parietal cells**.
- Parietal cells make **intrinsic factor** (and HCl).
- **No IF → vitamin B12 cannot be absorbed in the terminal ileum.**
- → **Pernicious anaemia** (megaloblastic, macrocytic).

### Q: What stimulates and what inhibits gastric acid secretion?

- **Stimulators:** **acetylcholine** (vagal), **gastrin**, **histamine** (the "triple stimulation" of parietal cells).
- **Inhibitors:** **secretin**, **somatostatin**, **prostaglandins**.

### Q: Four mucosal protective mechanisms of the stomach?

1. **Mucus layer** — physical barrier.
2. **Bicarbonate secretion** by surface cells — buffers acid at the epithelium.
3. **Epithelial barrier** — tight junctions, rapid cell turnover.
4. **Mucosal blood flow** — sweeps any acid that breaches into the circulation.

### Q: Two mechanisms by which a gastric ulcer heals?

- **Cell migration** — neighbouring epithelial cells flatten and creep across the defect.
- **Cell division** — proliferation refills the gap.

### Q: H. pylori — what's the most common outcome and the rare bad ones?

- **Most carriers asymptomatic.**
- Most symptomatic outcome → **chronic gastritis**.
- Less commonly → chronic active / atrophic gastritis → **peptic ulcer**.
- Rarely → **gastric adenocarcinoma** or **MALT lymphoma**.

### Q: Peptic ulcer — first-line treatment and when does surgery become necessary?

- Medical: **PPIs** (e.g. omeprazole) — block the proton pump, ↓ acid.
- Treat H. pylori if present (triple therapy).
- Surgery is **rare** — reserved for **complicated ulcer**: **perforation** or **bleeding**.

## Cross-cutting reasoning patterns

### Q: Reusable pattern — how do you discriminate mechanical vs functional dysphagia in clinic?

- **Solids alone, progressive, with weight loss** → mechanical → think malignancy → **OGD**.
- **Solids and liquids equally, intermittent, no weight loss** → functional → **manometry ± barium swallow**.
- **One question to ask in clinic:** *"Is it solids only, or both solids and liquids?"*

### Q: Reusable pattern — biliary investigation ladder for an SBA?

```
Suspect biliary disease
  └─► USS (cheap, no radiation, sensitive for stones in GB)
       └─► If LFTs deranged / ductal dilatation suspected
            └─► MRCP (non-invasive imaging of the duct)
                 └─► If stone confirmed in CBD
                      └─► ERCP (THERAPEUTIC — sphincterotomy + stone extraction)
                           └─► Then laparoscopic cholecystectomy on same admission
```

### Q: Reusable pattern — the "next investigation" SBA template?

🧠 Read the stem twice, then ask three questions: (1) what am I trying to confirm or rule out? (2) what's the cheapest/safest test that answers that question? (3) is the test I'm picking diagnostic or therapeutic? **USS = first-line for suspected GB stones. MRCP = best for suspected CBD stones. ERCP = therapy, not diagnosis.**

# Tutorial — Case 1: Perforated duodenal ulcer

### Q: Stem clues — Mr Zoot, 45M, carpenter — what to extract from the stem?

- **Background:** intermittent epigastric pain × 1 yr, **worse with hangover and smoking** + **regular ibuprofen** = NSAID-induced peptic ulcer risk factors.
- **Acute:** sudden generalised pain after doubling NSAID dose; vomiting.
- **Exam:** **rigid abdomen, four-quadrant tenderness** = peritonitis.
- **Obs:** T 37.9, HR 110, BP 100/60 = **septic shock**.
- **Bloods:** WCC 16.4, CRP 180 (inflammation); **amylase 105 = NORMAL**.

⚠️ **Trap: a normal amylase here matters.** It rules out pancreatitis as the cause of the acute abdomen. Keep amylase as a discriminator in every upper-abdo SBA.

### Q: Q1 — first investigations to establish the diagnosis?

- **Erect chest X-ray + plain abdominal X-ray.**
- Looking for **pneumoperitoneum** (free air under the diaphragm and Rigler's sign).

### Q: Q2 — what radiological signs and what's the diagnosis?

- **Erect CXR — free subdiaphragmatic air** (air silhouettes the dome of the diaphragm).
- **AXR — Rigler's sign** = gas visible on **both sides of the bowel wall** (because there's also free intraperitoneal air outside the bowel).
- Diagnosis: **perforated viscus → pneumoperitoneum.**

📎 Figure L1.1 — Erect CXR showing free subdiaphragmatic air (pneumoperitoneum).

📎 Figure L1.2 — AXR showing Rigler's sign (gas on both sides of bowel wall).

### Q: Q3 — most likely perforated organ given this stem?

**Duodenum** (perforated duodenal ulcer).

The classic combo points here: NSAIDs + smoking + alcohol + chronic epigastric pain + sudden generalised peritonitis. Distractors (stomach, sigmoid diverticular, appendix, small bowel, colitis) don't fit the chronic NSAID-aggravated epigastric history.

### Q: Q4 — pre-operative management of perforated peptic ulcer?

- **NGT** (decompress stomach).
- **NBM** (nil by mouth).
- **IV fluids** (resuscitate the septic-shock physiology).
- **Antibiotics** (broad-spectrum, cover gut flora).

### Q: Q4 — three operative principles for perforated peptic ulcer?

1. **Identify aetiology** of peritonitis.
2. **Eradicate the peritoneal source of contamination** (close the perforation — typically a **laparoscopic omental patch / Graham's patch**).
3. **Peritoneal lavage and drainage.**

### Q: Q4 — spectrum of definitive treatment for perforated ulcer?

- **Conservative — Taylor's approach** (NBM, NGT decompression, IV antibiotics, IV PPI; no surgery): used in select stable patients with sealed perforations.
- **Standard — laparoscopic omental patch** (most common today).
- **Radical — vagotomy ± gastrectomy:** rare in the modern era of PPIs.

### Q: Q5 — Day 3 post-op SOB, sats 99→87 on 2L, T 38.5, bibasal crepitations Rt > Lt — diagnosis and treatment?

- **Post-operative pneumonia** (likely **hospital-acquired** ± **aspiration**, given right-side dominance and post-laparotomy state).
- Treatment: **oxygen titrated to sats**, **chest physiotherapy**, sit up, **broad-spectrum antibiotics** per local protocol, sputum cultures, repeat bloods, consider VTE prophylaxis review.

📎 Figure L1.3 — Day-3 post-operative CXR with right basal consolidation.

⚠️ Common post-op complication SBA. The pattern: laparotomy → poor analgesia + splinting → atelectasis → pneumonia. Right base > left because the right main bronchus is more vertical → aspiration goes right.

# Tutorial — Case 2: Gallstone pancreatitis

### Q: Stem clues — Miss Floyd, 45F — what to extract?

- **Background:** episodic upper-abdo pain after **Mars bars** (= **fatty food trigger** = biliary colic).
- **Acute:** severe constant upper abdo pain + vomiting × 2 days.
- **Exam:** epigastric tenderness + guarding; **normotensive** (less shocked than Case 1).
- **Bloods:** **bilirubin 35, ALP 366** (cholestatic pattern), CRP 150, WCC 20, **amylase 2150** (>3× ULN = diagnostic of acute pancreatitis).
- **Pattern:** fatty-food trigger + cholestatic LFTs + amylase >3× ULN = **gallstone pancreatitis** until proven otherwise. Two of the three (fat trigger, LFTs) point to gallstones; amylase points to pancreatitis.

### Q: Q1 — most likely diagnosis from the option list?

**Gallstone pancreatitis.** Key discriminators: amylase >3× ULN (rules in pancreatitis), cholestatic LFTs and Mars-bar history (point to gallstones over alcohol).

### Q: Modified Glasgow severity criteria — what does the mnemonic stand for?

**P A N C R E A S** — within 48 hours of onset:

- **P** — PO₂ < 8 kPa
- **A** — Age > 55 yrs
- **N** — Neutrophils / WCC > 15
- **C** — Calcium < 2 mmol/L
- **R** — Renal: urea > 16 mmol/L
- **E** — Enzymes: AST > 200 IU/L, LDH > 600 IU/L
- **A** — Albumin < 32 g/L
- **S** — Sugar (glucose) > 10 mmol/L

⭐ **Score ≥ 3 within 48 h = severe pancreatitis → HDU.** Classic exam cut-off. (Alternative tool = Ranson's criteria.)

### Q: How does CRP fit into severity scoring?

- **CRP is an independent predictor of severity.**
- **CRP > 200 = severe pancreatitis.**

### Q: Four principles of acute pancreatitis management?

1. **Fluid resuscitation** — IV fluids, **urinary catheter, strict fluid balance**.
2. **Analgesia.**
3. **Pancreatic rest** ± nutritional support if recovery prolonged (**NJ feeding** preferred over **TPN**).
4. **Determine the underlying cause** (USS, lipid profile, calcium, alcohol history, drugs).

### Q: Two counter-intuitive points about acute pancreatitis management?

⚠️ Don't pick the obvious answer on these — both are tested deliberately.
- **Antibiotics are NOT routine.** Only commenced if **necrotising pancreatitis or infected necrosis**. The lecturer flags this as controversial.
- **Surgery is very rarely required.** **95 % settle conservatively.** Do not pick "laparotomy" unless the stem explicitly states necrosis or haemodynamic collapse.

### Q: Q2 — once resuscitated, what's the FIRST imaging investigation?

**USS abdomen** — looking for gallstones in the gallbladder. Cheap, no radiation, sensitive for cholelithiasis.

📎 Figure L1.4 — Abdominal ultrasound showing gallstones (echogenic foci with posterior acoustic shadowing).

### Q: Q3 — Day 5, clinically well but persistently deranged LFTs — next investigation?

**MRCP** (magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography) — non-invasive imaging of the biliary tree to look for a **retained CBD stone**.

🧠 Persistently deranged LFTs after gallstone pancreatitis = **assume retained CBD stone until proven otherwise**.

### Q: Q4 — the MRCP shows a filling defect in the CBD. Next step?

**ERCP** (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography) — **therapeutic**: sphincterotomy + stone extraction with a balloon or basket.

📎 Figure L1.5 — MRCP showing filling defect in CBD.

📎 Figure L1.6 — Schematic of ERCP at the major duodenal papilla.

📎 Figure L1.7 — ERCP fluoroscopy showing CBD stone (arrows).

### Q: Standard of care after ERCP for gallstone pancreatitis?

**Laparoscopic cholecystectomy on the same admission** (or shortly after) — recurrence risk is high if the gallbladder is left in situ.

## Interleaved SBA bank — mixed across the lecture

### Q: SBA 1 — 60M, progressive dysphagia × 6 weeks, solids only, 5 kg weight loss. Best initial investigation?

- **Answer: OGD with biopsy.**
- *Why right:* Solids-only + progressive + weight loss = **red flag for oesophageal cancer** → direct visualisation + tissue diagnosis.
- *Why not barium swallow:* slower, won't biopsy, lower yield for early lesions.
- *Why not manometry:* manometry is for *functional* dysphagia (solids + liquids together).

### Q: SBA 2 — 35F, post-binge vomiting, severe chest pain, neck crepitations, T 38. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Answer: Boerhaave's syndrome** (spontaneous oesophageal perforation).
- Site: lower oesophagus, posterolateral, **left**.
- Surgical emphysema = pathognomonic in this stem.
- Confirm with **CT chest + abdomen with oral contrast.**

### Q: SBA 3 — 50F, dysphagia to solids AND liquids, regurgitation of undigested food at night × 2 yrs, weight loss. Barium swallow shows tapered "bird's beak". Diagnosis?

- **Answer: Achalasia.**
- *Why not cancer:* both-solids-and-liquids dysphagia + insidious 2-year course + classic radiological sign.
- *Why not scleroderma:* aperistaltic dilated oesophagus on barium, not bird's beak; usually CREST features.
- 28-fold increased oesophageal cancer risk if long-standing.

### Q: SBA 4 — 70F with paraoesophageal hiatus hernia presents with sudden severe chest pain and inability to vomit. Most worrying complication?

- **Answer: gastric volvulus / strangulation.**
- Paraoesophageal type (fundus alongside oesophagus) carries higher mechanical risk than sliding type.
- Classic: **Borchardt's triad** — severe epigastric pain, retching without vomit, inability to pass an NG tube.

### Q: SBA 5 — 65F, fatigue, MCV 110, glossitis, peripheral paraesthesia. Long-standing dyspepsia. Underlying mechanism?

- **Answer: chronic atrophic (autoimmune) gastritis → loss of parietal cells → no intrinsic factor → B12 malabsorption → pernicious anaemia.**
- Distinguishing from H. pylori gastritis: this is **type A**, autoimmune, often anti-IF or anti-parietal-cell antibodies.

### Q: SBA 6 — 45M post-OGD with dilatation for achalasia develops sudden chest pain, dysphagia, neck crepitations 4 h later. Best investigation?

- **Answer: CT chest + abdomen with oral contrast.**
- Iatrogenic perforation is the **single most common cause** overall, and the risk is amplified during therapeutic procedures like dilatation.

### Q: SBA 7 — 50F, RUQ pain after fatty meals, T 37.8, ALP 366, bili 35, amylase 2150. Best initial imaging?

- **Answer: USS abdomen.**
- Confirms gallstones. CT is reserved for suspected complications (necrosis, collections); MRCP comes later if LFTs stay deranged.

### Q: SBA 8 — Patient with confirmed gallstone pancreatitis, Glasgow score 4, HR 120, sats 92% on air. Most appropriate next step?

- **Answer: HDU admission.**
- ≥3 on Glasgow within 48 h = **severe pancreatitis** → high-dependency level care, aggressive fluids, organ support.
- *Not surgery* — 95% settle conservatively. *Not routine antibiotics* — only if necrotic/infected.

### Q: SBA 9 — 45M, NSAID user, sudden generalised abdominal pain, rigid abdomen, T 37.9, BP 100/60. Erect CXR shows free air under right hemidiaphragm. Most likely perforated organ?

- **Answer: Duodenum** (perforated duodenal ulcer).
- NSAIDs, alcohol, smoking → peptic ulcer disease → posterior or anterior duodenal ulcer can perforate; anterior more often perforates into peritoneum.
- Manage as septic peritonitis: NBM, NGT, IV fluids, antibiotics, theatre for **omental (Graham's) patch**.

### Q: SBA 10 — Day 5 of admission for gallstone pancreatitis, patient clinically improving but ALP and bili still rising. Next investigation?

- **Answer: MRCP.**
- Pattern fits a **retained CBD stone** despite clinical recovery from pancreatitis.
- ERCP would be premature without imaging confirmation; CT abdomen doesn't image the duct as well as MRCP.

### Q: SBA 11 — Patient post-laparoscopic Heller's myotomy + Dor fundoplication develops left shoulder tip pain and falling Hb on day 1. Most likely cause?

- **Answer: splenic injury** — most commonly via **tear of short gastric vessels** during fundus mobilisation (1–5%).
- Distinguishing from oesophageal/gastric perforation: that would present with sepsis/leak rather than haemorrhagic pattern.

### Q: SBA 12 — 60M, 3 days post-laparotomy for perforated duodenal ulcer, now SOB, sats 87% on 2L, right basal crepitations, T 38.5. Most likely diagnosis?

- **Answer: post-operative pneumonia** (HAP ± aspiration).
- Right base > left = right main bronchus is more vertical, aspirate goes right.
- Manage: O₂, physio, sit up, broad-spectrum antibiotics, sputum culture, repeat bloods.
- *Not PE*: bibasal crepitations + fever fit infection; PE usually clear chest, pleuritic pain ± tachycardia.
