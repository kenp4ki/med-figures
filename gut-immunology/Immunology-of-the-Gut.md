## Foundations: the "restrained activation" problem

```
GI tract: 200 m², 10¹⁴ commensals, dietary antigens, pathogens
                          │
                "RESTRAINED ACTIVATION"
              must tolerate AND react
            ┌─────────────┴─────────────┐
         TOLERANCE                IMMUNOREACTIVITY
       food, commensals               pathogens
            │                              │
   physical / chemical /          MALT / GALT / Peyer's
   commensal niche / sIgA         M cells / DCs / IgA / homing
            │
       DYSBIOSIS  →  DISEASE   (canonical example: C. difficile)
```

### Q: What is the "restrained activation" problem of gut immunity?

- 200 m² surface exposed to a massive antigen load (10¹⁴ commensals + diet + pathogens)
- Must **tolerate** food and commensals
- Must **react** to pathogens
- → constant balance between tolerance and immunoreactivity

### Q: Why does the immune system *require* the microbiota to develop?

- Evidence: **gnotobiology** (germ-free mouse colonisation studies)
- Without commensals: Peyer's patches are fewer and less cellular (the headline finding)
- Also: thinner lamina propria, ↓ sIgA, ↓ functional Tregs (FOXP3), ↓ Paneth cell antimicrobial peptides
- Bottom line: **healthy gut immunity *requires* bacterial microbiota**

⭐ Lecturer: of all the germ-free mouse defects, the Peyer's-patch finding is the one to know.

## Gut microbiota

### Q: How big is the gut microbiota and what does it do?

- **10¹⁴ bacteria** — more than the body's 10¹³ cells
- 4 phyla: **Bacteroidetes, Firmicutes, Actinobacteria, Proteobacteria** (also viruses, fungi)
- "Virtual organ": gene content ~100× our genome
- Functions: digest indigestible compounds, supply nutrients, exclude pathogens

### Q: How does bacterial density change down the GI tract?

- **Stomach** (HCl pH 1.4, pepsin): 10¹ /g
- **Duodenum** (bile acids): 10³
- **Jejunum** (pancreatic enzymes): 10⁴
- **Ileum** (brush-border enzymes): 10⁷
- **Colon** (no host digestive factors): **10¹²**

🧠 Pattern: density tracks *absence* of host antibacterial factors. Stomach acid and brush-border enzymes keep proximal counts low; the colon has none, so it dominates.

## Dysbiosis

### Q: Define symbiosis, symbiont, commensal, pathobiont.

- **Symbiosis** — living together; no benefit implied
- **Symbiont** — lives with host without benefit or harm
- **Commensal** — benefits from association; no known effect on host
- **Pathobiont** — symbiont that *normally* causes no inflammation BUT under environmental triggers has the **potential** to cause dysregulated inflammation and disease

### Q: What causes dysbiosis?

- **Infection or inflammation**
- **Diet**
- **Xenobiotics** (drugs/medications)
- **Hygiene**
- **Genetics**

### Q: How does dysbiosis cause disease?

- Tips the immunological see-saw: commensals → regulation; pathobiont overgrowth → inflammation
- Dysbiotic metabolites linked to immune *and* metabolic disease
- Canonical clinical example: ***C. difficile*** colitis after antibiotics

## Mucosal defense

### Q: List the layers of mucosal defense.

- **Physical** — anatomical (epithelial barrier, peristalsis); chemical (enzymes, acidic pH)
- **Biological** — commensals occupy the "ecological niche" (competitive exclusion)
- **Immunological** — MALT + GALT (engaged after invasion)

### Q: What does the epithelial barrier consist of?

- **Mucus layer** — produced by goblet cells
- **Epithelial monolayer** with **tight junctions**
- **Paneth cells** — small intestine only

### Q: Where are Paneth cells and what do they do?

- Located at the **bases of crypts of Lieberkühn**
- Secrete **antimicrobial peptides (defensins)** and **lysozyme** into the lumen

⚠️ Paneth cells live **only** in the small intestine. The colon has crypts but **no Paneth cells**. Common SBA distractor.

## MALT (Mucosa-Associated Lymphoid Tissue)

### Q: What is MALT and where does it sit?

- Lymphoid tissue in the **submucosa** beneath mucosal surfaces
- Contains lymphoid follicles
- Surrounded by **HEVs** (high endothelial venules / postcapillary venules) — these allow lymphocyte trafficking from blood

### Q: Where are HEVs found — and crucially, where are they NOT?

- Found in lymph nodes, tonsils, Peyer's patches, all secondary lymphoid organs
- **NOT in the spleen**

### Q: Three functions of HEVs.

- **Immune surveillance** — detect foreign or altered self (e.g. neoantigens in cancer)
- **Lymphocyte recirculation** — extravasation from blood into tissue
- **Initiate / maintain** immune responses in lymph nodes

### Q: Where else does MALT exist beyond the bowel?

- Whole GI tract, including the **oral cavity**
- Tonsils: **palatine, lingual, pharyngeal (adenoid)**

## GALT (Gut-Associated Lymphoid Tissue)

### Q: What is GALT and how is it categorised?

- Largest mass of lymphoid tissue in the body
- Mediates **both innate and adaptive** responses
- Two categories: **non-organised** vs **organised**

### Q: What are the non-organised GALT structures?

- **Intra-epithelial lymphocytes (IELs)** — T cells, NK cells; ~**1/5 of intestinal epithelium**
- **Lamina propria lymphocytes**

### Q: What are the organised GALT structures?

- **Peyer's patches** (small intestine)
- **Caecal patches** (large intestine)
- **Isolated lymphoid follicles**
- **Mesenteric lymph nodes** (encapsulated)

📎 Figure L1.2 — GALT overview (villus, IELs, Peyer's patch, lymphatic drainage).

📎 Figure L1.3 — Peyer's patch on H&E.

### Q: Key structural differences between small and large intestine GALT.

- **Small**: villi + crypts, **Paneth cells** at crypt bases, **single mucus layer**
- **Large**: crypts only (no villi), **NO Paneth cells**, **double mucus layer** (outer with bacteria, inner sterile)

📎 Figure L1.4 — Small vs large intestine cellular cast.

## Peyer's patches — the immune sensors

⭐ Lecturer: *"An important slide for this lecture"* — Peyer's patches are heavily examined.

### Q: Where are Peyer's patches found?

- **Submucosa of small intestine, mainly distal ileum**
- Aggregated lymphoid follicles

### Q: What is the FAE? Three negatives that define it.

- **Follicle-associated epithelium** — the covering over a Peyer's patch
- **NO goblet cells**
- **NO secretory IgA**
- **LACKS microvilli**

### Q: How many Peyer's patches develop and when?

- **50** by last trimester of foetus
- **250** by teens
- Development requires bacterial microbiota exposure (germ-free mice never get there)

### Q: How do M cells sample antigen?

- **M (microfold) cells** sit within the FAE
- Take up antigens directly from the gut lumen
- Express **IgA receptors** → carry IgA-bacteria complexes into the patch
- Hand off to **sub-epithelial dome dendritic cells** → underlying B/T follicle

📎 Figure L1.5 — Peyer's patch architecture.

## Antigen sampling — the second route

### Q: What is the alternative to M-cell antigen sampling?

- **Trans-epithelial dendritic cells**
- DCs extend dendrites between epithelial cells, **opening tight junctions**
- Sample lumen directly **without breaching the barrier** (DC fills the gap)
- DC migrates to mesenteric LN to activate the adaptive response

📎 Figure L1.6 — Trans-epithelial dendritic cell sampling.

🧠 **Two routes for gut antigen sampling — know both:** (1) M cells in the FAE of Peyer's patches, (2) trans-epithelial dendritic cells between enterocytes. SBAs love asking which is which.

## B-cell adaptive response and sIgA

### Q: How does the gut B cell switch class?

- Naïve B cells in the Peyer's patch express **IgM**
- On antigen presentation → **class-switch to IgA**
- T cells + epithelial cells drive maturation via cytokines
- Mature into **IgA-secreting plasma cells** that populate the **lamina propria**

📎 Figure L1.7 — B-cell adaptive response in the Peyer's patch.

### Q: How is secretory IgA assembled and exported?

1. Plasma cell (submucosa) secretes **dimeric IgA** (held by **J chain**)
2. Binds **poly-Ig receptor** on basolateral side of epithelial cell
3. **Transcytosis** through epithelial cell in vesicle
4. **Enzymatic cleavage** at apical surface releases sIgA into lumen
5. Receptor remnant stays attached as the **secretory component**

📎 Figure L1.8 — Secretory IgA formation.

### Q: Why is sIgA the dominant gut antibody?

- **Up to 90% of gut B cells secrete IgA**
- Coats luminal antigens → blocks adhesion + invasion
- Does this **WITHOUT triggering inflammation**
- Critical to "restrained activation" — defends without chronic inflammation

⭐ Up to **90%** of gut B cells secrete IgA.

## Lymphocyte homing & circulation

### Q: Trace an activated gut lymphocyte from antigen to effector site.

```
Peyer's patch (Ag presentation, T/B activation)
        │
        ▼
Mesenteric lymph node (proliferation)
        │
        ▼
Thoracic duct  →  systemic circulation
        │
        ├──→ back to lamina propria of villi (local effect)
        └──→ skin, tonsils, BALT (mucosal-wide protection)
```

### Q: How does an activated T cell know to home back to the gut?

- Gut HEV expresses **MAdCAM-1** (Mucosal Addressin Cell Adhesion Molecule-1) — tissue-specific
- T cell expresses **α₄β₇ integrin** — binds MAdCAM-1
- Cascade: **rolling → chemokine activation → integrin tightening → arrest → transmigration**

📎 Figure L1.9 — Gut homing cascade (α₄β₇ / MAdCAM-1).

🧠 **Reusable pattern.** Same selectin/integrin/chemokine rolling cascade as inflammation — only the addressins are gut-specific (**MAdCAM-1 / α₄β₇**). When an SBA asks for the gut-homing receptor pair, it's this one.

## Enterocyte turnover

### Q: What is the enterocyte lifespan and why so short?

- ~**36 hours** (vs weeks–months for lung, vessels)
- Constant antigen + toxin exposure
- Self-cleaning escalator: damaged cells shed before damage accumulates
- Lesions are short-lived

### Q: What happens if enterocyte turnover is interrupted?

- Stem cells in crypts produce new enterocytes that climb the villus
- **Radiation / chemotherapy blocks production**
- → severe intestinal dysfunction (radiation enteritis, chemo mucositis)

## Infectious diarrhoea — territory map

**Quick clue → organism**

- **Rice-water stools** → **Cholera** (*V. cholerae* O1 / O139)
- **Child <5, watery** → **Rotavirus**
- **Closed-community outbreak** (cruise / hospital / care home) → **Norovirus**
- **Undercooked poultry, low dose** → **Campylobacter**
- **Bloody diarrhoea + HUS** → **E. coli O157** (EHEC/STEC)
- **Recent antibiotics** → ***C. difficile***

## Cholera

### Q: Cholera — organism, transmission, presentation.

- ***Vibrio cholerae*** serogroups **O1** and **O139**
- **Faecal-oral** via contaminated water/food
- Severe **watery diarrhoea (rice-water stools)**, vomiting, dehydration

### Q: Cholera — mechanism in 5 steps.

1. Cholera enterotoxin binds enterocyte
2. Activates **adenylate cyclase**
3. ↑ **cAMP**
4. **CFTR** pumps **Cl⁻** into lumen → Na⁺ + H₂O follow
5. Massive watery secretion → diarrhoea

📎 Figure L1.10 — Cholera mechanism (cAMP cascade).

### Q: Cholera — diagnosis, treatment, vaccine.

- **Diagnosis**: stool culture on **selective agar** (gold standard); rapid dipstick available
- **Treatment**: **oral rehydration is mainstay** — ~80% successfully treated this way
- **Vaccine**: **Dukoral** — oral, inactivated

⭐ **Rice-water stools = cholera** (lecturer's clue pattern).

---

## Rotavirus

### Q: Rotavirus — what it is, who it affects.

- **RNA virus**, replicates in enterocytes
- 5 types A–E; **type A** most common in humans
- **Most common cause of diarrhoea in infants & young children worldwide**
- Still ~200,000 deaths/year globally

### Q: Rotavirus — treatment and vaccine.

- Treatment: **oral rehydration therapy**
- Vaccine: **Rotarix** — live attenuated oral vaccine vs type A
- Introduced UK **July 2013** → marked drop in lab reports

---

## Norovirus

### Q: Norovirus — organism and transmission.

- RNA virus (Norovirus genus, Norwalk virus species)
- Incubation **24–48 h**
- **Faecal-oral**; infectious shedding **up to 2 weeks**
- ~685 million cases / 200,000 deaths per year globally

### Q: Norovirus — presentation, diagnosis, key clue.

- Acute gastroenteritis with **prominent vomiting**, recovery 1–3 days
- Diagnosis: **stool PCR**
- Distinctive: **outbreaks in CLOSED COMMUNITIES** — hospitals, care homes, cruise ships

⭐ "Closed community outbreak" is the pathognomonic stem clue for norovirus.

---

## Campylobacter

### Q: Campylobacter — species, transmission, dose.

- ***C. jejuni***, ***C. coli*** (curved Gram-negative)
- **Undercooked poultry**, untreated water, unpasteurised milk
- **Very low infective dose: <500 bacteria** can cause illness
- **Commonest cause of food poisoning in the UK** (~280,000 cases/year)

### Q: Campylobacter — treatment.

- Usually supportive only
- Antibiotic of choice: **azithromycin** (a macrolide)
- **Fluoroquinolone resistance is problematic**

---

## *Escherichia coli*

### Q: How many *E. coli* pathotypes are there, and which ones matter?

- **6 pathotypes total** (most environmental *E. coli* harmless)
- **Headline three**: ETEC, EIEC, EHEC/STEC
- Named-only: EPEC, EAEC, DAEC

### Q: ETEC vs EIEC vs EHEC — distinguish them.

- **ETEC** (enterotoxigenic): cholera-like toxin → **watery diarrhoea**
- **EIEC** (enteroinvasive): Shigella-like → **bloody diarrhoea**
- **EHEC / STEC** (***E. coli* O157**): Shigatoxin / verotoxin → **bloody diarrhoea**

### Q: Why is *E. coli* O157 a classic exam question?

- **5–10% develop haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS)**
- HUS = **loss of kidney function**
- Stem clue: bloody diarrhoea after barbecue / undercooked beef + AKI / falling Hb

⭐ Lecturer: *"this tends to be a classic exam question"* — 5–10% of *E. coli* O157 cases develop HUS.

---

## *Clostridium difficile*

### Q: How does *C. difficile* cause disease?

1. Healthy state — commensals dominant; *C. diff* present in low numbers
2. **Antibiotics kill commensals → dysbiosis**
3. *C. diff* overgrows
4. *C. diff* **toxin** production causes **both** symptoms **and** a self-supportive niche
5. **Asymptomatic colonisation** is possible if no toxin produced

📎 Figure L1.11 — *C. difficile* dysbiosis cycle.

### Q: *C. diff* — typical clinical picture.

- Crampy abdominal pain + diarrhoea
- Patient currently on or recently completed antibiotics
- **Highly contagious** — isolate

### Q: *C. diff* — management.

- **Isolate** patient
- **Stop** offending antibiotic if clinically possible
- **First-line: ORAL metronidazole**
- **Second-line: ORAL vancomycin**
- Recurrence **15–35%**; if recurrent → **FMT (faecal microbiota transplantation): 98% cure rate**

⭐ The drugs to know: **first-line ORAL metronidazole, second-line ORAL vancomycin.** Both must be ORAL.

## Cross-cutting patterns

🧠 **GI infection clue index (memorise as a reflex).** Rice-water → cholera. Child <5 → rotavirus. Closed community → norovirus. Undercooked poultry → Campylobacter. Bloody + HUS → *E. coli* O157. Recent antibiotics → *C. difficile*.

### Q: Which infections classically cause bloody diarrhoea?

- **EHEC / STEC** (*E. coli* O157)
- **EIEC**
- **Shigella, Campylobacter** (sometimes)
- ***Entamoeba histolytica***

### Q: Which two cases need antibiotics as part of standard management?

- **Severe / persistent Campylobacter** → azithromycin
- ***C. difficile*** → ALWAYS treat (oral metronidazole → oral vancomycin)
- *(Most other bacterial gastroenteritis: supportive only.)*

## Clinical assessment & case method

### Q: The four-question history-taking framework for acute diarrhoea.

1. **"When did it start?"** → onset (1–2 days post-meal = contaminated food)
2. **"What does it look like?"** → blood / mucus / pus = severe inflammation or infection
3. **"How do you feel?"** → associated symptoms (tenesmus, vomiting, fever, thirst)
4. **"Where have you been?"** → travel / diet / contacts / occupation

### Q: What does stool appearance point to?

- **Watery, high volume** → secretory (cholera, ETEC, viral)
- **Blood + mucus + pus** → severe inflammation / infectious cause
- **Mucus + pus alone** → chronic inflammatory cause OR infective pathogen

### Q: Key lifestyle / context cues that change the differential.

- Recent travel
- Meat / eggs / dairy / seafood ingestion
- Pet or cattle exposure
- Day-care / hospital / care-home occupation
- Sexual practice, alcohol, drug use
- Pre-existing **IBD** (0.5–1%) / **coeliac** (0.5–1%) / **IBS** (10–13%)

🧠 **Reusable case-method.** When an SBA gives an acute-diarrhoea stem, run the four questions in order *before* picking a diagnosis. Onset → stool look → associated symptoms → context. Answer falls out by clue-matching against the cross-cutting index above.

### Q: Pharmacy case — 39-year-old carer with 10 episodes of watery diarrhoea / 24 h, no red flags. What advice?

- **Oral rehydration** (water + isotonic sports drinks for K⁺)
- Bland, easily digestible foods with high water content
- **Infection control** — *do not return to work until 48 h after symptoms resolve* (vulnerable contacts)
- Safety net: GP review if not resolving in **7 days**

## Interleaved SBAs (mixed across the lecture)

### Q: SBA 1 — A 4-year-old, unvaccinated, presents with 2 days of profuse watery diarrhoea. His sister at nursery had similar illness. Most likely organism?

**Answer: B — Rotavirus.**

- A. *Campylobacter* — would have a poultry / food history and bloody-tinged stool
- **B. Rotavirus** ✓ — most common cause of diarrhoea in young children worldwide; nursery cluster + unvaccinated child fits perfectly
- C. *V. cholerae* — would need contaminated water exposure + rice-water stools
- D. *C. difficile* — requires antibiotic exposure
- E. EHEC O157 — would expect **bloody** diarrhoea + meat exposure

### Q: SBA 2 — A 28-year-old returns from rural Bangladesh with profuse watery "rice-water" stools and severe dehydration. Most likely?

**Answer: C — *Vibrio cholerae*.**

- A. Norovirus — vomiting prominent; not "rice-water"
- B. *Salmonella* — typically more inflammatory, fever, blood
- **C. *Vibrio cholerae*** ✓ — rice-water stools is the lecturer's pathognomonic clue
- D. *Campylobacter* — undercooked poultry, often bloody
- E. *C. difficile* — needs antibiotic history

### Q: SBA 3 — A 70-year-old finished co-amoxiclav 10 days ago for chest infection; now has 6 episodes/day of crampy watery diarrhoea. First-line treatment?

**Answer: A — Oral metronidazole.**

- **A. Oral metronidazole** ✓ — lecturer-stated first-line for *C. difficile*
- B. IV vancomycin — wrong route; *C. diff* needs ORAL vancomycin (it has to act in the lumen)
- C. IV metronidazole — second-choice route only if oral impossible
- D. Oral ciprofloxacin — fluoroquinolones are part of the cause, not the cure
- E. Loperamide — contraindicated in *C. diff* (slows clearance, risks toxic megacolon)

### Q: SBA 4 — A 35-year-old develops bloody diarrhoea 3 days after a barbecue. Five days later: oliguria, falling Hb, low platelets. Diagnosis?

**Answer: C — EHEC O157 with HUS.**

- A. Norovirus — watery + vomit, not bloody, no HUS
- B. *Campylobacter* — possible bloody diarrhoea but does not cause HUS
- **C. EHEC O157 with HUS** ✓ — Shigatoxin → 5–10% develop HUS (kidney failure, haemolysis, thrombocytopenia)
- D. *C. difficile* — wrong context (no antibiotics)
- E. ETEC — watery diarrhoea; no HUS

### Q: SBA 5 — Twelve passengers on a cruise ship develop vomiting and diarrhoea over 24 h. Most likely organism?

**Answer: A — Norovirus.**

- **A. Norovirus** ✓ — closed community + prominent vomiting = textbook stem
- B. Rotavirus — children, not cruise adults
- C. ETEC — traveller's diarrhoea, not "outbreak in closed setting"
- D. Cholera — needs contaminated water source on a developing-world scale
- E. *Campylobacter* — sporadic food poisoning, not point-source outbreak

### Q: SBA 6 — Which is **NOT** a feature of the FAE covering Peyer's patches?

**Answer: E — Contains Paneth cells.**

- A. Lacks goblet cells — true (defined as such)
- B. Lacks microvilli — true
- C. Lacks secretory IgA — true
- D. Contains M cells — true (M cells are *defining* feature of FAE)
- **E. Contains Paneth cells** ✓ wrong — Paneth cells live at the *bases of crypts*, not in the FAE dome

### Q: SBA 7 — Which adhesion molecule pair directs lymphocyte homing specifically to the gut?

**Answer: C — α₄β₇ integrin / MAdCAM-1.**

- A. P-selectin / PSGL-1 — initial rolling, generic (not gut-specific)
- B. ICAM-1 / LFA-1 — generic firm adhesion in inflammation
- **C. α₄β₇ integrin / MAdCAM-1** ✓ — MAdCAM-1 is the gut "postcode"; α₄β₇ is the reader
- D. E-selectin / sialyl-Lewis^x — skin homing
- E. VCAM-1 / α₄β₁ — generic vascular endothelium

### Q: SBA 8 — A patient eats undercooked chicken at a wedding. 3 days later: bloody diarrhoea + abdominal cramps. Severe enough that antibiotics are indicated. First-line?

**Answer: B — Azithromycin.**

- A. Ciprofloxacin — fluoroquinolone resistance is established for *Campylobacter*
- **B. Azithromycin** ✓ — macrolide, the standard antibiotic for *Campylobacter*
- C. Metronidazole — wrong target (used for *C. diff*, anaerobes, protozoa)
- D. Vancomycin — wrong target (*C. diff* second line)
- E. Trimethoprim — wrong spectrum

### Q: SBA 9 — Which cells lie at the bases of crypts of Lieberkühn and secrete defensins?

**Answer: C — Paneth cells.**

- A. Goblet cells — secrete mucus, distributed up the villus
- B. M cells — sit in the FAE over Peyer's patches
- **C. Paneth cells** ✓ — crypt base, defensins + lysozyme; small intestine only
- D. Enteroendocrine cells — secrete hormones, not defensins
- E. Intra-epithelial lymphocytes — T cells/NK cells, not crypt-resident, no defensins

### Q: SBA 10 — Where is the Peyer's-patch concentration highest?

**Answer: C — Distal ileum.**

- A. Duodenum — too proximal
- B. Jejunum — Peyer's patches present but fewer
- **C. Distal ileum** ✓ — explicitly stated on slide and in transcript
- D. Caecum — has caecal patches, not Peyer's
- E. Sigmoid colon — colon has isolated lymphoid follicles, not Peyer's patches

### Q: SBA 11 — Secretory IgA structure consists of:

**Answer: B — Dimeric IgA + J chain + secretory component.**

- A. Single IgA + J chain — incomplete
- **B. Dimeric IgA + J chain + secretory component (poly-Ig receptor remnant)** ✓
- C. Pentameric IgA — IgM is pentameric, not IgA
- D. Single IgA + secretory component — missing the dimer + J chain
- E. Dimeric IgM + J chain — wrong isotype

### Q: SBA 12 — In which secondary lymphoid organ are HEVs **NOT** found?

**Answer: D — Spleen.**

- A. Mesenteric lymph nodes — have HEVs
- B. Tonsils — MALT, have HEVs
- C. Peyer's patches — surrounded by HEVs (defining feature)
- **D. Spleen** ✓ — the explicit slide-stated exception
- E. Cervical lymph nodes — have HEVs

### Q: SBA 13 — Faecal microbiota transplantation has its highest reported cure rate in:

**Answer: C — Recurrent *C. difficile*.**

- A. UC — investigational, not headline indication
- B. Crohn's — investigational
- **C. Recurrent *C. difficile*** ✓ — ~98% cure rate (slide-stated)
- D. IBS — not standard
- E. Coeliac — gluten-mediated; FMT has no role

### Q: SBA 14 — A pharmacy customer with 24 h of watery diarrhoea, no blood, no travel, no immunocompromise, no chronic GI disease. Best advice?

**Answer: C — Oral rehydration + bland diet + 48-h post-symptom return-to-work delay.**

- A. Refer to GP immediately — no red flags; GP only if not resolving by day 7
- B. Loperamide + co-codamol — risks worsening any infection; the original case rejects this approach
- **C. Oral rehydration + bland diet + 48-h post-symptom return-to-work delay** ✓
- D. Prescribe ciprofloxacin — antibiotics not indicated for self-limiting acute diarrhoea
- E. Stool culture before treatment — overkill in primary care, no red flags
