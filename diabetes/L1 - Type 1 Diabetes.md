## Definition & classification

### Q: What is type 1 diabetes in one sentence?

- Autoimmune condition in which insulin-producing β-cells are progressively destroyed → **absolute insulin deficiency** → hyperglycaemia
- Requires **life-long insulin** treatment

### Q: What are the six WHO (2019) categories of diabetes?

- Type 1
- Type 2
- Hybrid forms
- Other
- Unclassified
- During pregnancy (gestational)

### Q: In the textbook T1 vs T2 dichotomy, what drives each, and what's the core contrast?

| | T1DM | T2DM |
|---|---|---|
| Trigger | Genetic risk + environmental trigger | Genetic risk + obesity |
| Mechanism | Autoimmune β-cell destruction | Insulin resistance + β-cell failure |
| Deficiency | **Absolute** | **Relative** |

🟨 The absolute vs relative distinction downstream-explains almost every other contrast (ketosis, reversibility, oral drugs).

### Q: Name four real-life deviations from the textbook T1/T2 dichotomy.

- **LADA** — autoimmune diabetes in adults, often misdiagnosed as T2
- **T2DM in youth** — increasingly common
- **DKA can occur in T2DM** — in acute illness or ketone-prone T2
- **MODY** — monogenic, phenocopies T1 or T2

🟥 Don't reject T1 because the patient is older, or reject T2 because they're lean.

## Pathogenesis

### Q: Draw the β-cell destruction cascade.

```
Genetic susceptibility (HLA-DR3/DR4)
        ↓
Environmental trigger (?)
        ↓
Immune activation (innate + adaptive)
        ↓
Auto-antigen presented to CD4⁺ T cells
        ↓
CD4⁺ activate CD8⁺ cytotoxic T cells
        ↓
CD8⁺ travel to islets → lyse β-cells
        ↓
Autoantibodies detectable (IAA, GAD-65, IA-2, ZnT8)
        ↓
β-cell mass falls → clinical T1DM
```

Underlying defect: **regulatory T-cell failure** to suppress autoreactive clones.

📎 Figure L1.6 — Immune pathogenesis cartoon.

### Q: Which HLA-DR alleles confer significant T1DM risk, and which is protective?

- **DR3** — significant risk
- **DR4** — significant risk
- **DR2** — protective

🟨 Three-token answer for almost any HLA-T1DM SBA.

### Q: Name the four pancreatic autoantibodies used diagnostically.

- **IAA** — insulin autoantibodies
- **GAD-65** — glutamic acid decarboxylase
- **IA-2** — insulinoma-associated-2
- **ZnT8** — zinc transporter 8

Presence of ≥2 defines pre-clinical stages.

### Q: Why is measuring C-peptide clinically useful?

- Proinsulin cleaves to insulin + C-peptide in equimolar amounts
- **Injected (exogenous) insulin contains no C-peptide**
- Low C-peptide = endogenous insulin production failed → supports T1DM
- Normal/high C-peptide = β-cells still working → supports T2DM/MODY

📎 Figure L1.3 — Proinsulin cleavage.

### Q: Describe the autoantibody-based staging of T1DM (DiMeglio 2018).

- **Stage 1:** Normal blood sugar + ≥2 autoAbs
- **Stage 2:** Abnormal blood sugar (IFG/IGT) + ≥2 autoAbs
- **Stage 3:** Clinical diagnosis (symptomatic) + ≥2 autoAbs
- **Stage 4:** Long-standing T1DM

🟦 Autoantibodies appear **before** glucose abnormality — T1DM has a silent pre-clinical phase.

📎 Figure L1.4 — Staging.

### Q: Why does the immune basis of T1DM matter clinically? (five reasons)

- Increased prevalence of other autoimmune disease (screen for coeliac, thyroid)
- First-degree relatives have 15× baseline risk
- β-cell destruction usually more complete than T2
- Autoantibodies are clinically useful when type is ambiguous
- Opens door to future immune-modulation therapy

### Q: Is all β-cell mass destroyed in T1DM?

- No — some patients retain low-level endogenous insulin production for decades
- Oram 2016: detectable C-peptide even at 40+ years duration
- Not enough to drop insulin therapy, but explains variable severity

📎 Figure L1.7 — Residual C-peptide.

### Q: 🟦 Reusable pattern — when to suspect a non-T1/T2 diabetes type

- Young + strong autosomal-dominant family history + mild hyperglycaemia → **MODY**
- Adult-onset + positive autoAbs + rapid progression to insulin → **LADA**
- Pregnancy → **gestational**
- Post-pancreatitis, CF, haemochromatosis → **pancreatogenic (secondary)**

## Biochemistry of insulin deficiency

### Q: Insulin is absent. What happens to muscle, liver, and adipose?

- **Muscle:** proteolysis → amino acids released (gluconeogenic substrate)
- **Liver:** ↑ hepatic glucose output (gluconeogenesis + glycogenolysis)
- **Adipose:** lipolysis → glycerol + NEFA released

📎 Figure L1.9 — Three-tissue insulin-deficiency schematic.

### Q: Which two hormonal changes flip on hepatic ketogenesis?

- **Insulin absent** (normally inhibits ketogenesis)
- **Glucagon unopposed** (normally activates ketogenesis when insulin is low)
- Result: fatty acyl-CoA → acetyl-CoA → acetoacetate → acetone + β-hydroxybutyrate → ketone bodies released

📎 Figure L1.10 — Hepatic ketogenesis.

### Q: Map each classical T1DM symptom onto its mechanism.

- Polyuria, polydipsia, glycosuria → osmotic diuresis (glucose exceeds renal threshold)
- Weight loss, cachexia, fatigue → catabolism (proteolysis + lipolysis)
- Pear-drop breath, Kussmaul hyperventilation → ketone accumulation + acidosis
- Blurred vision → lens osmolarity shift
- Thrush / infections → hyperglycaemia-driven immunocompromise

🟦 Every symptom derives from "insulin gone → anabolism off + osmotic losses on". Don't memorise — derive.

## Presentation & diagnosis

### Q: What is the Diabetes UK "4Ts" campaign?

- **Toilet** — polyuria, nocturia
- **Thirsty** — polydipsia
- **Tired** — fatigue
- **Thinner** — weight loss

### Q: What signs might be present at T1DM presentation?

- Dehydration, dry mucosae
- Cachexia
- **Kussmaul hyperventilation**
- **Smell of ketones** (pear drops)
- Glycosuria, ketonuria on urine dip

### Q: How is the diagnosis of T1DM actually made?

- **Clinical features + ketones** — enough to start insulin
- **Autoantibodies** — support the diagnosis when type is ambiguous
- **C-peptide** (low) — supports T1DM over T2DM
- OGTT is **not** used — patient is too sick to wait

🟨 Young, lean, rapid weight loss + ketones on urine = start insulin now, type later.

## Treatment principles

### Q: What three physiological features must an insulin regimen imitate?

- **Insulin never completely suppressed** — always a low baseline (basal)
- **Basal profile is flat** between meals
- **Prandial peak is two-phase** — fast first-phase spike, then sustained second phase

### Q: Name the short-acting and long-acting insulin analogues.

- **Short-acting analogues:** Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine
- **Long-acting analogues:** Glargine, Detemir, Degludec
- Human soluble short-acting: Actrapid
- Human long-acting: NPH (bound to zinc/protamine)

### Q: Describe the standard basal-bolus regimen.

- TDS short-acting analogue with meals (bolus)
- Once-daily long-acting (basal)
- First-line for most adults with T1DM

### Q: What is insulin pump therapy (CSII) and when is it indicated?

- Continuous subcutaneous infusion of short-acting analogue
- Programmable basal rate + patient-activated mealtime bolus
- Main indication: **problematic hypoglycaemia** (esp. impaired awareness)

### Q: How does a hybrid closed-loop system work?

- Real-time CGM measures glucose every ~5 min
- Algorithm calculates insulin requirement
- Pump delivers insulin; loop repeats
- "Hybrid" because patient still boluses manually for meals

📎 Figure L1.12 — Hybrid closed-loop.

### Q: Where are islet cells transplanted, and what are the limits?

- Infused into the **hepatic portal vein** (islets engraft in the liver)
- Requires **life-long immunosuppression**
- Limits: donor scarcity, incomplete graft survival, immunosuppression complications

🟨 "Hepatic portal vein" is the classic SBA detail.

### Q: What is DAFNE, and why is structured education important in T1DM?

- **DAFNE** = Dose Adjustment For Normal Eating — 5-day NICE-recommended structured education programme
- T1DM is a self-managed condition: patient spends ~1.5 hours/year with a consultant
- Education is the delivery vehicle for treatment

## Glucose monitoring & HbA1c

### Q: How does HbA1c form, and what is it actually measuring?

- Glucose + N-terminal valine of Hb β-chain → Schiff base (fast, hours) → Amadori product (slow, days) = HbA1c
- Non-enzymatic (**glycated, NOT glycosylated**)
- Reflects last ~3 months of glycaemia (RBC lifespan)
- Biased to the 30 days preceding measurement

📎 Figure L1.14 — HbA1c chemistry.

### Q: 🟦 Reusable pattern — how do you predict whether HbA1c will be spuriously raised or lowered?

- Anything **shortening** RBC lifespan → **lowers** HbA1c
- Anything **lengthening** RBC lifespan → **raises** HbA1c

### Q: List conditions that raise HbA1c spuriously.

- Iron deficiency (↓ erythropoiesis → older RBCs)
- B12 / folate deficiency
- Splenectomy (longer RBC lifespan)
- Alcoholism, CRF
- Hyperbilirubinaemia, some haemoglobinopathies

### Q: List conditions that lower HbA1c spuriously.

- Haemolysis (any cause)
- Splenomegaly
- Erythropoietin / iron / B12 treatment (young RBCs)
- Rheumatoid arthritis, antiretrovirals, ribavirin, dapsone
- Chronic liver disease

🟥 An "acceptable" HbA1c in a significantly anaemic patient may be hiding genuinely poor control — check FBC when numbers don't match clinical reality.

## Acute complications

### Q: State the four diagnostic criteria for DKA.

- **pH < 7.3**
- **HCO₃⁻ < 15 mmol/L**
- **Glucose > 11 mmol/L**
- **Ketones increased** (urine or capillary)

All four needed. Glucose level **alone** cannot rule DKA in or out.

### Q: 🟥 Trap — what is euglycaemic DKA?

- pH <7.3 + HCO₃⁻ <15 + ketones ↑ **but glucose normal**
- Classic cause: **SGLT2 inhibitor** (causes glucosuria while ketogenesis runs)
- Diagnose DKA on acidosis + ketones — not glucose

### Q: When does DKA occur?

- New-onset T1DM (~25% present this way)
- Established T1DM: acute illness, missed doses, inadequate doses
- **Any type of diabetes** — including T2DM in stress or on SGLT2i

### Q: What is the numerical definition of hypoglycaemia, and of *severe* hypoglycaemia?

- Numerical threshold: **< 3.6 mmol/L** (slide); clinically "4 is the floor"
- **Severe hypoglycaemia** = any event requiring **3rd-party assistance**

### Q: What are the adrenergic vs neuroglycopaenic symptoms of hypoglycaemia?

- **Adrenergic (warning layer):** tremor, palpitations, sweating, hunger
- **Neuroglycopaenic (brain fuel loss):** somnolence, confusion, incoordination, seizures, coma

Adrenergic comes first; neuroglycopaenic as glucose drops further. In impaired awareness, adrenergic layer is **lost**.

📎 Figure L1.15 — Hypoglycaemia symptoms.

### Q: Why does hypoglycaemia awareness blunt over time?

- Exogenous insulin cannot be switched off mid-action
- Glucagon response is lost after ~5 years of T1DM
- Patient depends on adrenaline warning
- Recurrent hypos blunt the adrenaline response → **impaired awareness** → straight to neuroglycopaenic without warning

### Q: When is hypoglycaemia "problematic"?

- Excessive frequency
- **Impaired awareness**
- Nocturnal hypos (unwitnessed)
- Recurrent severe hypos

Risks: seizure, coma, "dead in bed" syndrome, driving implications, cognitive impact.

### Q: Acute hypoglycaemia management ladder — by conscious level.

- **Alert & orientated:** oral CHO (rapid — juice/sweets; longer — sandwich)
- **Drowsy, swallow intact:** buccal glucose gel (Hypostop/glucogel), then complex CHO
- **Unconscious / swallow concern:** **IV 20% glucose**
- **No IV / refractory:** **IM or SC 1 mg glucagon**

🟦 The ladder steps down by swallow safety, not by glucose level.

📎 Figure L1.16 — Acute hypo management.

### Q: 🟥 Trap — when does glucagon fail?

- Dose-limited by liver glycogen stores
- Fails in **alcoholics, starvation, repeated recent hypos**
- These patients need IV glucose, not a second glucagon

## Cross-references

### Q: 🟦 How does the ketone-body pathway behave differently in T2DM?

- T1 = absolute deficiency → ketogenesis ON → DKA
- T2 = relative deficiency → residual insulin partly brakes ketogenesis → ketosis usually OFF
- But long-duration T2DM or SGLT2i-treated patients can still develop DKA
- See also: **L2 — Q: Why does T2DM typically not cause ketosis?**

### Q: 🟦 The HbA1c number you learn here (chemistry) has TWO separate clinical uses.

- **L2:** HbA1c ≥ **48 mmol/mol** = diagnostic threshold for T2DM
- **L3:** HbA1c **≤ 53 mmol/mol (< 7%)** = therapeutic target for reducing microvascular complications
- Same molecule, different numbers, different uses
- See also: **L2 — Q: What are the diagnostic cut-offs for T2DM?** and **L3 — Q: What HbA1c target reduces microvascular complication risk?**

### Q: 🟦 DKA (this lecture) vs HHS (T2DM equivalent).

- DKA = acidosis + ketones (absolute deficiency)
- HHS = severe osmolality + dehydration, minimal ketones (relative deficiency, elderly T2)
- Different diagnostic numbers, different management tempo
- See also: **L2 — Q: What are the diagnostic features of HHS?**

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA — A 14-year-old with polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, glucose 24 mmol/L, urine ketones ++. Which finding most strongly supports T1DM over MODY?

- A. Family history of diabetes in mother and maternal grandmother
- B. HLA-DR3 allele present
- C. Positive GAD-65 and IA-2 autoantibodies
- D. BMI below the 5th centile
- E. Age 14 at presentation

**Answer: C.** Autoantibodies are the discriminator — MODY is non-autoimmune. A strong autosomal-dominant family history actually raises MODY suspicion. HLA-DR3 is a risk allele but not diagnostic. Low BMI and age 14 fit both.

### Q: SBA — A 58-year-old woman of normal BMI, on metformin 18 months, now requires insulin. GAD-65 antibodies positive. Most likely diagnosis?

- A. T2DM progressing
- B. MODY
- C. LADA
- D. T1DM misdiagnosed
- E. Steroid-induced diabetes

**Answer: C.** LADA = autoimmune diabetes in adults, slower β-cell loss than classic T1, positive autoantibodies, rapid progression to insulin need.

### Q: SBA — 19-year-old, 2 weeks of polyuria, polydipsia, 5 kg weight loss. Drowsy, RR 28, fruity breath. Glucose 27 mmol/L, urine ketones ++++. Most important initial action?

- A. Send autoantibodies
- B. IV fluids + insulin for suspected DKA
- C. Oral metformin
- D. Commence SC basal-bolus
- E. Arrange OGTT

**Answer: B.** DKA until proven otherwise — resuscitate first. Autoantibodies can wait; typing happens later.

### Q: SBA — T1DM patient on SGLT2i for nephropathy presents with pH 7.18, HCO₃⁻ 10, ketones ++++, glucose 6.8 mmol/L. Diagnosis?

- A. HHS
- B. Lactic acidosis
- C. Euglycaemic DKA
- D. Salicylate poisoning
- E. Uraemic acidosis

**Answer: C.** Acidosis + ketones = DKA, even though glucose is "normal". SGLT2i drives glucosuria while ketogenesis proceeds.

### Q: SBA — Well-controlled T1DM (HbA1c 58) but two severe hypos requiring paramedics in the last month. Impaired awareness. Next step?

- A. Increase glargine
- B. Switch to twice-daily mixed
- C. Refer for insulin pump (CSII)
- D. Target a lower HbA1c
- E. Reduce carbohydrate intake

**Answer: C.** Problematic hypoglycaemia with impaired awareness is a named indication for CSII — variable basal rates allow reduction overnight and around exercise.

### Q: SBA — T2DM patient, HbA1c 41 mmol/mol, reports feeling well. Bloods: Hb 82, MCV 72. Most likely explanation?

- A. Excellent control
- B. Metformin initiation
- C. Spuriously low HbA1c due to abnormal RBC turnover
- D. Lab error
- E. Non-diabetic range

**Answer: C.** When HbA1c doesn't match clinical reality, check FBC. Anaemia with high RBC turnover (e.g. ongoing bleeding) lowers HbA1c. Principle: HbA1c ≠ glucose when red cells are abnormal.

### Q: SBA — Unconscious 28-year-old T1DM, glucose 1.9 mmol/L, IV access established. Most appropriate immediate treatment?

- A. IM 1 mg glucagon
- B. Oral glucogel into the cheek
- C. IV 20% glucose
- D. Oral sandwich
- E. Await improvement after IV fluids

**Answer: C.** Unconscious + IV access = 20% glucose IV. Glucagon is the answer only when IV access is impossible.
