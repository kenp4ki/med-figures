## Territory map

### Q: What are the six complications of diabetes, and how are they split?

- **Microvascular (small vessels):** retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy
- **Macrovascular (large vessels, accelerated atherosclerosis):** cerebrovascular disease, ischaemic heart disease, peripheral vascular disease

📎 Figure L3.1 — Micro vs macro complication map.

### Q: 🟦 What is the overarching management paradigm?

- **Microvascular disease** is HbA1c-driven → control glucose below target
- **Macrovascular disease** is multifactorial → control BP, lipids, smoking, weight, glucose together
- Glucose-only management does not prevent CV disease in diabetes
- This paradigm shapes every SBA in this territory

## Drivers of complication risk

### Q: What HbA1c target reduces microvascular complication risk?

- **53 mmol/mol (< 7%)** — target from Skyler 1996 data
- Below this, microvascular risk is low
- Above, retinopathy / nephropathy / neuropathy / microalbuminuria all rise steeply

🟥 Don't confuse with the T2DM **diagnostic** cut-off of 48 mmol/mol (L2). Diagnosis ≠ target.

📎 Figure L3.2 — HbA1c–risk curve.

### Q: What else drives complication risk besides HbA1c? 🟨

- **Duration** of diabetes
- **Smoking** → endothelial dysfunction
- **Genetic factors** — some develop complications despite reasonable control
- **Hyperlipidaemia**
- **Hyperglycaemic memory** — early poor control → higher later risk even if HbA1c improves

### Q: 🟦 What is hyperglycaemic (metabolic) memory, and what does it imply?

- Damage from early poor control is not fully reversed by later good control
- DCCT/UKPDS: initial aggressive control gave benefits persisting decades after glucose differences disappeared
- Clinical implication: **early control matters most**

## Mechanism of damage

### Q: Briefly, how does hyperglycaemia damage vessels?

- **AGE-RAGE:** glucose reacts non-enzymatically with proteins/lipids → AGEs bind RAGE on endothelium → inflammation
- **Oxidative stress:** mitochondria overwhelmed → ROS → damage
- End result: endothelial damage → **leaky capillaries + ischaemia**

🟦 Every microvascular finding (exudates = leakage; cotton wool = ischaemia; microaneurysms = weak walls) traces back to these two endpoints.

📎 Figure L3.4 — AGE/RAGE pathway.

## Retinopathy — stages + treatment

### Q: Why is retinopathy screened annually?

- Main cause of visual loss in diabetes
- **Main cause of blindness in working-age adults** in the UK
- Early stages **asymptomatic** → patient won't report
- Annual retinal photography for all DM patients in UK

📎 Figure L3.5a — Normal retina (optic disc + macula).

### Q: Background retinopathy — what do you see, and what do you do?

- Microaneurysms ("dots"), blot haemorrhages, **hard exudates** (yellow/cheese-coloured lipid)
- Pathology = leaky capillaries
- **Management = observe, annual screening only**

📎 Figure L3.5b — Background retinopathy.

### Q: Pre-proliferative retinopathy — what do you see, and what do you do?

- **Cotton wool spots** (= soft exudates = nerve fibre layer infarcts = ischaemia)
- More haemorrhages
- **Management = early pan-retinal photocoagulation (PRP)** before new vessels form

📎 Figure L3.5c — Pre-proliferative.

### Q: Proliferative retinopathy — what do you see, and what do you do?

- **New vessels** — on disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
- Fragile → bleed → vitreous haemorrhage → retinal detachment
- **Management = PRP** + optimise HbA1c, stop smoking, lipids, BP < 130/80

📎 Figure L3.5d — Proliferative.

### Q: Diabetic maculopathy — what do you see, and what do you do?

- Hard exudates + oedema **near or on the macula**
- Same disease as background but threatens central vision
- **Management = intravitreal anti-VEGF injections** + grid photocoagulation

🟨 Treatment pairings: **PRP → proliferative**; **anti-VEGF → maculopathy**.

📎 Figure L3.5e — Maculopathy.

### Q: 🟥 Hard exudates vs cotton wool spots — the classic trap.

- **Hard exudates** — yellow, well-defined, **lipid** from leaky vessels → background
- **Cotton wool spots** ("soft exudates") — white, fluffy, ill-defined, **ischaemic** nerve fibre infarcts → pre-proliferative
- Don't swap

### Q: 🟦 Why does PRP work? The counter-intuitive logic.

- Ischaemic retina secretes VEGF → neovascularisation
- PRP ablates peripheral retina → reduces total VEGF drive
- Trade peripheral vision for central vision preservation
- Works by removing ischaemic tissue, not by killing new vessels directly

📎 Figure L3.5f — Post-PRP retina.

### Q: Mentimeter answer — which stage is observed, not treated?

- **Background retinopathy.** Only annual screening.
- Every other stage has a specific treatment.

## Nephropathy — surveillance + management

### Q: Why does diabetic nephropathy matter?

- Leading cause worldwide of CKD + ESRF
- Develops in **30–35%** of DM patients (T1 and T2)
- Marker of increased cardiovascular risk — not just renal

### Q: Risk factors for diabetic nephropathy.

| Modifiable | Non-modifiable |
|---|---|
| Hyperglycaemia | Duration (esp. T1 onset <20y) |
| Hypertension | Ethnicity (African American, Pima Indian, Mexican American) |
| Hyperlipidaemia / smoking / obesity | |
| **Retinopathy** | |

🟨 Retinopathy is a risk factor for nephropathy — they travel together.

### Q: How is nephropathy screened for?

- **Every DM patient: annual urine albumin-creatinine ratio (ACR)**
- **Microalbuminuria:** ACR **> 3 mg/mmol**
- **Proteinuria (macroalbuminuria):** ACR **> 30 mg/mmol**
- **Nephrotic range:** > 3000 mg/24hr

### Q: 🟥 What causes false positive urine ACR, and what do you do about it?

- UTI, fever, recent exercise, menstruation
- A single positive **must be repeated** before acting on it

### Q: What is the mechanism of diabetic nephropathy?

- Hyperglycaemia + hypertension → glomerular hypertension
- Hyperfiltration → proteinuria → glomerular + interstitial fibrosis → GFR decline → renal failure
- Multiple mechanisms: metabolic, inflammatory, fibrotic, haemodynamic

📎 Figure L3.6 — Nephropathy mechanism.

### Q: Why do ACEi / ARB work, and what's the special rule in diabetes?

- Dilate the efferent arteriole → ↓ glomerular pressure → slow progression
- Effect independent of systemic BP lowering
- **Start ACEi or ARB even if normotensive once microalbuminuria is present**
- Don't combine ACEi + ARB (no added benefit, more hyperkalaemia + AKI)

📎 Figure L3.7 — RAS physiology.

### Q: Why is microalbuminuria a cardiovascular risk marker, not just a renal one?

- Marker of **systemic endothelial dysfunction**
- Predicts ischaemic stroke, MI, all-cause mortality in T1 and T2
- Finding microalbuminuria → address CV risk factors (lipids, BP, smoking)

📎 Figure L3.8 — Microalbuminuria predicts CV events.

### Q: Management bundle for diabetic nephropathy. 🟨

- HbA1c **< 53 mmol/mol**
- **ACEi or ARB** even if normotensive
- BP **< 130/80 mmHg** (usually via ACEi/ARB)
- Stop smoking
- **SGLT2 inhibitor if T2DM** — reduces CKD progression
- See also: **L2 — Q: SGLT2 inhibitors — what do they do?**

### Q: What is the earliest feature of diabetic nephropathy?

- **Microalbuminuria** (ACR > 3 mg/mmol)
- Creatinine rises and eGFR falls later, after significant damage

## Neuropathy + diabetic foot

### Q: Why does diabetic neuropathy happen, and what are the risk factors?

- Small vessels supplying nerves = **vasa nervorum** → blocked by microvascular disease → nerve ischaemia
- DM is the most common cause of neuropathy and of non-traumatic lower-limb amputation
- Risk factors: age, duration, poor control, **height (longer nerves)**, smoking, presence of retinopathy

### Q: Three patterns of diabetic neuropathy.

- **Peripheral sensory** — glove-and-stocking, longest nerves first (feet earliest)
- **Mononeuropathy** — single nerve ischaemia (wrist drop, foot drop, cranial palsy)
- **Autonomic** — GI, CV, bladder

### Q: Describe the peripheral sensory pattern and its danger.

- **Glove-and-stocking** distribution — feet first
- Can be painful (burning, shooting, allodynia)
- **Clinical danger: loss of protective sensation** — patient doesn't feel injury → ulcer → amputation

### Q: Describe diabetic CN III palsy.

- Sudden-onset double vision + ptosis
- **Eye deviated "down and out"** (unopposed lateral rectus + superior oblique)
- **Pupil-sparing** classically — ischaemic infarct of core somatic fibres spares superficial parasympathetic fibres

🟥 Pupil-involving CN III palsy = think compressive (posterior communicating aneurysm, tumour) until proven otherwise.

📎 Figure L3.9 — CN III palsy + distribution.

### Q: Features of autonomic neuropathy.

- **GI: gastroparesis** (nausea, vomiting, delayed emptying → makes prandial insulin timing tricky); constipation / nocturnal diarrhoea
- **CV: postural hypotension** (disabling); cardiac autonomic denervation → **sudden cardiac death**
- Bladder: retention, incontinence

🟨 Gastroparesis connects back to L1: if food doesn't leave the stomach, injected short-acting insulin acts before food is absorbed → hypoglycaemia.

### Q: What is the annual diabetic foot check?

- **Inspect** for deformity, ulceration, pressure points
- **Sensation** — 10-g monofilament + ankle jerks
- **Pulses** — **dorsalis pedis + posterior tibial**

📎 Figure L3.10 — Foot exam + neuropathic ulcer.

### Q: 🟦 Why is foot ulceration a "dual hit" problem?

- **Neuropathy** → can't feel injury
- **Peripheral vascular disease** → can't heal injury
- Patients with both are highest risk; annual foot check assesses both

### Q: 🟥 Neuropathic vs ischaemic ulcer — distinguishing features.

- **Neuropathic ulcer:** warm, well-perfused, insensate, at pressure points (plantar surface of great toe / metatarsal heads). Pulses present
- **Ischaemic ulcer:** cold, pulseless, painful, at foot edges or toes. Distinct management (revascularisation)

### Q: Management of peripheral neuropathy — no ulcer vs with ulcer.

- **Neuropathy alone:** regular self-inspection, good footwear, no barefoot walking, podiatry prn
- **With ulceration:** **multidisciplinary diabetic foot clinic** → offloading, revascularisation if PVD, antibiotics if infected, orthotic footwear, amputation if all else fails

🟨 "Multidisciplinary diabetic foot clinic" is the correct referral pathway — not general orthopaedics/surgery.

## Macrovascular complications + CV risk

### Q: What are the three macrovascular complications, and what is their shared pathology?

- **Cerebrovascular disease** — stroke, TIA
- **Ischaemic heart disease** — MI, angina, heart failure
- **Peripheral vascular disease** — claudication, rest pain, critical limb ischaemia
- All three = **accelerated atherosclerosis** — earlier, more extensive, more diffuse than non-diabetic

### Q: How does macrovascular prevention differ from microvascular prevention? 🟨

- Microvascular → HbA1c targeting (53 mmol/mol)
- Macrovascular → multifactorial (BP + lipids + smoking + weight + albuminuria + glucose)
- Glucose control alone has **minor** effect on CV risk

### Q: Numerical CV risk targets in T2DM — memorise.

- BP **< 130/80 mmHg** if microvascular complication or increased metabolic risk (often needs multiple agents)
- **Total cholesterol < 4 mmol/L**
- **LDL < 2 mmol/L**
- Annual microalbuminuria screen (itself a CV risk marker)

### Q: Why do diabetic complications need annual screening?

- Most start **asymptomatic** — patient won't report
- Retina, glomerulus, nerves all show changes before symptoms
- Annual cadence balances early detection with healthcare resource use
- See also: **L2 — Q: What is assessed at each annual T2DM review?**

## Cross-references

### Q: 🟦 Why is the HbA1c target **53** here, but the diagnostic threshold in L2 was **48**?

- 48 mmol/mol = diagnostic line for T2DM
- 53 mmol/mol = therapeutic target to reduce microvascular complications
- Same molecule, two different numbers, two different uses
- See also: **L1 — Q: How does HbA1c form?** and **L2 — Q: What are the diagnostic cut-offs for T2DM?**

### Q: 🟦 ACEi/ARB — same drug, very different indication in nephropathy.

- Normally used as antihypertensives
- In diabetic nephropathy: given **even in normotensive patients** once microalbuminuria present, because they dilate the efferent arteriole and reduce intra-glomerular pressure
- See also: **L2 — Q: Numerical CV targets in T2DM.**

### Q: 🟦 SGLT2i — glucose drug with a second job.

- In L2: glucose-lowering with CV/HF benefit
- Here: drug of choice (in T2DM) to slow CKD progression regardless of glucose
- Indication expanded beyond HbA1c
- See also: **L2 — Q: SGLT2 inhibitors — what do they do?**

### Q: 🟦 Gastroparesis affects insulin timing — link to L1.

- Autonomic neuropathy → delayed gastric emptying
- Short-acting prandial insulin peaks before food is absorbed → hypoglycaemia
- May be an indication for pump or changed timing
- See also: **L1 — Q: Why does hypoglycaemia awareness blunt over time?**

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA — Annual screening photo: yellow deposits around the temporal arcade + dots + small blot haemorrhages. Macula uninvolved. Stage + action?

- A. Proliferative — urgent PRP
- B. Maculopathy — anti-VEGF
- C. Background — continue annual screening
- D. Pre-proliferative — PRP
- E. Normal retina

**Answer: C.** Hard exudates + microaneurysms + blot haemorrhages = background. No cotton wool, no new vessels, macula uninvolved. Observe.

### Q: SBA — 62-year-old with T1DM: fluffy white patches in retinal periphery + scattered haemorrhages. No new vessels. Next step?

- A. Continue annual screening
- B. Pan-retinal photocoagulation
- C. Intravitreal anti-VEGF
- D. Grid photocoagulation
- E. Vitrectomy

**Answer: B.** Cotton wool spots = pre-proliferative. Early PRP prevents progression.

### Q: SBA — 48-year-old woman with T2DM. ACR 8 mg/mmol, BP 124/78, eGFR 78, on metformin. Next?

- A. No action
- B. Repeat urine ACR to confirm
- C. Start ACEi immediately
- D. Refer to nephrology
- E. Arrange renal biopsy

**Answer: B.** False positives common (UTI, fever, exercise). Confirm before acting.

### Q: SBA — Earliest feature of diabetic nephropathy?

- A. Raised serum creatinine
- B. Microalbuminuria
- C. Proteinuria
- D. Reduced eGFR
- E. Haematuria

**Answer: B.** Creatinine and eGFR change later. Haematuria suggests alternative glomerular pathology.

### Q: SBA — 65-year-old with T2DM. Sudden double vision. Right eye down and lateral; right pupil equal and reactive. Diagnosis?

- A. PCom artery aneurysm
- B. Diabetic (ischaemic) CN III palsy
- C. Myasthenia gravis
- D. Thyroid eye disease
- E. Horner's syndrome

**Answer: B.** Down-and-out + pupil-sparing = diabetic ischaemic CN III. Compressive causes typically involve the pupil.

### Q: SBA — 70-year-old with T2DM. Painless plantar ulcer on great toe. Warm foot, palpable pulses. Monofilament lost. Primary mechanism?

- A. Peripheral vascular disease
- B. Peripheral sensory neuropathy
- C. Charcot arthropathy
- D. Osteomyelitis
- E. Venous insufficiency

**Answer: B.** Warm + pulses = perfused. Painless + monofilament loss = sensory neuropathy. Neuropathic ulcer.

### Q: SBA — 58-year-old man with T2DM, HbA1c 52, BP 142/88, TC 4.8, LDL 2.4, ACR 5 (confirmed), BMI 31, non-smoker. Single most impactful CV risk intervention?

- A. Lower HbA1c target to < 48
- B. Intensify BP control and start a statin
- C. Start aspirin
- D. Bariatric surgery
- E. Repeat urine ACR

**Answer: B.** Glucose at target. Uncontrolled levers: BP (>130/80 with microalbuminuria) and lipids (TC>4, LDL>2). Statin indicated at his age with T2DM. Aspirin only for secondary prevention.

### Q: SBA — Which stage of diabetic retinopathy can be observed rather than treated?

- A. Background
- B. Pre-proliferative
- C. Proliferative
- D. Maculopathy
- E. Vitreous haemorrhage

**Answer: A.** Only background is observed. All other stages have specific interventions (PRP, anti-VEGF, grid photocoagulation, vitrectomy).

### Q: SBA — T2DM with microalbuminuria. BP 128/76. What drug class is indicated?

- A. None — BP already at target
- B. ACEi or ARB regardless of BP
- C. Calcium channel blocker
- D. Thiazide diuretic
- E. Beta-blocker

**Answer: B.** Reno-protective by reducing glomerular pressure — indicated even when normotensive once microalbuminuria present.

### Q: SBA — 72-year-old with T2DM and stage 3 CKD (eGFR 40). ACR 45 mg/mmol. On metformin + empagliflozin + ramipril. HbA1c 54. Additional step to slow CKD progression?

- A. Stop empagliflozin
- B. Add amlodipine
- C. Add an ARB
- D. Optimise existing therapy and monitor — no new drug
- E. Refer for dialysis planning

**Answer: D.** Patient already on metformin (still OK at eGFR 40 with caution), SGLT2i (reno-protective in T2DM), and ACEi (gold standard for proteinuria). Don't combine ACEi + ARB. Stopping SGLT2i would remove renal protection. Focus on optimisation and surveillance.
