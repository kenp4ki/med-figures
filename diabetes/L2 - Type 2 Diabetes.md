## Definition

### Q: What is T2DM in one sentence?

- Combination of **insulin resistance + β-cell failure** → hyperglycaemia
- **Associated with obesity but not always**
- May be managed initially with diet/weight loss; **may be reversible** early on
- With time: glucose-lowering therapy ± insulin needed

🟥 Don't reject T2DM because a patient is lean. Don't reject lifestyle as the "best next step" in early T2DM — DiRECT showed substantial remission with weight loss.

## Pathophysiology — the tug-of-war

### Q: 🟦 What is the single core idea driving the whole pathophysiology of T2DM?

- Tissues become resistant to insulin (they ignore the signal)
- β-cells compensate by making more insulin
- For years they win → glucose normal
- When they "fall off the hyperbolic curve" → glucose rises → T2DM diagnosis
- Every drug either reduces resistance or supports β-cell output

### Q: What happens in each tissue under insulin resistance?

- **Muscle:** reduced glucose uptake → less glycogen, less oxidation
- **Liver:** insulin fails to suppress gluconeogenesis → **↑ hepatic glucose output**
- **Adipose:** lipolysis unopposed → **↑ NEFA flux** → NEFAs worsen resistance everywhere (vicious loop)

🟨 Fasting hyperglycaemia in T2DM is driven by hepatic glucose production, not diet. This is why metformin (↓ HGO) is first-line.

📎 Figure L2.13 — Organ-level consequences of insulin resistance.

### Q: What is the secretion-sensitivity hyperbola, and what does "falling off" it mean?

- Hyperbolic relationship: as sensitivity ↓, compensatory secretion ↑
- Healthy people sit on the curve
- People developing T2DM **secrete less than expected** for their sensitivity — off the curve
- Implies T2DM = resistance **plus** a secretion defect

📎 Figure L2.12 — Secretion-sensitivity curve.

### Q: What change in insulin release is detectable early in T2DM?

- **Loss of first-phase insulin release** on IV glucose challenge
- Normal = rapid spike then sustained second phase
- T2DM = blunted, delayed, low-grade response only
- Explains exaggerated post-prandial glucose spikes

📎 Figure L2.9 — First-phase loss.

### Q: At the point of T2DM diagnosis, what is β-cell function?

- **~50% of normal** (Holman UKPDS)
- Has been declining for ~10 years before diagnosis
- Continues to decline on treatment

🟦 T2DM is not a sudden disease — it has a silent decade. Half the pancreas is already lost at diagnosis.

📎 Figure L2.7 — β-cell function at diagnosis.

### Q: Why does T2DM typically not cause ketosis?

- Residual insulin at the hepatocyte **partly brakes** ketogenesis
- T1 (absolute deficiency): no brake → ketogenesis ON → DKA
- T2 (relative deficiency): partial brake → ketogenesis usually OFF
- See also: **L1 — Q: Which two hormonal changes flip on hepatic ketogenesis?**

🟥 "Usually off" is not "never". Long-duration T2DM, severe illness, or SGLT2i use can still trigger DKA.

## Genetics

### Q: Contrast monogenic vs polygenic diabetes.

- **Monogenic** — single high-penetrance gene mutation (e.g. MODY). "Born with it". Autosomal dominant, strong family history
- **Polygenic** — many small-effect polymorphisms. "High risk but not born with it". Gene × environment determines who develops it
- Common T2DM = polygenic

### Q: Name three examinable MODY subtypes and their key features.

- **GCK-MODY (MODY 2):** mild stable hyperglycaemia from birth. No treatment. No complications
- **HNF1A-MODY (MODY 3):** progressive hyperglycaemia. **Sulfonylurea-responsive** at low doses
- **HNF4A-MODY (MODY 1):** like HNF1A-MODY + macrosomia / neonatal hypoglycaemia

🟨 A lifelong "mildly raised HbA1c" with strong multigenerational family history of "mild diabetes" = consider GCK-MODY, not T2DM.

### Q: How does gene × environment interaction play out in T2DM?

- High genes + strong environment → highest risk
- Low genes + strong environment → moderate
- High genes + weak environment → moderate
- Low genes + weak environment → lowest
- Environmental arm is **modifiable**

## Epidemiology

### Q: Three key epidemiological facts about T2DM.

- ~**90–95% of all diabetes** is T2
- Global rise: 135 million (1995) → 366 million projected (2030)
- Highest prevalence in Southeast Asia, Western Pacific; urbanisation accelerates risk

### Q: Why does early-onset T2DM matter?

- Worst survival deficit of any T2DM subgroup (Sattar 2019)
- Patient diagnosed at 25 loses more life-years than one diagnosed at 75
- Justifies aggressive early treatment in younger patients

## Diagnosis

### Q: What are the diagnostic cut-offs for T2DM? 🟨

- **Fasting plasma glucose ≥ 7 mmol/L**
- **2-hr OGTT ≥ 11.1 mmol/L**
- **HbA1c ≥ 48 mmol/mol**
- **Random glucose ≥ 11.1 mmol/L + symptoms** (one test, no repeat needed)

📎 Figure L2.5 — Diagnostic thresholds.

### Q: 🟦 Repeat-testing rule for diagnosis.

- **Asymptomatic** → need two abnormal results (same test twice, or two different tests)
- **Symptomatic** → one random glucose ≥ 11.1 is enough
- Don't over-commit on a single asymptomatic borderline result

### Q: Define the intermediate "pre-diabetes" ranges.

- **IFG** (impaired fasting glycaemia): fasting 6.1–6.9 mmol/L
- **IGT** (impaired glucose tolerance): 2-hr OGTT 7.8–11.0 mmol/L
- **Pre-diabetic HbA1c:** 42–47 mmol/mol

Progression to T2DM at ~5–10%/year — window for lifestyle intervention.

### Q: 🟥 When should HbA1c NOT be used for diagnosis?

- Pregnancy, children
- Suspected T1DM (rapid onset)
- Haemoglobinopathies
- Recent blood loss / transfusion
- Haemolytic anaemia, CKD on EPO
- Use fasting glucose or OGTT instead
- See also: **L1 — Q: List conditions that lower HbA1c spuriously.**

## Presentation

### Q: What are the three ways T2DM presents?

- **Asymptomatic** — most common modern presentation; picked up on routine HbA1c
- **Classical hyperglycaemic symptoms** — milder than T1 (polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue, blurred vision, thrush)
- **Presenting with a complication** — MI, stroke, neuropathy, foot ulcer, ED, new proteinuria

🟥 ~30% of T2DM patients have a complication at diagnosis — silent decade means damage has already started.

### Q: What skin sign suggests insulin resistance and why does it matter?

- **Acanthosis nigricans** — velvety hyperpigmented skin in folds (neck, axillae)
- Marker of insulin resistance (not diabetes per se)
- Common in obese adolescents → screen for T2DM

## Treatment principles

### Q: What are the four pillars of T2DM management?

- Lifestyle (diet, weight, exercise)
- Glucose-lowering therapy
- CV risk management (BP, lipids, smoking, weight)
- Complication surveillance (annual review)

🟦 An SBA asking "best next step" for a new T2DM patient is rarely *just* a glucose drug — it's lifestyle + a drug matched to the patient's profile.

### Q: What lifestyle changes are first-line, and what's DESMOND?

- Mediterranean / low-GI diet
- 5–10% weight loss → reliably improves glycaemia; 15%+ → possible **remission** (DiRECT)
- 150 min/week moderate exercise
- **DESMOND** = Diabetes Education and Self-Management for Ongoing and Newly Diagnosed

## Drug ladder — by class

### Q: Metformin — mechanism and key points.

- **Biguanide** — ↓ hepatic glucose output, ↑ peripheral insulin sensitivity
- **No hypoglycaemia** (doesn't force insulin release)
- Weight-neutral
- Side effects: GI (nausea, diarrhoea), B12 deficiency, rare lactic acidosis
- **Contraindicated eGFR < 30**; caution 30–45

### Q: SGLT2 inhibitors (-gliflozins) — what do they do and what's unique?

- Block SGLT2 in proximal tubule → glucosuria → ↓ plasma glucose
- Examples: dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, canagliflozin
- **CV protective** + **renal protective** + **HF benefit** — this is the unique selling point
- Weight loss (calories peed out)
- SE: UTIs, genital thrush, euglycaemic DKA (rare), volume depletion

### Q: GLP-1 receptor agonists (-tides) — what do they do?

- Mimic incretin → ↑ glucose-dependent insulin secretion, ↓ glucagon, ↓ gastric emptying, ↑ satiety
- Examples: liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide, exenatide
- **Substantial weight loss** + **CV benefit**
- SE: nausea, vomiting, pancreatitis (rare)

### Q: Sulfonylureas (-ides) — what do they do and what's the main downside?

- Close K-ATP channel in β-cell → forced insulin release (glucose-independent)
- Examples: gliclazide, glimepiride, glipizide
- **High hypoglycaemia risk** + weight gain
- Main niche: **HNF1A-MODY** (exquisitely responsive)

### Q: Pioglitazone — mechanism and critical contraindication.

- **PPARγ agonist** → ↑ insulin sensitivity
- Weight gain
- Fluid retention → **contraindicated in heart failure**
- SE: fractures (women), modest bladder cancer signal

### Q: DPP-4 inhibitors (-gliptins) — how do they compare to GLP-1 agonists?

- Block dipeptidyl peptidase-4 → prolong endogenous GLP-1
- Weight-neutral, oral, low hypo risk
- **No CV or renal benefit signal**
- 🟦 Both work on incretin axis, but DPP-4i prolongs native GLP-1 (small effect) while GLP-1a supplies pharmacological doses (big effect, weight loss)

### Q: Drug-ladder comparison — one-table view.

| Class | Hypo risk | Weight | CV benefit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Metformin | None | Neutral | Modest |
| SGLT2i | Low | **Loss** | **Yes** |
| GLP-1a | Low | **Loss** | **Yes** |
| Sulfonylurea | **High** | **Gain** | — |

(DPP-4i, pioglitazone, insulin covered in their own toggles.)

### Q: 🟦 Which drug for which patient — applied algorithm.

- Obese + established CVD / HF / CKD → **SGLT2i** ± **GLP-1a** after metformin
- Elderly, falls, lives alone → **avoid sulfonylurea + insulin** (hypo risk)
- HF patient → **avoid pioglitazone**
- eGFR < 30 → **avoid metformin**; SGLT2i to eGFR ~20
- HNF1A-MODY → **sulfonylurea** first-line

### Q: 🟥 Insulin in long-duration T2DM — what must you not do?

- Do **not** stop abruptly — β-cell reserve may be exhausted, risking DKA
- Residual-insulin brake on ketogenesis fails when β-cells are near-gone

## Cardiovascular risk management

### Q: Why does glucose control alone barely prevent macrovascular disease in T2DM?

- Macrovascular disease is multifactorial — glucose is only one driver
- BP, lipids, smoking, weight, albuminuria all contribute
- Glucose-only management minimally reduces MI / stroke / PVD risk
- Prevention requires simultaneous attack on multiple risk factors
- See also: **L3 — Q: How does macrovascular prevention differ from microvascular prevention?**

### Q: 🟨 Numerical CV targets in T2DM with microvascular complications.

- **BP < 130/80 mmHg** (if microvascular complication or increased metabolic risk)
- **Total cholesterol < 4 mmol/L**
- **LDL < 2 mmol/L**
- Smoking: cessation support
- Weight: lifestyle ± pharmacological ± bariatric

### Q: What's the rule on aspirin in T2DM?

- **Secondary prevention only** (established CVD)
- **Not for routine primary prevention** — bleeding risk outweighs benefit

## Acute complication — HHS

### Q: What are the diagnostic features of hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (HHS)?

- **Glucose > 30 mmol/L** (often markedly higher)
- **Osmolality > 320 mOsm/kg**
- **pH > 7.3** (no significant acidosis)
- **Ketones minimal or absent**
- Slow onset (days–weeks), elderly T2DM, often with precipitating infection
- Mortality ~15–20% — higher than DKA

### Q: DKA vs HHS — the one-line contrast.

- **DKA** = acidosis + ketones (absolute deficiency, sudden, usually younger)
- **HHS** = severe osmolality + dehydration (relative deficiency preserved, slow, usually elderly)
- Same underlying story (insulin deficiency), different balance of consequences
- See also: **L1 — Q: State the four diagnostic criteria for DKA.**

### Q: 🟥 How does HHS management differ from DKA management?

- **Fluids given more slowly** — aggressive resuscitation risks cerebral / pulmonary oedema in frail patients
- **Insulin given lower and later** — glucose often falls with fluids alone; start low
- **VTE prophylaxis essential** — hyperviscosity → thrombotic risk
- Replace electrolytes (K⁺); treat the precipitant

## Annual review

### Q: What is assessed at each annual T2DM review?

- HbA1c, BP, lipid panel, weight, smoking status
- **Urine ACR** (microalbuminuria screen)
- eGFR / creatinine
- **Foot exam** — inspection, 10-g monofilament, dorsalis pedis + posterior tibial pulses
- **Retinal photography**
- Medication review

🟦 Every item maps to a specific complication that is screened for early because symptoms come late.

- See also: **L3 — Q: Why do diabetic complications need annual screening?**

## Cross-references

### Q: 🟦 Classification overlap — how do LADA and MODY fit with T2DM?

- LADA = autoimmune diabetes in adults; looks like T2 but positive autoantibodies, progresses to insulin
- MODY = monogenic; strong family history, young age, often non-insulin requiring
- Both deviate from the T2 pattern despite adult/older presentation
- See also: **L1 — Q: Name four real-life deviations from the textbook T1/T2 dichotomy.**

### Q: 🟦 HbA1c — diagnostic threshold here, therapeutic target in L3.

- **L2 (here):** HbA1c ≥ 48 mmol/mol = diagnostic
- **L3:** HbA1c ≤ 53 mmol/mol = target to reduce microvascular complications
- Same molecule, two different numbers, two different uses — don't swap
- See also: **L1 — Q: How does HbA1c form?** and **L3 — Q: What HbA1c target reduces microvascular complication risk?**

### Q: 🟦 SGLT2i has a second job beyond glucose lowering.

- Here: a glucose-lowering drug with CV/HF/renal benefit
- In L3: the drug of choice (in T2DM) to slow CKD progression in diabetic nephropathy — regardless of glucose
- Indication expanded based on endpoint, not HbA1c
- See also: **L3 — Q: Management bundle for diabetic nephropathy.**

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA — Asymptomatic 52-year-old man, HbA1c 50 mmol/mol. Next step?

- A. Diagnose T2DM and start metformin
- B. Repeat HbA1c to confirm diagnosis
- C. Arrange OGTT
- D. Reassure — within normal range
- E. Start lifestyle without labelling as T2DM

**Answer: B.** Asymptomatic → two abnormal tests needed. 50 mmol/mol is above diagnostic threshold but needs confirmation.

### Q: SBA — 68-year-old woman, CKD + anaemia (Hb 88), on EPO. Evaluating for possible diabetes. Best diagnostic test?

- A. HbA1c
- B. Random glucose
- C. Fasting plasma glucose or OGTT
- D. C-peptide
- E. Glycated albumin

**Answer: C.** EPO + anaemia + CKD invalidates HbA1c. Direct glucose measurement preferred.

### Q: SBA — 47-year-old obese woman. OGTT: fasting 6.3, 2-hr 9.2. Correct classification?

- A. T2DM
- B. Impaired glucose tolerance
- C. IFG only
- D. Normal
- E. Reactive hypoglycaemia

**Answer: B.** 2-hr 9.2 is within IGT range (7.8–11.0). Fasting 6.3 is within IFG range. The higher-yield label here is IGT.

### Q: SBA — 58-year-old with non-healing plantar ulcer, absent ankle jerks, random glucose 14.2. Diagnosis?

- A. PVD
- B. Undiagnosed T2DM presenting with neuropathy + foot ulceration
- C. T1DM
- D. Charcot neuroarthropathy
- E. Vasculitic ulcer

**Answer: B.** Classic "presents with a complication". Random glucose ≥ 11.1 with symptoms (neuropathy + ulcer) diagnoses diabetes on a single test.

### Q: SBA — Pre-diabetic with BMI 34. Which intervention prevents progression best?

- A. Low-carb diet
- B. Prophylactic metformin
- C. Weight loss ≥5–7% via diet + exercise
- D. SGLT2 inhibitor
- E. GLP-1 agonist

**Answer: C.** DPP / Finnish trials: ~58% reduction in progression vs ~31% with metformin. Lifestyle wins in pre-diabetes.

### Q: SBA — 62-year-old woman with T2DM (HbA1c 69 on metformin) + HFrEF. Best second-line agent?

- A. Gliclazide
- B. Pioglitazone
- C. Empagliflozin
- D. Sitagliptin
- E. Insulin glargine

**Answer: C.** SGLT2i has proven HF benefit (reduces HF hospitalisation + CV death). Pioglitazone worsens HF (fluid retention). Gliclazide + insulin risk hypoglycaemia without CV benefit.

### Q: SBA — 74-year-old, 1-week confusion after chest infection. Glucose 48 mmol/L, Na⁺ 152, pH 7.34, HCO₃⁻ 21, urine ketones trace, osmolality 345. Diagnosis?

- A. DKA
- B. HHS
- C. Euglycaemic DKA
- D. Septic encephalopathy
- E. Mixed DKA/HHS

**Answer: B.** Glucose > 30 ✓, osmolality > 320 ✓, pH > 7.3 ✓, ketones trace ✓. Textbook HHS.

### Q: SBA — New T2DM, BMI 32, FH early MI, no established CVD, eGFR 82. First-line pharmacology alongside lifestyle?

- A. Gliclazide
- B. Metformin
- C. Empagliflozin
- D. Semaglutide
- E. Pioglitazone

**Answer: B.** Metformin remains first-line for most. SGLT2i / GLP-1a come in second-line (or first alongside metformin if established CVD/HF/CKD — which he doesn't have yet).

### Q: SBA — T2DM, microalbuminuria confirmed. BP 138/82, TC 4.8, LDL 2.6. On metformin. Next step?

- A. Reassure
- B. Intensify BP control and start a statin
- C. Start sulfonylurea
- D. Increase metformin
- E. Repeat ACR in 6 months

**Answer: B.** Microalbuminuria → BP target < 130/80. Lipids are above T2DM targets. Glucose is fine (HbA1c not mentioned). Right target, right intervention.
