## Framework — the balance model

### Q: What two "teams" are balanced in normal haemostasis, and what happens when each team weakens?

- **Pro-clotting team**: coagulant factors + platelets.
- **Anti-clotting team**: anticoagulant proteins + fibrinolytic factors.
- Pro-clotting weakened → **bleeding**.
- Anti-clotting weakened → **thrombosis**.
- Drugs deliberately weaken one team to treat the other.

### Q: What are the four sequential stages of haemostasis after vessel injury?

1. **Vessel constriction** (VSMC contract, limits flow).
2. **Primary haemostasis** — unstable platelet plug (adhesion + aggregation).
3. **Secondary haemostasis** — fibrin stabilisation via coagulation cascade.
4. **Fibrinolysis** — clot dissolution and vessel repair.

### Q: 🟦 How does the bleeding pattern itself tell you which stage is broken?

| Stage broken | Bleeding pattern | Tests abnormal |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Mucocutaneous, **immediate** | Platelet count, PFA-100; PT/APTT usually **normal** |
| Secondary | Deep (joints, muscles), **delayed**, restarts | PT and/or APTT prolonged |
| Vessel wall | Easy bruising, normal labs | All normal |

## Part 1 — Primary haemostasis

### Q: What are the three "players" needed to form a platelet plug?

- **Wall** — exposed collagen at injury site.
- **Bridge** — von Willebrand factor (VWF).
- **Pilers-up** — platelets (number + function).

Disease categories write themselves: platelet problems, VWF problems, vessel wall problems.

### Q: Name the three platelet glycoprotein receptors and what each binds.

- **GpIb** — binds VWF (adhesion, indirect route, dominant at high shear).
- **GpIa** — binds collagen directly (adhesion).
- **GpIIb/IIIa** — binds fibrinogen + Ca²⁺ to bridge platelet ↔ platelet (aggregation; irreversible).

📎 Figure L1.1 — Platelet adhesion and aggregation pathways

### Q: What two substances are released from activated platelets to recruit more platelets?

- **ADP** (from dense granules) — activates P2Y12 on other platelets.
- **Thromboxane A₂** — made by platelet COX from arachidonic acid.

🟨 These are the two targets of antiplatelet drugs: clopidogrel blocks P2Y12; aspirin blocks COX.

---

### Q: What are the three mechanisms of thrombocytopenia and an example of each?

- **Not made** (marrow failure): leukaemia, B12 deficiency, chemotherapy.
- **Destroyed** (accelerated clearance): ITP, DIC.
- **Pooled/destroyed in spleen**: hypersplenism.

### Q: What is ITP and how is it treated?

- Autoimmune: antiplatelet autoantibodies coat platelets → macrophages in spleen eat them.
- Marrow still produces normally; destruction outpaces production.
- First-line: **prednisolone** (immunosuppression).
- Second-line: **splenectomy** (removes destruction site).

🟨 ITP is a very common cause of thrombocytopenia — "isolated low platelets, well patient, bruising/petechiae" → think ITP.

### Q: Platelet count thresholds — what bleeding risk at each level?

- Normal: **140–400 × 10⁹/L**.
- **<100**: no spontaneous bleeding, bleeds with trauma.
- **<40**: spontaneous bleeding common.
- **<10**: severe spontaneous bleeding — danger zone (cerebral bleeds).

|---|---|---|
| **Glanzmann's thrombasthenia** | GpIIb/IIIa | Aggregation — severe bleeding |
| **Bernard–Soulier syndrome** | GpIb | Adhesion — less severe |
| **Storage pool disease** | Dense granules | Activation (no ADP release) |

📎 Figure L1.2 — Platelet anatomy and hereditary platelet defects

### Q: How does aspirin work on platelets, and why does the effect last 7–10 days?

- Irreversibly blocks **COX** in platelets → no thromboxane A₂ → no aggregation signal.
- 🟨 Platelets have **no nucleus** → cannot resynthesise COX → effect lasts the **7–10 day platelet lifespan**.
- Endothelial prostacyclin recovers faster (endothelial cells do have nuclei).

📎 Figure L1.3 — Arachidonic acid pathway: aspirin's mechanism on COX

### Q: How does clopidogrel differ in mechanism from aspirin?

- **Clopidogrel**: irreversibly blocks the **P2Y12 ADP receptor** on platelets.
- **Aspirin**: irreversibly blocks **COX** → ↓ thromboxane A₂.
- Both are irreversible and last the platelet lifespan.

---

### Q: What are the two functions of VWF?

1. Bridges platelets to collagen (GpIb → VWF → collagen).
2. **Carrier and stabiliser for Factor VIII** in plasma.

The second function is the source of every VWD trap.

### Q: 🟥 Why is the coagulation screen usually NORMAL in VWD?

- APTT depends on FVIII, not directly on VWF.
- Most patients keep enough FVIII for a normal APTT despite low VWF.
- Only in **severe VWD** (Type 3) does FVIII fall enough to prolong APTT.
- Diagnostic test is a **VWF assay**, not a clotting screen.

🟥 Classic examiner trap. "Normal coag screen" does NOT exclude a bleeding disorder.

### Q: Name the VWD types and their defects.

- **Type 1** — partial quantitative ↓ (function normal). Mild, common.
- **Type 2** — abnormal function.
- **Type 3** — complete absence. Severe, autosomal recessive.

VWD is the commonest **inherited** bleeding disorder. Inheritance usually **autosomal**.

---

### Q: Name the inherited and acquired causes of vessel wall bleeding.

**Inherited (rare):**
- Hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT).
- Ehlers-Danlos and other connective tissue disorders.

**Acquired (common):**
- Steroid therapy.
- Age-related purpura (not "senile").
- Vasculitis.
- Scurvy (vitamin C deficiency).

🟦 If labs are all normal but there's bruising/purpura, suspect vessel wall.

---

### Q: List the typical features of primary haemostasis bleeding.

- **Immediate** onset after injury.
- Mucocutaneous: epistaxis >20 min, gum bleeding, menorrhagia.
- Prolonged bleeding from cuts.
- Easy/spontaneous bruising (ecchymosis).
- Petechiae (in thrombocytopenia only).
- Purpura — **do not blanch** with pressure.

📎 Figure L1.4 — Petechiae and purpura on skin
📎 Figure L1.5 — Clinical appearances (vasculitis, AML, ITP, senile purpura, ecchymosis, connective tissue)

### Q: Why do petechiae specifically suggest low platelet COUNT rather than platelet dysfunction?

- Petechiae are tiny capillary leaks that require sealing by individual platelets.
- Platelet dysfunction usually leaves enough numerical platelets to seal capillaries.
- Only **thrombocytopenia** depletes the count enough to let petechiae form.
- Purpura is broader — low count OR vascular.

### Q: What tests are used for disorders of primary haemostasis?

- **Platelet count** + morphology.
- **PFA-100** (in-lab bleeding time surrogate).
- **VWF antigen + activity assay**.
- **Coagulation screen** — mainly to exclude a coag disorder (usually normal here).

---

### Q: Match the treatment principle to the mechanism of primary haemostasis failure.

| Mechanism | Treatment | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Failure of production/function | Replace | VWF concentrate, platelet transfusion, stop aspirin |
| Immune destruction | Suppress | Prednisolone; splenectomy for ITP |
| Increased consumption | Treat cause + replace | Antibiotics in DIC + products |

### Q: What is DDAVP (desmopressin), and in whom does it work?

- **Vasopressin analogue**.
- Releases endogenous **VWF and FVIII** from endothelial Weibel-Palade bodies (**2–5× rise**).
- Only works in **mild** VWD and mild haemophilia A — severe disease has no stores to release.

🟨 Always a favourite SBA distractor — DDAVP will not help severe VWD (Type 3) or severe haemophilia.

### Q: What does tranexamic acid do, and when is it used?

- **Antifibrinolytic** — blocks plasminogen → plasmin conversion, so clots are not broken down.
- Particularly effective for **mucosal bleeding** (oral, GI, menorrhagia, post-partum).
- Used in haemophilia for dental extractions, trauma, heavy periods.

### The coagulation cascade skeleton

```
      │                                     │

                COMMON PATHWAY
                     │

                     │  + Va + Pl + Ca²⁺

```

📎 Figure L1.10 — Coagulation cascade with PT and APTT overlays

### Q: What is the single purpose of the entire coagulation cascade?

- To generate **thrombin (IIa)**.
- Thrombin converts soluble **fibrinogen** → insoluble **fibrin**.
- Every factor deficiency = less thrombin = clot falls apart.

### Q: Which factors does APTT measure vs PT?

- **APTT** — intrinsic + common: **XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, V, II, fibrinogen**.
- **PT** — extrinsic + common: **VII, X, V, II, fibrinogen**.
- Common pathway (X, V, II, fibrinogen) is shared.

🟦 Mnemonic: PT = "play **seven**s" (VII sits alone in the extrinsic pathway).

### Q: 🟦 How do you interpret any PT/APTT screening result?

| Pattern | Differential |
|---|---|
| ↑ APTT only | FXII, XI, IX, VIII; heparin |
| ↑ PT only | FVII deficiency; early liver disease; early warfarin |
| Both ↑ | Liver disease; warfarin; DIC; dilution; common-pathway defect |

Then check platelets + D-dimer + fibrinogen to discriminate.

---

### Q: What is haemophilia A vs B, and what is the inheritance?

- **Haemophilia A** = FVIII deficiency.
- **Haemophilia B** = FIX deficiency.
- Both **X-linked recessive** — males affected, females carriers.
- Incidence **~1 in 10⁴** live births.

### Q: What is the clinical hallmark of haemophilia, and why does it happen?

- **Haemarthrosis** (spontaneous joint bleeds).
- Platelets still work → superficial cuts seal immediately.
- But plug cannot be stabilised with fibrin → fails under joint/muscle stress → delayed deep bleed.
- Recurrent joint bleeds → target joint → chronic arthropathy.

📎 Figure L1.7 — Acute haemarthrosis (swollen knee)
📎 Figure L1.8 — Chronic haemarthrosis with muscle wasting

### Q: 🟥 What one procedure is absolutely contraindicated in haemophilia, and why?

- **Intramuscular injections**.
- Muscle bleed can be massive, compress nerves/vessels, and be fatal.
- Lecturer flagged explicitly: "note number two, for many years to come."

📎 Figure L1.9 — Haematoma from IM injection in haemophilia patient

### Q: Coagulation factor deficiencies are NOT all the same. Summarise by factor.

| Factor deficient | Phenotype |
|---|---|
| **II (prothrombin)** | Lethal — not survivable |
| **VIII / IX** (haemophilia) | Severe spontaneous joint/muscle bleeds |
| **XI** | Bleeds after trauma, not spontaneously |
| **XII** | No bleeding; APTT prolonged |

🟥 FXII: prolonged APTT but patient doesn't bleed. Don't delay surgery — confirm with factor assay.

---

### Q: What are the four acquired causes of a coagulation disorder, commonest in hospital?

- **Liver disease** — ↓ production of most factors (VII falls first; VIII preserved).
- **Anticoagulant drugs** — warfarin, heparin, DOACs.
- **Dilution** — massive RBC transfusion (RBCs no longer contain plasma).
- **DIC** — increased consumption.

### Q: What is DIC, and what triggers it?

- **Widespread inappropriate tissue factor expression** → systemic coagulation activation.
- Factors and platelets consumed faster than replaced.
- Fibrin deposited in small vessels → organ failure; fibrinolysis activates → D-dimer ↑↑.
- Triggers: **sepsis** (commonest), trauma/burns, malignancy, obstetric emergencies.

### Q: What does the DIC lab picture look like?

- Platelets ↓.
- PT and APTT ↑ (both).
- Fibrinogen ↓.
- D-dimer ↑↑.

🟥 DIC paradox: patient **bleeds AND clots at the same time**. Replacement alone feeds the fire — must treat the underlying cause.

---

### Q: What does each blood product contain, and when is each used?

| Product | Contains | Key use |
|---|---|---|
| **FFP** | All coagulation factors | Liver disease, warfarin (if no PCC), dilution |
| **Cryoprecipitate** | Fibrinogen, FVIII, VWF, **FXIII** | Low fibrinogen (DIC, massive haemorrhage) |
| **PCC** | **II, VII, IX, X** | Emergency warfarin reversal |
| Factor concentrates | Single factor | Haemophilia (FVIII, FIX) |

🟥 Factor concentrates exist for **every factor except Factor V** — use FFP to replace V.

### Q: What is emicizumab and how does it work?

- **Bispecific monoclonal antibody** — binds FIXa and FX simultaneously.
- **Mimics Factor VIII** by bringing FIXa and FX together.
- Subcutaneous, weekly.
- Used in haemophilia A (transformed care in last 5 years).

📎 Figure L1.11 — Emicizumab mechanism and RNA silencing

### Q: 🟦 How do you distinguish clinically between platelet and coagulation bleeding?

| Feature | Platelet/vascular | Coagulation |
|---|---|---|
| Site | Superficial, mucocutaneous | Deep — joints, muscles |
| Onset | Immediate | Delayed, often restarts |
| Superficial cuts | Bleed prolonged | Don't bleed at all |
| Petechiae | Yes (if low count) | No |

## Part 3 — Thrombosis

### Q: 🟦 What is Virchow's triad and which limb dominates where?

- **Blood** (hypercoagulability) — dominant in **venous** thrombosis.
- **Vessel wall** — dominant in **arterial** thrombosis.
- **Blood flow** (stasis) — contributes to both.

🟦 Arterial clots are platelet-rich (→ aspirin). Venous clots are fibrin-rich (→ anticoagulants).

### Q: Name the three natural anticoagulant proteins and what each inactivates.

- **Antithrombin** — inhibits thrombin (IIa) **and** Xa.
- **Protein C** (activated by thrombin-thrombomodulin) — inactivates **Va and VIIIa**.
- **Protein S** — cofactor for Protein C.

📎 Figure L1.13 — Natural anticoagulant proteins

### Q: Rank the inherited thrombophilias by risk (odds ratio) and frequency.

| Defect | Frequency | Odds ratio |
|---|---|---|
| **Antithrombin deficiency** | 1–2% | **25–50** (highest) |
| Protein C deficiency | 2–4% | 10–20 |
| Protein S deficiency | 2–4% | 10–20 |
| Factor V Leiden | ~5–7% | 6.6 |

🟨 Pattern: severe deficiencies are **rare but potent**; FV Leiden is **common but mild**.

📎 Figure L1.14 — Martinelli survival curves by thrombophilia

### Q: What is the Factor V Leiden mechanism, and why does it often need a second hit?

- Point mutation at the activated Protein C cleavage site on FV → FVa resistant to inactivation.
- Loss of the natural "off switch" for FV → mild pro-coagulant state.
- Most heterozygotes **never clot** — usually requires a trigger (OCP, pregnancy, surgery, flight).

🟥 FV Leiden + combined OCP is multiplicative — classic SBA synergy.

### Q: What background age-related absolute VTE risk should you quote?

- Age 20: **1/10,000 per year**.
- Age 60: **1/1,000 per year** — a 10× rise from age alone.

📎 Figure L1.15 — VTE rate ratio by age (Stein 2004)

---

### Q: When should you consider inherited thrombophilia screening?

- Thrombosis at **young age** (<45).
- **Spontaneous** (unprovoked) thrombosis.
- **Multiple / recurrent** thromboses.
- **Unusual sites** (cerebral, splanchnic).
- Thrombosis **despite** anticoagulation.
- Strong family history.

🟦 Don't screen provoked clots in older patients — it won't change management.

### Q: What are the features of a PE and which is the easiest to miss?

- Tachycardia — often earliest, sometimes only sign.
- Hypoxia, sudden dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain.
- Haemoptysis, sudden death in massive PE.

🟥 Young patient with **unexplained tachycardia** (especially post-op, pregnant, on OCP) = consider PE.

📎 Figure L1.12 — Thrombosis presentations (PE, DVT, MI, stroke)

### Q: 🟥 Why don't anticoagulants dissolve existing clots?

- Anticoagulants prevent **propagation** and **embolism** of clot and formation of new clot.
- The body's fibrinolytic system dissolves the existing clot over weeks.
- Only **thrombolytics** (tPA) actively dissolve clots — used for stroke and massive PE, not routine DVT.

## Part 4 — Anticoagulants

### Anticoagulant target map (on the cascade)

```
Warfarin       ──▶ blocks SYNTHESIS of II, VII, IX, X + Protein C, S
                    (inhibits VKOR, stops vitamin K recycling)

UFH            ──▶ potentiates ANTITHROMBIN: inactivates IIa + Xa
LMWH           ──▶ potentiates ANTITHROMBIN: inactivates Xa mainly

"xabans"       ──▶ directly inhibit Xa
  (rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban)

Dabigatran     ──▶ directly inhibits IIa (thrombin)
```

📎 Figure L1.24 — DOAC targets on the cascade

### Q: What are the five main indications for anticoagulation?

**Therapeutic:**
- **Atrial fibrillation** — commonest indication (~1% population; embolic stroke prevention).
- **VTE** — initial and long-term (DVT, PE).
- **Mechanical prosthetic heart valve**.

**Preventative (thromboprophylaxis):**
- Hospital admission (medical or post-surgical).
- Pregnancy / high-risk obstetrics.

---

### Q: What does warfarin actually inhibit, and what proteins does that affect?

- Inhibits **VKOR** (vitamin K epoxide reductase) — stops recycling of vitamin K.
- Vitamin K is needed to **γ-carboxylate** proteins so they can bind Ca²⁺ and work.
- Affected: **II, VII, IX, X + Protein C + Protein S**.

🟨 Mnemonic: 1972 (factors 10, 9, 7, 2) — plus both protein C and S.

📎 Figure L1.17 — Vitamin K cycle and warfarin's target

### Q: 🟥 Why can warfarin cause skin necrosis in the first few days?

- Protein C has a **short half-life** — falls faster than Factor II.
- Procoagulants not yet depleted → transient **hypercoagulable state**.
- In pre-existing Protein C deficiency → microvascular thrombosis in subcutaneous fat.
- Classic sites: thighs, breasts, abdomen, 2–3 days after starting.
- Prevent by **bridging with heparin** for the first 5 days.

📎 Figure L1.18 — Factor depletion time course on warfarin
📎 Figure L1.19 — Warfarin skin necrosis and purple toe syndrome

### Q: What is the INR, and what is the usual target?

- INR = standardised PT, corrected for lab thromboplastin via the ISI.
- Normal unanticoagulated = **1.0**.
- Target INR **2–3** for most indications.
- Target **2.5–3.5** for mechanical mitral valves.

🟨 Above INR **4**, bleeding risk rises very steeply; at INR ≥7, major bleed rate ~100/100 pt-years.

📎 Figure L1.21 — Bleeding events per 100 patient-years vs INR

### Q: How do you reverse warfarin in a major bleed vs a high INR without bleeding?

- **Major bleed**: **IV vitamin K + PCC** (PCC works in minutes; replaces II, VII, IX, X).
- **INR 5–8, no bleeding**: omit 1–2 doses, reduce maintenance, recheck.
- **INR >8, no bleeding**: oral vitamin K; stop warfarin.
- **FFP** is second-line to PCC — slower, larger volume, less predictable.

🟥 PCC > FFP for emergency reversal. Vitamin K alone takes 4–6 hours to work.

### Q: Name the main side effects of warfarin.

- **Bleeding** — minor 5% pa, major 0.9–3.0% pa, fatal 0.25% pa.
- **Skin necrosis** (Protein C deficiency).
- **Purple toe syndrome** (cholesterol emboli from atheromatous plaques).
- **Teratogenicity** — chondrodysplasia punctata, 1st trimester (weeks 6–12 highest risk).

📎 Figure L1.20 — Chondrodysplasia punctata

### Q: Name four causes of apparent warfarin "resistance".

- **Non-compliance** (commonest) — check with PIVKA or warfarin levels.
- **High dietary vitamin K** — greens, cranberries.
- **↑ metabolism** — CYP2C9 polymorphism.
- **Reduced binding** — VKORC1 polymorphism.

---

### Q: How do UFH and LMWH both work, and why do they have different effects?

- Both potentiate **antithrombin** via a pentasaccharide sequence.
- **Inactivating Xa** needs pentasaccharide only → short chain sufficient.
- **Inactivating thrombin (IIa)** needs a long chain to bridge AT and thrombin.
- UFH has long chains → inactivates both IIa and Xa → prolongs APTT.
- LMWH has short chains → mainly Xa → minimal APTT effect.

📎 Figure L1.22 — UFH mechanism
📎 Figure L1.23 — LMWH mechanism

### Q: Compare UFH and LMWH on route, monitoring, and reversal.

| Feature | UFH | LMWH |
|---|---|---|
| Route | IV infusion | Subcutaneous |
| Monitoring | APTT (1.5–2.5×) | Usually none; anti-Xa if needed |
| Half-life | Short | Longer |
| Reversal | Protamine sulfate (full) | Protamine (partial only) |

🟨 UFH when you need **rapid on/off** (cardiac surgery, renal failure, high bleed risk). LMWH for routine prophylaxis and most treatment.

### Q: Name the four DOACs and their targets.

- **Rivaroxaban** — Xa.
- **Apixaban** — Xa.
- **Edoxaban** — Xa.
- **Dabigatran** — IIa (thrombin).

🟨 "**-xaban = Xa**; dabigatran is the odd one out (thrombin)."

### Q: Compare warfarin and DOACs.

| Feature | Warfarin | DOACs |
|---|---|---|
| Onset / offset | Slow | Rapid |
| Dosing | Variable (INR-titrated) | Fixed |
| Monitoring | INR needed | None routinely |
| Interactions | Many (food, drugs) | Few |

### Q: What are the specific antidotes for DOACs?

- **Dabigatran** → **idarucizumab** (monoclonal antibody; fully effective).
- **Xa inhibitors** → **andexanet alfa** (modified decoy Xa; partial, expensive).
- If antidote unavailable → PCC as a stopgap.

---

### Q: 🟦 Which anticoagulant for which patient? Decision ladder.

- **Pregnancy** → **LMWH** (warfarin teratogenic; DOACs cross placenta).
- **Mechanical prosthetic valve** → **Warfarin only** (DOAC trials failed).
- **Severe renal failure** → Warfarin or UFH (avoid DOAC/LMWH — renally excreted).
- **Cardiac surgery / CPB** → **UFH IV** (rapid on/off, protamine reversible).
- **Acute DVT/PE (stable, normal renal function)** → **DOAC** first-line.
- **AF or long-term VTE prevention** → DOAC first-line, warfarin second.
- **Cancer-associated thrombosis** → DOAC or LMWH (avoid warfarin — chemo interactions).

🟥 Top 5 anticoagulant traps: (1) mechanical valves = warfarin only; (2) pregnancy = LMWH only; (3) severe renal failure = avoid DOAC/LMWH; (4) major warfarin bleed = PCC not FFP; (5) -xaban targets Xa, dabigatran targets IIa.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: 🟦 How do you decide between thrombocytopenia, coagulation defect, DIC, liver disease, and VWD when given a clotting screen?

```
Step 1: Look at PT and APTT.
Step 2: Classify:
        - Both normal    → VWD (if bleeding) or platelet issue
        - APTT only ↑    → intrinsic (haemophilia, FXII) or heparin
        - PT only ↑      → early liver/warfarin, FVII
        - Both ↑         → liver, warfarin, DIC, dilution
Step 3: Check platelets + fibrinogen + D-dimer:
        - Platelets ↓ + D-dimer ↑↑ + fibrinogen ↓ → DIC
        - Platelets ↓ in isolation → ITP
        - All normal with bleeding → VWD (check VWF assay)
```

### Q: 🟦 What single question should you ask first about any bleeding or clotting stem?

- **"Is this primary or secondary haemostasis?"** (if bleeding)
- **"Is this arterial or venous?"** (if clotting)
- The answer cuts the differential in half and points to the correct drug class.

### Q: 🟦 "Young person with VTE" — what's the workup trigger list?

Suspect inherited thrombophilia if ANY of:
- Age <45.
- Unprovoked.
- Recurrent.
- Unusual site (cerebral, splanchnic).
- Thrombosis while anticoagulated.
- Strong family history.

Screen: FV Leiden (commonest positive), prothrombin mutation, antithrombin, protein C, protein S, antiphospholipid antibodies.

### Q: 🟦 When to give PCC vs FFP vs cryoprecipitate vs factor concentrate?

- **PCC** — emergency warfarin reversal (contains II, VII, IX, X).
- **FFP** — multi-factor deficiency (liver, DIC, dilution); Factor V replacement.
- **Cryoprecipitate** — low fibrinogen (DIC, massive haemorrhage).
- **Factor concentrate** — single-factor replacement (haemophilia).

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA — 24F with menorrhagia, gum bleeding, easy bruising since menarche. Mother similar. Platelets 280, PT 11, APTT 29. Diagnosis?

- **Von Willebrand disease**.
- Mucocutaneous bleeding pattern + autosomal family history + **normal platelets and coag screen** = classic VWD paradox.
- Haemophilia A would prolong APTT and is X-linked.
- ITP would have low platelets.
- 🟥 "Normal coag screen" is the VWD signature, not an exclusion criterion.

### Q: SBA — 68F on long-term prednisolone. Easy bruising. Platelets 240, PT/APTT/VWF normal. Cause?

- **Age-related + steroid-induced vessel wall fragility**.
- All labs normal → by elimination, vessel wall is the culprit.
- Two stacking causes (age, steroids) loss of connective tissue support.
- 🟦 If labs are normal and patient bruises, it's vascular.

### Q: SBA — 32M severe haemophilia A. Swollen knee after football. Which analgesic to avoid?

- **IM diclofenac** — double error.
- IM route → risks muscle haematoma.
- NSAID → impairs platelet function.
- Paracetamol, oral codeine, IV morphine all acceptable.
- 🟥 Two rules in one: no IM, no NSAIDs in haemophilia.

### Q: SBA — 67F urosepsis, hypotensive, oozing cannula. Platelets 48, PT 22, APTT 52, fibrinogen 0.9, D-dimer ↑↑. Most important next step?

- **IV antibiotics and source control**.
- This is DIC secondary to sepsis — replacing factors alone feeds the fire.
- Product replacement is supportive, not definitive.
- 🟦 In DIC, always treat the cause first.

### Q: SBA — 28F on OCP, right-sided chest pain and SOB after flight. HR 112, SpO₂ 93%. Mother had DVT at 35. CTPA confirms PE. Which factor contributed MOST?

- **Inherited Factor V Leiden** (family history of young VTE).
- OCP is the trigger; FV Leiden is the underlying predisposition.
- Multi-causal model: genetic + acquired + age.
- 🟦 Young VTE + FHx → look for inherited defect; OCP unmasks it.

### Q: SBA — 72M 4 days post-hip replacement. Proximal DVT, no personal/family history. Next step?

- **Treatment-dose anticoagulation for 3 months**.
- Provoked clot + older patient → no thrombophilia screen needed.
- Trigger resolves (hip surgery) → time-limited course.
- 🟦 3 months for provoked, longer for unprovoked.

### Q: SBA — 78M on warfarin, head injury, subdural haematoma, INR 3.8. Reversal?

- **IV vitamin K + PCC**.
- PCC works in minutes, contains II/VII/IX/X.
- IV vitamin K sustains reversal once PCC wears off.
- FFP second-line; oral vitamin K too slow.

### Q: SBA — 34F 10 weeks pregnant, new DVT. Anticoagulant of choice?

- **LMWH**.
- Warfarin teratogenic 1st trimester (chondrodysplasia punctata).
- DOACs cross placenta — contraindicated.
- UFH works but LMWH more convenient.

### Q: SBA — 14M with recurrent knee bleeds, maternal uncle affected. PT 11, APTT 82, platelets normal. Next test?

- **Factor VIII assay**.
- X-linked + joint bleeds + isolated APTT prolongation = haemophilia A likely.
- Haemophilia A is 5× more common than B → start with FVIII.
- PFA-100 or VWF assay wrong — this is a coagulation (secondary) defect.

### Q: SBA — 55F type 1 VWD for dental extraction. Given IV DDAVP. Mechanism?

- **Releases stored VWF and FVIII from endothelial Weibel-Palade bodies**.
- 2–5× rise within 30 min.
- Requires pre-existing stores → only useful in mild disease.
- 🟥 Not a synthesis stimulator — relies on endogenous stores.

### Q: SBA — 72M on warfarin for AF. Routine INR 5.2, no bleeding. Management?

- **Omit 1–2 doses, reduce maintenance, recheck**.
- INR 5–8 without bleeding doesn't warrant vitamin K or PCC.
- Full reversal for a non-bleeding high INR causes warfarin resistance for days.
- 🟦 Management escalates with bleeding risk, not INR value alone.

### Q: SBA — 6F with widespread petechiae after viral URTI. Platelets 12, PT/APTT normal, film otherwise normal. Diagnosis?

- **Immune thrombocytopenic purpura**.
- Post-viral, isolated thrombocytopenia, well child = classic paediatric ITP.
- Leukaemia would show blasts; DIC would have prolonged coag screen; HUS would have AKI + haemolysis.
- Usually self-limiting in children.

### Q: SBA — 40F starts warfarin for DVT. Day 2: purple necrotic thigh lesions. Underlying cause?

- **Protein C deficiency**.
- Protein C has short half-life → falls faster than Factor II on warfarin.
- Pre-existing Protein C deficiency → severe hypercoagulable state in first 2–3 days.
- Prevented by bridging with heparin.

### Q: SBA — 27F on OCP, FV Leiden positive. Mechanism of prothrombotic effect?

- **FV is resistant to cleavage by activated Protein C**.
- Loss of the natural "off switch" for FVa.
- FVa stays active longer → more thrombin → thrombosis.
- Synergy with OCP (oestrogen raises procoagulants).

### Q: SBA — 65M on warfarin for mechanical mitral valve. Emergency appendicectomy. Strategy?

- **Stop warfarin, bridge with IV UFH, stop UFH 4–6h pre-op**.
- Mechanical valve = highest thrombotic risk → cannot go unprotected.
- 🟥 DOACs contraindicated in mechanical valves.
- UFH reversible with protamine if needed urgently.

### Q: SBA — 30M on IV UFH for submassive PE. Monitoring test?

- **APTT** (target 1.5–2.5× baseline).
- UFH inactivates IIa + Xa → prolongs APTT significantly.
- LMWH would be monitored (if needed) with anti-Xa.
- 🟦 UFH = APTT; LMWH = anti-Xa; warfarin = INR; DOAC = none routinely.

### Q: SBA — 26F severe haemophilia A (FVIII <1%), ongoing ooze post-dental extraction. Adjunct?

- **Tranexamic acid**.
- Antifibrinolytic — stabilises existing clot.
- Particularly effective for mucosal bleeding.
- DDAVP ineffective in severe haemophilia (no stores).
- Platelets/vitamin K/FFP wrong mechanism.

### Q: SBA — 62F metastatic breast cancer on chemo. New DVT. Long-term anticoagulant?

- **Apixaban** (DOAC).
- Cancer-associated thrombosis: DOAC or LMWH first-line, avoid warfarin (chemo interactions, unstable INR).
- Aspirin/clopidogrel wrong class (antiplatelet not anticoagulant).
- UFH IV impractical long-term.
