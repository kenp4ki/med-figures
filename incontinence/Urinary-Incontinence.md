## Core intuition

⭐ Continence is a tug-of-war. **Bladder pressure** wants urine out; **outlet pressure** (bladder neck + external sphincter + pelvic floor) keeps it in. Storage: outlet > bladder. Voiding: bladder > outlet. Every disease here is one side losing.

🧠 Map the three clinical conditions onto the tug-of-war:

| Condition | Failure | Phase affected | Sex |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stress UI | Outlet too weak | Storage (cough, sneeze, run) | Mostly women |
| OAB / urge UI | Bladder fires when it shouldn't | Storage | Both |
| BPH | Outlet too tight | Voiding → secondary storage | Men only |

## Anatomy of the urinary tract

📎 Figure L1.2 — Urinary tract overview (kidneys at L1, ureters, bladder, urethra, ballotting).

### Q: Where do the kidney hila sit, and where do the ureters run?
- Kidney hila at **L1**, retroperitoneal.
- Ureters descend along **tips of lumbar transverse processes**.
- **Cross sacroiliac joint** into pelvis.
- Pass forward next to **rectum (and vagina in women)** before entering the bladder trigone.

### Q: Why are ureters at risk in gynaecological surgery?
- They run immediately lateral to the cervix and vaginal fornices.
- A hysterectomy or oophorectomy can transect or ligate them.
- Classic stem: post-hysterectomy flank pain or anuria → suspect ureteric injury.

### Q: Basic ureter facts (length, diameter, layers)?
- ~**25 cm** long, ~**3 mm** diameter.
- Three wall layers: outer fibrous, middle muscle (peristalsis), inner urothelium.
- Upper half abdominal, lower half pelvic.

### Q: Why does each kidney get its own renal artery direct from the aorta?
- Kidneys receive ~**20% of cardiac output**.
- That volume cannot come off a smaller branch.
- Critical for blood pressure regulation and homeostasis.

### Q: What is "ballotting" and what does it detect?
- Bimanual examination: posterior hand pushes from the loin, anterior hand catches the bounce.
- The kidney is the only abdominal organ classically ballotted.
- Used to confirm an enlarged or palpable kidney.

⚠️ Loin pain in a patient with a known **solitary** kidney is an emergency until proven otherwise — any obstruction threatens immediate AKI of the only working unit.

## Embryology (verbal-only — examiner tell)

### Q: What germ layer gives rise to the kidney vs the bladder?
- **Kidney** = mesoderm (intermediate mesoderm).
- **Bladder + urethra** = endoderm (hindgut → split off by urogenital septum).
- Different origins explain why they develop independently.

### Q: Three timing milestones in urinary tract development?
- Week **5**: ureteric bud emerges from mesonephric duct.
- Week **9**: kidney finishes migrating from pelvis to L1.
- Week **12**: urachus (bladder dome → umbilicus) obliterates.

### Q: What is a patent urachus and how does it present?
- Failure of urachus to obliterate by week 12.
- Urine leaks from the **umbilicus**, more common in children.
- Rare in adults but can persist.

📎 Figure L1.4 — Embryology: cloaca → urogenital sinus → adult urinary tract.

## Anatomic variants

### Q: Three kidney variants and one to flag as an emergency consideration?
- **Single kidney** (1% of population) — loin pain = emergency.
- **Horseshoe kidney** — fused lower poles.
- **Ectopic kidney** — abnormal position.

### Q: Why does a horseshoe kidney sit lower than normal?
- Ureteric buds form too medially → kidney blastemas fuse early.
- The fused mass migrates upward but **gets stuck under the inferior mesenteric artery (IMA)**.
- That's why horseshoe kidneys typically sit around L3.

### Q: Two patterns of ureter duplication and the quirk of complete duplication?
- Partial duplication: one bladder insertion, two upper segments.
- Complete duplication: two ureters all the way to the bladder.
- Quirk: in complete duplication the **upper-pole ureter inserts lower** in the bladder (Weigert–Meyer rule).

## "Hold-up" points in the ureter

⭐ The lecturer flagged this as a *classic exam question*.

```
                  KIDNEY (renal pelvis)
                       │
            ① PUJ — pelvi-ureteric junction
                       │
                       │   ← ureter on transverse process tips
                       │
            ② PELVIC BRIM — crosses iliac vessels
                       │
                       │   ← ureter in pelvis
                       │
            ③ VUJ — vesico-ureteric junction (oblique entry)
                       │
                    BLADDER
```

📎 Figure L1.6 — Three ureteric hold-up points with obstruction films and referred pain map.

### Q: Why is the PUJ a classic obstruction site?
- It is a true anatomical join (ureteric bud meeting metanephric mesenchyme).
- The lumen can be congenitally narrow.
- A diuretic challenge (e.g. several pints of lager) can unmask it as renal colic.

### Q: Why does the ureter take an oblique course through the bladder wall at the VUJ?
- Anti-reflux mechanism.
- Rising bladder pressure squeezes the oblique segment shut.
- Stops urine refluxing back to the kidney — but creates a stone hold-up site.

### Q: Pain pattern by ureteric stone location?
- **PUJ**: flank/loin (renal-type pain).
- **Pelvic brim**: loin → lower abdomen.
- **VUJ**: groin and ipsilateral testicle (men) / labia (women).

### Q: Renal colic vs ureteric colic — what's the difference?
- **Renal colic**: pain from the kidney itself (e.g. PUJ obstruction).
- **Ureteric colic**: pain from the ureter (radiates loin → groin).
- Lecturer is explicit: don't conflate the terms.

⚠️ Colicky ureteric pain → patient is restless and cannot lie still. Peritonitis → patient lies absolutely still. This is a discriminator on history alone.

## The bladder

📎 Figure L1.7 — Bladder anatomy (dome, trigone, VUJ, urethral meatus, external sphincter).

### Q: Detrusor muscle and bladder lining — what cell type and why?
- Detrusor = smooth muscle.
- Lining = **urothelium** (transitional epithelium).
- Waterproof — needed because urine is hyperosmolar and would otherwise damage tissue.

### Q: Slide vs reality — what shape is the bladder?
- Slide/textbook: **4-sided pyramid** (4 angles, 4 surfaces). Write this in the exam.
- Reality (lecturer): a smooth dome — the pyramidal shape is a formalin-fixation artefact in cadavers.

### Q: Bladder sensation timeline as it fills?
- ~200 mL: **first sensation** (subliminal).
- 300–400 mL: **normal desire to void** (can defer).
- ~500 mL: **strong desire to void** (must excuse oneself).
- Average capacity ~500 mL; women ~50 mL more than men.

### Q: What is bladder compliance and why does it matter?
- Compliance = ΔV / ΔP.
- Bladder fills to ~500 mL with **virtually no rise in pressure**.
- Same concept as pulmonary compliance.
- Without it, every additional millilitre would trigger a contraction.

### Q: How much of a 24-hour day is spent voiding vs storing?
- ~6 voids × ~20 s = ~2 min/day voiding.
- Storage occupies the remaining 23 h 58 min.
- Storage is the dominant phase — most LUT pathology presents as storage symptoms.

### Q: What is the upper limit for a normal post-void residual (PVR)?
- **<50 mL**.
- Above this = incomplete emptying.
- Suggests outflow obstruction (BPH) or detrusor underactivity (neurogenic).

## Female vs male lower urinary tract

📎 Figure L1.9 — Female pelvis sagittal section.
📎 Figure L1.10 — Male pelvis sagittal section.

### Q: Female urethra essentials?
- **3–4 cm long**, opens at external meatus in vaginal vestibule.
- No true anatomical bladder neck sphincter.
- External sphincter = skeletal, **pudendal nerve**, voluntary.
- Pelvic floor (levator ani) supports the urethra — childbirth weakens this.

### Q: Male prostate — size, lobes, secretion?
- ~**4 × 3 × 2 cm**, conical.
- **Two lateral lobes** (± median lobe), surrounds proximal urethra.
- Secretes **75% of seminal fluid** (liquefies coagulated semen).

### Q: What controls the male bladder neck and why does it matter?
- **Sympathetic NS**, α1 receptors.
- **Shut at all times except during voiding**.
- At ejaculation it must stay shut so semen tracks **forward** through the urethra, not backward into the bladder.

⭐ Three causes of retrograde ejaculation — high-yield association:
- **Prostate surgery** (TURP cuts the bladder neck).
- **Spinal cord neurological disease**.
- **Retroperitoneal lymph node dissection (RPLND)** for testicular cancer — sympathetic fibres run retroperitoneally and are severed.

### Q: Coordination of ejaculation — what is happening at each level?
- Bladder neck: **SHUT** (sympathetic α1).
- External sphincter: **OPEN** (pudendal withdrawal).
- Both must occur simultaneously for antegrade ejaculation.

## The micturition cycle

```
   STORAGE  ◀────────────▶  VOIDING
   (default)                (only when permitted)

   detrusor RELAXED         detrusor CONTRACTED   (PNS, M3)
   bladder neck SHUT        bladder neck OPEN     (sympathetic withdrawal)
   ext. sphincter SHUT      ext. sphincter OPEN   (pudendal withdrawal)
   pelvic floor active      pelvic floor relaxed
                            PVR < 50 mL after void
```

### Q: Three things that must happen in coordination for normal voiding?
- Detrusor contracts (parasympathetic, M3).
- Bladder neck opens (sympathetic withdrawal).
- External sphincter relaxes (pudendal withdrawal).

### Q: Map each clinical condition onto the failure point?
- **Detrusor contracts when it shouldn't** = OAB / urge UI.
- **Sphincter doesn't stay closed** = stress UI.
- **Bladder neck/urethra obstructs voiding** = BPH.

## Neural control of micturition

📎 Figure L1.11 — Neural control: cortex, PMC, T10–L2 and S2–S4.
📎 Figure L1.12 — Functional roles of each level.

### Q: Top-down hierarchy of micturition control — name and function of each level?
- **Prefrontal cortex**: permission for voiding (social appropriateness).
- **Limbic cortex**: emotional/fear modulation.
- **Periaqueductal grey (PAG)**: visceral sensory relay to cortex.
- **Pontine micturition centre (PMC)**: hardwired storage→voiding switch.

### Q: At what bladder volume does the PAG push awareness up to the cortex?
- Around **400 mL**.
- Below this, bladder afferents are processed subconsciously.
- The PAG decides which visceral signal earns conscious attention (e.g. nail-in-foot wins over bladder).

### Q: Three spinal nuclei controlling the lower urinary tract?
- **Sympathetic intermediolateral column (T10–L2)** → bladder neck (α1).
- **Parasympathetic nucleus (S2–S4)** → detrusor (M3, M2).
- **Onuf's nucleus (S2–S4)** → external sphincter (pudendal, somatic).

⭐ Memorise the **functions** of Onuf's nucleus, not its name. The lecturer explicitly said the name isn't required — but the function (somatic motoneurons to the external sphincter via the pudendal nerve) is.

### Q: How does the PMC switch from storage to voiding?
- **Activates** the parasympathetic nucleus → detrusor contracts.
- **Inhibits** Onuf's nucleus → external sphincter relaxes.
- Only flips when given permission by the prefrontal cortex.

### Q: Why do infants need nappies?
- PMC connections are immature.
- Voiding is a local sacral spinal reflex triggered at threshold pressure.
- Potty training = the cortex–PMC pathway maturing enough to override the reflex.

### Q: Pattern of dysfunction by lesion level?

| Lesion site | Pattern | Example cause |
|---|---|---|
| Above PMC (cortex) | Inappropriate voiding, coordination preserved | Dementia, frontal stroke |
| Between PMC and sacral cord | Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia, high pressures | Spinal cord injury (e.g. T8) |
| Sacral cord / cauda equina | Areflexic flaccid bladder, retention with overflow | Cauda equina syndrome |

⚠️ Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (suprasacral cord lesion) generates **high intravesical pressures against a closed sphincter** → hydronephrosis → renal failure. Always look for hydronephrosis in spinal cord injury.

## Autonomic pharmacology of the lower urinary tract

⭐ Lecturer flagged: receptors are an exam favourite because every drug class hangs off them.

📎 Figure L1.13 — Pelvic innervation and autonomic drug targets.

### Q: Match structure → receptor → drug class → example?

| Structure | Receptor | Drug class | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bladder neck (men) | α1 (α1A) | α1 blocker | **Tamsulosin** |
| Detrusor — block contraction | M3 (and M2) | Antimuscarinic | **Solifenacin** |
| Detrusor — drive relaxation | β3 | β3 agonist | **Mirabegron** |
| Erectile tissue | NO/cGMP (PDE5) | PDE5 inhibitor | **Sildenafil** |

### Q: Why is solifenacin preferred over oxybutynin for OAB?
- Oxybutynin is older and "dirty" — many anticholinergic side effects (dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision, confusion in elderly).
- Solifenacin is more selective and better tolerated.
- Both block M3 → reduce detrusor contractions.

### Q: Why isn't there a drug for the external urethral sphincter?
- It's **skeletal muscle** controlled by the somatic pudendal nerve.
- No autonomic receptor to target.
- That's why **physiotherapy / pelvic floor exercises** are first-line for stress UI.

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern.** When an SBA asks "which drug?", first decide which **structure** is the lever (bladder neck, detrusor, sphincter, erectile). The drug class falls out from the table. If the answer would target the sphincter, expect a non-pharmacological option (PFMEs, sling).

## ATLAS — the three clinical conditions

Same template per card: definition → epidemiology + risk factors → pathophysiology → symptoms → investigations → management ladder → complications → exam trap.

### Stress urinary incontinence (SUI)

### Q: Definition of stress urinary incontinence?
- Involuntary leakage on **effort or exertion, sneezing, or coughing** (ICS).
- No urgency; leak is synchronous with the abdominal pressure rise.
- Bladder itself is silent.

### Q: SUI epidemiology and risk factors?
- Affects up to **40% of women**; **1 in 5 women over 40**.
- Risk factors: ageing, **obesity**, **smoking** (cough), **pregnancy** and route of delivery.
- Mostly female; rare in men except post-prostatectomy.

### Q: Pathophysiology of SUI?
- Vaginal childbirth stretches **posterior** pelvic-floor support of the urethra.
- **Pubo-urethral ligaments anteriorly stay tight** → urethra dragged slightly open.
- Sphincter cannot close fully against rising abdominal pressure.

### Q: Classic SUI history and examination findings?
- Leakage with cough, sneeze, lifting, running.
- Patients often curtail exercise to avoid the symptom.
- Vaginal exam: descent of pelvic floor, **positive stress test** (visible leak on cough).

### Q: SUI on urodynamics — what's the signature?
- Leakage during a **rise in abdominal pressure** with **no detrusor contraction**.
- Often the **first cough doesn't leak** (clenched pelvic floor) but the **second/third do** — sequence-of-coughs pattern.
- This distinguishes SUI from urge UI on the trace.

📎 Figure L1.15 — Urodynamics: stress UI (3 coughs → 2 leak spikes).

### Q: SUI management ladder?
- **First-line**: physiotherapist-supervised pelvic floor muscle exercises (PFMEs) for ≥3 months.
- **Second-line (surgical)**: mid-urethral sling, colposuspension, periurethral bulking injection.
- ~50% respond to PFMEs alone.

⚠️ "What's the first-line treatment of SUI?" — the trap is reaching for a drug. There **is no drug** that meaningfully treats SUI at MBChB level. The answer is **PFMEs**.

### Overactive bladder (OAB) / urge urinary incontinence

### Q: Definition of overactive bladder?
- Urinary **urgency**, usually with **frequency** and **nocturia**, ± urgency UI.
- Urgency = sudden compelling desire to void, hard to defer.
- Storage-phase problem: detrusor contracts when it shouldn't.

### Q: OAB epidemiology and risk factors?
- Prevalence **16.6%** in men and women over 40.
- Up to ~40% experience urgency with age.
- Risk factors: age, **prolapse** (women), high BMI, bladder irritants (**caffeine**, nicotine).

### Q: Pathophysiology of OAB?
- Involuntary detrusor contractions during storage.
- **Idiopathic** in most cases.
- **Neurogenic** when CNS lesion strips inhibition (MS, stroke, Parkinson's, spinal cord disease).

### Q: OAB on urodynamics — what's the signature?
- **Pdet rises during storage** (detrusor contracts when it shouldn't).
- Often accompanied by leakage (incontinence on the flow trace).
- Distinguishes OAB from SUI (where Pdet stays flat).

📎 Figure L1.16 — Urodynamics: detrusor overactivity in storage.

### Q: OAB investigations and the reasoning for each?
- **Urine dipstick / MSU** — exclude UTI (mimics urgency exactly).
- **Bladder diary** (3 days) — confirms frequency, fluid/caffeine pattern.
- **Bladder scan PVR** — high PVR suggests obstruction (BPH) causing secondary OAB.
- Urodynamics — second-line, used pre-invasive treatment.

### Q: OAB management ladder?
- **Behavioural / lifestyle** — reduce caffeine, manage fluids, weight loss.
- **Bladder retraining** — gradually defer voiding.
- **Antimuscarinic** (solifenacin) → **β3 agonist** (mirabegron).
- **Botox** 100 units intra-detrusor (lasts ~6 months).
- **Sacral neuromodulation** (implanted electrode + battery).

⚠️ Always **rule out UTI and high PVR** before diagnosing primary OAB — otherwise you'll treat the wrong thing. Antimuscarinics in retention worsen everything.

### Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)

### Q: Definition and epidemiology of BPH?
- Non-malignant hyperplasia of prostate tissue; commonest cause of LUTS in men.
- **50–60%** of men in their 60s; **80–90%** over 70.
- Risk factor is hormonal — long-term DHT exposure.

### Q: Pathophysiology of BPH?
- **Testosterone → DHT** in the prostate by **5α-reductase**.
- DHT drives hyperplasia of stroma (smooth muscle + fibrous) and glands.
- Lateral lobes (± median lobe) compress the urethra → bladder outflow obstruction.

⭐ Most enlargement is **outward** (felt on DRE) but does not compress the urethra. The dangerous component is **inward** compression — which you cannot feel. Normal-feeling DRE does not exclude clinically significant BPH.

### Q: Cardinal voiding symptoms of BPH?
- **Hesitancy** (waiting before flow starts).
- **Slow / poor stream** (the archetypal complaint).
- **Post-micturition dribbling**.
- Can present with **acute urinary retention**.

### Q: BPH differential — what must be excluded before settling on the diagnosis?

| Differential | Cue | Test |
|---|---|---|
| Bladder cancer | Haematuria | Urine dip, cystoscopy |
| Prostate cancer | Hard/irregular DRE, raised PSA | PSA, biopsy |
| UTI / prostatitis | Dysuria, fever | Urine MSU |
| Urethral stricture | Prior trauma, instrumentation | Flow studies |

### Q: BPH investigations and why each is done?
- **DRE** — feels prostate; checks for cancer features and distended bladder.
- **Urine dip / MSU** — excludes haematuria (cancer) and UTI.
- **Bladder scan PVR** — detects chronic retention.
- **PSA** — excludes prostate cancer (raised in BPH too, so not specific).
- **Renal ultrasound** — looks for hydronephrosis = back-pressure on kidneys.
- **Flow studies / urodynamics** — confirm outflow obstruction.

### Q: BPH management ladder?
- **Lifestyle**: weight loss, reduce caffeine + evening fluids, avoid constipation.
- **α-blocker (tamsulosin)** — fast-onset symptom relief (days).
- **5α-reductase inhibitor (finasteride)** — slow shrinkage over months.
- **TURP** (transurethral resection) — debulks the obstructing tissue; can use laser.

📎 Figure L1.17 — TURP: endoscopic resection and before/after cystoscopic view.

⚠️ **TURP ≠ radical prostatectomy.** TURP debulks the core for **BPH**. Radical prostatectomy removes the whole gland for **cancer**. The lecturer flagged this distinction explicitly.

### Q: BPH complications — name two patterns of retention and which is more dangerous?
- **Acute** retention: sudden, painful → catheterise immediately.
- **Chronic painless** retention: progressive bladder distension → backpressure → bilateral hydronephrosis → CKD.
- Chronic painless is **worse** because it goes unrecognised until the kidneys fail.

🧠 **Reusable pattern.** Painful = acute = catheterise. Painless = chronic = sinister, threatens kidneys. Whenever an older man presents with unexplained renal failure, palpate the bladder.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: First-line management for SUI, OAB, BPH — what do they have in common?
- All three are **conservative first**, drugs second.
- SUI → PFMEs.
- OAB → behavioural + bladder retraining.
- BPH → lifestyle (caffeine, fluids, constipation).

### Q: Discriminating UI subtypes from the trigger?
- Cough/sneeze/lift/run → stress UI.
- Urgency, "couldn't make it" → urge UI / OAB.
- Continuous leak in older man with palpable bladder → overflow from BPH chronic retention.
- Urine from umbilicus in an infant → patent urachus.

### Q: Urodynamic discriminator between SUI and OAB?
- SUI: leakage on cough, **Pdet flat**.
- OAB: **Pdet rises during storage**, ± leakage.
- BPH: **low flow + high voiding pressure**, often raised PVR.

### Q: Which urinary conditions threaten the kidneys?
- **Stress UI**: no — quality-of-life only.
- **Uncomplicated OAB**: no.
- **BPH with chronic retention**: yes — back-pressure → CKD.
- **Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (neurogenic)**: yes — high pressures against closed sphincter.

🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern for unseen stems.** Decide three things in order: (1) Storage or voiding symptoms? (2) Where in the tug-of-war is the failure — bladder side or outlet side? (3) Is there a kidney threat (palpable bladder, hydronephrosis, raised creatinine)? The answer to most management questions falls out from these three.

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 32-year-old man has sudden right loin pain radiating to the right testicle. He cannot lie still. Where is the stone? (A) PUJ (B) Mid-ureter at pelvic brim (C) VUJ (D) Bladder dome (E) Renal pelvis
- **C — VUJ.**
- Testicular radiation localises pain to the bottom end of the ureter.
- PUJ → flank only. Pelvic brim → lower abdomen. Bladder dome and renal pelvis aren't ureteric stone sites.
- Restlessness vs lying still is the colic-vs-peritonitis discriminator.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 67-year-old woman with stress UI has done supervised PFMEs for 6 months without improvement. No urgency. Next step? (A) Solifenacin (B) Mirabegron (C) Mid-urethral sling (D) Tamsulosin (E) Botox
- **C — mid-urethral sling.**
- SUI failed first-line conservative → surgical: sling, colposuspension, or bulking.
- A and B treat detrusor (wrong subtype). D treats male outflow. E treats OAB.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 72-year-old man, nocturia ×4, palpable bladder to umbilicus, creatinine 280 (baseline 90). Most urgent step? (A) Tamsulosin (B) TURP (C) Catheterise (D) Renal US (E) PSA
- **C — catheterise.**
- Chronic painless retention with post-renal AKI; drainage is the priority.
- US, PSA, and definitive treatment follow after decompression.
- PSA is unreliable post-retention (retention itself raises it).

### Q: SBA 4 — A 28-year-old man post-cord-transection at T8 has high-pressure bladder contractions, intermittent flow, hydronephrosis. Mechanism? (A) Loss of cortical permission (B) Loss of afferent sensation (C) Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (D) Detrusor underactivity (E) Pudendal transection
- **C — detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia.**
- Cord injury between PMC and sacral nuclei → loss of coordination.
- Detrusor and sphincter both contract → high pressure → hydronephrosis.
- A is a cortical lesion picture; D and E don't fit the high-pressure pattern.

### Q: SBA 5 — A 45-year-old with MS has urgency, frequency 12/day, occasional leakage, PVR 30 mL. Drug if conservative fails? (A) Tamsulosin (B) Solifenacin (C) Finasteride (D) Sildenafil (E) Duloxetine
- **B — solifenacin.**
- Neurogenic OAB — first-line drug is an antimuscarinic blocking M3.
- Normal PVR means antimuscarinics safe.
- A and C are male outflow drugs; D is for ED; E targets SUI.

### Q: SBA 6 — A 64-year-old man with BPH starts a drug; hesitancy improves within 48 hours via prostatic stromal smooth muscle relaxation. Receptor? (A) M3 (B) β3 (C) α1 (D) β2 (E) Nicotinic
- **C — α1.**
- Tamsulosin, fast onset (days). Receptor is α1A on prostate stroma and bladder neck.
- M3 = OAB. β3 = OAB (different mechanism). β2 = airways. Nicotinic ≠ smooth muscle.

### Q: SBA 7 — A 28-year-old post-RPLND for testicular cancer has dry orgasms and cloudy post-coital urine. Cause? (A) Urethral stricture (B) ED (C) Retrograde ejaculation (D) Anejaculation from cord injury (E) Prostatitis
- **C — retrograde ejaculation.**
- RPLND severs sympathetic fibres → bladder neck doesn't close at orgasm.
- Semen tracks backwards into bladder; passed in next void as cloudy urine.
- A would impair voiding; B affects erection not ejaculation; D doesn't fit RPLND.

### Q: SBA 8 — A 70-year-old woman has urgency she can't defer, frequency 11/day, clean dipstick. First-line? (A) Solifenacin (B) Mirabegron (C) Behavioural changes + bladder retraining (D) Sling (E) Cystoscopy
- **C — behavioural + bladder retraining.**
- OAB first-line is always conservative.
- A and B are second-line. D treats SUI. E is for haematuria/cancer concern.

### Q: SBA 9 — Urodynamics: leak on the third cough but no rise in detrusor pressure. No urgency in history. Diagnosis? (A) Detrusor overactivity (B) SUI (C) Mixed UI (D) BOO (E) Detrusor underactivity
- **B — stress UI.**
- Leak with rise in abdominal pressure and **flat Pdet** = SUI signature.
- Sequence-of-coughs pattern is classic (failed pelvic floor clench).
- A would show Pdet spike; C requires features of both.

### Q: SBA 10 — Spinal cord level for parasympathetic drive to detrusor? (A) T6–T10 (B) T10–L2 (C) L2–L4 (D) S2–S4 (E) S1–S3
- **D — S2–S4.**
- Parasympathetic nucleus and Onuf's nucleus both at S2–S4.
- T10–L2 is the sympathetic level (bladder neck).

### Q: SBA 11 — An 80-year-old with Alzheimer's voids in inappropriate places, no urgency, PVR 20 mL, normal urine. Lesion site? (A) Sacral cord (B) PMC (C) PAG (D) Prefrontal cortex (E) Detrusor
- **D — prefrontal cortex.**
- Frontal disease destroys permission/inhibition; downstream coordination preserved.
- A would give flaccid retention. B would give dyssynergia. E doesn't cause socially-inappropriate voiding.

### Q: SBA 12 — A 60-year-old man with BPH cannot pass urine for 6 hours; bladder palpable to umbilicus, severe pain. Immediate step? (A) Reassure (B) PSA + culture + US (C) Catheterise (D) Start tamsulosin (E) Refer for TURP
- **C — catheterise.**
- Acute urinary retention is an emergency; pain relief and decompression first.
- Investigations and definitive treatment follow.
- The lecturer's anecdote: never let this be triaged low.
