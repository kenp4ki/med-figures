## Lecturer's voice

**Flagged as exam-critical**
- Central vestibular system — repeated as the high-yield part neurology trainees miss.
- HINTSplus exam — the headline clinical tool for acute dizziness in ED.
- Hearing loss is a red flag in acute vertigo (AICA territory).
- Direction-changing nystagmus / vertical skew = central until proven otherwise.
- Pure vertical, pure downbeat, pure torsional nystagmus is **always central**.

**De-emphasised**
- Peripheral vestibular anatomy in isolation — referred to as already-known.
- Ménière's disease — common in ENT diagnosis but actually rare in practice.
- Labyrinthitis — over-diagnosed, exclude stroke first.

**Spent disproportionate time on**
- Eye movement abnormalities and how to localise lesions from them.
- Distinguishing peripheral from central in the acutely dizzy patient.
- Vestibular migraine vs BPPV vs stroke triage in ED.

## The one idea

The vestibular system runs on **ASYMMETRY**. Each side has a tonic (continuous) baseline firing rate at rest. Head movement excites one side and inhibits the other — the brain reads the difference. Damage one side acutely and the intact side's continued firing is misread as continuous rotation → vertigo. This single principle explains why acute unilateral loss causes vertigo while bilateral loss doesn't, why nystagmus beats away from the lesion, why compensation takes weeks, and why HINTS works.

## Section 2 — Vestibular organ

### Two functional units

### Q: What does the vestibular organ contain, and what does each part detect?

- **Otolith organs (utricle + saccule)** → linear acceleration + head tilt.
- **Three semicircular canals (anterior, posterior, lateral)** → rotational / angular acceleration.
- All connected, share endolymph (K⁺-rich, same fluid as cochlea).

📎 Figure L2.2 — Vestibular organ anatomy.

### Otolith organs

### Q: What is the orientation of each otolith macula, and what does each detect?

- **Utricle macula — HORIZONTAL plane** → horizontal linear movement (forward/back, side-to-side).
- **Saccule macula — VERTICAL plane** → vertical linear movement (up/down) and head tilt.
- Heavy calcium carbonate **otoliths (otoconia)** sit on a gelatinous matrix on top of the hair cells.

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> Students mix up which organ does which. Mnemonic: **Saccule** sounds like "sack" — sacks hang **vertically**. Utricle does the **horizontal** plane.

### Q: How do the otoliths actually trigger hair cell deflection?

- When you tilt or accelerate, **inertia** keeps the heavy crystals lagging behind.
- The crystals shear the gelatinous matrix sideways.
- The matrix bends the underlying hair cell stereocilia.
- Bend toward kinocilium → excitation; bend away → inhibition.

### Semicircular canals

### Q: How does a semicircular canal detect rotation?

- Each canal is filled with endolymph and capped at one end by the **ampulla**.
- Inside the ampulla: hair cells on the **crista**, embedded in a gelatinous **cupula** that blocks the canal.
- Head turns → bony canal turns with it, but endolymph **lags by inertia** → fluid pushes against cupula → bends hair cells.

### Q: Which canals work in pairs across ears?

- **Right lateral ↔ Left lateral** (horizontal plane).
- **Right anterior ↔ Left posterior** (oblique).
- **Right posterior ↔ Left anterior** (oblique).
- One side excited, the other inhibited by the same head movement.

### Vestibular hair cells

### Q: What's different between vestibular and cochlear hair cells?

- Vestibular have a **tall kinocilium** (cochlear lose theirs after development).
- Vestibular hair cells fire **tonically at rest** — continuous baseline discharge.
- Cochlear hair cells only fire when sound arrives.

### Q: What is the basal discharge concept and why does it matter?

- At rest, **both vestibular nerves fire continuously at the same baseline rate**.
- Movement modulates this — one side up, one side down.
- Brain reads the **asymmetry**.
- Acute unilateral loss → intact side keeps firing → asymmetry → brain perceives rotation = **vertigo**.

> ⭐ **High-yield**
> The basal discharge concept is the single explanation for: acute vertigo with unilateral loss, no vertigo in bilateral loss, why compensation works, why nystagmus beats away from the lesion. Own it.

### Vestibular nerve and nuclei

### Q: Where do vestibular afferents project, and what are the four key output pathways?

- Afferents end in **vestibular nuclei in the pons** (via Scarpa's ganglion).
- Outputs:
  - **MLF → CN VI (abducens) + CN III (oculomotor)** = VOR highway.
  - **Cerebellum** = fine-tuning gain.
  - **Vestibulospinal tract** = posture (VSR).
  - **Cortex** = conscious awareness of head position.

📎 Figure L2.9 — Vestibular nerve + nuclei + connections.

### Vestibular blood supply

### Q: What is the arterial supply to the vestibular organ?

- **Basilar → AICA → Internal Auditory Artery** → splits into:
  - Anterior Vestibular A. → anterior + lateral canals + utricle.
  - Posterior Vestibular A. (off Common Cochlear A.) → posterior canal + saccule.
- Same tree as the cochlea.

> 🧠 **Cross-reference**
> AICA supplies BOTH cochlear and vestibular branches. One occlusion = combined audiovestibular stroke. **See also: L1 — Inner ear blood supply.**

## Section 3 — Vestibular reflexes

### Vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR)

### Q: What is the purpose of the VOR?

- Keep images **fixed on the retina** during head movement.
- Without it, vision blurs every time you walk or turn.
- Normal **gain ≈ 1** (eye velocity / head velocity).

### Q: Trace the VOR circuit when the head turns RIGHT.

```
R lateral SCC excited
   ↓
R vestibular nerve ↑ firing
   ↓
R vestibular nuclei
   ↓ via MLF
   ├── L abducens (CN VI) → L lateral rectus
   └── R oculomotor (CN III) → R medial rectus
   ↓
Both eyes move LEFT (opposite to head)
```

### Q: What is the VOR rule for eye movement?

- **Opposite direction** to head movement.
- **Same velocity, same amplitude** (gain = 1).
- If gain is off, image slips on retina → patient feels dizzy with head movement.

📎 Figure L2.13 — VOR circuit.

### Vestibulo-spinal reflex (VSR)

### Q: What does the VSR do?

- Vestibular nuclei → vestibulospinal tract → spinal motor neurons → postural muscles.
- Keeps you upright; adjusts posture in response to head/body tilt.
- Damage → imbalance, falls.

## Section 4 — Vestibular disorders

### Location axis

### Q: Classify vestibular disorders by location.

| Location | Site | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Peripheral | Vestibular organ / CN VIII | Vestibular neuritis, BPPV, Ménière's |
| Central | Brainstem / cerebellum | Stroke, vestibular migraine, MS, tumour |

### Atlas cards

### Q: Vestibular neuritis — key features?

- **Peripheral, acute.** Inflammation of vestibular nerve (often post-viral).
- Sudden severe vertigo lasting **hours to days**, then gradual recovery.
- **Normal hearing** (vestibular nerve only, not cochlear).
- HINTS: abnormal head impulse, unidirectional nystagmus, no skew.

### Q: BPPV — key features?

- **Peripheral, intermittent.** Otoliths displaced into a SCC (usually posterior).
- Brief (< 1 min) vertigo triggered by **position change** (rolling in bed, getting up).
- **No symptoms at rest, no hearing loss.**
- Diagnosis: **Dix-Hallpike** (posterior canal) or **Roll test** (horizontal canal).
- Treatment: **Epley** or **Semont** manoeuvre.

### Q: Ménière's disease — key features?

- **Peripheral, recurrent.** Endolymphatic hydrops.
- Triad: episodic vertigo + tinnitus + fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss + aural fullness.
- Less common than ENT diagnoses suggest.

### Q: Stroke (central) — key features?

- **Central, hyper-acute.** Posterior circulation (vertebrobasilar, AICA, PICA).
- "Bam, dizzy" onset.
- Often gait-impaired — **can't sit upright.**
- HINTS: normal head impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, skew deviation, hearing loss (AICA).

### Q: Vestibular migraine — key features?

- **Central, recurrent.** Now the most common cause of recurrent vertigo.
- Recurrent vertigo ± headache, photophobia, phonophobia.
- Definition: ≥ 5 episodes with migraine features in ≥ 50%.

### Timing axis

### Q: Match timing pattern to typical diagnosis and clinical test.

| Timing | Diagnosis | Test |
|---|---|---|
| Acute | Vestibular neuritis, Stroke | **HINTS exam** |
| Intermittent | BPPV | Dix-Hallpike, Roll |
| Recurrent | Migraine, Ménière's | History |
| Progressive | Schwannoma, MS | MRI |

### Red flags

### Q: Three red flags for central cause in an acutely dizzy patient?

- **Gait problems** — especially "can't sit upright".
- **Hyper-acute onset** — instant from fine to severe.
- **Hearing loss** — acute unilateral = AICA territory.

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> Vestibular neuritis patients are miserable but CAN sit with help. **Stroke patients cannot even sit.** Use this at the bedside.

## Section 5 — Clinical assessment

### Nystagmus

### Q: What is nystagmus and how is its direction named?

- Jerky eye movement with **slow phase (pathology)** + **fast phase (corrective saccade)**.
- **Direction is named by the FAST phase.**
- Slow drift right + fast jerk left = "left-beating nystagmus".

### Q: Nystagmus patterns and what they mean.

| Pattern | Location |
|---|---|
| Unidirectional, horizontal ± torsional | Peripheral |
| Direction-changing / bi-directional | Central |
| Pure vertical (upbeat or downbeat) | Central — always |
| Pure torsional | Central — always |

> ⚠️ **Trap — directly flagged by lecturer**
> Pure vertical or pure torsional nystagmus is **never peripheral**. See it → image the brain.

### HINTSplus exam

### Q: What does HINTSplus stand for?

- **H**ead **I**mpulse — does the eye stay locked on target during rapid head turn?
- **N**ystagmus — direction-changing or unidirectional?
- **T**est of **S**kew — vertical misalignment on cover-uncover?
- **Plus** — hearing test.

### Q: How do HINTSplus findings differ between vestibular neuritis and stroke?

| Component | Vestibular neuritis | Stroke |
|---|---|---|
| Head Impulse | **Abnormal** (catch-up saccade) | **Normal** |
| Nystagmus | Unidirectional, away from lesion | Direction-changing |
| Skew | Absent | Present |
| Hearing | Normal | Asymmetric loss |

> ⭐ **High-yield — counter-intuitive rule**
> A **NORMAL** head impulse in an acutely vertiginous patient = **stroke until proven otherwise**. Eye stays locked → peripheral VOR is intact → vertigo must be central.

### Head impulse test mechanics

### Q: How is the head impulse test performed and interpreted?

- Patient looks at examiner's nose; examiner rapidly turns the patient's head.
- **Normal** — eyes stay locked on target. Peripheral VOR intact.
- **Abnormal** — eyes move with head, then make a **catch-up saccade** back. Indicates peripheral lesion on the side of the turn.

### Dix-Hallpike

### Q: How is Dix-Hallpike performed and what is a positive result?

- Turn head 45° toward suspected affected ear.
- Rapidly lie patient back with head hanging off bed.
- **Positive:** torsional upbeating nystagmus with latency, crescendo-decrescendo, lasting < 1 min.
- Treat immediately with **Epley manoeuvre**.

## Cross-lecture integration

> 🧠 **Why is hearing tested in HINTSplus?**
> AICA supplies both cochlea and vestibular end-organs. Asymmetric hearing loss in an acutely dizzy patient localises the stroke to AICA territory. **See also: L1 — Causes of central hearing impairment.**

> 🧠 **Why does BPPV affect balance but not hearing?**
> Otoliths displaced from the utricle fall into a semicircular canal (usually posterior). Canals process rotation; the cochlea processes sound. The two are anatomically separate end-organs even though they share an arterial supply. **See also: L3 — BPPV mechanism.**
