## Why this overview exists

- **AICA stroke** is the named downstream syndrome that requires BOTH L1 (auditory branch consequences) and L2 (vestibular branch consequences) knowledge to recognise.
- **HINTSplus** is the clinical test that **literally combines** vestibular eye signs (L2) with the auditory exam (L1) — its "Plus" component is the L1 piece.
- **L3** is the applied case where you decompose hearing + vertigo symptoms across the L1/L2 dividing line.

If you only know L1 and L2 separately, the integrated SBAs in this section are unanswerable.

## The unifying intuition

**The inner ear is one organ supplied by one artery, with two functional halves that share most hardware.** Cochlea and vestibular apparatus sit next to each other, share endolymph, share hair cell physiology, share CN VIII, share blood supply via AICA → internal auditory artery. This is why some diseases hit both at once, and why the clinical exam has dedicated tools to flag when that's happening.

## Convergence 1 — AICA stroke syndrome

### Q: What does AICA supply?

- Branches off the **basilar artery**.
- Gives rise to the **internal auditory artery**, which splits into:
  - Common cochlear artery → cochlea + posterior vestibular branch.
  - Anterior vestibular artery → anterior + lateral SCCs + utricle.
- Also supplies the lateral pons + cerebellar territory.

### Q: How does an AICA infarct present?

- **Acute, simultaneous unilateral hearing loss + vertigo.**
- Often with cerebellar signs, gait ataxia, facial weakness, dysarthria.
- Patient may be unable to sit upright (gait red flag).
- **NOT vestibular neuritis** — neuritis spares hearing.

### Q: What clinical test confirms AICA pattern at the bedside?

- **HINTSplus.**
- Stroke pattern: **normal head impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, skew deviation present, asymmetric hearing loss.**
- The "Plus" (hearing test) is the L1 contribution — added precisely to catch AICA.

> ⚠️ **Trap — directly emphasised by lecturer**
> **MRI misses up to 20% of strokes within 24 hours.** Do not discharge an audiovestibular-loss patient on a normal MRI alone. HINTSplus performed correctly is more sensitive than early MRI.

## Convergence 2 — Peripheral vs central, applied to both systems

### Q: What is the parallel peripheral/central dichotomy across auditory and vestibular?

| Axis | Auditory (L1) | Vestibular (L2) |
|---|---|---|
| Peripheral | Outer/middle (conductive); cochlea/CN VIII | Vestibular organ / CN VIII |
| Central | Brainstem pathway; cortex | Brainstem; cerebellum |
| Test that interrogates pathway | ABR, speech-in-noise | HINTSplus |

### Q: Why can a peripheral test be normal when there is real pathway damage?

- Peripheral tests interrogate end-organ function only.
- **PTA can be normal** with central auditory lesions (patient still complains of speech-in-noise).
- **Head impulse test can be normal** with central vestibular lesions (that's the whole point of the HINTS rule).
- Always use a pathway-interrogating test alongside.

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern — pathway-completeness**
> When a stem says "test X was normal" and the patient still has the symptom, ask: which test of the next level up has NOT been done? In auditory: ABR. In vestibular: HINTSplus. In stroke: repeat or delayed MRI.

## Convergence 3 — Shared hair cell mechanism

### Q: What is shared between cochlear and vestibular hair cells?

- **Stereocilia, K⁺ influx from endolymph, glutamate release** onto the afferent.
- Same machinery, different stimulus.
- Both vulnerable to ototoxicity (cisplatin, aminoglycosides).

### Q: What is different?

- Cochlear hair cells have no kinocilium (lost in development); vestibular keep theirs.
- Cochlear hair cells have **no tonic discharge**; vestibular fire continuously at rest.
- This basal discharge is why acute unilateral vestibular loss causes vertigo, but acute unilateral hearing loss doesn't cause hallucinated sound.

## Cross-lecture SBA block

### Q: SBA 1 — A 74yo man with HTN presents with sudden vertigo, vomiting, can't sit. HINTS: normal head impulse, right-beating on right gaze and left-beating on left gaze, vertical skew on cover test. Reduced left ear hearing since this morning. Most likely diagnosis?

- **D. Left AICA territory infarction.**
- Every HINTSplus finding is central. Asymmetric hearing loss is the "Plus" giveaway.
- Vestibular neuritis would give abnormal head impulse + unidirectional nystagmus + normal hearing.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 6-month-old fails newborn screening. OAE present bilaterally; ABR absent bilaterally. What does this combination indicate?

- **Auditory neuropathy.**
- OAE present = OHCs working = cochlea fine.
- ABR absent = signal not transmitted past the nerve.
- Important because cochlear implant outcome depends on lesion site relative to synapse.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 30yo woman has brief vertigo episodes lasting < 1 min, triggered by turning over in bed. No hearing loss. Dix-Hallpike provokes torsional upbeating nystagmus with latency. Best next step?

- **Epley manoeuvre.**
- Posterior canal BPPV — diagnosis confirmed by Dix-Hallpike, treated by Epley in the same visit.
- MRI not indicated; PTA not indicated; vestibular suppressants delay compensation.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 45yo carpenter has 5 years of difficulty hearing in restaurants. PTA shows symmetric SNHL with a notch at 4 kHz recovering at 8 kHz. Most likely cause?

- **Noise-induced hearing loss.**
- 4 kHz notch is the signature.
- Presbycusis would slope without a notch; otosclerosis is conductive; schwannoma is unilateral; central processing disorder gives a normal PTA.

### Q: SBA 5 — A 68yo man, 18 months of progressive R-sided hearing loss + recent unsteadiness. Audiogram shows R-sided SNHL. Best imaging finding?

- **Right cerebellopontine angle mass (vestibular schwannoma).**
- Progressive unilateral SNHL in an adult mandates MRI.
- As the tumour grows it compresses CN VIII vestibular fibres → unsteadiness.

### Q: SBA 6 — A 22yo with MS struggles to follow conversation in lecture halls but is fine in quiet rooms. PTA normal. Where is the lesion?

- **Inferior colliculus.**
- The IC filters background noise. MS plaques in the brainstem damage this.
- PTA normal because the cochlea is fine. ABR or speech-in-noise testing needed.

## The reusable cross-lecture skill

When a patient presents with a sensory or balance complaint, **don't stop at the end-organ**. Ask: where along the pathway could this complaint arise, and what test interrogates each level?

The logic is identical in both halves:

- Peripheral test (PTA, head impulse) tests end-organ + nerve.
- Pathway test (ABR, full HINTSplus) tests central function.
- A normal peripheral test does NOT rule out a central lesion.
- The "plus" information (asymmetric hearing in vertigo, speech-in-noise on normal PTA) is often the answer.

This pattern transfers to MS, stroke imaging, optic neuritis, and any sensory-system case in clinical exams.
