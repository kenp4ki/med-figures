## The one idea

**Two symptoms in the same patient don't automatically share a cause.** The trap the case tests is the assumption that because the inner ear produces both hearing and balance, a patient with both hearing loss and vertigo must have one disease. The skill is decomposing the timeline: two symptoms with different onsets, patterns, and mechanisms are usually two separate diseases that coexist.

---

## Section 1 — Case stem

### Q: Summarise the relevant features of the case stem.

- 81 y/o male, 2-day history.
- Sudden, intermittent vertigo crisis. "Room spinning like a carousel" on getting up.
- Episodes triggered by **lying down or getting up**, last minutes, settle when still.
- **No symptoms when standing still.**
- Hearing loss for **10 years**, progressive, NOT temporally linked to vertigo.

---

## Section 2 — Q1: Diagnosis

### Q: What is the diagnosis and why?

- **Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV).**
- Reasoning: intermittent + position-triggered + short duration + no hearing change with episodes = peripheral, canal-only.
- Hearing loss is unrelated (separate, chronic, progressive).

### Q: Walk through the BPPV mechanism.

- **Otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals) detach from the utricle macula.**
- They fall into a semicircular canal (usually **posterior** — sits lowest).
- When the head moves, the loose crystals make endolymph flow more than it should.
- Cupula bends excessively → brain reads exaggerated rotation → vertigo.
- Episode ends when crystals settle → < 1 min duration.

> ⭐ **High-yield**
> BPPV affects **only the semicircular canals**. The cochlea is untouched. **No hearing loss in BPPV.**

### Q: Which manoeuvre confirms the diagnosis?

- **Dix-Hallpike** for posterior canal BPPV.
- Roll test for horizontal canal BPPV.
- Positive sign: **torsional upbeating nystagmus** with latency, lasting < 1 min.

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> Don't diagnose BPPV without performing Dix-Hallpike. The history strongly suggests it but doesn't prove it.

### Q: How is BPPV treated?

- **Epley manoeuvre** for posterior canal BPPV (or Semont alternative).
- Five-step gravity-driven sequence rolls crystals back into the utricle.
- BPPV does **not** cause permanent damage and does **not** affect hearing.
- One of the most satisfying cures in medicine — works in minutes.

## Section 3 — Q2: Supporting features

### Q: Which features of the history support the BPPV diagnosis?

- Sudden, intermittent, characteristic vertigo crisis.
- **Short duration** (minutes, not hours).
- **Triggered by getting up / lying down.**
- **No symptoms when standing still.**
- No NEW hearing symptom accompanying vertigo episodes.

---

## Section 4 — Q3: Are the hearing loss and dizziness related?

### Q: In general, can hearing loss and vertigo come from the same cause?

- Yes — the inner ear handles both. Examples: Ménière's (endolymphatic hydrops), AICA stroke, vestibular schwannoma.
- **In this case, no** — BPPV affects canals only; cochlea untouched.

### Q: What is causing this patient's hearing loss?

- 10 years, progressive, no triggers, older adult → **presbycusis** (age-related sensorineural).
- Affects hearing only — does not cause dizziness.
- Coexists with BPPV; not caused by it.

> 🧠 **Reusable reasoning pattern**
> When a stem gives two symptoms, ask:
> - Did they start together?
> - Do they flare together?
> - Same vascular territory or nerve?
> - Same natural history fits?
>
> If the answers are no → treat as separate diseases.
