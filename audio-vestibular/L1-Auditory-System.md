## Lecturer's voice

**Flagged as exam-critical**
- Central auditory pathway and its 6 stations — repeated as the core of the lecture.
- AICA blood supply tree — flagged as "ignored in neurology".
- Cochlear implant outcome rule: pre-synaptic excellent, post-synaptic poor.
- A normal pure tone audiogram does **NOT** rule out central auditory dysfunction.
- OAE + ABR are complementary; MRI is structural, ABR is functional.

**De-emphasised**
- Inner ear / cochlear anatomy — "learn to ignore the inner ear" (read on own time).
- Bedside whisper test — "as an audiologist I hate this test".
- Labyrinthitis as a diagnosis — over-diagnosed by ENT, exclude stroke first.

**Spent disproportionate time on**
- Central auditory processing and stroke-related hearing impairment.
- Background-noise filtering (inferior colliculus) and speech-in-noise complaints.
- Why MRI alone misses up to 20% of strokes within 24 hours.

## The one idea

Hearing is a **pathway event, not a cochlear event**. The cochlea converts vibration into a signal at the base of the brain; the brain still has to sort, cross-compare, filter, and interpret along a long central pathway. Three exam consequences follow: a normal PTA does NOT mean normal hearing; central lesions cause real hearing complaints with a clean cochlear test; cochlear implants work for cochlea/nerve damage and not for central damage.

## Section 1 — The mechanism chain

### Sound

### Q: What two physical properties define a sound, and what units are they measured in?

- **Frequency / pitch** — Hz (cycles per second) — perceived as tone.
- **Amplitude / loudness** — dB SPL — perceived as volume.
- Human range: **20–20,000 Hz**, **0–120 dB SPL**.

### Q: What frequency range covers human speech, and why does it matter?

- **250–8,000 Hz** — exactly the range tested on a standard audiogram.
- High-frequency hearing declines from age 16 onward; by 50–60 the loss reaches 4–8 kHz.
- This is why presbycusis bites the high frequencies first.

### Outer ear

### Q: Three functions of the outer ear?

- **Capture sound** and funnel it to the tympanic membrane.
- **Amplify ~10 dB** by canal resonance (upper speech frequencies).
- **Protect** deeper structures.

### Middle ear

### Q: What is the function of the middle ear and how much does it amplify?

- **Mechanical amplification, ~20–30 dB** via the ossicular chain.
- Ossicles in order: **malleus → incus → stapes** (smallest bones in the body).
- Overcomes the impedance mismatch between air (outer) and fluid (cochlea).

### Inner ear — cochlea structure

### Q: Name the three cochlear compartments and what they contain.

- **Scala vestibuli** — bony, **perilymph (Na⁺-rich)**.
- **Scala tympani** — bony, **perilymph (Na⁺-rich)**.
- **Scala media** — membranous, **endolymph (K⁺-rich)**, contains the **Organ of Corti**.

> ⭐ **High-yield**
> The K⁺-rich endolymph above the hair cells and Na⁺-rich perilymph below is what makes the unusual K⁺-influx depolarisation work. Memorise this asymmetry.

### Basilar membrane and tonotopy

### Q: Where on the basilar membrane are high vs low frequencies detected?

- **Base (near stapes)** — narrow + tight → **high frequencies**.
- **Apex (far end of coil)** — wide + loose → **low frequencies**.
- Arrangement = **tonotopy** (think xylophone).

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> "Base" of the cochlea = the START near the stapes, NOT "low" or "bottom". Base = high frequencies. The basal hair cells are exposed to every sound entering the cochlea, which is why presbycusis and noise damage hit high frequencies first.

📎 Figure L1.7 — Basilar membrane tonotopy.

### Hair cells

### Q: Compare inner vs outer hair cells.

| | IHC | OHC |
|---|---|---|
| % of fibres | 95% afferent | 95% efferent |
| Direction | Input TO brain | Output FROM brain |
| Function | **Sound transduction** | **Modulation of sensitivity** (contract/expand) |

### Q: Which hair cell type produces otoacoustic emissions?

- **Outer hair cells.**
- Their contraction/expansion produces tiny sounds that travel back out of the ear → picked up by a probe microphone.
- Basis of newborn hearing screening.

📎 Figure L1.10 — IHC vs OHC innervation schematic.

### Transduction — the K⁺ trick

### Q: Walk through the transduction sequence from sound to action potential.

1. Basilar membrane vibrates → stereocilia shear against tectorial membrane.
2. Deflection **toward kinocilium → opens K⁺ channels**.
3. **K⁺ flows IN from K⁺-rich endolymph** → cell depolarises.
4. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → **glutamate** released onto afferent nerve.
5. Action potential in CN VIII.

### Q: What happens when stereocilia deflect away from the kinocilium?

- K⁺ channels **close** → cell **hyperpolarises** → reduced firing.
- This is why hair cells encode the sound wave bidirectionally.

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> The depolarising ion is **K⁺, not Na⁺**. Most excitable cells use Na⁺; cochlear hair cells use K⁺ because the endolymph above them is uniquely K⁺-rich. SBA distractors love this.

📎 Figure L1.11 — Hair cell transduction mechanism.

### Central auditory pathway

### Q: Trace the central auditory pathway from cochlea to cortex.

```
Cochlea → CN VIII
   ↓
1. Cochlear nuclei (pons)         — monaural
   ↓
2. Superior olivary complex (pons) — first crossing; LOCALISATION
   ↓ (bilateral from here)
3. Lateral lemniscus (pons)
   ↓
4. Inferior colliculus (midbrain)  — background-noise filter, attention
   ↓
5. Medial geniculate body (thalamus)
   ↓
6. Auditory cortex (temporal lobe) — pitch, location, voice, music
```

### Q: What is the specific role of the superior olivary complex?

- **Sound localisation.**
- Compares **interaural time difference + level difference** between the two ears.
- First point where information becomes bilateral.

### Q: What is the specific role of the inferior colliculus?

- **Background-noise suppression** and **attention switching**.
- Lets you focus on a single speaker in a noisy room.
- Lesion here → "I can't follow conversation in noisy places" with normal PTA.

> ⭐ **High-yield — direct examiner rule**
> If a stroke or MS patient complains of hearing trouble but pure tone audiometry is normal, **do NOT tell them their hearing is fine**. PTA tests only the cochlea. The lesion is likely in the inferior colliculus or auditory cortex. Order ABR or speech-in-noise testing.

📎 Figure L1.13 — Central auditory pathway.

### Blood supply

### Q: Trace the arterial tree to the inner ear.

```
Basilar artery
   ↓
AICA (anterior inferior cerebellar)
   ↓
Internal Auditory Artery (= Labyrinthine)
   ↓ splits:
   ├── Common Cochlear A. → Main Cochlear A. + Posterior Vestibular A.
   └── Anterior Vestibular A.
```

### Q: Why does AICA occlusion cause a combined audio-vestibular syndrome?

- AICA gives off the internal auditory artery, which supplies **both cochlear and vestibular end-organs**.
- One vessel lesion → **acute hearing loss + vertigo on the same side**.

### Q: Arterial supply to each central auditory nucleus?

| Nucleus | Supply |
|---|---|
| Cochlear nucleus | AICA, indirect PICA |
| Superior olivary complex | Pontine branches, AICA |
| Lateral lemniscus | Pontine branches, SCA, PCA |
| Inferior colliculus / MGB | Thalamo-geniculate, SCA, PCA |

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> **MRI misses up to 20% of strokes** within 24 hours. Sudden audiovestibular loss + vascular risk factors → do NOT discharge on a normal MRI. Admit and observe.

## Section 2 — Classifying hearing loss

### Q: Classify hearing loss by anatomical site.

| Type | Site | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Conductive | Outer / middle ear | Wax, otitis media, otosclerosis |
| Sensorineural | Cochlea or CN VIII | Presbycusis, noise, schwannoma |
| Central | Brainstem / cortex | Stroke, MS, dementia |

### Q: Classify hearing loss by timing.

- **Sudden** — minutes to days. Vascular (stroke) until proven otherwise.
- **Progressive** — months to years. Presbycusis, genetic, schwannoma.

### Causes of conductive loss

### Q: Common causes of conductive hearing loss?

- **Outer ear:** wax (cerumen impaction), foreign body.
- **Middle ear:** **otitis media** (children; bubbles behind TM), **otosclerosis** (stiff stapes).

📎 Figure L1.17 — Otoscopy of conductive causes.

### Causes of sensorineural loss

### Q: Common causes of sensorineural hearing loss?

- **Inner ear:** **noise** (4 kHz notch), **presbycusis** (high-freq slope), **ototoxicity** (cisplatin, aminoglycosides).
- **Nerve:** **vestibular schwannoma** (unilateral → MRI mandatory), **auditory neuropathy** (OAE present, ABR abnormal).

### Q: Audiogram pattern of noise-induced hearing loss?

- **Notch at 4 kHz** with recovery at 8 kHz.
- Distinguishes from presbycusis, which slopes steadily without a notch.

📎 Figure L1.18 — Vestibular schwannoma MRI.

### Causes of central impairment

### Q: Common causes of central hearing impairment?

- **Brainstem:** stroke (AICA, central branches), MS, mitochondrial disease.
- **Cortex:** stroke, dementia, TBI.

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> **Labyrinthitis** is genuinely rare. In older adults with vascular risk + sudden vertigo + hearing loss, AICA stroke is far more likely. Do not accept a labyrinthitis label without excluding stroke.

## Section 3 — Clinical assessment

### Q: What does each hearing test interrogate?

| Test | Site | Question answered |
|---|---|---|
| Tuning fork (Weber/Rinne) | Air vs bone conduction | Conductive vs sensorineural? |
| Pure tone audiometry (PTA) | Cochlea + middle ear | Thresholds, air-bone gap? |
| OAE | Outer hair cells only | Cochlea functional? |
| ABR | Nerve + brainstem pathway | Signal reaching brainstem? |

### Tuning fork tests

### Q: How do you interpret a Weber test?

- **Normal** — equal in both ears.
- **Conductive loss** — lateralises to the **affected (bad) ear**.
- **Sensorineural loss** — lateralises to the **unaffected (good) ear**.

### Q: How do you interpret a Rinne test?

- **Normal / SNHL** — air conduction > bone conduction → **Rinne positive**.
- **Conductive loss** — bone > air → **Rinne negative**.
- Standard fork: **512 Hz**.

> ⚠️ **Easy-to-miss exam detail**
> **128 Hz tuning forks are for sensory examination of limbs**, NOT hearing. Frequent SBA distractor.

### Audiogram reading

### Q: What does each symbol on a pure tone audiogram mean?

- **Red O** — right ear, air conduction.
- **Blue X** — left ear, air conduction.
- **< or >** — bone conduction (mastoid).
- Y-axis inverted: **0 dB at top, lower = worse hearing**.
- Normal threshold ≤ 20 dB.

### Q: How do you distinguish conductive from sensorineural loss on an audiogram?

- **Conductive** — bone conduction normal, air conduction depressed → **air-bone gap**.
- **Sensorineural** — air and bone conduction depressed together → **NO gap**.
- The air-bone gap IS the diagnostic feature of conductive loss.

📎 Figure L1.21 — Audiogram severity bands.
📎 Figure L1.22 — Conductive vs sensorineural audiogram.

### Q: Severity bands on an audiogram?

- **Normal:** 0–20 dB
- **Mild:** 21–40 dB
- **Moderate:** 41–70 dB
- **Severe:** 71–90 dB
- **Profound:** > 90 dB

### Special tests — OAE and ABR

### Q: What does an otoacoustic emissions (OAE) test measure, and what's its main use?

- Tests **outer hair cell function** specifically.
- The cochlea's OHCs produce tiny sounds that a probe microphone records.
- Main use: **newborn hearing screening**; ototoxicity monitoring.

### Q: What does the auditory brainstem response (ABR) test measure?

- Electrical activity along the auditory pathway up to the brainstem / inferior colliculus.
- Scalp electrodes record waves I–VII at characteristic latencies.
- Tests **function**, not structure (MRI tests structure).

### Q: Which ABR wave maps to which anatomical generator?

| Wave | Latency | Generator |
|---|---|---|
| I | 1.9 ms | Auditory nerve |
| II | 3.0 ms | Cochlear nuclei |
| III | 4.1 ms | Superior olivary complex |
| V | 5.9 ms | Inferior colliculus |

(IV = lateral lemniscus, VI = MGB, VII = auditory radiations.)

📎 Figure L1.24 — ABR waves mapped to auditory pathway.

> 🧠 **Reasoning pattern — OAE + ABR are complementary**
> OAE present + ABR absent = cochlea fine, signal lost in nerve = **auditory neuropathy**. OAE absent + ABR absent = cochlear damage. MRI normal does NOT rule out functional pathway damage; ABR can.

📎 Figure L1.23 — Newborn OAE screening.

## Section 4 — Treatment

### Q: Four pillars of hearing loss management?

- Treat the **underlying cause** (ENT surgery, steroids, watch-and-wait).
- **Hearing aids + assistive devices** (loop systems improve signal-to-noise ratio).
- **Cochlear implants** (electrically stimulate CN VIII).
- **Counselling + auditory training**.

### Q: What determines cochlear implant outcome?

- **Pre-synaptic** lesion (cochlea, auditory nerve) → **excellent outcome**.
- **Post-synaptic** lesion (brainstem, cortex) → **poor outcome**, especially for speech-in-noise.
- The implant bypasses only the cochlea; damaged pathway above can't process the input.

> ⚠️ **Trap**
> **Hearing aids do NOT help auditory processing disorders.** Amplification makes noise louder too. In central lesions, hearing aids fail. Identify the site first.

## Cross-lecture integration

> 🧠 **Why does AICA stroke cause both hearing loss AND vertigo?**
> AICA → internal auditory artery supplies BOTH cochlea (via common cochlear A.) AND vestibular end-organs (via anterior + posterior vestibular A.). One vessel lesion takes out both. **See also: L2 — Vestibular blood supply** and **HINTS-plus** ("Plus" component checks hearing).

> 🧠 **Why is the patient in L3 hearing-impaired AND vertiginous if BPPV doesn't affect hearing?**
> The two are unrelated. The 10-year progressive hearing loss is **presbycusis** (this lecture, sensorineural causes). The BPPV is from displaced otoliths (L2/L3). Two diseases in one patient. **See also: L3 — Q3 reasoning**.
