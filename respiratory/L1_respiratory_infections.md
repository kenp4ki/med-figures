## The single unifying intuition

### Q: What single principle explains why two people with the same respiratory virus can have radically different outcomes?
Severity = pathogen factor × host factor × environment.
- Pathogen decides **where** it can infect (receptor tropism).
- Host decides **how badly** it reacts (IFN pathway, barriers, co-morbidity).
- Most airway tissue damage = **host immune response**, not the pathogen directly.

🟦 **Reusable pattern.** When a stem gives "same virus, opposite outcomes" — suspect host response (IFN, age, co-morbidity, smoking).

## Part 1 — Burden and risk

### Q: Why do acute lung infections rank only 4th in global deaths but 1st in global DALYs?
- **DALY = YLL (Years of Life Lost) + YLD (Years Lost to Disability).**
- Respiratory infections kill under-5s and >70s disproportionately.
- A paediatric death loses ~70 YLL; an MI death at 78 loses ~5 YLL.
- DALY arithmetic punishes paediatric mortality → top rank.

### Q: What are the three frequency/severity headline numbers for adult respiratory infections?
- **4–6 colds per year** per adult.
- **500 GP visits per 1,000 patients/year** (bulk of UK primary care).
- **~1% severe disease, <0.005% fatality.**

### Q: What's the age pattern of CAP incidence — the Ewig 2009 numbers?
- <60: ≤1.34 per 1,000.
- 70–79: **7.39**
- 80–89: **17.62**
- ≥90: **35.81** (~100× the young-adult rate).

📎 Figure L1.5 — Hospitalised CAP incidence per 1,000 by age class (Ewig 2009)

### Q: Why do children <2 and adults >65 sit at the two peaks of risk?
- **<2:** immune system still maturing; narrow airways plug with mucus easily.
- **>65:** immunosenescence; weak cough reflex; swallowing problems → aspiration; co-morbidities.

### Q: What are the 5 risk-factor buckets for pneumonia?
| Bucket | Key items | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Demographic/lifestyle | Age <2 or >65, smoking, alcohol | Major |
| Social | Child contact <15y, poverty, overcrowding | Moderate |
| Medications | Inhaled corticosteroids, immunosuppressants, **PPIs** | Moderate |
| Medical history | COPD, HIV, **hyposplenism**, previous pneumonia | Major |
| Pathogen-specific | Geography, animal contact, healthcare contact | Case |

🟥 **Easy-to-miss:** PPIs (gastric colonisation → aspiration) and hyposplenism (can't clear encapsulated bacteria like *S. pneumoniae*).

### Q: Which respiratory infection is explicitly excluded from this lecture?
***Mycobacterium tuberculosis.*** "So distinct — will be covered separately." Don't use this material to answer TB questions.

### Q: How often have respiratory pandemics occurred historically, and what causes most?
- Roughly **every 20–30 years** since records began.
- **Majority = influenza** (1918, 1957, 1968, 1977, 2009).
- COVID-19 was the exception to the "always influenza" rule.

## Part 2 — Bacterial pneumonia

### Q: What is the depth-of-airway rule for inflammation severity?
- Higher in the tract → less consequential (sinusitis = annoying).
- Lower in the tract → blocks gas exchange → dangerous.
- Alveolar inflammation = medical emergency because fluid blocks O₂ diffusion.

### Q: What are the 5 respiratory inflammation terms by anatomical level?
```
Nose/sinuses       → SINUSITIS
Trachea/bronchi    → BRONCHITIS
Bronchioles        → BRONCHIOLITIS   (dangerous in infants)
Alveoli            → PNEUMONIA       (medical emergency)
Lung tissue (any)  → PNEUMONITIS     (umbrella term)
```

📎 Figure L1.10 — Anatomical map of respiratory tract inflammations and alveolar histology

### Q: What are the three downstream pathways of bacterial pneumonia?
- **Lung injury** → arterial hypoxaemia → ARDS.
- **Bacteraemia** → organ infection → sepsis.
- **Systemic inflammation** → multi-organ dysfunction → deterioration.

📎 Figure L1.11 — Downstream consequences of bacterial pneumonia (Quinton 2018)

### Q: What defines ARDS — all three Berlin criteria?
- **Acute onset** (within 1 week of insult).
- **Bilateral opacities** on CXR or CT.
- **PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio < 300 mmHg.**
- Must be **non-cardiogenic** (normal LV function excludes HF-driven oedema).

### Q: Why does the PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio work as a severity marker?
It normalises PaO₂ for the oxygen the patient is receiving. Example: PaO₂ 90 on 50% FiO₂ → ratio 180 (ARDS). Same PaO₂ on room air (21%) → ratio 570 (not ARDS). A low ratio means the lung can't oxygenate blood even with extra supplied O₂.

### Q: What's the difference between an "opportunistic pathogen" and a "pathobiont"?
- **Opportunistic pathogen** — microbe that exploits a *host* change (e.g. immunosuppression). Classic: *S. pneumoniae*.
- **Pathobiont** — commensal microbe in the *wrong anatomical site*. E.g. gut flora aspirated into lung.

🟦 **Discrimination point.** Most bacterial pneumonia causes are opportunistic/pathobiont (already living on you). **Respiratory viruses are NEITHER** — they are true pathogens.

### Q: What are the dominant bacterial pathogens in CAP, HAP and VAP?
| Setting | Top organisms | Why different? |
|---|---|---|
| **CAP** | ***S. pneumoniae* (40–50%)**, *M. pneumoniae*, *S. aureus*, *H. influenzae* | Upper-airway commensals |
| **HAP** | *S. aureus*, ***P. aeruginosa***, *Klebsiella*, *E. coli* | Skin + Gram-neg hospital flora |
| **VAP** | ***P. aeruginosa* (25%)**, ***S. aureus* (20%)**, *Enterobacter* | ET tube bypasses all upper defences |

🟨 **Must-know headline:** *S. pneumoniae* = Gram-positive, extracellular, opportunistic; causes 40–50% of CAP.

### Q: What's the typical vs atypical pneumonia distinction?
- **Typical:** *S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*, *M. catarrhalis* → acute, rapid onset.
- **Atypical:** *M. pneumoniae*, *C. pneumoniae*, *L. pneumophila* → slow, mild, "walking pneumonia".
- Same empirical approach; atypicals just need **longer duration** (7–14 days).

### Q: Why don't penicillins work against *Mycoplasma pneumoniae*?
***M. pneumoniae* has no cell wall at all.** No cell wall = no target for β-lactams (which block cell-wall transpeptidation). Treat with a **macrolide** (e.g. clarithromycin) which targets the ribosome.

🟥 **Slide error alert.** Slide 22 calls *M. pneumoniae* "Gram-negative". This is wrong — it doesn't Gram-stain either way because it has no cell wall. A common SBA trap.

### Q: What are the five CURB-65 components and their thresholds?
```
C — Confusion
U — Urea > 7 mmol/L          (hospital only; use CRB-65 if no bloods)
R — Respiratory rate ≥ 30/min
B — BP < 90 systolic AND/OR ≤ 60 diastolic
65 — Age ≥ 65 years
```
1 point each. **Score 0 = home; 1–2 = consider admission; 3–5 = urgent admission + IV antibiotics.**

### Q: Why is each CURB-65 component a marker of severity?
- **C**onfusion → cerebral hypoperfusion or sepsis-driven encephalopathy.
- **U**rea → renal hypoperfusion from sepsis (or dehydration from fever).
- **R**R ≥ 30 → respiratory compensation failing.
- **B**P drop → septic shock / haemodynamic compromise.
- **65** → poor physiological reserve.

### Q: What is the single most important intervention in severe bacterial pneumonia?
**Rapid empirical IV antibiotics. Do NOT wait for culture.**
- Risk of sepsis death rises ~7–8% per hour of delay.
- Cultures are sent *alongside* antibiotics, not before.
- Broad-spectrum first, narrow down later.

🟨 **Classic SBA trap.** If the stem asks "next best step" for CURB-65 ≥3, the answer is **empirical IV antibiotics now**, not "await culture".

### Q: What's the empirical antibiotic ladder for CAP by CURB-65?
| CURB-65 | First-line CAP regimen |
|---|---|
| 0 | Amoxicillin PO (clarithro/doxy if pen-allergic) |
| 1–2 | Amoxicillin + clarithromycin PO |
| 3–5 | BenzylPenicillin **IV** + clarithromycin PO |
| Duration | 5–7 days (**7–14 for atypicals**) |

### Q: What's the empirical regimen for severe HAP?
**Tazocin (piperacillin-tazobactam) IV ± gentamicin IV.** Broader Gram-negative cover than CAP regimens because HAP flora = *Pseudomonas*, *Klebsiella*, etc.

### Q: What are the one-line mechanisms for the two main antibiotic classes used in CAP?
- **Penicillins** (amoxicillin) — β-lactam; bind cell-wall PBPs → block **transpeptidation** → osmotic lysis.
- **Macrolides** (clarithromycin) — bind bacterial ribosome → block **protein synthesis**; cover atypicals (no cell-wall requirement).

### Q: Why does a patient develop hypoxaemia in pneumonia?
- Alveoli fill with **exudate, neutrophils, fibrin**.
- Gas can no longer diffuse across the alveolar-capillary membrane.
- Mechanical blockade of gas exchange — not bacteria consuming O₂.

## Part 3a — Respiratory viruses

### Q: What single rule lets you predict viral disease severity from its biology?
**Where a virus binds = where it infects = how severe it is.**
- Upper-tract receptor → URTI → mild, transmits well.
- Lower-tract receptor → alveolar infection → severe, transmits poorly.

📎 Figure L1.14 — Viral receptor tropism: sialic acid distribution and ACE2 in the respiratory tract

### Q: Where are α2,6 and α2,3 sialic acids expressed, and which viruses use them?
- **α2,6 SA** — upper tract → used by H1N1 seasonal flu HA → mild URTI, high transmission.
- **α2,3 SA** — lower tract → used by H5N1 avian flu HA → severe pneumonia, poor transmission.

🟦 **Evolutionary rule.** Viruses long-established in humans adapt to bind **upper-tract** cells because that's what gets coughed into the next host. H5N1 is severe precisely because it's poorly adapted to us.

### Q: Which receptor does SARS-CoV-2 use, and why does it cause a "split" clinical picture?
- Binds **ACE2** via spike (S) protein.
- ACE2 expressed **high in nasal epithelium AND high in type 2 pneumocytes**; low in between.
- Explains: anosmia/ageusia + sudden jump to alveolar pneumonia with few "middle" symptoms.
- **Smokers have ↑ ACE2** at both sites → worse COVID.

### Q: Which receptors does rhinovirus use?
- **Major group** → **ICAM-1**.
- **Minor group** → **LDLR**.
- Both → upper-tract infection → common cold.

### Q: Why do we keep catching colds (rhinovirus)?
- **100–300+ serotypes** (RV-A/B/C).
- Each serotype gives long-lasting antibody immunity *for that serotype only*.
- The pool of novel serotypes is effectively inexhaustible.

### Q: How does influenza evade prior immunity?
- **Drift** — minor HA/NA point mutations each season → new seasonal strain.
- **Shift** — major gene reassortment (e.g. in a pig co-host) → novel HA/NA combo → **pandemic potential**.
- 2009 H1N1 swine flu = shift event.

### Q: Why does RSV repeatedly infect children despite having only ~2 serotypes?
**Natural antibodies wane rapidly** — the adaptive immune response is short-lived. Limited surface antigen mutation, but you just lose your antibodies within years.

### Q: What makes RSV particularly dangerous in infants?
- **Leading cause of infant hospitalisation** in the developed world.
- 50% infected in year 1; essentially all by year 3.
- **1% develop severe bronchiolitis.**
- Narrow infant airways occlude easily when inflamed.
- Risk factors: **prematurity, congenital heart/lung disease.**

📎 Figure L1.15 — Clinical signs of severe RSV bronchiolitis in infants

### Q: What are the examinable signs of severe RSV bronchiolitis?
- Nasal flaring, chest wall retractions.
- Expiratory wheeze, prolonged expiration, rales/rhonchi.
- Croupy cough.
- **Hypoxaemia + cyanosis.**
- Tachypnoea with **apnoeic episodes**.

### Q: Compare surface antigen strategy across the four viruses.
| Virus | Serotypes | Re-infection because... |
|---|---|---|
| **Rhinovirus** | 100–300+ | Endless new serotypes |
| **Influenza** | Few, but drifts/shifts | **Antigenic drift + shift** |
| **RSV** | ~2 | **Antibodies wane rapidly** |
| **SARS-CoV-2** | Novel, now drifting | Wane + mutation |

📎 Figure L1.20 — Comparative surface antigen biology: rhinovirus, influenza, RSV, SARS-CoV-2

### Q: In the slide-34 case study (two pandemic-H1N1 patients, one home in 3 days, one in ITU for 19), what explains the difference?
**"It is all host response to that infection."** — Harker, verbatim.
- Same virus, similar demographics.
- Difference is how the host's immune system reacts.
- → leads directly into the host-immunity section.

## Part 3b — Host immunity (cascade)

```
UPPER AIRWAY (nose → trachea)
  │
  ├─ Tier 1: Physical barriers
  │     ├─ Hairs & cilia → particulate capture
  │     └─ Mucus → traps pathogens
  │
  ├─ Tier 2: Physical + chemical
  │     ├─ Tight junctions  (block systemic invasion)
  │     ├─ Mucociliary escalator  (constant clearance)
  │     ├─ Antimicrobials  (defensins, lactoferrin)
  │     ├─ PRRs  (TLRs, RIG-I)
  │     └─ Interferon pathway  (curbs viral infection)
  │
  └─ Tier 3: Immune fortification
        ├─ INNATE (resident + recruited)
        │     alveolar macrophages · dendritic cells
        │     neutrophils · NK cells · monocytes
        │
        └─ ADAPTIVE (≥1 week lag)
              CD8 T cells (cytotoxic + memory)
              B cells → plasma cells (antibody)
LOWER AIRWAY (alveoli)
```

### Q: What's the design logic of airway defence across the three tiers?
- Barrier thickness **decreases** as you descend (trade-off with gas exchange).
- Active immune surveillance **increases** as you descend (compensates).
- At the alveolus: 1 cell thick, but patrolled by alveolar macrophages + recruited immune cells.

📎 Figure L1.17 — Cellular architecture of the respiratory epithelium (Invernizzi 2020)

### Q: What is the mucociliary escalator and why does smoking matter?
- Coordinated cilial beating sweeps mucus upward toward the pharynx to be swallowed.
- **Smoking paralyses cilia** → mucus pools → bacterial colonisation → recurrent LRTI.
- A direct mechanism linking smoking → pneumonia risk.

### Q: What are the two main PRR types in the respiratory epithelium and what do they detect?
- **TLRs** (cell surface + endosomes) — detect LPS, flagellin, **dsRNA** (TLR3).
- **RIG-like receptors** (cytosolic) — detect cytosolic viral ssRNA.
- Two systems needed: one for extracellular pathogens, one for viruses already inside the cell.

### Q: What do type I interferons do at the cellular level?
- Bind IFNAR1/IFNAR2 on neighbouring cells (and autocrine).
- **Upregulate antiviral proteins** (via STAT1/STAT2 → ISGs).
- **Trigger apoptosis** of infected cells.
- Net effect: "interfere" with viral replication — hence the name.

### Q: What does the IFN-I signalling pathway look like from viral RNA to antiviral proteins?
```
Viral RNA
   │
   ▼  TLR3 (endosome) / RIG-I (cytosol)
   ▼
UNC93B1 → TRIF, TRAF3, TBK1, NEMO
   │
   ▼
IRF3, IRF7 activated
   │
   ▼
Type I IFN secreted → IFNAR1/IFNAR2 (on nearby cells)
   │
   ▼
STAT1/STAT2 → IRF9 → ISGs → antiviral proteins + apoptosis
```

📎 Figure L1.18 — Type I interferon pathway: inborn and acquired defects in severe SARS-CoV-2

### Q: What did Zhang 2020 and Bastard 2020 reveal about severe SARS-CoV-2?
- **Zhang**: inborn loss-of-function mutations in **IFNAR1, IRF7, TLR3** cause severe disease.
- **Bastard**: ~**10%** of life-threatening cases had **neutralising auto-antibodies** against type I IFNs.
- Both groups had no prior history of severe respiratory infection — COVID unmasked hidden susceptibility.

🟨 **Examinable takeaway.** "Interferon dysfunction is a known cause of severe viral infections" — stated verbatim by Harker.

### Q: Which 5 innate immune cells matter in the airway?
| Cell | Role |
|---|---|
| Alveolar macrophages | Clear surfactant, phagocytose pathogens |
| Dendritic cells | **Bridge to adaptive** — present antigen |
| Neutrophils | First responders; **double-edged** (cause damage) |
| NK cells | Kill virus-infected cells |
| Monocytes | Recruited → become inflammatory macrophages |

### Q: Why are neutrophils "double-edged" in respiratory infection?
- Arrive early, in large numbers.
- Release proteases + ROS indiscriminately.
- Kill pathogens BUT damage host tissue.
- A major source of the "host response damage" that drives severe symptoms in young, otherwise-healthy patients.

### Q: Why does adaptive immunity take ≥1 week to develop and why does that matter?
- B and T cells need time to expand specific clones + undergo class-switching.
- Innate immunity does the early work.
- **This is why vaccines help** — they pre-prime adaptive memory so it's ready on day 1 of real exposure.

### Q: What does Jozwik 2015 show about CD8 T cells in respiratory viral infection?
- More **CD8 tetramer+ cells in BAL fluid** correlates with:
  - Lower cumulative **symptom score** (r = −0.69).
  - Lower cumulative **viral load** (r = −0.67).
- Direct evidence that airway-resident CD8 memory protects against reinfection.

📎 Figure L1.19 — Adaptive immunity and CD8 tetramer cells vs symptom and viral load (Jozwik 2015)

### Q: Why is IgA enriched in the upper airway but IgG in the alveolar space?
- **Upper airway** — protease-rich mucus → need a protease-resistant antibody. **IgA dimer** (J-chain-linked) is exceptionally stable; exported via **poly-Ig receptor**.
- **Alveolar space** — thin-walled (single cell + capillary). Plasma **IgG** diffuses straight across. No special machinery needed.

🟦 **Design rule.** The airway chooses its antibody class to match the physical environment of each compartment.

## Interplay & synthesis

### Q: Why does influenza often kill via secondary bacterial pneumonia?
- Virus damages epithelium → cilia lost → barrier reduced.
- Bacteria (*S. pneumoniae*, *S. aureus*) opportunistically colonise alveoli.
- 1918 flu autopsy data: *S. pyogenes* 36%, pneumococcus 29%, *H. influenzae* 25% in fatal cases.

📎 Figure L1.22 — Faces of influenzo-pneumonic septicaemia, 1918–19

### Q: What's the timing pattern of secondary bacterial infection after a respiratory virus?
- **Day 0–9:** viral load peaks.
- **Day 12–15:** bacterial load rises.
- **Exam cue:** patient with viral URTI who deteriorates **5–10 days later** with new fever / purulent cough / lobar consolidation → think secondary bacterial pneumonia.

📎 Figure L1.23 — Rhinovirus-induced secondary bacterial infection in COPD (Mallia 2012)

### Q: What proportion of rhinovirus-infected COPD patients develop bacterial co-infection, and why does it matter?
- **55%** develop concurrent bacterial infection.
- Rhinoviruses are the **most common cause of asthma AND COPD exacerbations**.
- Viral bronchiolitis in infancy also predisposes to later asthma development.

### Q: Compare the two-phase treatment logic that applies to severe viral pneumonia (e.g. COVID).
| Phase | What's driving the pathology | Treatment | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Early** | Viral replication | **Antiviral** | Remdesivir, Paxlovid |
| **Late** | Host immune response | **Anti-inflammatory** | Dexamethasone, Tocilizumab |

🟥 **Trap.** Anti-inflammatories given too early suppress the antiviral response. Antivirals given too late are useless (virus already cleared — damage is host-driven).

### Q: What are the three SARS-CoV-2 treatment buckets and an example of each?
- **Supportive** — O₂, fluids, analgesia.
- **Preventive (vaccines)** — viral vector (Oxford/AZ), **mRNA** (Pfizer/BioNTech). Target: spike.
- **Therapeutic** — Remdesivir (RdRp blocker), Paxlovid (protease inhibitor), monoclonal nAbs (casirivimab/imdevimab).

### Q: What's the 3-circle Venn framework for assessing respiratory-infection risk on any case stem?
- **Pathogen risk** — virulent? novel?
- **Host (in-born) risk** — genetic (IFN pathway), age, co-morbidity, sex.
- **Environmental risk** — obesity, pollution, smoking, existing infection.
- Overlap zone = severe disease.

🟦 **Reusable.** Use this Venn to dissect any "why severe?" exam stem.

## Reusable reasoning patterns

### Q: What are the 8 reasoning patterns that transfer to unseen exam stems from this lecture?
1. **Depth predicts severity** — higher in tract = milder; lower = hypoxaemia.
2. **Receptor → tropism → severity** — α2,6 SA upper / α2,3 SA lower / ACE2 split.
3. **"Same pathogen, different outcome" = host response** — check IFN, age, co-morbidity.
4. **"Severe X after flu" = secondary bacterial** — timing 5–10 days post-viral.
5. **Bacterial pneumonia = opportunistic commensal** — not "caught"; think broken defences.
6. **"Walking pneumonia" = atypical** — young, functional, interstitial → macrolide (no β-lactams).
7. **CURB-65 ≥ 3 = urgent IV antibiotics** — don't wait for culture.
8. **Early phase antiviral, late phase anti-inflammatory** — match treatment to current driver.

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA — A 62-y man, 5 days post-abdominal surgery, fever + productive cough. Rapidly deteriorates. CXR: bilateral patchy opacities. FiO₂ 50%, PaO₂ 90 mmHg. Echo normal. Diagnosis?
A. Cardiogenic pulmonary oedema
B. **ARDS** ✓
C. HAP without ARDS
D. PE

**Why B:** PaO₂/FiO₂ = 180 (< 300) ✓, acute onset ✓, bilateral opacities ✓, non-cardiogenic (normal LV) ✓.
**Not A:** echo rules out HF.
**Not C:** he has HAP *plus* ARDS — question asks which best fits given oxygenation.
**Not D:** PE doesn't give bilateral inflammatory opacities.

### Q: SBA — 72-y woman, RR 34, BP 85/55, confused, urea 9, RLL consolidation. CURB-65 and next step?
A. Score 2 — PO amoxicillin, discharge
B. Score 3 — PO amoxicillin, observe
C. **Score 5 — urgent IV benzylpenicillin + clarithromycin** ✓
D. Score 5 — wait for sputum culture

**Why C:** C✓ U✓ R✓ B✓ 65✓ = 5/5. High severity → urgent admission + IV empirical.
**Not D:** delayed antibiotics = sepsis mortality climbs hourly.

### Q: SBA — 24-y student, 10 days dry cough, malaise, still at lectures. CXR bilateral interstitial infiltrates. Best empirical?
A. Amoxicillin
B. Benzylpenicillin IV
C. **Clarithromycin** ✓
D. Piperacillin-tazobactam

**Why C:** walking-pneumonia vignette → atypical (*Mycoplasma*). Macrolide needed — atypicals have no cell wall or live intracellularly, so β-lactams fail.

### Q: SBA — 4-month-old, January, 3 days coryza → noisy breathing, chest wall retractions, nasal flaring, wheeze. Born 32 weeks. Sat 91%. Most likely pathogen?
A. *S. pneumoniae*
B. Influenza A
C. **RSV** ✓
D. Rhinovirus

**Why C:** infant + winter + bronchiolitis signs + **prematurity** (classic RSV risk factor). RSV = leading cause of infant hospitalisation.

### Q: SBA — 35-y from a poultry-farming trip in SE Asia, rapid severe dyspnoea, PaO₂/FiO₂ 180, bilateral infiltrates, **household contacts asymptomatic**. Most likely pathogen?
A. H1N1
B. **H5N1 avian flu** ✓
C. *S. pneumoniae*
D. SARS-CoV-2

**Why B:** zoonotic exposure + severe LRT disease + poor household transmission = H5N1 binding α2,3 SA (lower tract). Severe in infected individuals, but too deep in lung to cough out effectively → low human-to-human spread.

### Q: SBA — 70-y COPD smoker, 8 days after rhinovirus infection, new fever + **purulent sputum**, LLL consolidation. Explanation?
A. Progression of rhinovirus
B. AECOPD without infection
C. **Secondary bacterial pneumonia** ✓
D. PE

**Why C:** timing (8 days post-viral), sputum turning purulent, new consolidation. Mallia 2012: 55% of rhinovirus-infected COPD patients get bacterial co-infection.

### Q: SBA — Two previously-healthy adults develop life-threatening SARS-CoV-2. Most likely molecular defect?
A. ACE2 mutations
B. **Loss-of-function in IFNAR1 / IRF7 / TLR3** ✓
C. HA mutations
D. Decreased IgA

**Why B:** Zhang & Bastard 2020 — inborn IFN-pathway defects OR auto-antibodies against type I IFN account for a major fraction of severe COVID in previously-healthy individuals.
**Not A:** less ACE2 would be protective (no receptor).
**Not C:** HA is an influenza protein.

### Q: SBA — Why is *M. pneumoniae* intrinsically resistant to β-lactams?
A. It produces β-lactamase
B. **It has no cell wall** ✓
C. It lives intracellularly
D. Altered PBPs

**Why B:** Mycoplasmas have no cell wall. β-lactams target cell-wall transpeptidation → no target = no effect.

### Q: SBA — COPD patient treated for severe pneumonia asks if the dexamethasone she received killed the bacteria. Best explanation?
A. Yes — dexamethasone is bactericidal
B. **No — dexamethasone suppressed the harmful inflammatory response; antibiotics killed the bacteria** ✓
C. Dexamethasone blocks bacterial adhesion
D. Dexamethasone targets viruses

**Why B:** steroids are anti-inflammatory, not antimicrobial. They dampen host-driven lung injury.

### Q: SBA — Why is urea >7 mmol/L part of CURB-65?
A. Dehydration causes pneumonia
B. **Marker of renal hypoperfusion from sepsis** ✓
C. Urea is toxic to alveoli
D. Distinguishes bacterial from viral

**Why B:** raised urea in pneumonia reflects either renal hypoperfusion from septic shock or dehydration from fever — both physiological red flags.

### Q: SBA — Patient with pneumonia develops hypoxaemia. Mechanism?
A. Bacteria consume alveolar oxygen
B. **Alveoli filled with exudate and cells → blocked gas exchange** ✓
C. Reflex bronchoconstriction
D. Pulmonary vasoconstriction reduces flow

**Why B:** mechanical blockade of O₂ diffusion across the alveolar-capillary membrane.
**Not D:** hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction is a *response* to local hypoxia, not a cause of systemic hypoxaemia.

### Q: SBA — A novel virus binds α2,3 sialic acids. Based on receptor alone, predicted clinical pattern?
A. Mild URTI, high transmission
B. **Severe LRT disease, poor transmission** ✓
C. Asymptomatic in adults, severe in infants
D. GI-predominant symptoms

**Why B:** α2,3 SA is lower-tract. Deep infection → severe pneumonia; aerosols don't leave the lung well → poor transmission. Classic H5N1 profile.

### Q: SBA — What distinguishes antigenic drift from antigenic shift in influenza?
A. Drift = major reassortment; shift = point mutations
B. **Drift = point mutations in HA/NA; shift = whole-segment reassortment** ✓
C. Drift affects NA only; shift affects HA only
D. Both terms mean the same thing

**Why B:** drift is gradual (seasonal epidemics); shift is abrupt and can produce pandemic strains (e.g. 2009 H1N1).

### Q: SBA — A patient has pneumonia. Which antibody class would you expect to dominate IN THE ALVEOLAR SPACE?
A. IgA
B. IgE
C. **IgG** ✓
D. IgM

**Why C:** thin-walled alveoli allow plasma IgG to diffuse in. IgA dominates the mucus-rich upper airway. IgE and IgM are not compartment-dominant in this axis.
