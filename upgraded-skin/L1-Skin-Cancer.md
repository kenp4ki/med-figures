## Core mechanism — the one idea everything derives from

```
        EPIDERMIS              ← no blood vessels, no lymphatics
   ───────────────────         ← "in situ" lives here = trapped = curable
              │
   ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ ← DERMO-EPIDERMAL JUNCTION (DEJ)
              ▼                ← crossing here = "invasive" = can spread
        DERMIS                 ← blood vessels + lymphatics = metastasis route
```

📎 Figure L1.01 — Epidermis: keratinocyte and melanocyte (cells of origin)

### Q: What single concept underlies the danger of every skin cancer?

- The danger is set by **whether (and how fast) the cancer crosses the dermo-epidermal junction (DEJ)** into the dermis.
- Epidermis has no vessels or lymphatics → cancer trapped above the DEJ cannot metastasise.
- Once across the DEJ, the cancer accesses lymphatics and blood vessels → systemic disease becomes possible.

### Q: What does "in situ" mean for a skin cancer, and what does it imply for treatment?

- **In situ** = malignant cells limited to the epidermis; **DEJ not breached**.
- Cannot metastasise → **local treatment is curative** (topical, cryotherapy, excision).
- Examples: melanoma in situ, lentigo maligna, Bowen's disease (SCC in situ).

### Q: What's the difference between horizontal (radial) and vertical growth?

- **Horizontal/radial:** sideways spread within the epidermis. Lesion gets bigger but stays above DEJ.
- **Vertical:** downward through the DEJ into the dermis — the clinically dangerous phase.
- Subtypes differ massively in how long they spend in each phase — this drives prognosis.

### Q: Why is nodular melanoma the most aggressive subtype despite being uncommon?

- Has **minimal or absent horizontal growth phase** — goes vertical early.
- Crosses the DEJ before the lesion gets big enough to alarm the patient.
- Reaches lymphatics/blood vessels early → highest metastatic potential.

⭐ Pattern: speed of vertical growth, not size or appearance, is what kills patients with melanoma. Breslow thickness captures this.

### Q: Why can a mole never become a BCC or SCC?

- Different **cell of origin** — moles are melanocyte proliferations; BCC/SCC are keratinocyte cancers.
- The lineages do not interconvert.
- Exam corollary: *changing pigmented lesion arising from a mole* → think melanocyte → melanoma. *Scaly red patch on sun-exposed skin* → think keratinocyte → AK/Bowen's/SCC spectrum.

### Q: What is Breslow thickness measured between, and what does it determine?

- Vertical distance from the **granular layer of the epidermis** to the **deepest tumour cell** (mm).
- Determines (1) the **wide local excision margin** and (2) **prognosis** (thicker = worse).
- Single most important prognostic variable in primary melanoma.

## Background — Fitzpatrick, biopsy choice, normal naevi

### Q: What does the Fitzpatrick scale capture, and why is it an exam-relevant risk marker?

- Six skin-type categories from **Type 1** (always burns, never tans) to **Type 6** (never burns, deeply pigmented).
- Captures **baseline UV-protection level**: paler types burn easily → higher risk of UV-driven cancers (melanoma, BCC, SCC).
- Concept-only — don't memorise the descriptions verbatim.

📎 Figure L1.02 — Fitzpatrick skin type scale

### Q: What are the four biopsy types and when do you use each?

- **Excision** — entire lesion removed with margin. Default for suspected melanoma.
- **Punch** — small full-thickness cylinder. Good for SCC/BCC where the lesion is uniform; or for huge lesions where excision is impractical (LM/LMM).
- **Incisional** — wedge through part of a large lesion.
- **Shave** — flat slice off the surface. Good for pyogenic granuloma and superficial benign lesions; not for suspected melanoma (too shallow).

📎 Figure L1.03 — Skin biopsy techniques: shave, punch, incision, excision

### Q: What are the three normal melanocytic naevus types, and how do they differ histologically?

- **Junctional** — melanocytes at the dermo-epidermal junction. **Flat**, well-circumscribed.
- **Compound** — some at DEJ, some migrated into dermis. **Slightly raised**.
- **Intradermal** — most melanocytes in dermis. **Raised, often skin-coloured** (deep pigment less visible).

⭐ Trap: a raised mole is NOT automatically a malignant mole — it just means the melanocytes have migrated deeper. Common reason for unnecessary referral.

## Card 1 — Melanoma

### Epidemiology and risk

### Q: What's the headline epidemiology stat for melanoma?

- ~**4% of skin cancers** but **>75% of skin-cancer deaths**.
- Commonest cancer in **15–34 year olds**; highest incidence > 80 yo.
- Worldwide incidence rising.

### Q: Which UV pattern drives melanoma risk, and why does it matter clinically?

- **Intermittent severe blistering sunburn**, especially in **childhood** — distinct from cumulative chronic UV.
- Explains why melanoma can occur in young patients (late teens, 20s, 30s).
- Contrast with BCC/SCC, which need cumulative lifetime exposure → older patients.

### Q: Which naevus features increase melanoma risk?

- **>100 melanocytic naevi** total, OR
- **>5 atypical naevi**, OR
- **Giant congenital melanocytic naevi**.

⭐ Trap: most melanomas (>50%) arise **de novo** from previously normal skin, not from a pre-existing mole. Atypical/large/multiple naevi raise risk; they don't account for most melanomas.

### Q: Which genetic and immune factors raise melanoma risk?

- **CDKN2A** mutations — familial melanoma.
- **Xeroderma pigmentosum** — defective DNA repair → very young presentation.
- Immunosuppression, especially solid-organ transplant recipients.

### Pathophysiology

### Q: Which signalling pathways are mutated in melanoma, and which is the named exam target?

- **MAPK pathway** (NRAS → BRAF → MEK → ERK) and **PI3K/AKT/mTOR**.
- **BRAF** is the high-yield mutation (40–60% of melanomas).
- BRAF status is tested on tumour samples → determines targeted-therapy eligibility and informs prognosis.

📎 Figure L1.08 — Melanoma signalling: MAPK and PI3K/AKT pathways

### In situ vs invasive — the prognostic dividing line

### Q: What separates melanoma in situ from invasive melanoma?

- **In situ** — malignant melanocytes proliferate within the epidermis only.
- **Invasive** — cells have crossed the DEJ into the dermis.
- The DEJ crossing is what enables metastasis; everything else (Breslow, staging, SLNB) follows from this.

📎 Figure L1.09 — Melanoma-in-situ vs invasive melanoma (diagrams)
📎 Figure L1.10 — Horizontal vs vertical growth phases

### Subtypes

### Q: Name the five melanoma subtypes and the one variant.

- **Superficial spreading melanoma (SSM)** — commonest.
- **Nodular** — most aggressive.
- **Lentigo maligna** (in situ) vs **lentigo maligna melanoma** (invasive).
- **Acral lentiginous** (incl. **subungual**).
- **Amelanotic** — no pigment.

### Q: How does superficial spreading melanoma classically look and behave?

- Flat-ish lesion with **irregular border**, **multiple colours**, often with areas of **regression** (depigmented patches = immune-cleared).
- Often arises from a pre-existing mole that starts changing.
- Long horizontal growth phase → ABCDE criteria detect it.

📎 Figure L1.11 — Superficial spreading melanoma (clinical, irregular multicoloured)
📎 Figure L1.12 — Superficial spreading melanoma (lateral spread diagram)

### Q: How does nodular melanoma look, and why is it easy to miss?

- **Uniform-coloured, dome-shaped, rapidly growing bump.**
- Doesn't look like the "ABCDE multi-coloured mole" picture students are trained to spot.
- Vertical growth dominates from the start → already invasive when seen.

📎 Figure L1.14 — Nodular melanoma (clinical, dome-shaped)
📎 Figure L1.15 — Nodular melanoma (vertical growth diagram)

### Q: What's the difference between lentigo maligna and lentigo maligna melanoma?

- Same lesion, different stage.
- **Lentigo maligna** = melanoma in situ on chronically sun-damaged head/neck (older patients).
- **Lentigo maligna melanoma** = invasive form (cells through the DEJ); a new bumpy/raised area within an LM patch suggests progression.

📎 Figure L1.16 — Lentigo maligna (in situ, face)
📎 Figure L1.17 — Lentigo maligna melanoma (scalp, invasive)

### Q: Why does acral lentiginous melanoma break the "darker skin = lower melanoma risk" rule?

- ALM occurs on **palms, soles, fingers, toes, nail unit** — sites that don't see much UV.
- So it's **not** UV-driven, and the protective effect of melanin doesn't apply.
- Relatively over-represented in patients of South Asian or Afro-Caribbean origin.
- Easy to miss because soles aren't routinely examined.

📎 Figure L1.18 — Acral lentiginous melanoma (sole)

### Q: What is Hutchinson's sign and what does it indicate?

- **Pigment extending onto the proximal nail fold skin**.
- Suggests melanocyte spread beyond the nail unit.
- Strongly suspicious for **subungual melanoma** (a subtype of ALM).

📎 Figure L1.19 — Subungual melanoma (pigmented streak)
📎 Figure L1.20 — Hutchinson's sign (proximal nail fold pigment)

|---|---|---|---|
| Hutchinson's sign | Negative | Negative | Often **positive** |
| Stability | Stable for years | **Clears** as nail grows | Progressive |
| Dermoscopy | Uniform thin band | **Blood globules** | Atypical melanocytic pattern |

### Q: What is amelanotic melanoma and what mimics it?

- Melanoma without melanin pigment → skin-coloured/pink lump.
- **Hardest melanoma to diagnose** — relies on history (rapid growth, risk factors) + biopsy.
- Main mimic: **pyogenic granuloma** (benign post-traumatic vascular proliferation).

📎 Figure L1.24 — Amelanotic melanoma (pink nodule, ear)

### Investigations

### Q: What is ABCDE for melanoma?

- **A**symmetry
- **B**order irregular / blurred / ragged / bleeding
- **C**olour variation within the lesion
- **D**iameter > 6 mm (soft rule, not absolute)
- **E**volving (changing in size, shape, or colour over time)

📎 Figure L1.13 — ABCDE signs (mole vs melanoma)

### Q: How does the Glasgow 7-point checklist work, and what triggers a 2WW referral?

- **Major (2 points each):** change in size, shape, colour.
- **Minor (1 point each):** diameter ≥ 7 mm, inflammation, crusting/bleeding, mild itch / altered sensation.
- **3+ points → 2-week-wait (2WW) referral** to dermatology.
- **Dermoscopy suspicious of melanoma → 2WW** regardless of points.

⭐ Two independent triggers for 2WW: 3+ points OR dermoscopy alone. Either is sufficient.

### Q: What is the biopsy of choice for suspected melanoma, and why?

- **Excision biopsy** with **2 mm peripheral margin down to subcutaneous fat**.
- Removes the entire lesion → no sampling error.
- Punch biopsy risks sampling a benign-looking part of a heterogeneous melanoma and falsely reassuring.

### Q: When is the punch/incisional biopsy used instead of excision for suspected melanoma?

- **Lentigo maligna / LMM on head, neck, scalp** — the only routine exception.
- Lesions are often very large; complete excision with margin would mean removing a chunk of cheek or scalp.
- Sample the most suspicious area (sometimes multiple "mapping biopsies").

### Staging

### Q: What does Breslow thickness do clinically?

- Determines the **wide local excision margin** for the second surgical step.
- Is the dominant **prognostic** variable.
- Measured in mm, granular layer to deepest tumour cell.

### Q: At which stage is sentinel lymph node biopsy first offered?

- **Clinical stage IB and above.**
- Stage IA disease is too thin to plausibly have reached lymph nodes.
- SLNB upstages IB+ patients into stage III if positive.

### Q: When are PET-CT and MRI brain indicated in melanoma?

- **Stage III and IV only** (lymph node involvement or distant metastasis confirmed/suspected).
- Looking for systemic and brain metastases to map disease burden.
- Earlier stages don't warrant whole-body imaging.

### Treatment ladder

### Q: Walk through the standard surgical pathway for newly diagnosed primary melanoma.

1. **Excision biopsy** — 2 mm margin to subcutaneous fat (also diagnostic).
2. **Wide local excision (WLE)** — margin determined by Breslow depth.
3. **Sentinel lymph node biopsy** — if stage IB+; offered with informed consent (risks: seroma, lymphoedema).
4. **PET-CT + MRI brain** — if stage III/IV.
5. **MDT discussion** mandatory for every new diagnosis.

### Q: What does targeted therapy in melanoma actually target?

- **BRAF inhibitors** (e.g. vemurafenib) — only work if BRAF-mutated.
- **MEK inhibitors** (e.g. trametinib) — downstream of BRAF.
- **Immune checkpoint inhibitors** — anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab), anti-PD-1 (pembrolizumab, nivolumab); often combined.
- Used in **advanced** disease, not for resectable primaries.

📎 Figure L1.31 — Targeted therapy in melanoma (drug targets on MAPK pathway)

⭐ Drug names are NOT examined; the **targets and concept** are. BRAF status is tested because it gates targeted therapy.

### Prognosis and exam trap

### Q: What's the single biggest exam trap for melanoma management?

- ⚠️ **Trap:** sentinel LN biopsy is **not** for all melanomas — only stage IB and above. PET-CT/MRI brain are **not** for all melanomas — only stage III/IV. A thin, non-ulcerated stage IA melanoma gets WLE alone.

### The cascade — how a sun-damaged keratinocyte becomes invasive cancer

```
   Chronic cumulative UV
            │
            ▼
   ┌──────────────────┐    ┌──────────────────┐    ┌──────────────────┐
   │ ACTINIC          │    │ BOWEN'S DISEASE  │    │ INVASIVE SCC     │
   │ KERATOSIS        │ ─► │ (SCC in situ)    │ ─► │                  │
   │                  │    │                  │    │                  │
   │ PARTIAL-thickness│    │ FULL-thickness   │    │ FULL-thickness   │
   │ dysplasia of     │    │ dysplasia of     │    │ dysplasia        │
   │ epidermis        │    │ epidermis        │    │   +              │
   │                  │    │                  │    │ INVASION of      │
   │ NO DEJ invasion  │    │ NO DEJ invasion  │    │ dermis           │
   │                  │    │                  │    │                  │
   │ Pre-cancerous    │    │ In situ          │    │ Can metastasise  │
   │ Topical / cryo   │    │ Topical / PDT /  │    │ Surgical         │

   └──────────────────┘    └──────────────────┘    └──────────────────┘
```

### Q: What is the unifying logic of the AK → Bowen's → SCC cascade?

- Same cell type (keratinocyte), same driver (UV).
- Difference between stages: **how much of the epidermal thickness is dysplastic, and has the DEJ been breached**.
- Once across the DEJ → **invasive SCC**, with metastatic potential.

### Actinic keratosis (AK)

### Q: How does an AK present clinically?

- **Rough, scaly, erythematous patch** on sun-exposed skin.
- Often **felt before seen** ("sandpaper" texture).
- Slow, non-tender, may be itchy.
- Multiple lesions common in chronically sun-damaged patients ("field damage").

📎 Figure L1.36 — Actinic keratosis (multiple, scalp)
📎 Figure L1.37 — Actinic keratosis (close-up keratotic horn)
📎 Figure L1.38 — Actinic keratosis (forehead field damage)

### Q: What features suggest an AK is becoming an invasive SCC?

⭐ The four red-flag features (lecturer's red callout):

- **Tender**
- **Thickened**
- **Ulcerated**
- **Enlarging**

Per-lesion progression rate to SCC: 0.025–16% per year (low individually, accumulates across many lesions).

### Q: Which SCC-specific risk factors should you ask about?

- Cumulative lifetime UV exposure (older patients, gardeners, builders).
- **Smoking** — especially **lip SCC**.
- **Long-standing wound or scar** — chronic inflammation drives keratinocyte dysplasia ("Marjolin-like").
- Immunosuppression — solid-organ transplant patients have much higher SCC risk.

### Bowen's disease

### Q: How does Bowen's disease look, and how does it relate to SCC?

- **Sharply demarcated erythematous plaque**, slow-growing, with **superficial scale**.
- Histologically = **full-thickness dysplasia of the epidermis** with no DEJ invasion = SCC in situ.
- Still amenable to topical and destructive treatments.

### Invasive SCC

### Q: What are the classical clinical features of invasive SCC?

- **Enlarging, ill-defined, scaly or crusted lump or nodule**.
- May ulcerate.
- **Tender or painful** (unlike BCC).
- Rapidly growing (weeks to months).
- May arise within pre-existing AK or Bowen's, or de novo.

### Treatment

### Q: How are AK and Bowen's treated, and what's the named exam exclusion?

Topical and destructive options (epidermis-only disease):

- **Diclofenac gel (Solaraze)**
- **5-fluorouracil cream (Efudix)**
- **Imiquimod cream (Aldara)**
- **Tirbanibulin ointment (Klysiri)** — AK only
- **Cryotherapy** (liquid nitrogen)
- **Photodynamic therapy** (daylight or red light)
- **Surgery** — shave or curettage and cautery

⚠️ Trap: **radiation is NOT used for AK**. It's reserved for invasive cancers (BCC, SCC) when surgery isn't an option.

### Q: What is the treatment of choice for invasive SCC?

- **Surgical excision.**
- Topical creams will not clear an invasive SCC.
- Risk-stratified follow-up (low / high / very high) determines duration and frequency — concept only, no need to memorise the table.

### Keratoacanthoma — the SCC mimic

### Q: What is a keratoacanthoma and how is it managed?

- Common, **rapidly growing**, dome-shaped lesion with central keratin plug.
- May **regress spontaneously** with scarring.
- **Clinically indistinguishable** from well-differentiated SCC.
- Default management: **surgical excision** for histological confirmation.

## Card 3 — BCC

### Q: What is the headline epidemiology of BCC?

- **Most common skin cancer overall** (BCC : SCC ≈ 4 : 1).
- Driven by cumulative lifetime UV → older patients, men > women.
- **Locally invasive but rarely metastasises** — defining biological feature.

### Subtypes — clinical recognition

### Q: How do you recognise a nodular BCC?

- **Pearly, shiny, translucent** appearance.
- **Arborising telangiectasia** (branching tree-like vessels across the surface).
- **Rolled edges with central crater/ulcer** ("rodent ulcer").
- **Common on the face**.
- Slow-growing; may bleed spontaneously.

📎 Figure L1.48 — Nodular BCC (clinical, central crater)
📎 Figure L1.49 — Nodular BCC (dermoscopy: arborising telangiectasia)
📎 Figure L1.50 — Nodular BCC (pearly, translucent)

### Q: How does superficial BCC differ from nodular BCC?

- **Pink, scaly, irregular plaque** rather than a shiny nodule.
- Common on **upper trunk/shoulders**, not the face.
- Slow-growing; superficial enough to respond to topical therapy.

📎 Figure L1.51 — Superficial BCC (pink, scaly plaque)

### Q: Why is infiltrative BCC the dangerous BCC subtype?

- **Skin-coloured, waxy** with **ill-defined edges** — easy to miss.
- Spread is more extensive than the visible lesion.
- **Prone to recur** → **Mohs micrographic surgery** is needed.

📎 Figure L1.52 — Infiltrative BCC (waxy, ill-defined edges)

### Q: How is pigmented BCC managed and what's the trap?

- Brown, blue, or grey pigmentation; can be nodular or superficial.
- **May resemble melanoma**.
- **Treat as melanoma until proven otherwise** — i.e. excision biopsy with margin, then re-plan based on histology.

📎 Figure L1.54 — Pigmented BCC (melanoma mimic)

### Treatment ladder

### Q: How is BCC treated, by subtype?

| Subtype | First-line treatment | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Superficial | Topical (imiquimod, 5-FU) or cryotherapy | Disease is superficial → local Rx works |
| Nodular | Surgical excision (4 mm margin) or curettage + cautery | Bulky → creams won't clear it |
| Infiltrative at high-risk site | Mohs micrographic surgery | Edges ill-defined → real-time margin mapping |
| Recurrent | Mohs micrographic surgery | Same logic — clear margins essential |

Other options: **radiotherapy** (frail patient or awkward site), and **prevention** with **nicotinamide** or **acitretin** in high-recurrence-risk patients.

### Q: What is Mohs micrographic surgery and when is it indicated?

- Layer-by-layer excision with **real-time microscopy** of each layer until margins are clear.
- Removes the **minimum** amount of normal skin while achieving the **highest** cure rate.
- Indicated for: **aggressive subtypes (esp. infiltrative)**, **critical sites (central face / H-zone)**, **recurrent BCC**.

📎 Figure L1.53 — Mohs micrographic surgery (diagram)
📎 Figure T.06 — Face high-risk zones for BCC (Mohs indication)

⚠️ Trap: Mohs has **not** replaced standard excision for most BCCs. It's reserved for the indications above.

## Card 4 — Benign mimics

### Q: What is seborrhoeic keratosis and how do you recognise it?

- Benign **keratinocyte** proliferation — cannot become melanoma (different cell line).
- **Stuck-on, warty, cerebriform** (brain-like) surface, well-circumscribed, often multiple, on trunk.
- Older patients; sites of friction.
- Diagnose clinically; reassure; no NHS removal because benign.

📎 Figure L1.26 — Seborrhoeic keratosis (distribution, trunk)
📎 Figure L1.27 — Seborrhoeic keratosis (cerebriform close-up)

### Q: What is a dermatofibroma and what is the named diagnostic sign?

- Benign fibrous reaction, often post-trauma (insect bite — patient may not recall).
- **Pinch sign positive** = lesion **dimples inward** when squeezed laterally (tethered to underlying fibrosis).
- Feels firm/fibrous on palpation.
- Diagnose clinically; reassure; no biopsy needed.

📎 Figure L1.28 — Dermatofibroma (pinch sign positive)
📎 Figure L1.29 — Dermatofibroma (lesion unsqueezed)

### Q: What is a pyogenic granuloma and why is it the key amelanotic-melanoma mimic?

- Benign vascular proliferation, often **post-traumatic**, on extremities.
- Small, red, fleshy, friable — bleeds easily.
- Looks like an amelanotic melanoma → usually **shave biopsy** to get histology and remove the lesion.

🧠 Pattern: any time pigment is *absent* but a lump is rapidly growing on a high-trauma site, you need histology — pyogenic granuloma vs amelanotic melanoma is decided in the lab, not the clinic.

## Cross-cutting patterns

### Q: What is the cell-of-origin filter for any skin lesion stem?

- **Brown/black + arising from a mole + changing** → melanocyte → **melanoma**.
- **Pink/red + scaly + sun-exposed site** → keratinocyte → **AK / Bowen's / SCC / BCC**.
- Caveat: pigmented BCC and seborrhoeic keratosis can both look brown but are keratinocyte-origin — discriminate by other features (BCC = pearly + vessels; SK = warty + stuck-on).

### Q: What's the speed × pain × site discriminator for non-pigmented lesions?

| Speed | Pain | Most likely diagnosis |
|---|---|---|
| Years, no change | None | AK / Bowen's / seborrhoeic keratosis |
| Months, slow | Bleeds, painless | **BCC** |
| Weeks–months, rapid | **Painful** | **SCC** |
| Weeks–months, rapid (pigmented, from mole) | Usually painless | **Melanoma** |

🧠 Pattern: this single grid solves most of the case-based stems. Speed and pain do most of the work.

### Q: What's the biopsy-choice rule across all skin cancers?

- **Suspected melanoma (most sites)** → **excision biopsy** with 2 mm margin.
- **Suspected LM/LMM on head/neck** → punch/incisional biopsy of worst area.
- **Suspected BCC/SCC, diagnostic uncertainty** → punch biopsy.
- **Clinically obvious nodular BCC for surgery** → skip diagnostic biopsy → therapeutic excision.
- **Suspected pyogenic granuloma** → shave biopsy (gets histology and removes lesion).

### Q: What investigations are triggered at which melanoma stage?

- **Stage IA** (thin, non-ulcerated): excision + WLE only.
- **Stage IB and above**: + sentinel lymph node biopsy.
- **Stage III** (lymph node involvement): + PET-CT, MRI brain.
- **Stage IV** (distant metastasis): same — PET-CT, MRI brain to map disease.

### Q: How do risk-factor patterns let you guess the cancer type from history alone?

- Young patient + **intermittent severe childhood blistering sunburn** → melanoma.
- Older patient + **cumulative lifetime UV / outdoor occupation** → BCC or SCC.
- Smoker + **lip lesion** → SCC.
- Long-standing **wound or scar** that's now changing → SCC.
- **Transplant patient** → all skin cancers ↑, especially SCC and BCC.
- Patient of darker skin type with lesion on **sole, palm, or under nail** → acral lentiginous melanoma.

### Q: What does "treat as melanoma until proven otherwise" actually mean?

- For a pigmented lesion that *might* be melanoma but might also be pigmented BCC or atypical SK.
- Default to **excision biopsy with 2 mm margin** and let histology decide.
- Cost of over-treatment is small; cost of missing melanoma is large.

## Interleaved SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 (Tutorial Case 1) — A 70-year-old woman presents with a mole on her left leg present for years that has changed in size, shape and colour over 2 months and become itchy. No PMHx/FHx of skin cancer. Burns easily, enjoys gardening. Which is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Actinic keratosis
B. BCC
C. Bowen's disease
D. **Melanoma (superficial spreading)**
E. SCC

📎 Figure T.01 — Case 1: SSM, leg

**Answer: D.** Long-standing pigmented mole + recent change in size, shape AND colour (3 major Glasgow points) + Fitzpatrick I/II + UV exposure = SSM.

- A wrong: AK is a flat, scaly red patch — not a pigmented mole.
- B wrong: BCC is pearly with arborising vessels, not pigmented (rare exception aside).
- C wrong: Bowen's is a flat erythematous plaque, not pigmented.
- E wrong: SCC is keratotic and tender, not a changing pigmented mole.

### Q: SBA 2 (Tutorial Case 2) — An 84-year-old woman has a 3-year history of a lesion on her left arm. It has become **itchy, crusty and rough** over the last year but is **not painful**. She is on chemotherapy for breast cancer, lived in Australia, and had blistering childhood sunburns. Plays tennis outdoors. Most likely diagnosis?

A. **Actinic keratosis**
B. BCC
C. Keratoacanthoma
D. Melanoma
E. SCC

📎 Figure T.02 — Case 2: AK, arm

**Answer: A.** Long Hx (3 years), flat scaly red patch, **non-tender**, classic chronic UV profile.

- B wrong: BCC is shiny/pearly with telangiectasia, classically on head/neck.
- C wrong: KA is rapidly growing (weeks) with central keratin plug.
- D wrong: melanoma is pigmented and arising from a mole; not described.
- E wrong: SCC is rapidly growing AND tender — both missing here.

### Q: SBA 3 (Tutorial Case 3) — A 51-year-old woman has a lesion on her right shoulder present for **3 years with no change in size, shape or colour**, but itchy, crusty and rough. Mother had melanoma. Lived in Italy, cycles outside. Lesion is warty, stuck-on. Most likely diagnosis?

A. Actinic keratosis
B. BCC
C. Bowen's disease
D. Melanoma
E. **Seborrhoeic keratosis**

📎 Figure T.03 — Case 3: seborrhoeic keratosis, shoulder

**Answer: E.** **No change in 3 years** rules out melanoma. Warty, stuck-on, cerebriform = SK. Family Hx + UV exposure don't override stable lesion behaviour.

- A wrong: AK is flat and scaly, not warty/stuck-on.
- B wrong: BCC is shiny/pearly with vessels.
- C wrong: Bowen's is a flat erythematous plaque.
- D wrong: stability over 3 years strongly argues against melanoma.

### Q: SBA 4 (Tutorial Case 4) — An 85-year-old woman has a lesion on her right cheek for 7 months that has changed in shape over the last 3 months, **bleeding and crusty/rough**. PMHx of SCC face. Severe blistering childhood sunburns. On exam: shiny erythematous nodule with arborising telangiectasia and central ulceration. Most likely diagnosis?

A. Actinic keratosis
B. **Nodular BCC**
C. Bowen's disease
D. Melanoma
E. SCC

📎 Figure T.04 — Case 4: nodular BCC, cheek

**Answer: B.** Slow growth + bleeding + pearly + **arborising telangiectasia** + face = nodular BCC.

- A wrong: AK is a flat scaly patch, not a pearly nodule.
- C wrong: Bowen's is a flat erythematous plaque.
- D wrong: not pigmented; no mole.
- E wrong: SCC is rapidly growing AND tender.

### Q: SBA 5 (Tutorial Case 5) — A 79-year-old woman has a 1-month history of a lesion on her forehead that is **rapidly growing and painful**. PMHx BCC and SCC. Lived in India with severe blistering childhood sunburns. Most likely diagnosis?

A. Actinic keratosis
B. BCC
C. Bowen's disease
D. Melanoma
E. **Invasive SCC**

📎 Figure T.05 — Case 5: invasive SCC, forehead

**Answer: E.** **Short Hx + painful + rapidly growing + sun-damaged background + previous keratinocyte cancers** = invasive SCC.

- A wrong: AK is slow and non-tender.
- B wrong: BCC is slow-growing and usually painless.
- C wrong: Bowen's is slow-growing and non-tender.
- D wrong: not pigmented; no melanocytic features.

### Q: SBA 6 — Which is NOT a treatment option for actinic keratosis?

A. Cryotherapy
B. Topical 5-fluorouracil
C. Imiquimod
D. Photodynamic therapy
E. **Radiation therapy**

**Answer: E.** AK is partial-thickness epidermal dysplasia and responds to topical/destructive therapies. Radiation is reserved for invasive BCC/SCC when surgery isn't an option.

### Q: SBA 7 — Which statement about BCC management is correct?

A. Diagnostic biopsy is required prior to definitive treatment in all cases
B. Topical treatments are appropriate for all subtypes of BCC
C. Mohs micrographic surgery has replaced traditional excision for most BCCs
D. **Mohs micrographic surgery is indicated for an infiltrative BCC on the nose**
E. Radiotherapy is contraindicated in BCC

📎 Figure T.06 — Face high-risk zones for BCC (Mohs indication)

**Answer: D.** Infiltrative subtype + central facial site = both Mohs indications.

- A wrong: clinically obvious nodular BCC can go straight to excision.
- B wrong: topicals only for superficial BCC.
- C wrong: Mohs is reserved for aggressive subtypes / critical sites / recurrent.
- E wrong: radiotherapy is an option (especially in unfit patients).

### Q: SBA 8 — Which is true regarding keratinocyte carcinoma / dysplasia?

A. Actinic keratoses typically evolve into squamous cell carcinoma
B. Basal cell carcinoma is more likely than squamous cell carcinoma to metastasise
C. Squamous cell carcinoma is three times more common than basal cell carcinoma
D. **Keratinocyte carcinomas are more common in men than in women**
E. Actinic keratoses are most commonly found on sun-protected sites

**Answer: D.**

- A wrong: only a small proportion of AKs evolve to SCC.
- B wrong: BCC virtually never metastasises.
- C wrong: BCC > SCC, ratio ≈ 4 : 1.
- E wrong: AKs are on sun-exposed skin.

### Q: SBA 9 — A 78-year-old woman has a 1.2 mm thick, ulcerated melanoma on her back. Histology confirms invasive disease. What is the most appropriate next staging step?

A. PET-CT and MRI brain
B. **Wide local excision and sentinel lymph node biopsy**
C. Reassurance with annual review
D. Topical imiquimod
E. Radiotherapy

**Answer: B.** Breslow 1.2 mm + ulceration → at least stage IB → SLNB is offered alongside WLE.

- A wrong: PET-CT/MRI brain only at stage III/IV.
- C wrong: invasive melanoma is never just observed.
- D wrong: imiquimod is for superficial BCC and AK.
- E wrong: not standard primary management for resectable invasive melanoma.

### Q: SBA 10 — A 35-year-old man of South Asian heritage presents with a 6-month history of a darkly pigmented, slowly enlarging patch on the sole of his foot. Which subtype of melanoma is most likely?

A. Superficial spreading melanoma
B. Nodular melanoma
C. Lentigo maligna melanoma
D. **Acral lentiginous melanoma**
E. Amelanotic melanoma

**Answer: D.** Acral site + ethnic profile = ALM. Sole isn't UV-exposed, breaking the usual "darker skin = lower risk" rule.

### Q: SBA 11 — Which is **false** regarding melanoma?

A. Melanoma can arise in the eye
B. Fair skin is a risk factor
C. Pre-existing atypical naevi increase the risk
D. **Most melanomas arise from a pre-existing naevus**
E. Melanoma accounts for >75% of skin cancer deaths

**Answer: D.** **>50% of melanomas arise de novo** from previously normal skin. Atypical/giant/multiple naevi are risk factors but most melanomas are still de novo.

### Q: SBA 12 — Which feature most strongly supports a diagnosis of nodular basal cell carcinoma?

A. Tender, rapidly growing nodule
B. Asymmetric pigmented mole with irregular border
C. **Pearly, shiny nodule with arborising telangiectasia and rolled edges**
D. Stuck-on warty plaque with cerebriform surface
E. Erythematous scaly plaque on the lower leg

**Answer: C.** Textbook nodular BCC. Pearly + arborising vessels is essentially pathognomonic.

### Q: SBA 13 — A 65-year-old woman with a long-standing leg ulcer at the site of a previous burn injury notes that one part of the wound edge has become heaped up, bleeding, and tender. What should you suspect?

A. Pyogenic granuloma
B. **Squamous cell carcinoma arising in chronic inflammation**
C. Melanoma
D. Basal cell carcinoma
E. Bowen's disease

**Answer: B.** Long-standing wound/scar + new tender, bleeding, raised area = SCC arising in chronic inflammation.

### Q: SBA 14 — What is the most appropriate biopsy technique for a 4 cm pigmented patch on the cheek of an 80-year-old woman, suspected to be lentigo maligna?

A. Excision biopsy with 2 mm margin
B. **Punch or incisional biopsy of the most suspicious area**
C. Shave biopsy
D. Cryotherapy without biopsy
E. Imiquimod cream without biopsy

**Answer: B.** Head/neck exception. A 4 cm cheek patch can't be excised wholesale without major reconstruction.

### Q: SBA 15 — A 60-year-old man presents with a 4-week history of a rapidly growing, dome-shaped lesion with a central keratin plug on the dorsum of his hand. Non-tender. Clinically difficult to distinguish from a well-differentiated SCC. Most appropriate management?

A. Reassurance with review in 3 months
B. Topical 5-FU
C. Cryotherapy
D. **Surgical excision and histology**
E. Radiotherapy

**Answer: D.** Keratoacanthoma can regress, but is **clinically indistinguishable** from well-differentiated SCC. Standard management is surgical excision so histology can decide.

### Q: SBA 16 — A patient has a thin (Breslow 0.4 mm), non-ulcerated melanoma. Which adjunctive investigation is most appropriate at this stage?

A. PET-CT
B. MRI brain
C. Sentinel lymph node biopsy
D. **None — proceed with wide local excision alone**
E. Bone scan

**Answer: D.** Stage IA disease → WLE alone. SLNB starts at IB; PET-CT/MRI brain at III/IV.

### Q: SBA 17 — A patient asks why BRAF testing is performed on her newly diagnosed melanoma. Best explanation?

A. To confirm the histological diagnosis
B. **To determine eligibility for targeted therapy and inform prognosis**
C. To assess depth of invasion
D. To stage lymph node involvement
E. To rule out a benign lesion

**Answer: B.** BRAF status determines whether BRAF/MEK inhibitors can be used and gives prognostic information. Diagnosis = histology. Depth = Breslow. Staging = AJCC.

### Q: SBA 18 — Which combination of features most strongly differentiates SCC from BCC in an exam stem?

A. SCC is shiny and pearly; BCC is rough and crusty
B. **SCC is rapidly growing and tender; BCC is slow-growing and usually painless**
C. SCC almost never metastasises; BCC frequently metastasises
D. SCC is more common than BCC
E. SCC has arborising telangiectasia; BCC is keratotic

**Answer: B.** Speed and pain are the cleanest discriminators. All other options invert the truth.
