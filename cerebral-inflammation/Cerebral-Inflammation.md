## Part 1 — Definitions of terms

The suffix **-itis** means inflammation. The prefix names the layer.

### Q: What does meningitis mean?

Inflammation of the **meninges** — the membranes covering the brain and cord. Caused by viral or bacterial infection.

### Q: What does encephalitis mean?

Inflammation of the **brain parenchyma** itself. Caused by infection (most often viral) or autoimmune mechanisms.

### Q: What does myelitis mean? Etymology hook?

Inflammation/infection of the **spinal cord**. From Greek *myelos* = "marrow" — the cord sits inside the vertebral column the way marrow sits inside bone.

### Q: What does encephalomyelitis mean?

Inflammation of **both brain and spinal cord** simultaneously.

### Q: What is cerebral vasculitis (= angiitis)?

Inflammation of the **walls of cerebral blood vessels**. The two terms are interchangeable.

⭐ The lecturer explicitly flagged the definitions as examinable: terms are often misused, so the right vocabulary scores easy marks.

## Part 2 — The Blood-Brain Barrier

**The one intuition.** The BBB is border control for the brain. Three things at any border: a wall, gates with passport checks, and patrol agents stabilising both. Endothelial tight junctions = wall. Specific transporters = gates. Astrocytes + basal lamina = patrol stabilisers.

**BBB structure (lumen → brain):**

```
  Lumen (blood)
       |
  Endothelial cell — TIGHT JUNCTION — Endothelial cell    [wall]
       |
  Basal lamina                                            [stabiliser]
       |
  Astrocyte end-feet (glia limitans)                      [patrol]
       |
  Brain parenchyma
```

📎 Figure L1.4 — Anatomy of the BBB
📎 Figure L1.5 — Astrocyte end-feet defining the cerebral vasculature

### Q: What is the BBB in one sentence?

A selectively permeable barrier formed by **tight junctions between cerebral capillary endothelial cells**, stabilised by basal lamina and astrocyte end-feet, that controls what enters and leaves CNS tissue.

### Q: What 19th-century experiment proved the BBB exists?

📎 Figure L1.2 — Historical demonstration of the BBB

IV-injected dye accumulated in **skin, intestine, kidney and liver** but the **brain stayed unstained** — proving cerebral capillaries exclude things peripheral capillaries let through.

### Q: How densely vascularised is the brain, and why does it matter?

📎 Figure L1.3 — Scanning EM cast of CNS vasculature

- **No neuron is more than 100 µm from a capillary.**
- Brain takes ~20% of cardiac output.
- Implication: the BBB is *long* — every one of those capillaries is part of the border.

### Q: Three structural layers of the BBB working from blood to brain?

1. **Endothelial cells with tight junctions** — the wall.
2. **Basal lamina** — stabilising membrane.
3. **Astrocyte end-feet** (glia limitans) — patrol.

Pericytes also contribute.

### Q: What is the Virchow-Robin space?

The **perivascular space surrounding a vessel as it dives from the pial surface into the brain**. It is contiguous with subarachnoid CSF and is where immune cells accumulate in viral encephalitis and MS (perivascular cuffing).

### Q: What is the glia limitans?

A **continuous membrane formed by astrocyte end-feet** wrapping cerebral vessels. GFAP staining of astrocytes alone outlines the vessels perfectly because the end-feet trace them.

### Q: How does anything get across the BBB?

By **active transport via specific membrane transporters** — both:

- **Influx** transporters (e.g. GLUT1 for glucose) — into CNS.
- **Efflux** transporters — back out to blood.

Passive diffusion across tight junctions is essentially blocked.

### Q: Why are CNS infections hard to treat with peripheral antibiotics?

Most antibiotics that cross peripheral capillaries by diffusion **cannot cross the BBB** — there is no transporter for them. Hence high-dose IV regimens for CNS infections (e.g. bacterial meningitis) and reliance on lipophilic drugs that can cross (e.g. aciclovir for HSV).

### Q: What is the BBB disruption cascade?

📎 Figure L1.6 — BBB disruption sequence

```
1. Endothelial cells separate          (gaps in wall)
        ↓
2. Fibrinogen leaks out                (clotting protein into brain)
        ↓
3. Astrocyte end-feet retract          (glia limitans pulls back)
        ↓
4. Collagen builds up around vessel    (small-vessel disease, stiffening)
        ↓
5. Astrogliosis                        (reactive astrocyte change)
```

### Q: Which protein is the named marker of BBB breakdown?

**Fibrinogen.** A clotting protein that should never be in brain parenchyma. Its presence on histology is the classic marker of BBB disruption.

⚠️ **Trap.** In option lists, "fenestrated capillaries" is wrong for cerebral vessels — CNS capillaries are **non-fenestrated**, and that's the whole point of the BBB. (Circumventricular organs are an exception, beyond this lecture.)

## Part 3 — Cerebral inflammation: the three diseases

**The one intuition.** Three failure modes, three anatomical layers:

```
THE WALL (BBB)
    |
    +-- OUTSIDE the wall (meninges)  →  MENINGITIS
    |
    +-- BREACHED, parenchyma infected →  ENCEPHALITIS
    |
    +-- INSIDE, immune system attacks myelin → MULTIPLE SCLEROSIS
```

Layer drives symptoms (meningeal signs vs focal brain signs vs disseminated demyelination), investigations (CSF vs MRI), and treatment (kill bug vs suppress immunity).

### Pathophysiology and histology

| Feature | Meningitis | Encephalitis | MS |
|---|---|---|---|
| Layer affected | Subarachnoid space | Brain parenchyma | White matter, periventricular |
| Mechanism | Bug invades CSF | Bug or autoimmune attack | Autoimmune attack on myelin |
| Histology hallmark | Subarachnoid pus / cell exudate | **Perivascular cuffing** | **Perivascular cuffing + plaques** |

📎 Figure L1.1 — Meningitis vs viral encephalitis histology
📎 Figure L1.11 — Perivascular immune-cell cuffing in MS

### Q: What is perivascular cuffing?

**Immune cells aggregated around a brain blood vessel** in the perivascular (Virchow-Robin) space — the histological signature that immune cells crossed the BBB via the vasculature.

### Q: Which two diseases share perivascular cuffing histology?

**Viral encephalitis and multiple sclerosis.** Same cellular pattern, different driving cause — clinical context distinguishes them.

### Q: Which immune-cell markers are seen in MS perivascular infiltrates?

- **CD3** — pan-T-cell
- **CD8** — cytotoxic T-cell
- **CD20** — B-cell

Both T-cells and B-cells contribute → why anti-CD20 antibody therapies work in MS.

### Q: What is the post-mortem histological hallmark of meningitis?

A **purulent exudate filling the subarachnoid space** — cells packed where there should only be CSF.

🧠 **Pattern.** Perivascular cuffing means "the immune system breached the BBB." The BBB disruption cascade you learned in Part 2 is what enables it. Same picture, different trigger (virus in encephalitis, autoimmunity in MS).

### Causes and epidemiology

Bacterial meningitis pathogens (memorise the population for each):

| Organism | Population | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Meningococcal (*N. meningitidis*) | UK adults, halls of residence | **Most common UK** |
| Pneumococcal (*S. pneumoniae*) | Adults | **Most serious** |
| Hib (*H. influenzae b*) | Children <5, unvaccinated | Reduced by Hib vaccine |
| Group B Streptococcus | **Newborns** | Main cause in newborns |

Encephalitis cause hierarchy: **viral first, HSV at the top.** Other named viruses: measles, varicella (chickenpox), rubella. Other categories: insect-borne (mosquito, tick — Africa/Far East), bacterial/fungal, trauma (breach of brain coverings), autoimmune.

### Q: Most common cause of bacterial meningitis in UK adults?

**Meningococcal infection — *Neisseria meningitidis*.** Lecturer: "by far the most common cause." University halls of residence are a hotspot.

### Q: Why do newborns get streptococcal meningitis specifically?

**Group B Streptococcus** is acquired **perinatally from the maternal genital tract.** Any neonatal meningitis case → think GBS first.

### Q: Why is Hib much less common now?

The **Hib vaccine** introduced in childhood schedules dramatically reduced incidence. Unvaccinated children remain at risk.

### Q: Most common viral cause of encephalitis?

**Herpes simplex virus (HSV).** Other viral causes: measles (returning with reduced vaccination), varicella, rubella.

### Q: How do bacteria typically reach the meninges?

Initial **upper respiratory tract infection** → **haematogenous spread** through the blood to the brain.

### Q: What did COVID brain post-mortems show, and what does it imply?

- Virus found in **cerebral vascular endothelial cells** but **NOT in brain parenchyma**.
- Inflammation centred on **vessels**, not parenchyma.
- → COVID neurological disease is **vasculitis**, not encephalitis.

⚠️ "Most common ≠ most serious." Meningococcal is the most common bacterial meningitis in UK; **pneumococcal is the most serious** (highest mortality and morbidity). Watch which the stem is asking for.

### Clinical presentation — the discrimination table

This is the highest-yield table in the lecture. The tutorial drills it.

| Feature | Meningitis | Encephalitis |
|---|---|---|
| Meningeal signs (nuchal rigidity, photophobia) | **Often** | Sometimes |
| Mental status alteration | Often | **Nearly always** |
| Seizures | **Uncommon** | **Often** |
| Focal neuro findings | ~50% later | **Hallmark** |

### Q: Classic features of meningitis?

Sudden onset of:

- **Fever**
- **Severe headache**
- **Nuchal rigidity** (stiff neck)
- **Photophobia**
- Nausea/vomiting, drowsiness
- ± non-blanching rash (meningococcal)

### Q: Classic features of encephalitis?

- **Fever and headache** (flu-like prodrome)
- Then over hours-to-weeks: **confusion, behaviour change, seizures, focal deficits, loss of consciousness**.
- Mental status is **nearly always** altered.

### Q: Most common first presentation of MS?

**Optic neuritis** — visual field disturbance, often with reduced colour vision, due to demyelination of the optic nerve/tract. Lecturer-emphasised but not on slides.

### Q: What proportion of MS is relapsing-remitting at onset, and how many progress?

- **80%** start as relapsing-remitting.
- Of those, **80%** eventually convert to **secondary progressive**.
- A minority is **primary progressive** from onset (no remissions).

📎 Figure L1.7 — MS disease course (Compston graph)

### Q: What are the four cellular processes in MS pathology?

1. **Inflammation**
2. **Demyelination**
3. **Axonal loss**
4. **Neurodegeneration**

📎 Figure L1.8 — MS pathology

### Q: Why does function reduce, not vanish, in MS?

Demyelination removes saltatory conduction → axons still fire but **conduction slows.** Function is **reduced, not lost**, until axonal loss accumulates.

### Q: Why do early MS relapses recover (remission)?

**Oligodendrocytes can remodel and remyelinate axons** — partial recovery between relapses. Remyelination capacity diminishes over time, contributing to progression.

⭐ **The MS triad to recognise in stems:** young woman + episodic deficits **in different anatomical territories** + recovery between + **periventricular** MRI lesions = MS.

### Investigations

CSF profiles by cause:

| | Bacterial meningitis | Viral encephalitis | Normal CSF |
|---|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Cloudy / turbid | Clear | **Clear and colourless** |
| Glucose | **Low** | Normal | Normal |
| Cells | Neutrophils ↑↑ | Lymphocytes ↑ | None / few |

📎 Figure L1.9 — MRI of relapsing-remitting MS over 9 months

### Q: What does normal CSF look like?

**Clear and colourless.** Any cloudiness, blood-staining or yellow tint is abnormal.

### Q: CSF profile in bacterial meningitis?

- **Cloudy** appearance
- **Low glucose** (bugs eat it)
- **Raised neutrophils** + raised protein

### Q: CSF profile in viral encephalitis?

- Clear
- Normal glucose
- **Lymphocytic pleocytosis**

PCR for HSV is the diagnostic test of choice.

### Q: Where do MS lesions cluster on MRI?

**Periventricular white matter.** Susceptibility is well-recognised but mechanistically unexplained ("for reasons still not entirely clear" — lecturer).

### Q: What does "dissemination in time" mean on MRI?

**New T2 lesions appearing on a follow-up MRI** that weren't present on the first — radiological proof that disease is occurring at separate time points. Combined with "dissemination in space" (lesions in different anatomical territories), this is the imaging basis for diagnosing MS.

⚠️ **A normal CT does NOT exclude encephalitis.** HSV encephalitis can have a normal early CT. Need MRI (temporal lobe changes) and CSF PCR.

### Management

Treatments by disease:

| | Meningitis (bacterial) | Encephalitis | MS |
|---|---|---|---|
| First-line | **IV antibiotics — urgently** | **Aciclovir** (empirical for HSV) | DMTs (anti-inflammatory antibody therapies) |
| Adjuncts | Steroids | Steroids; **anti-convulsants** (often prophylactic) | Steroids for acute relapse |
| Supportive | Analgesia; ventilation if severe | Analgesia; ventilation if severe | Symptomatic, physio |

### Q: Why are anti-convulsants given in encephalitis even without a seizure?

**Prophylactically** — seizures are a common encephalitis complication, so anti-convulsants are often started before any seizure to head them off. Lecturer-emphasised: "more likely as prophylaxis."

### Q: Why are steroids given in encephalitis?

To **reduce inflammatory swelling.** The brain has nowhere to swell inside a rigid skull → raised intracranial pressure causes major secondary damage. Steroids buy room.

### Q: Urgency principle in suspected bacterial meningitis?

**Give IV antibiotics fast — do not wait for confirmation.** Lecturer: "want to get those antibiotics in there quickly." Delay increases mortality and morbidity.

### Q: How has MS prognosis changed?

**Anti-inflammatory antibody therapies** have transformed outcomes — MS patients now have **near-normal life expectancy** with reasonable quality of life. Disability accumulation slowed but not abolished.

### Complications and prognosis

### Q: What proportion of meningitis survivors have lasting impairment?

**1 in 5 (20%)**, particularly children.

### Q: Long-term complications of meningitis?

📎 Figure L1.10 — MS post-mortem variability (analogous illustration of how location drives sequelae)

- **Hearing loss / deafness / tinnitus**
- **Epilepsy**
- Memory loss, learning disabilities, poor concentration
- Weakness/paralysis/spasms; balance/coordination problems
- Speech and visual problems

### Q: Why are children particularly badly affected by meningitis?

Their **brain is still developing** — disruption at critical neurodevelopmental windows leaves persistent deficits even after the infection clears.

## Cross-cutting patterns

🧠 **Reusable diagnostic approach to any cerebral inflammation stem.**

```
Step 1.  Where is the inflammation?
         (meninges / parenchyma / white matter / vessels)
Step 2.  What symptoms follow from that location?
         (meningism / focal+confusion / dispersed deficits / multifocal)
Step 3.  What is the most likely cause given demographics + exposure?
         (UK adult, newborn, young woman, halls of residence,
          recent viral illness, vaccination status)
Step 4.  Which test confirms it?
         (CSF for meningeal/parenchymal infection;
          MRI for MS / HSV temporal lobe)
Step 5.  What is the urgent treatment?
         (IV antibiotics fast; aciclovir + steroids
          + prophylactic anti-convulsants; DMT for MS)
Step 6.  What complications to anticipate?
         (hearing loss/epilepsy in meningitis;
          cognitive sequelae in encephalitis; disability in MS)
```

This sequence handles every cerebral inflammation question and transfers to unseen pathogens (TB meningitis, neurosyphilis, autoimmune encephalitis from antibody therapy).

## Mixed SBA block

### Q: SBA — A 22-year-old fresher in his second week of university halls is brought in unconscious with fever, neck stiffness and a non-blanching purpuric rash. CSF shows neutrophilic pleocytosis and low glucose. Most likely organism?

A) *Streptococcus pneumoniae*
B) ***Neisseria meningitidis*** ✅
C) *Haemophilus influenzae* type b
D) Group B Streptococcus
E) Herpes simplex virus

**Reasoning.** Halls of residence + non-blanching rash + bacterial CSF profile = meningococcal. *N. meningitidis* is the most common UK cause. Pneumococcal is most *serious* but less common. Hib reduced by vaccination. GBS affects newborns. HSV causes encephalitis.

### Q: SBA — A 7-year-old presents with 24h of fever, severe headache, vomiting, photophobia and a stiff neck. He is drowsy but oriented when roused. No focal signs, no seizures. Most likely diagnosis?

A) **Bacterial meningitis** ✅
B) Herpes simplex encephalitis
C) Multiple sclerosis
D) Migraine
E) Subarachnoid haemorrhage

**Reasoning.** Sudden fever + photophobia + nuchal rigidity = meningism. Mental status only mildly altered, no seizures, no focal signs argues against encephalitis. MS doesn't present with fever and meningism. Migraine doesn't cause fever. SAH gives thunderclap headache without fever in first 24h.

### Q: SBA — A 29-year-old presents with 3 days of confusion, behavioural change and a generalised tonic-clonic seizure. Low-grade fever. CT unremarkable. CSF: lymphocytes, normal glucose. Most likely diagnosis?

A) Bacterial meningitis
B) **Viral encephalitis (HSV)** ✅
C) MS flare
D) Migraine with aura
E) First-presentation epilepsy

**Reasoning.** Behaviour change + seizure + altered mental status + fever = encephalitis. Lymphocytic CSF with normal glucose = viral profile. HSV is the most common viral encephalitis and characteristically affects temporal lobes (behaviour, language, focal seizures).

### Q: SBA — A 26-year-old woman had transient blurred vision in her right eye 8 months ago that resolved over three weeks. She now has 2 weeks of left-leg weakness and numbness. MRI shows multiple T2-hyperintense periventricular lesions. Most likely diagnosis?

A) Stroke
B) Migraine
C) Bacterial meningitis
D) **Multiple sclerosis** ✅
E) Brain tumour

**Reasoning.** Young woman + episodes in **two anatomical territories** (optic nerve then spinal cord/motor) + **recovery between** + periventricular lesions = dissemination in time and space → MS.

### Q: SBA — A 4-day-old neonate presents with fever, lethargy, poor feeding and a bulging fontanelle. LP confirms bacterial meningitis. Most likely organism?

A) *N. meningitidis*
B) *S. pneumoniae*
C) **Group B Streptococcus** ✅
D) *H. influenzae* b
E) Herpes simplex virus

**Reasoning.** Newborn → GBS, acquired perinatally from maternal genital tract. Lecturer: "any signs of meningitis in newborn — likely streptococcal."

### Q: SBA — A 31-year-old man with confusion, fever and behaviour change is started on aciclovir for suspected viral encephalitis. He has not had a seizure. Why might he also be started on an anti-convulsant?

A) Because of an unwitnessed seizure
B) Aciclovir lowers seizure threshold
C) **Prophylaxis — seizures are a common encephalitis complication** ✅
D) To treat raised intracranial pressure
E) Steroids cause seizures

**Reasoning.** Lecturer-emphasised teaching: anti-convulsants in encephalitis are often started **before** any seizure, as prophylaxis. ICP is the role of steroids/mannitol, not anti-convulsants.

### Q: SBA — A 19-year-old with sudden fever, photophobia and stiff neck. CT normal. LP performed. Which CSF profile best supports the suspected diagnosis?

A) Clear, normal glucose, lymphocytic pleocytosis
B) **Cloudy, low glucose, neutrophilic pleocytosis** ✅
C) Clear, normal glucose, no cells
D) Bloody, normal glucose, RBCs only
E) Clear, raised glucose, lymphocytic pleocytosis

**Reasoning.** Bacterial meningitis CSF = cloudy, low glucose, raised neutrophils. A is the viral profile. C is normal. D is traumatic tap or SAH. E (raised CSF glucose) is fabricated.

### Q: SBA — A 67-year-old woman dies during the COVID pandemic. Brain post-mortem shows virus localised to cerebral capillary endothelial cells but absent from parenchyma; inflammation is centred on cerebral vessels. Best description?

A) Viral encephalitis
B) **Vasculitis** ✅
C) Bacterial meningitis
D) MS plaque
E) Normal post-mortem

**Reasoning.** Lecturer's specific COVID teaching: virus in vessels but not brain substance, vessel-centred inflammation = vasculitis (angiitis). Encephalitis would require parenchymal infection.

### Q: SBA — A 14-year-old has 1 week of fever and headache, now sudden behavioural change, expressive aphasia and a focal motor seizure. CSF: lymphocytes, normal glucose. Most likely organism?

A) *S. pneumoniae*
B) *N. meningitidis*
C) **Herpes simplex virus** ✅
D) Group B Streptococcus
E) *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*

**Reasoning.** Subacute prodrome + behavioural change + aphasia + focal seizure = HSV encephalitis (HSV preferentially affects temporal lobes → behaviour, language, focal seizures). CSF lymphocytic + normal glucose fits viral.

### Q: SBA — A 58-year-old man with chronic systemic vasculitis has new cognitive decline. MRI: small white-matter lesions consistent with small-vessel disease. Histology: thickened perivascular collagen and retraction of astrocyte end-feet. Which protein would best demonstrate BBB breakdown adjacent to the vessel?

A) Albumin
B) **Fibrinogen** ✅
C) IgG
D) Haemoglobin
E) Myelin basic protein

**Reasoning.** Fibrinogen is the lecturer-named marker of BBB breakdown. Albumin would also leak but isn't the named teaching answer. IgG suggests intrathecal immune response. Haemoglobin = haemorrhage. MBP is a myelin component.

### Q: SBA — Which feature of cerebral capillaries best explains why an antibiotic effective in vitro fails to clear CNS infection when given orally?

A) Reduced cardiac output to brain
B) Astrocytes phagocytose the drug
C) Capillary fenestration in CNS
D) **Endothelial tight junctions limiting solute crossing** ✅
E) Active efflux pump for the drug

**Reasoning.** Tight junctions block passive solute movement; only molecules with a transporter or sufficient lipophilicity cross. A is opposite of true (brain gets *high* output). C is **wrong** — CNS capillaries are non-fenestrated. B is fabricated. E is overcomplicated.

### Q: SBA — A 35-year-old unvaccinated woman returning from a measles-affected community develops fever, confusion and a generalised seizure. CT unremarkable. CSF: lymphocytic pleocytosis. Most likely diagnosis?

A) Bacterial meningitis
B) MS first presentation
C) **Viral encephalitis** ✅
D) Migraine
E) Anti-amyloid antibody encephalitis

**Reasoning.** Confusion + seizure + fever + lymphocytic CSF = encephalitis. Measles is a named viral cause; lecturer noted measles is "making a comeback."
