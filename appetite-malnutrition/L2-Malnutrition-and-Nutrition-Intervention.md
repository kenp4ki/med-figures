## Lecturer's voice

**Flagged as exam-critical:**
- **1 in 3 malnourished** at acute admission (lecturer's stated take-home)

- Refeeding only happens once **glucose is reintroduced** — not in a starved patient sitting NBM
- Enteral is superior to parenteral — "if the gut works, use it"

## Defining malnutrition

### Q: State the Elia 2000 definition.
- A state of **deficiency, excess or imbalance** of energy, protein or other nutrients
- Resulting in a **measurable adverse effect** on **body composition, function and clinical outcome**

> 🧠 **Note:** The definition technically covers obesity too — that's why both extremes appear in the bundle. L2 focuses on under-nutrition in hospital.

### Q: How common is malnutrition at acute admission?
- **1 in 3** at admission
- Hospitalisation exacerbates risk
- **70% have lost weight by discharge — mostly muscle**
- Rises with age (peaks 46% in 90+); varies by ward (CoE/oncology 37%; ortho/trauma 17%)

## Causes of malnutrition (three buckets)

### Q: Three categories of cause?
- Reduced intake
- Maldigestion / malabsorption
- Altered metabolism

### Q: Five examples of reduced intake in hospital?
- **Disease-related anorexia** (commonest)
- Taste changes (oncology on treatment)
- Nil by mouth (often prolonged in MDT decision-making)
- Poor food options, depression, fatigue
- Oral health problems

### Q: Causes within the maldigestion / malabsorption bucket?
- Reduced gut function
- Reduced gut length (e.g. short bowel post-resection)
- High losses (stoma, fistula, diarrhoea)
- Drug-nutrient interactions

### Q: Cuthbertson's metabolic response to injury — three phases?
- **Ebb** (1–2 days) — hypometabolic; ↓ rate, ↓ O₂ use, ↓ temp; goal: survival
- **Early flow** (2–7 days) — hypermetabolic, catabolic; REE peaks ~150% baseline; goal: cover metabolic needs
- **Late flow** (weeks) — anabolic; goal: muscle recovery

📎 Figure L2.2 — Cuthbertson's metabolic response curve

> ⭐ **High-yield:** In the acute phase, the goal is **weight maintenance, never weight gain**. Pushing calories during catabolism just generates metabolic complications.

## Impact of malnutrition

### Q: What did Studley (1936) demonstrate?
- Patients undergoing surgery for perforated duodenal ulcer
- Postoperative mortality **10× greater** in those with ≥20% preoperative weight loss
- First demonstration that malnutrition kills surgical patients

### Q: Effects of malnutrition on outcomes?
- ↑ mortality, sepsis, post-surgical complications
- ↑ length of stay, pressure sores, readmissions, dependency
- ↓ wound healing, treatment response, rehab potential, quality of life

## Diagnostic pipeline

### Q: Three-step pipeline?
- **Screen** — simple tool (MUST), nurse-led on admission
- **Assess** — systematic dietitian-led data collection
- **Diagnose** — dietitian decides nutritional status and plan

### Q: What is MUST and what does it use?
- **Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool**
- Inputs: BMI, **unintentional weight loss**, acute nutritional score (eating or not)
- Output: referral score that triggers dietitian review

### Q: Why is BMI alone not enough to diagnose malnutrition?
- Isolated value — doesn't tell you fat-to-muscle ratio
- Doesn't capture trajectory
- BMI 21 → 18.5 = trouble; lifelong BMI 17 with stable function = probably not

> 🧠 **Pattern:** Trajectory beats threshold. **Unintentional weight loss** is the key signal. Always think dry weight (subtract ascites/oedema).

## When to start nutrition support (NICE 2006)

### Q: NICE — definition of "malnourished"?
- BMI **<18.5**, OR
- Unintentional weight loss **>10%** in past 3–6 months, OR
- BMI **<20** + unintentional weight loss **>5%** in past 3–6 months

### Q: NICE — definition of "at risk of malnutrition"?
- Eaten little/nothing for **>5 days** AND/OR likely to eat little for next 5 days
- OR poor absorptive capacity
- OR high nutrient losses
- OR increased nutritional needs from catabolism

> ⭐ **High-yield:** These NICE numbers (>5 days, >10%, BMI <18.5/<20) recur in the refeeding criteria. Memorise once.

## Stratton & Elia algorithm — three nutrition support routes

### Decision spine

```
Nutrition support indicated
    │
    ▼
Is oral nutrition possible & safe?
    ├── YES → Oral nutritional support (fortification, ONS, counselling)
    │
    └── NO → Is GI tract functional & accessible?
              ├── YES → Enteral tube feeding
              │          ├─ Short-term (<2-4 wks) → fine-bore naso-enteral
              │          └─ Long-term (>4 wks)   → gastrostomy / jejunostomy
              │
              └── NO → Parenteral nutrition
```

📎 Figure L2.4 — Stratton & Elia treatment algorithm

> 📘 **Always framed by** consent and law/ethics (Druml 2016).

### Card 1 — Oral nutritional support (ONS)

### Q: When is oral support used and what are the tools?
- First-line if patient can swallow safely and gut works
- Tools: meal fortification, altered meal patterns, sip feeds, dietetic counselling

### Q: How much do ONS sip feeds provide?
- ~**300–400 kcal** per bottle
- ~**12–20 g protein** per bottle
- Some patients take 6–7 bottles/day if solely reliant on ONS

📎 Figure L2.5 — ONS sip feed examples

### Q: Contraindications to oral support?
- Unsafe swallow
- Inadequate GI function
- End-of-life care where no benefit anticipated

---

### Card 2 — Enteral nutrition

### Q: Why is enteral superior to parenteral?
- Maintains gut integrity and microbiome
- **Preserves gut hormone signalling** (ghrelin / PYY / GLP-1)
- Lower infection risk
- "If the gut works, use it" — return to enteral → oral as soon as possible

### Q: Choosing the tube — gastric vs distal?
- Gastric feeding possible → **NGT (nasogastric)**
- Gastric feeding NOT possible (e.g. gastric outlet obstruction) → **NDT or NJT**
- Long-term (>3 months) → **gastrostomy or jejunostomy**

📎 Figure L2.6 — Enteral access routes

### Q: How is NGT position confirmed (Imperial practice)?
- **Aspirate pH ≤5.5** from paper test
- If pH >5.5 → CXR by trained professional
- CXR is NOT first-line at Imperial
- **PPI use can falsely elevate pH** — check the drug chart first

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Don't default to CXR. The acid aspirate test is fast, cheap, and the standard first step. NPSA 2005–2011: misplaced NGTs caused 21 deaths + 79 cases of harm.

📎 Figure L2.8 — Misplaced NGT in left lower lobe

### Q: Three categories of enteral complications?
- **Mechanical** — misplacement, blockage, buried bumper
- **Metabolic** — hyperglycaemia, deranged electrolytes
- **GI** — aspiration, nasopharyngeal pain, laryngeal ulceration, vomiting, diarrhoea

---

### Card 3 — Parenteral nutrition (PN)

### Q: Define PN and indications.
- Delivery of nutrients, electrolytes, fluid **directly into venous blood**
- Indications (NICE): inadequate or unsafe oral and/or enteral intake, OR
- Non-functioning, inaccessible or perforated GI tract

### Q: Where does PN go (Imperial practice)?
- **Central venous catheter (CVC)**
- Tip at superior vena cava and right atrium
- Imperial uses central only — never peripheral

📎 Figure L2.9 — Vascular access routes for PN

### Q: Three categories of PN complications?
- **Metabolic** — deranged electrolytes, hyperglycaemia, abnormal LFTs, oedema, hypertriglyceridaemia
- **Mechanical** — pneumothorax, haemothorax, thrombosis, arrhythmias, occlusion, thrombophlebitis, extravasation
- **Catheter-related infection** — biggest single risk, especially in home PN

📎 Figure L2.10 — PN complications and pneumothorax CXR

### Q: Who manages PN?
- Nutrition Support MDT (NCEPOD 2010)
- Gastroenterologist, dietitian, pharmacist, vascular access nurse
- ± palliative care

> 🧠 **The route ladder:** oral → enteral → parenteral. Move down only when the rung above fails. Move back up as soon as clinically possible. The single most testable concept in L2.

## Albumin (the trap)

### Q: Why does albumin drop in acute illness?
- **Albumin is a NEGATIVE acute phase protein**
- Cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) act on liver
- Liver upregulates positive APPs (CRP, SAA, fibrinogen) and downregulates albumin and transferrin
- CRP rises, albumin falls — inversely linked

📎 Figure L2.12 — Acute phase response cascade

### Q: Is albumin a valid marker of malnutrition in the acute hospital setting?
- **No.**
- Tracks inflammation and fluid status, not nutritional intake
- Acute albumin drop → treat the underlying infection/inflammation, not order more feeds

📎 Figure L2.13 — Inverse CRP–albumin time course

### Q: When IS albumin meaningful?
- As a **longer-term** marker in stable outpatients (e.g. chronic oncology)
- Reflects low muscle mass and prolonged poor intake
- Not in the acute setting

> ⚠️ **Trap pattern:** Junior doctor sees low albumin → orders nutrition referral → real problem is sepsis. Recognise this exam stem.

## Refeeding syndrome (cascade)

### Pathogenesis skeleton

```
Starvation / malnutrition
        │
        ▼
Glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism
        │
        ▼
Cellular depletion of protein, fat, minerals, vitamins
        │
        ▼
REFEEDING (carbohydrate reintroduced)
        │
        ▼
↑ Insulin secretion
        │
        ▼
Insulin drives K⁺, Mg²⁺, PO₄³⁻ INTO cells (alongside glucose)
↑ Thiamine demand (cofactor for glucose metabolism)
        │
        ▼
Serum: ↓K⁺, ↓Mg²⁺, ↓PO₄³⁻ + thiamine deficiency + Na/water retention
        │
        ▼
Arrhythmias, respiratory depression, encephalopathy, seizures, death
```

📎 Figure L2.14 — Refeeding syndrome cycle

### Q: Define refeeding syndrome (Solomon & Kirby 1990).
- A group of biochemical shifts and clinical symptoms
- In the malnourished or starved individual
- On **reintroduction of oral, enteral or parenteral nutrition**

### Q: What specifically triggers it?
- **Reintroduction of carbohydrate (glucose)**
- Cannot occur in a starved patient sitting NBM
- Only kicks in once feeding starts — common conceptual error per the lecturer

### Q: Why do K⁺, Mg²⁺ and PO₄³⁻ drop in refeeding?
- Insulin surge drives them INTO cells (alongside glucose)
- Patient already had low total body stores from starvation
- Serum levels crash quickly

### Q: Why does thiamine become critical in refeeding?
- Thiamine is a cofactor for glucose metabolism (transketolase, pyruvate dehydrogenase)
- Sudden glucose load → demand spikes
- Pre-existing deficiency becomes critical → **Wernicke's encephalopathy**

### Q: Lethal complications of refeeding?
- Cardiac — arrhythmia, tachycardia, CHF → cardiac arrest, sudden death
- Respiratory depression
- Encephalopathy, coma, seizures, rhabdomyolysis
- Wernicke's encephalopathy

### NICE risk criteria

### Q: NICE — "at risk" of refeeding?
- Very little or no food intake for **>5 days**

### Q: NICE — "high risk" criteria?
- **≥1 of:** BMI <16, weight loss >15% in 3–6/12, no nutrition >10 days, low K⁺/Mg²⁺/PO₄³⁻ pre-feeding
- **OR ≥2 of:** BMI <18.5, weight loss >10% in 3–6/12, no nutrition >5 days, alcohol/drug history (insulin, chemo, antacids, diuretics)

### Q: NICE — "extremely high risk"?
- BMI **<14**, OR
- Negligible intake **>15 days**

### Q: Can a patient with NORMAL pre-feeding K⁺/Mg²⁺/PO₄³⁻ develop refeeding?
- **Yes.**
- Pre-feeding electrolytes can be normal because cells haven't been challenged
- Levels can still crash on refeeding
- Same trap for **overweight patients with prolonged starvation**

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Refeeding syndrome can occur in obese patients who have eaten little for several days. Don't let body habitus lull you.

### Management

### Q: Management sequence for refeeding risk?
- Refer to dietitians
- **Thiamine 30 min before and for first 10 days of feeding**
- Correct and monitor electrolytes daily
- **Start at 10–20 kcal/kg, CHO 40–50% of energy, micronutrients from onset**
- Monitor fluid shifts; minimise fluid and Na⁺ overload

📎 Figure L2.15 — RFS management sequence

### Q: Compare normal hospital target to refeeding start.
- Normal: **30–35 kcal/kg/day**
- Refeeding start: **10–20 kcal/kg/day** (sometimes 10 in extremely high risk)
- Less than half normal — build up cautiously

## Cross-references

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: Gut hormones → enteral preference**
> ### Q: How do L1's gut hormones relate to L2's preference for enteral feeding?
> L1: PYY and GLP-1 are released when food reaches the small bowel/colon. L2: enteral feeding preserves these signals; PN bypasses them entirely. Part of why "if the gut works, use it". See also: L1 — "PYY", "GLP-1"

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: Cuthbertson vs Biggest Loser**
> ### Q: How does the Cuthbertson curve contrast with L1's metabolic adaptation?
> L1 (Biggest Loser): chronic dieting → metabolic rate DROPS. L2 (Cuthbertson): acute illness → metabolic rate RISES in early flow phase. Same hormonal players (cortisol, catecholamines, cytokines) in opposite directions. See also: L1 — "Lessons from the Biggest Loser"

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: Insulin's two faces**
> ### Q: How does refeeding's electrolyte shift relate to GLP-1's incretin effect?
> Both involve insulin-driven cellular processes. L1: physiological (handles ingested glucose). L2: pathological (insulin drives K⁺/Mg²⁺/PO₄³⁻ INTO depleted cells, crashing serum levels). Same mechanism, different outcome based on baseline state. See also: L1 — "GLP-1's pleiotropic effects"

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: BMI <18.5 — same number, different uses**
> ### Q: BMI <18.5 across the bundle?
> L1 tutorial: "underweight" nutritional category. L2 NICE: threshold for "malnourished" and one of the refeeding criteria. See also: L1 — "BMI categories"

## SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 65-year-old with metastatic gastric cancer is admitted with vomiting and weight loss. CT shows gastric outlet obstruction. Oral intake impossible. Most appropriate route?
- A — Nasogastric tube
- B — Naso-jejunal tube
- C — Parenteral nutrition via peripheral cannula
- D — Parenteral nutrition via CVC
- E — PEG (gastrostomy)
- **Answer: B.** Gut works distal to the obstruction; bypass with NJT. NGT (A) and PEG (E) feed into the obstructed stomach → vomiting. PN (D) is unnecessary if the gut works distally.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 25-year-old with anorexia nervosa, BMI 13.5, no oral intake for 12 days. Bloods: K⁺ 3.5, Mg²⁺ 0.7, PO₄³⁻ 0.9 (low-normal). First feeding step?
- A — Start at 30 kcal/kg/day to rebuild weight rapidly
- B — Withhold all nutrition until electrolytes are above normal
- C — IV thiamine, replace electrolytes, start cautious feed at 10 kcal/kg/day
- D — Oral diet ad lib because she needs calories urgently
- E — Parenteral nutrition because gut function is doubtful
- **Answer: C.** BMI <14 + intake <15 days = extremely high refeeding risk. Withholding (B) continues starvation; aggressive feeding (A, D) precipitates refeeding; PN (E) is unnecessary if the gut works.

### Q: SBA 3 — A 70-year-old with sepsis from pneumonia has albumin 22 (was 38 four days ago). Junior wants urgent sip feeds. Most appropriate response?
- A — Start sip feeds — albumin drop reflects malnutrition
- B — Start TPN — gut absorption likely impaired
- C — Treat the infection; albumin reflects acute phase response, not malnutrition
- D — Stop all oral intake to allow gut rest
- E — Send urgent dietitian referral as priority over antibiotics
- **Answer: C.** Acute albumin drop in inflammation is the negative acute phase response. Treating sepsis is what brings albumin back up. The patient may still need nutritional support but not because of the albumin.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 52-year-old with BMI 36 and T2DM is admitted with severe pneumonia. Day 5: eaten little, lost 6% bodyweight, signs of catabolism. Team suggests "low-calorie hospital diet to optimise weight". Most appropriate response?
- A — Continue low-calorie plan; obesity makes weight loss desirable
- B — Start a GLP-1 agonist now to capitalise on weight loss
- C — Aim for weight maintenance; assess refeeding risk before any plan
- D — Start parenteral nutrition because oral intake has been inadequate
- E — Stop nutrition until infection resolves
- **Answer: C.** Bundle's core trap. Looks like an L1 case (obese, T2DM) but is now an L2 case (acute, catabolic, possible refeeding risk despite obesity). Weight maintenance, never loss/gain during illness.

### Q: SBA 5 — Following NGT insertion, aspirate gives pH 6.2. Patient is on omeprazole. Next step?
- A — Start the feed — pH within acceptable limits
- B — Withdraw and reinsert
- C — Withhold feed; arrange CXR interpreted by trained professional
- D — Replace with NJT
- E — Switch to PN
- **Answer: C.** NPSA: pH must be ≤5.5. PPIs raise gastric pH so the test can be falsely positive for misplacement. CXR is the next step, not first-line. Don't feed through an unconfirmed tube.

> 🧠 **Reusable pattern:** Setting + direction + goal decides which framework to apply. Acute hospital + deficient intake + maintenance = L2. Outpatient + excess intake + loss = L1. Mixed cases (obese inpatient): apply L2 in the acute phase.
