## Hydration mini-system

### Q: What are the three stimuli for thirst, and which is most potent?
- ↑ body fluid osmolality
- ↓ blood volume
- ↓ blood pressure
- **Plasma osmolality is most potent** — 2–3% change → strong desire to drink
- ↓ blood volume / BP needs **10–15%** drop for the same response

### Q: Why does osmolality win?
- Sensed almost instantly by osmoreceptors
- Volume changes register slowly because of vascular buffering
- Faster signal wins

### Q: What is ADH and where is it stored?
- Antidiuretic hormone (vasopressin)
- Acts on kidney **collecting duct** via **aquaporin-2** channels
- **Stored in posterior pituitary**

📎 Figure L1.2 — ADH at the collecting duct

### Q: Plasma ADH high vs low — effect on urine?
- High ADH → small volume of concentrated urine (anti-diuresis)
- Low ADH → large volume of dilute urine (water diuresis)

### Q: Where in the hypothalamus are the osmoreceptors?
- **OVLT** — organum vasculosum of lamina terminalis
- **SFO** — subfornical organ
- Both circumventricular organs with leaky BBB to sample plasma directly

📎 Figure L1.3 — Circumventricular organs

### Q: How does an osmoreceptor cell sense ↑ osmolality?
- Plasma more concentrated → water leaves cell → cell **shrinks**
- TRPV1 cation channels open → membrane depolarises
- Fires onto ADH-producing neurons → ↑ ADH release
- Reverse for ↓ osmolality

📎 Figure L1.4 — Push-activation osmoreceptor model

### Q: Why does thirst stop after a few sips, before water is absorbed?
- Receptors in mouth, pharynx, oesophagus give brief feedback
- Relief is **short-lived**
- True satisfaction only when plasma osmolality (or BP/volume) corrected

> ⭐ **Surgical anchor:** Post-op NBM patients can have sips for comfort — small volume, similar to saliva. Mouth/pharynx receptors give psychological relief without harm.

### Q: Walk through RAAS in one chain.
- ↓ BP → JG cells of renal afferent arteriole → **renin**
- Renin + angiotensinogen (liver) → angiotensin I
- ACE (lung) → **angiotensin II**
- Four effects: thirst, aldosterone (Na⁺ retention, K⁺ excretion), vasoconstriction + ↑ sympathetic, ADH secretion
- Triggered by ↑ K⁺ and ↓ Na⁺

📎 Figure L1.5 — RAAS cascade

### Q: Define polydipsia, adipsia, anorexia, obesity.
- **Polydipsia** — excessive thirst or excess drinking
- **Adipsia** — inappropriate lack of thirst
- **Anorexia** — lack or loss of appetite for food
- **Obesity** — abnormal or excessive fat accumulation that presents a risk to health

### Q: Three causes of primary polydipsia?
- Mental illness — psychogenic (schizophrenia, mood disorders, anorexia, drugs)
- Brain injuries
- Organic brain damage — demyelination (CPM, Alzheimer's)

### Q: Five causes of secondary polydipsia?
- Chronic conditions: **DI, DM, kidney failure, Conn's, Addison's, sickle cell**
- Medications: **diuretics, laxatives, antidepressants**
- Dehydration: acute illness, sweating, fever, vomiting, diarrhoea

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Don't reach for diabetes insipidus first in an elderly patient on diuretics. The drug history is the answer.

## Body weight homeostasis

### Q: Core observation about adult body weight (Neuman 1902, Passmore 1971)?
- Adults maintain stable weight over long periods without conscious effort
- Implies an **active homeostatic regulator**

### Q: What does the body do when fat mass falls?
- ↑ food intake
- ↓ energy expenditure
- **Strong, well-characterised** defence against weight loss

### Q: What does the body do when fat mass rises rapidly?
- ↓ food intake (in theory)
- ↑ energy expenditure (in theory)
- **Defence is much weaker — and largely uncharacterised**
- This asymmetry is why obesity exists as a pandemic

📎 Figure L1.6 — Underfed vs overfed homeostasis

### Q: What did the Biggest Loser study show?
- 14 contestants lost ~58 kg over 30 weeks (diet + exercise)
- After 6 years they regained ~40 kg
- Resting metabolic rate stayed depressed by **600–700 kcal/day** even at 6 years
- Demonstrates **metabolic adaptation to dieting**

📎 Figure L1.7 — Biggest Loser body weight + RMR

> 🧠 **Pattern:** When intake drops, the body lowers metabolic rate. The lower rate persists after dieting stops. Diet alone almost always fails long-term.

## The hypothalamic appetite circuit

### Q: Name the four key hypothalamic regions for appetite.
- **Arcuate nucleus** — sensor (bottom, near 3rd ventricle, incomplete BBB)
- **Paraventricular nucleus (PVN)** — output integrator
- **Lateral hypothalamus** — feeding centre (orexigenic)
- **Ventromedial hypothalamus** — satiety centre (anorectic)

📎 Figure L1.8 — Hypothalamic nuclei

### Q: Define orexigenic and anorectic.
- **Orexigenic** — appetite-stimulating
- **Anorectic** — appetite-suppressing

### Q: Why is the arcuate nucleus the key appetite sensor?
- Has an **incomplete blood-brain barrier**
- Circulating hormones (leptin, ghrelin, PYY, GLP-1, insulin) directly access it
- Most of the brain is shielded — arcuate is the deliberate exception

### Q: What are the two arcuate neuron populations?
- **NPY/AgRP neurons** — stimulatory → ↑ feeding
- **POMC neurons** — inhibitory → ↓ feeding (release α-MSH)
- Both project to PVN

📎 Figure L1.9 — Two arcuate populations + immunostain

> ⭐ **Highest-yield single sentence:** Leptin **inhibits NPY/AgRP** and **stimulates POMC** — both arms push toward less food intake.

📎 Figure L1.10 — Arcuate → PVN circuit summary

### Q: Trace the melanocortin pathway.
- POMC neuron in arcuate → α-MSH at PVN
- α-MSH activates **MC4R (melanocortin-4 receptor)** → ↓ food intake
- AgRP also projects to PVN → blocks MC4R → ↑ food intake

📎 Figure L1.11 — Melanocortin system

> 🧠 **Pattern:** MC4R is the critical decision node. Mutations cause monogenic obesity in humans.

### Q: What other brain regions feed into the hypothalamus for appetite?
- **Amygdala** — emotion, memory (stress eating)
- Lateral hypothalamus
- **Vagus nerve** — gut → brainstem → hypothalamus

## The four hormones (peripheral inputs)

> 🧠 **Atlas pattern:** Only ghrelin makes you hungry. Leptin, PYY, GLP-1 all make you full. The hunger system has one signal; the satiety system has multiple, redundant ones.

### Leptin

### Q: What does "leptin" mean and where is it made?
- From Greek **leptos** = thin (discovered 1994)
- Made by **white adipose tissue** and (acutely after meals) **gastric mucosa**

### Q: What does leptin signal?
- Reflects energy reserves — low when fat low, high when fat high
- Acts on hypothalamus → ↓ food intake, ↑ thermogenesis

### Q: Tell me about the ob/ob mouse.
- Classic 1950s experiment
- Mouse missing functional leptin gene → severely obese
- Leptin replacement → weight reduction
- Proved leptin's role in regulating fat mass

📎 Figure L1.12 — ob/ob mouse vs lean controls

### Q: Congenital leptin deficiency in humans?
- Very rare (first described 1997)
- Severely obese children with low serum leptin despite high fat mass
- **Leptin replacement is effective** in these children
- Too rare to explain the obesity pandemic

📎 Figure L1.13 — Congenital leptin deficiency growth curves

### Q: If most obese people have HIGH leptin, why are they still obese?
- **Leptin resistance** — hormone present but doesn't signal effectively
- Dominant failure mode (not absent leptin)
- Leptin therapy failed as a weight-loss drug for this reason

📎 Figure L1.14 — Serum leptin vs body fat % (Considine NEJM 1996)

### Q: Three theoretical failure modes of the leptin system?
- **(a) Absent leptin** — ob/ob mouse, congenital deficiency
- **(b) Regulatory defect** — normal leptin levels, signal not translating
- **(c) Leptin resistance** — high leptin levels, signal blunted (most common)

📎 Figure L1.15 — Aberrant leptin signalling: three modes

### Ghrelin

### Q: Where is ghrelin made and when is it highest?
- Made in the **stomach** (enteroendocrine cells), discovered 1999
- **Highest before meals**, drops within 1h after eating
- Clear circadian rhythm — rises during day, falls overnight
- Correlates positively with age

📎 Figure L1.17 — Plasma ghrelin time-course

### Q: How does ghrelin act on the arcuate?
- **Stimulates NPY/AgRP, inhibits POMC** — opposite of leptin
- → ↑ appetite
- Also affects reward, taste, memory, circadian rhythm

### Q: Effect of IV ghrelin in humans?
- Wren 2001: significantly ↑ caloric intake at free-choice buffet
- Causal proof in humans

📎 Figure L1.18 — Ghrelin dose-response in rats
📎 Figure L1.19 — Ghrelin → ↑ food intake in humans

### PYY

### Q: What is PYY and where is it released?
- **Peptide tyrosine tyrosine** — 36 amino acids
- Released from **terminal ileum and colon** in response to feeding
- Proportional to meal calorie content

### Q: How does PYY act on the arcuate?
- **Stimulates POMC, inhibits NPY release** → ↓ appetite
- Sustained suppression of hunger out to 12h after IV PYY₃₋₃₆

📎 Figure L1.20 — PYY₃₋₃₆ → ↓ food intake (mice)
📎 Figure L1.21 — PYY₃₋₃₆ in humans

### GLP-1

### Q: What is GLP-1 and what makes it special?
- **Glucagon-like peptide-1** — 30 aa peptide released by gut on feeding
- Reduces appetite
- **Incretin** — increases insulin release in response to ingested glucose

### Q: GLP-1's pleiotropic effects?
- Brain — ↓ appetite, neuroprotection
- Stomach — ↓ gastric emptying
- Pancreas — ↑ insulin, ↓ glucagon, ↑ β-cell survival
- Liver — ↓ glucose production
- Heart — cardioprotection
- Adipose/muscle — ↑ glucose uptake, ↑ insulin sensitivity

📎 Figure L1.22 — GLP-1 pleiotropic effects

### Q: What did the semaglutide trial show (Wilding NEJM 2021)?
- ~2000 adults with BMI ≥30, randomised 2:1 semaglutide vs placebo
- **~16% body weight loss vs ~3% placebo over 68 weeks**
- The result that "shook the status quo" of bariatric surgery

📎 Figure L1.23 — Semaglutide weight loss curve

📎 Figure L1.16 — Gut hormones by anatomical site

### The four hormones — comparison

| Hormone | Source | Direction | Time window |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leptin | Adipose tissue | Anorectic | Long-term (fat status) |
| Ghrelin | Stomach | Orexigenic | Pre-meal peaks |
| PYY | Terminal ileum / colon | Anorectic | Post-meal, sustained |
| GLP-1 | Small intestine | Anorectic | Post-meal + incretin |

## Obesity in context

### Q: Five major obesity comorbidities?
- T2DM, hypertension, MI/stroke, sleep apnoea, osteoarthritis
- Also: depression, bowel cancer, peripheral vascular disease, gout

📎 Figure L1.1 — Two extremes of disordered eating
📎 Figure L1.24 — Obesity comorbidities map
📎 Figure L1.25 — US obesity prevalence 1985 → 2016
📎 Figure L1.26 — UK obesity hotspots

### Q: How much of obesity variance is genetic?
- **>40%** by twin studies
- ~250 genes implicated
- **Monogenic obesity is <0.1%**
- "Thrifty genes" evolved to store energy when food was scarce — mismatched to abundance

📎 Figure L1.27 — Gene × environment interaction

### Q: BMI categories?
- Underweight: **<18.5**
- Healthy: 18.5–<25
- Overweight: 25–<30
- Obesity Class 1: 30–<35
- Class 2: 35–<40
- Class 3: **≥40**

### Q: When is bariatric surgery indicated?
- BMI **≥40**, OR
- BMI **≥35 with significant comorbidity** (T2DM, HTN, sleep apnoea)
- GLP-1 analogues now used as a non-surgical alternative

## Cross-references

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: Gut hormones → enteral preference**
> ### Q: How do gut hormones from L1 link to nutrition support in L2?
> L1's ghrelin/PYY/GLP-1 are released when food traverses the gut. L2 prefers enteral feeding partly because it preserves these signals. PN bypasses the entire gut hormone system. See also: L2 — "Why is enteral superior to parenteral?"

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: Insulin's two faces**
> ### Q: How does GLP-1's incretin effect connect to refeeding syndrome?
> GLP-1 → ↑ insulin (physiological — handles ingested glucose). In refeeding, the same insulin surge drives K⁺/Mg²⁺/PO₄³⁻ INTO starved cells → serum levels crash. Same mechanism, different outcome based on baseline state. See also: L2 — "Why do K⁺, Mg²⁺ and PO₄³⁻ drop in refeeding?"

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: Metabolic adaptation, opposite directions**
> ### Q: How does Biggest Loser metabolic adaptation contrast with hospital catabolism?
> L1: chronic dieting → metabolic rate DROPS (defending against fat loss). L2 (Cuthbertson): acute illness → metabolic rate RISES in early flow phase. Both protective in original context, both maladaptive in modern medicine. See also: L2 — "Cuthbertson's metabolic response to injury"

> 🧠 **Cross-lecture: BMI <18.5 — same number, different uses**
> ### Q: BMI <18.5 across the bundle?
> L1 tutorial: "underweight" nutritional category. L2 NICE: defines "malnourished" and is one of the refeeding criteria. See also: L2 — "NICE definition of malnourished"

## SBAs

### Q: SBA 1 — A 35-year-old man recently underwent sleeve gastrectomy and reports markedly reduced appetite and faster fullness. Which gut hormone change best explains this?
- A — ↑ ghrelin
- B — ↓ ghrelin and ↑ GLP-1
- C — ↑ leptin
- D — ↓ PYY
- E — ↓ GLP-1
- **Answer: B.** Sleeve removes ghrelin-producing fundus → ↓ ghrelin (less hunger). Accelerated nutrient delivery → ↑ GLP-1 and PYY (more satiety). Leptin (C) tracks fat mass and doesn't change acutely.

### Q: SBA 2 — A 6-year-old with severe early-onset obesity has a homozygous LEP gene mutation. First-line treatment?
- A — Bariatric surgery
- B — Caloric restriction alone
- C — Recombinant leptin replacement
- D — GLP-1 agonist
- E — MC4R agonist
- **Answer: C.** Congenital leptin deficiency is one of the few situations where leptin replacement works — the failure mode is "absent leptin", not resistance. Direct restoration of the missing input.

### Q: SBA 3 — Semaglutide is randomised against placebo for weight loss. Which hypothalamic mechanism does it primarily exploit?
- A — Direct stimulation of POMC neurons in the arcuate
- B — Antagonism of MC4R
- C — Inhibition of leptin signalling
- D — Inhibition of NPY/AgRP via accelerated gastric emptying
- E — Stimulation of orexin in lateral hypothalamus
- **Answer: A.** GLP-1 receptor activation → POMC firing → ↑ α-MSH → ↑ MC4R activation → ↓ food intake. Slows gastric emptying too, but central appetite effect is via POMC stimulation.

### Q: SBA 4 — A 45-year-old with abdominal obesity, T2DM and BMI 39 has tried dieting without success. What therapy should be offered?
- A — Continued dietary advice
- B — Metformin only
- C — Bariatric surgery
- D — Liver transplant assessment
- E — Behavioural therapy alone
- **Answer: C.** Bariatric surgery is indicated for BMI ≥35 with significant comorbidity (T2DM here). GLP-1 analogues are an emerging non-surgical alternative.

### Q: SBA 5 — An 84-year-old on diuretics post-MI presents with marked thirst and dry lips despite drinking large amounts. Most likely cause?
- A — Diabetes insipidus
- B — Psychogenic polydipsia
- C — Diuretic-induced secondary polydipsia
- D — Conn's syndrome
- E — Addison's disease
- **Answer: C.** Diuretics → volume loss → RAAS activation → angiotensin II drives thirst. Lips remain dry because total body water is low. Drug history is the answer.

> ⚠️ **Trap pattern:** Leptin therapy fails in common obesity (resistance) but works in congenital deficiency. Same drug, opposite outcome — depends on the failure mode.
