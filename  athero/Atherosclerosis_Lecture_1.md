## 1. Epidemiology and risk factors

### Q: Why does atherosclerosis matter globally now?

- Minor cause in 1990s → **32% of global deaths by 2020**.
- Developing world converging on developed world: better infection control, Westernised diet.
- Driven by rising **obesity and diabetes**.

### Q: List the modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors.

| Modifiable | Non-modifiable |
|---|---|
| Smoking | Age |
| Lipids / LDL | Sex |
| Blood pressure | Genetic background |
| Diabetes |  |
| Obesity |  |
| Sedentary lifestyle |  |

### Q: By what factor is risk multiplied with smoking + hypertension + high cholesterol together?

- **×16** (combined).
- Single: smoking ×1.6 · HTN ×3 · cholesterol ×4.
- Two together: smoking+HTN ×4.5 · smoking+chol ×6 · HTN+chol ×9.

🟨 Risk factors **multiply, not add** — repeated examiner signal.

### Q: How has atherosclerosis epidemiology changed over the last decade?

- **Reduced hyperlipidaemia** (statins).
- **Reduced hypertension** (antihypertensives).
- **Increased obesity → increased diabetes**.
- New diabetes drugs help microvascular but have **doubtful effect on macrovascular** disease.

🟥 Trap: assuming new diabetes drugs reduce atherosclerosis. They don't — microvascular yes, macrovascular no.

### Q: Why is atherosclerosis focal when risk factors are systemic?

- Disease of **large arteries only**.
- Localises to **branches and bends** — carotid bifurcation, LAD origin, aortic arch, iliac bifurcation.
- **Disturbed (eddy) flow** activates endothelium locally.
- Concorde vortex-lift analogy: same eddy physics.

- If a stem asks why disease localises focally despite a systemic driver → answer is **local flow disturbance at branches/bends activating endothelium**.
- Transfers to abdominal aortic aneurysm location, berry aneurysm location at circle of Willis.

## 2. Main cell types in atherosclerosis

### Q: What do the five main cell types do in a plaque?

| Cell | Primary role in plaque |
|---|---|
| Vascular endothelium | Barrier function; leukocyte recruitment |
| Monocyte-macrophages | Foam cell formation; ROS, cytokines, MMPs |
| Vascular smooth muscle cells | Collagen synthesis; fibrous cap |
| Platelets | Thrombus generation after rupture |
| T lymphocytes | Th1 → activate macrophages; CTL → kill VSMCs |

### Q: Why does "atherosclerosis is not a fatberg" matter?

- A fatberg is **passive silting** — inert lipid deposition.
- Atherosclerosis is an **active immune response** to modified lipid.
- Blocking **one cytokine (IL-1)** reduces clinical events (CANTOS trial).

🟨 Signature teaching point. Any MCQ option framing atherosclerosis as passive deposition is wrong.

### Q: What did the CANTOS trial show?

- High-risk patients given **canakinumab** (anti-IL-1β antibody).
- Reduced **MACE** (stroke, MI) vs placebo. **HR 0.86, p = 0.031**.
- Proves atherosclerosis has an inflammatory basis.

📎 Figure L1.4 — CANTOS trial Kaplan-Meier curve

📎 Figure L1.5 — Cholesterol crystals activating macrophages

### Q: What are the two main functional types of macrophage?

- **Inflammatory** — adapted to kill pathogens.
- **Non-inflammatory / resident** — homeostatic (alveolar = surfactant recycling; spleen = iron recycling).
- **Atherosclerotic macrophages behave as inflammatory** because they think oxLDL is a microbe.

🟦 Core intuition: atherosclerosis is your immune system mistaking modified fat for a germ.

## 3. LDL biology

### Q: What are LDL, HDL, and oxidised LDL?

- **LDL** = "bad" cholesterol; liver → tissues. Substrate that gets trapped and oxidised.
- **HDL** = "good" cholesterol; tissues → liver ("reverse cholesterol transport").
- **OxLDL** = LDL chemically modified in the wall by free radicals, enzymes, aggregation; inflammatory and toxic.

### Q: Describe the LDL-C J-curve.

- Risk has a **nadir around 100 mg/dL (≈ 2 mmol/L)**.
- Steeply rises above nadir; modest rise below.
- Same J-shape for **MI and ischaemic stroke**.
- Populations sit above the nadir → lower-is-better in practice.

📎 Figure L1.6 — J-curve LDL-C vs risk

### Q: Why does an LDL particle have a lipid MONOLAYER not a bilayer?

- LDL has **one aqueous face** (outside only).
- Inside is hydrophobic cargo fat.
- Needs only **one molecule-thick** phospholipid layer; heads out, tails in.
- Cell membranes need a bilayer because both faces contact water.

🟥 Classic Q3 trap. Monolayer is correct. "Bilayer" is wrong.

📎 Figure L1.7 — LDL particle structure

### Q: What is familial hypercholesterolaemia?

- **Autosomal dominant** (main form), gene-dosage effect.
- Defective **LDL receptor** → failure to clear LDL from blood.
- Cholesterol **> 20 mmol/L** (normal 1–5 mmol/L).
- Xanthomas + early atherosclerosis; untreated → **fatal MI before age 20**.

🟨 Treatment threshold: **LDL > 5 mmol/L → statin indicated** in its own right.

## 4. LDLR / PCSK9 / statins — the feedback loop

```
   Blood LDL ──► LDLR (hepatocyte) ──► cholesterol enters cell
                     ▲                           │
                     │                           │ inhibits transcription
              PCSK9 ┤ degrades LDLR              ▼
                                           HMGCR ↓
                                             │
                                             ▼
                                   less cholesterol synthesised
```

### Q: How do statins lower blood LDL despite acting inside hepatocytes?

- Block **HMG-CoA reductase** → less cholesterol synthesis.
- Hepatocyte senses "cholesterol-hungry" state → **upregulates LDLR**.
- More LDLR pulls more LDL from blood → plasma LDL falls.

### Q: How do PCSK9 inhibitors work, and why are they additive to statins?

- PCSK9 normally **degrades LDLR**.
- Inhibiting PCSK9 → **more LDLR survives** on hepatocyte surface.
- More hepatic LDL uptake → plasma LDL falls.
- Hit the same loop at two points → additive.

🟥 Trap: "PCSK9 degrades LDL" — wrong. PCSK9 degrades the **LDL receptor**.

### Q: What forms of PCSK9 inhibitors are available?

- Monoclonal antibodies.
- Antisense oligonucleotides.
- siRNA.

Used for severe or statin-resistant hyperlipidaemia. PCSK9-deficient humans are naturally protected.

### Q: What is reverse cholesterol transport?

- Foam cells export cholesterol via **ABCA1 / ABCG1** pumps.
- Pumps are selective for **Apolipoprotein A** (on HDL).
- HDL carries cholesterol to liver → broken down.
- **The only protective outlet** from a plaque.

## 5. Scavenger receptors and foam cells

### Q: 🟦 What is the fundamental regulatory difference between LDLR and scavenger receptors?

- **LDLR** is under negative feedback — cell has enough cholesterol → LDLR downregulated.
- **Scavenger receptors are NOT** under feedback — they keep taking up oxLDL.
- Result: foam cells form because scavenger uptake never switches off.

🟦 Reusable pattern: receptors without feedback → accumulation → toxicity → cell death.

### Q: Name the two main macrophage scavenger receptors and their binding partners.

| Receptor | Alias | OxLDL | Pathogen |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scavenger receptor A | **CD204** | Yes | Gram-positive bacteria (Staph, Strep) |
| Scavenger receptor B | **CD36** | Yes | Malaria parasites |

Both also bind dead cells.

🟥 Trap: **malaria and hepatitis B are NOT atherosclerotic risk factors**, even though CD36 binds malaria. Binding partner ≠ clinical risk.

### Q: Why do scavenger receptors exist at all?

- Evolved as **pathogen-detection machinery**.
- OxLDL has chemical patterns that mimic pathogen surface → accidental binding.
- Brown & Goldstein deduced a "second LDL receptor" after seeing cholesterol accumulate in LDLR-negative FH macrophages.

### Q: What is a foam cell and why do they look foamy?

- Macrophage stuffed with oxLDL via scavenger receptors.
- Cytoplasm fills with lipid vacuoles → frothy appearance.
- Stained with **anti-CD68** antibody (brown) — CD68 is a macrophage lysosome marker.

🟥 CD68 is a **marker**, not a scavenger receptor. Don't confuse with CD204/CD36.

📎 Figure L1.11 — Foam cell progression on CD68 histology

### Q: What is the balance that determines whether oxLDL causes inflammation or homeostasis?

- **Inflammation side**: scavenger receptors activate "bug-detector" pathways.
- **Homeostasis side**: safe clearance via reverse cholesterol transport (HDL).
- Tipping depends on oxLDL input vs HDL-mediated rescue capacity.

## 6. What activated macrophages do — four outputs

```
                     Activated macrophage / foam cell
                                   │
         ┌──────────┬──────────────┼──────────────┬───────────┐
         ▼          ▼              ▼              ▼           ▼
       ROS      Cytokines     Chemokines    Growth factors   MMPs
     (oxidise   (IL-1 →       (MCP-1 →      (PDGF, TGF-β →  (degrade
      more LDL) recruit +     recruit        VSMC collagen   cap collagen)
                inflame)      monocytes)    synthesis)
```

### Q: Name the three oxidant systems in activated macrophages.

- **NADPH oxidase** → superoxide (O₂⁻).
- **Myeloperoxidase (MPO)** → HOCl (bleach) and ONOO⁻ (peroxynitrite).
- Generation of **H₂O₂**.

Close a positive feedback loop: oxidants generate more oxLDL → more activation.

### Q: What does IL-1 do in atherosclerosis?

- Triggered by **cholesterol crystals** and **NFκB**.
- Activates endothelial adhesion molecules (recruits monocytes).
- Drives elevated **CRP**.
- Coordinates cell death and proliferation.
- Blocked therapeutically by **canakinumab** (CANTOS).

🟨 IL-1 is *the* cytokine of atherosclerosis for exam purposes.

### Q: What does MCP-1 do, and why can't we just block it in patients?

- **Chemokine** binding to **CCR2** (GPCR) on monocytes.
- Recruits monocytes into plaques.
- Knockout mice are almost resistant to atherosclerosis.
- **In humans: blocking CCR2 = immunosuppression** — can't use broadly.

🟦 Reusable pattern: anti-inflammatory cardiovascular drugs hit a clinical ceiling of **immunosuppression**. Specific drugs (anti-IL-1) win over broad recruitment blockers.

### Q: What growth factors do plaque macrophages release, and what do they do?

- **PDGF** → VSMC chemotaxis, survival, mitosis.
- **TGF-β** → increased collagen synthesis, matrix deposition.
- Together drive VSMC phenotypic switch (contractile → synthetic) → builds the **fibrous cap**.

### Q: What is the VSMC phenotypic switch?

| Phenotype | Contractile filaments | Matrix/collagen genes |
|---|---|---|
| Contractile (normal media) | ↑ | ↓ |
| Synthetic (atherosclerotic) | ↓ | ↑ |

Synthetic VSMCs behave like fibroblasts — they make collagen, not contraction. This is **protective** (cap formation).

### Q: What are MMPs and why do they matter?

- **Matrix metalloproteinases** — ~28 homologous enzymes.
- **Zinc-dependent** catalytic mechanism.
- **Activate each other** by proteolysis (cascade).
- **Degrade collagen** → thin the fibrous cap.

🟨 The single most exam-relevant mechanism of plaque rupture: **MMPs digest the cap faster than VSMCs can build it**.

📎 Figure L1.12 — Macrophages vs collagen on coronary plaque histology

### Q: What kills foam cells and what does their death release?

- Toxic oxLDL metabolites — especially **7-keto-cholesterol** — overwhelm survival pathways.
- Foam cells undergo **apoptosis**.
- Release **tissue factor** and toxic oxylipids into the **lipid necrotic core**.
- Walled off by the fibrous cap until rupture.

### Q: What are the six main functions of plaque macrophages (summary)?

- Secrete inflammatory cytokines and chemokines.
- Phagocytose, process and export cholesterol (reverse cholesterol transport).
- Secrete oxidants (ROS, HOCl, ONOO⁻).
- Accumulate cholesterol → become overloaded and activated.
- Secrete MMPs → degrade fibrous cap collagen.
- Initiate death of VSMCs.

## 7. NFκB — the master regulator

```
           Scavenger   Toll-like    Cytokine      Cholesterol
           receptors   receptors    receptors     crystals
                │          │          │              │
                └──────────┴─────┬────┴──────────────┘
                                 ▼
                              ┌─────┐
                              │NFκB │   "master regulator"
                              └─────┘   non-redundant network hub
                                 │
                ┌────────┬───────┼────────┐
                ▼        ▼       ▼        ▼
              MMPs      iNOS    IL-1    (many others)
```

### Q: What is NFκB and why is it "non-redundant"?

- Transcription factor — the **master regulator** of inflammation.
- Integrates multiple upstream signals into coordinated gene expression.
- "Non-redundant" = **no parallel pathway substitutes** for it.
- Output gene IL-1 is also an upstream activator → self-amplifying loop.

🟥 Trap: transcript says "I'll just skate over this" — but NFκB is the **correct answer to Q6**. Treat as high-yield despite verbal down-pedal.

### Q: 🟦 Reusable pattern — "master regulator" MCQs.

- Stem describes ONE transcription factor activated by MANY inputs that coordinates MANY inflammatory genes in concert.
- In cardiovascular inflammation → **NFκB**.
- Same shape elsewhere: HIF-1α (hypoxia), p53 (DNA damage), STATs (cytokine signalling). Recognise the architecture.

## 8. Plaque rupture, Virchow's triad, and clinical events

### Q: What are the features of a vulnerable (rupture-prone) plaque?

- **Thin fibrous cap**.
- **Large soft eccentric lipid-rich necrotic core**.
- Reduced VSMC content; **increased VSMC apoptosis**.
- Reduced collagen.
- Infiltrate of activated macrophages expressing **MMPs**.

Stable plaques are the mirror image (thick cap, small core, abundant VSMCs and collagen).

📎 Figure L1.13 — Gross section of ruptured plaque with thrombus

📎 Figure L1.14 — Ruptured plaque H&E with inflammatory infiltrate

### Q: How does a ruptured plaque hit all three legs of Virchow's triad?

- **Abnormal vessel wall** — tissue factor exposed, collagen exposed, endothelium gone, oxylipids and dead cells exposed.
- **Abnormal blood** — increased clotting factors in at-risk patients, smoking raises coagulability.
- **Abnormal flow** — tight stenosis → turbulent flow.

All three at once → clot forms in seconds.

### Q: What is the sequence from foam-cell death to MI?

- Macrophages digest cap collagen via MMPs.
- VSMCs apoptose → no replacement collagen.
- Cap thins → blood pressure snaps it.
- Blood meets **tissue factor** in necrotic core → **instant thrombosis**.
- Artery occludes → downstream tissue dies.

### Q: What are the classic clinical presentations?

- **MI**: severe central crushing chest pain, fear, dizziness, nausea.
- **Ischaemic stroke**: loss of function one side of body.
- **Angina**: milder pain, **exercise-triggered, relieved by rest or GTN**.
- **Downstream tissue death** wherever the occlusion occurred.

### Q: How do you recognise an anteroseptal STEMI?

- Occluded vessel: **left anterior descending (LAD)**.
- Infarct territory: **anterior wall and septum**.
- Heart looks: **pale wedge** of dead myocardium (mitochondria released).
- ECG: **ST-elevation in leads V1–V4**, T-wave inversion follows later.

🟦 Reusable pattern: supply territory = infarct territory = ECG lead pattern. Occlude LAD → anteroseptal → V1–V4.

## 9. Acute treatment logic

### Q: Why does low-dose aspirin selectively block platelet TxA₂?

- Aspirin irreversibly acetylates **COX** in both platelets and endothelium.
- **Platelets are anucleate** — cannot resynthesise COX (~7–10 day lifespan).
- **Endothelial cells have nuclei** — resynthesise COX.
- Net: platelet TxA₂ permanently blocked; endothelial PGI₂ recovers → balance tips against aggregation.

```
Arachidonic acid ──(COX)── Cyclic endoperoxides
                              │
              ┌───────────────┴────────────────┐
              ▼                                ▼
        Thromboxane synthetase         Prostacyclin synthetase
         (platelets)                    (endothelium)
              │                                │
              ▼                                ▼
         TxA₂ → aggregation             PGI₂ → inhibits aggregation
```

### Q: What is the mechanism of clopidogrel?

- Irreversibly blocks the platelet **ADP receptor (P2Y12)**.
- Blocks platelet-platelet recruitment signal.
- Used with aspirin ("**dual antiplatelet therapy**") — hits two independent activation pathways.

### Q: What is the mechanism of thrombolytics?

- Activate **plasmin** from plasminogen → plasmin **cuts fibrin**.
- **tPA** and **streptokinase** cleave an inhibitor off plasmin.
- Clotting factors are serine proteases that cut other proteins.

🟥 Antiplatelets (aspirin, clopidogrel) ≠ thrombolytics (tPA, streptokinase). Antiplatelets prevent new aggregation; thrombolytics dissolve existing fibrin. Both are needed in acute MI.

### Q: Map acute MI drugs onto Virchow's triad.

- **Abnormal vessel wall** → PCI (stent) — mechanical fix.
- **Abnormal blood (platelets)** → aspirin + clopidogrel.
- **Abnormal blood (fibrin)** → tPA / streptokinase.
- **Abnormal flow** → restored by PCI or successful thrombolysis.

### Q: Reproduce the seven-step narration of the whole lecture.

- **1.** Risk factors + eddy flow → endothelial activation.
- **2.** LDL leaks in → trapped by proteoglycans.
- **3.** Free radicals oxidise LDL → oxLDL.
- **4.** Macrophages take up oxLDL via scavenger receptors (no feedback) → foam cells.
- **5.** Foam cells secrete ROS, cytokines/chemokines, growth factors (build cap), and MMPs (digest cap).
- **6.** Overloaded foam cells apoptose → tissue factor + toxic oxylipids → lipid necrotic core.
- **7.** MMPs > VSMCs/collagen → cap ruptures → thrombosis → MI or stroke.

### Q: Name the three positive feedback loops driving atherosclerosis progression.

- **Oxidative**: ROS → more oxLDL → more activation → more ROS.
- **Recruitment**: chemokines → more monocytes → more chemokines.
- **Transcriptional**: NFκB → IL-1 → IL-1R → NFκB.

Breaking any one slows progression; CANTOS broke the transcriptional loop.

### Q: What is the examiner's one-sentence summary of atherosclerosis?

> Atherosclerosis is an inflammatory disease that causes most heart attacks and strokes — LDL is oxidised to activate macrophages, which damage the artery wall in multiple ways regulated by **NFκB**, and plaque rupture from this damage, **particularly activation of collagen degradation**, is what produces the clinical event.

## 11. Reusable exam-reasoning patterns

### Q: 🟦 How to spot a "not a fatberg" distractor.

- Options framing disease as "silting," "build-up," "passive deposition" are nearly always wrong.
- Atherosclerosis is **active immune response** to modified fat.

### Q: 🟦 How to spot a "binding partner vs risk factor" trap.

- CD36 binds malaria parasites — does **NOT** mean malaria is a cardiovascular risk factor.
- CD204 binds Staph/Strep — same logic.
- Binding partners are receptor targets; risk factors are clinical predictors. Don't swap them.

### Q: 🟦 How to spot an overcomplicated MCQ distractor.

- Answers larded with obscure CD numbers (CD15, CD20), IL numbers (IL-15, IL-4 Th2), or telomere biology in a pathology stem.
- Lecturer's rule: "if somebody makes the MCQ answer too complicated, it's because they're making stuff up."

### Q: 🟦 How to recognise a vulnerable plaque stem.

- Five features cluster: thin cap, large soft lipid core, high macrophage/MMP, low VSMC, increased VSMC apoptosis, low collagen.
- If ANY option inverts one of them ("excess collagen," "insufficient proteases") → that option is wrong.
